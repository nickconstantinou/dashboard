## Introduction

In the aftermath of a catastrophic decision to wage war against Iran—one that Carlson views as potentially ending Trump's presidency and gravely harming the United States—the former Fox News host sat down with Lulu Garcia Navarro of The New York Times in Maine for a wide-ranging and at times surreal interview that reveals both the depth of his break with Trump and the strange psychology of his relationship with the man he helped propel into power.

The interview arrives at a moment of profound rupture in American conservatism. Carlson, who built his media empire on proximity to and support for Trump, now characterizes himself as having been "baffled" and "panicked" by decisions he sees as fundamentally betraying the promise of Trumpism. His journey from fierce Trump defender to vocal critic traces a path that many MAGA loyalists are now quietly questioning, even if they lack the platform to say so publicly.

What makes this conversation particularly compelling is not just its content but its context. Carlson is not simply disagreeing with a policy; he is making a series of extraordinary claims about presidential autonomy, foreign influence, and the mechanisms by which American foreign policy gets made. He alleges that Trump felt "he had no choice" and describes the president as more "a hostage than a sovereign decision maker." These are not rhetorical flourishes—they are specific claims about the nature of power in Washington that deserve scrutiny.

The interview also touches on questions that Carlson has raised on his show, including whether Trump might be "the antichrist," and addresses his controversial interview with white nationalist Nick Fuentes. But the Iran war and its aftermath provides the connective tissue that makes sense of Carlson's broader transformation.

---

## The Pre-War Conversations: Inside the Deliberation

Carlson paints a detailed picture of the weeks leading up to the February 28th strike on Iran. He says he flew to Washington three separate times in the five or six weeks before the attack, meeting with Trump in the Oval Office in meetings that included the White House chief of staff and the secretary of state. On at least one occasion, he had lunch with the president privately. He also spoke by phone many times on the topic.

The core of these conversations, according to Carlson, was his argument that attacking Iran would be catastrophically bad for the United States—not out of allegiance to Iran, but because of basic geography. Carlson emphasizes that "Iran is not a military power. It's an economic power," pointing to its control of "the greatest span of coastline along the Persian Gulf, which is the source of a fifth of the world's energy." He argues this was "well known" to Trump at the time.

Carlson claims Trump "never seemed enthusiastic about it, ever" and that he detected "no evidence at all of dementia, mental decline," describing Trump as possessing "kind of remarkable powers of insight into people and power dynamics." He states flatly: "You don't get to be president by accident. The guy's smart in the ways that matter politically."

Yet despite understanding the consequences, Carlson says his strong impression was that "he felt he had no choice, and that he was resigned to it." He describes Trump ending one conversation with the phrase "it's gonna be alright," and when Carlson asked "how do you know that?", Trump replied "because it always is." Carlson interprets this not as optimism but as "a kind of justification from a man who feels he has no choice."

The key question becomes: choice against what? And who or what constrains a president who commands the most powerful military in the world?

---

## The Pressure from Outside: "No One in the Building Was Pushing for This"

Carlson is adamant that the decision did not originate from within the administration. "I never saw nor did I hear about anybody who works for the Trump administration, anybody who was enthusiastically pushing this war on Trump," he says. He specifically mentions conversations with the secretary of state, saying he "didn't hear a single time from anyone...any enthusiasm for doing this."

Instead, he identifies external pressure as the driving force: "all the pressure was coming from outside. Constant calls from donors and people with influence over the president, well known Rupert Murdoch, Mary Madelson, etcetera, etcetera." He also names "a small constellation of, I guess, they'd be called influencers, beginning with Mark Levin, but there were others, Sean Hannity, you know, pushing the president to do this and telling him that you will be a figure out of history, you will save and redeem Israel or something."

This framing—that a president surrounded by paid staff who won't push for war is overruled by outside voices with direct phone access—is central to Carlson's critique. He also notes that "Trump engenders cowardice in the people around him through intimidation," creating a situation where internal dissent is silenced while external influencers have the president's ear.

Carlson draws a parallel to the resignation of Joe Kent, the head of the counterterrorism center and one of the top intelligence officials in the country, who resigned shortly after the war began and said "this decision is connected to a series of seemingly disconnected events, all of which revolve around violence." Kent was "dismissed and threatened with an FBI investigation" and "no one kind of followed up on that." Carlson says this reinforces his view that "this was not a normal decision making process."

---

## The Netanyahu Problem: "That Is Slavery"

Carlson's critique of Israeli influence represents the most incendiary portion of the interview. He is explicit: "Trump is being held hostage by whom? By who? By Netanyahu and by his many advocates in the United States."

He supports this claim with specific evidence. After Trump announced what he described as a ceasefire and willingness to negotiate, "Israel publicly in a way that was designed to get the attention of everyone, including the Iranians...starts killing civilians in Lebanon." This, Carlson argues, was "designed to end any talk of a negotiated settlement, to keep this going until Iran was destroyed and chaotic, which is the Israeli goal."

When Navarro challenges this framing by noting Israel would argue they are "neutralizing the threat," Carlson responds: "They invaded Lebanon in 1982. Okay? So that was forty four years ago. They've had a lot of experience in Lebanon. A lot. They've had a lot of time to fix Lebanon. They killed Nasrallah. They blew up Hezbollah with explosive pagers. Like, they've done a lot since October 7 in Lebanon. They chose that moment to derail the negotiations."

Carlson's broader argument is about the nature of the U.S.-Israel relationship. "We're the United States. We're a country of 350,000,000 people. You are wholly dependent on us. You're a country of 9,000,000 people with no natural resources. I'm not against you, but, like, we're not co equals here." He argues that Trump "could not restrain Netanyahu. Netanyahu is the one person Trump could not say, hey. Settle down, or we'll just defund you and your country will collapse in about ten minutes, which is true. Israel can't defend itself without the United States."

The conclusion is stark: "Trump said, I want a negotiated settlement. Israel stopped the settlement. Trump refused to even criticize Netanyahu in public. Are you joking? That's slavery. That is total control of one man by another." Carlson adds that he expects this critique to be met with accusations of antisemitism: "I don't and no amount of, like, oh, you're an anti Semite, which I'm not, and I'm never gonna be, is gonna stop me from noting that that's outrageous."

## The Spell of Trump: "There May Be a Supernatural Component to It"

One of the most unusual passages in the interview finds Carlson describing his personal experience of Trump's effect on those around him. He says: "Trump engenders cowardice in the people around him through intimidation, and there is a kind of quality that he has that's spellbinding, and I think it probably literally is a spell, and the effect is to weaken people around him and make them more compliant and more confused."

He continues: "And I've experienced this myself. You spend a day with Trump and sort of like you're in this kind of dreamland. It's like smoking hash or something. It's interesting. Very interesting. And there may be a supernatural component to it. I'm not a theologian, but I it's real, and anyone who's been around him can tell you it's real."

This description sits alongside Carlson's earlier statement that he has wondered on his show whether Trump might be "the antichrist," something Navarro notes in the introduction. Carlson doesn't directly address this in the provided transcript, but the passage reveals a man grappling with what he perceives as a genuinely unusual quality in the president—one that he frames as potentially supernatural and that he connects to his understanding of how Trump was able to make a decision he seemingly did not want to make.

---

## The Historical Context: Iraq and the Pattern of Foreign Influence

Carlson places the current crisis in a longer history of American presidents prioritizing Israeli interests over American ones. He identifies the Iraq war as "a very obvious example of that," stating: "Cheney's office was completely controlled...by people who were putting Israel's interests above America's interests. So I think the Iraq war was, to a great extent, a product of that."

He notes that this is not unique to Trump: "Many American presidents have put Israel's interests before our own." The difference, he suggests, is scale and transparency. "To switch on something this big in the space of a few months," after years of Trump telling supporters that "our leadership is weak, they act against our interests, they're stupid, they're foolish, they're bought off by foreign powers and by domestic donors—that was Trump's case, that was his whole pitch, that's why he got elected—I mean, that bears some examination."

Carlson acknowledges he cannot fully explain what changed: "What was it about this moment that allowed a foreign leader to have this level of influence over an American leader? I don't know the answer, but, again, I think it's worth finding out."

## Detailed Takeaways

**The distinction between understanding a decision and endorsing it matters.** Carlson repeatedly frames his critique not as defense of Iran but as concern for American interests. He argues Trump "knew" the consequences would be bad for the United States but felt compelled anyway. This suggests that in foreign policy, the question is not whether leaders understand the risks but what constrains their choices—and external pressure from donors and media can apparently override even a president's own assessment of national interest.

**The "hostage" framing implies that presidential autonomy is more constrained than we assume.** Carlson's claim that Trump was "more a hostage than a sovereign decision maker" is significant precisely because it suggests American foreign policy is made through networks of influence that operate outside formal decision-making structures. If no one in the building wanted the war but it happened anyway, that reveals something about where power actually resides.

**Accusations of antisemitism can function as a shield against criticism of Israeli government policy.** Carlson's preemptive statement—"no amount of, like, oh, you're an anti Semite, which I'm not, and I'm never gonna be"—anticipates the response to his critique. He is explicitly separating opposition to Israeli government actions from opposition to Jews as a people or Judaism as a faith, arguing that conflating the two silences legitimate debate about whether American policy serves American interests.

**Charismatic authority may be a factor in political decision-making that gets underdiscussed.** Carlson's description of the "spell" Trump casts—and his suggestion of a "supernatural component"—is unusual for a political interview but reflects a genuine phenomenon in concentrated power. Whether framed psychologically or spiritually, the effect of proximity to powerful leaders on their advisors' willingness to dissent is a real constraint on good governance.

**The Venezuela precedent matters as context for understanding the Iran decision.** Navarro raises the point that Trump may have felt emboldened by the successful removal of Maduro. Carlson rejects this interpretation, noting that Trump's own stated rationale in their conversations was not "this will be easy" but resignation. However, the existence of a successful small-scale intervention may have created conditions that made larger risk-taking seem justifiable to outside influencers pushing for war.

## Who This Is For

This interview is essential reading for anyone trying to understand the fracture lines within American conservatism and the broader debate about foreign policy, Israel, and the influence of money and media on presidential decision-making. Carlson occupies a unique position: a figure who was deeply embedded in the Trump world, who has the platform to speak honestly about what he witnessed, and who has incentives to position himself as the true heir to Trumpism post-Trump. Understanding his perspective—even if one rejects his conclusions—illuminates dynamics that are otherwise hidden from public view.

For journalists and analysts, the interview offers specific claims about the decision-making process that could be investigated further: Who exactly called Trump? What exactly did they say? What is the mechanism by which a president who understands a decision is bad nonetheless makes it? These are questions that go to the heart of how American power functions.

For voters, particularly those who supported Trump based on his stated opposition to endless wars and his promise to put America first, Carlson's account provides a framework for understanding a bewildering policy shift. Whether or not one finds his explanation persuasive, it represents the most detailed insider account available of how the Iran war came to pass—and the role of foreign influence in shaping American foreign policy that voters may feel they never consented to.</p>
