# The FPL One-Season Wonders: A Nostalgic Look at Fantasy Football's Most Elusive Gems

For millions of Fantasy Premier League managers, the holy grail of the game is identifying value—finding that budget player who delivers elite-level returns before the rest of the market catches on. But for every shrewd pick that sustains their form, there's a rarer creature: the one-season wonder. These are players who, whether through circumstance, unsustainable form, or perfect storm of factors, delivered extraordinary FPL returns for a single campaign before fading back into irrelevance. A new retrospective from Fantasy Football Scout celebrates these fleeting phenoms, assembling a nostalgic squad of 15 players who briefly burned bright before cooling into budget options or irrelevance.

The article spans a "squad of 15"—two goalkeepers, five defenders, five midfielders, and three forwards—though the piece focuses primarily on the goalkeepers before cutting off. Among those highlighted, Vito Mannone stands out as a quintessential example of the one-season wonder phenomenon. The Italian goalkeeper spent 11 seasons in the Premier League, but only in 2013/14 did he start more matches than not—and even then, he began the campaign as Sunderland's number two. Mannone stepped into the starting XI in Gameweek 10 after Kieran Westwood suffered an injury, and at just £4.2m, he was an absolute bargain. Over 28 starts, he accumulated 11 clean sheets and save points in 20 of those appearances—including a remarkable victory over Chelsea where he made 14 saves. He finished the season with 134 points and an average of 4.6 points per game, outperforming every other first-choice goalkeeper. Mannone was so good that Fantasy Football Scout readers voted him into their Team of the Season. The following campaign, however, his spell ended abruptly when he lost his place in Gameweek 10—shortly after Sunderland's infamous 8-0 defeat at Southampton. He would only return sporadically for Sunderland before departing for Reading, eventually playing in the United States, Denmark, and France before retiring.

Michel Vorm represents another quintessential one-season wonder, though his story carries extra resonance given current FPL frustrations. In 2011/12, the Dutch goalkeeper joined newly promoted Swansea City for their inaugural Premier League season, immediately establishing himself as first choice after Dorus de Vries departed. At a mere £4.0m, Vorm delivered a phenomenal campaign: 14 clean sheets, 143 saves, and 158 points. That points tally matches what Jordan Pickford—the league's top-scoring keeper in 2024/25—accumulated last season, and only the title-winning Joe Hart at £7.0m bested Vorm that year. Managers who identified this budget gem were rewarded handsomely, watching his price rise from £4.0m to a high of £5.3m while becoming one of the most-owned players in the game by season's end. Our site users voted him as the Team of the Season goalkeeper—a testament to just how dominant his returns were relative to his cost.

The broader significance of this retrospective extends beyond mere nostalgia. FPL managers constantly face the challenge of distinguishing sustainable improvement from statistical anomalies. These one-season wonders serve as cautionary tales and learning opportunities: identifying when a player's value is artificially inflated by unsustainable save percentages, favorable run of fixtures, or temporary number-one status can mean the difference between climbing the ranks and hemorrhaging points. The article implicitly reminds readers that context matters enormously—Mannone's emergence coincided with Sunderland's injury crisis, while Vorm benefited from Swansea's newly promoted defensive structure. Understanding these circumstances helps managers avoid the trap of anchoring to previous-season prices or assuming last year's bargain will repeat. As FPL continues to grow in popularity, with millions of players competing globally, these lessons become increasingly valuable for anyone seeking an edge over the competition.
