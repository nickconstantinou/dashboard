Az Discusses His Latest Draft & Rank History | Pre Season | Fantasy Premier League Tips 2025/26

Introduction

With less than two weeks remaining until the 2025/26 Fantasy Premier League season kicks off, the FDL Blackbox team is delivering its annual procession of team reveals and strategic analysis. In this video, Az—one of the channel's three main presenters—takes his turn walking viewers through his current draft, examining the fixtures, and explaining the reasoning behind his player selections. The channel, which also features Andy and the recently returned Luke, has established itself as a hub for FPL enthusiasts seeking deeper analytical content beyond surface-level team tips.

However, what makes this particular video particularly compelling is not merely the tactical breakdown ofAz's draft—which will certainly interest those building their own teams—but rather his unexpected detour into transparency about his recent performance struggles. Addressing what he describes as "the elephant in the room," Az uses this platform to directly respond to the criticism he has faced regarding his FPL results over the past three seasons. This moment of candor transforms the video from a standard preseason preview into something more confessional: an honest reckoning with public failure that will resonate with any FPL manager who has experienced the pain of a disastrous season.

The Transparency About Recent FPL Performance

Az opens by acknowledging the "quite a lot of negativity" circulating around content creators on Twitter and similar platforms, stating that he has received "a fair bit of stick" for what he describes as "quite a poor performance over the last couple of years." He immediately concedes that "some of it rightly so" before proceeding to present his complete FPL record—a remarkable display of transparency that few content creators would emulate.

His historical performance reveals a player who experienced significant success in the middle years of his FPL career before a dramatic decline. Az explains that his first three seasons from 2007 represented a learning period, with an average rank of 186K. The turning point came between 2010 and 2012, when he achieved his first top 5K finish (placing 3,800th in 2010), followed by 175K in 2011 and an agonizing 10,600th place finish in 2012—missing the top 10K by just one point. The period from 2013 to 2016 proved his most successful, with an average rank of 20K and another 5K finish, including a remarkable 2,000th place in 2014.

His best-ever season came in 2017/18, when Az finished an extraordinary 800th in the world—a result he clearly treasures. He followed that with a 62K finish the following year, giving him a three-year average of approximately 40K during this golden period. The FDL Blackbox venture began in 2020, and while that initial season produced a 135K finish, he bounced back strongly in 2021/22 with what he describes as "a really good season," bringing his three-year average for that period to 52K. During this successful stretch, he was "quite high up in the hall of fame tables and things," though he notes he "never broke into the top 10."

The past three seasons, however, tell a dramatically different story. In 2022/23, Az finished 143K, though he notes he was around one million in the world with six weeks remaining before mounting a comeback. The 2023/24 season was the nadir—finish of 445K, which he openly calls "embarrassing" and "really bad." He admits that with around 250K remaining, he made a series of high-risk "tilt decisions," including ignoring Erling Haaland at home to Wolves, captaining Cole Palmer, and choosing Cristian Romero over Pedro Porro. None of these gambles paid off, and he "tanked down the ranks" to his disastrous finish. Last season (2024/25) produced a 265K finish, which Az describes as a continuation of his struggles, admitting he was "too slow" to react to premium picks like Mohamed Salah.

The Self-Critical Analysis

What distinguishes this video from mere defensiveness is Az's genuine attempt to understand why his results have deteriorated so significantly. He identifies several interconnected factors that have contributed to his decline.

First, Az believes he has been "too slow to react" to the template—meaning the popular set of players that most successful FPL managers target. He explains that he has "completely ignored things like review" and "tried to go against the template too much," thinking that his own research and intuition could outperform the collective wisdom of the FPL community. He acknowledges that while this approach might have worked in earlier seasons when fewer people played and fewer analytical tools existed, the modern FPL landscape is vastly different.

Second, Az acknowledges that he and Mark (his Blackbox co-host) have "pushed too much against" data-driven approaches in recent years. He specifically mentions that "models and things like they need to be incorporated a little bit because they are so good at synthesizing that data." This represents a significant admission from a content creator who has often emphasized qualitative analysis over statistical modeling. He notes that neither he nor Mark performed particularly well during this period, joking that their only battle that mattered was "me beating [Mark] by one point on that last day" in 2022/23.

Third, Az reflects on how his relationship with FPL has changed since his early days playing the game. He recalls that "back in the day" he "watched a lot more football" and used that viewing to make more informed decisions. Now, with a full-time job and the demands of content creation, he has less time for live matches—though he still tries to maintain this habit.

His Position on Being Called an "Expert"

Az addresses the irony of being marketed as an "expert" by the FPL Show and Scout (both of whom he works for) while acknowledging his recent struggles. He states he would "never call myself" an expert "with anything really, let alone FPL," calling it "obviously the marketing term they use." When asked why he doesn't ask them to stop using the label, Az responds pragmatically: he's "not going to tell the Premier League, you know, how to market me" and "not going to turn down work because, you know, I love working for the Premier League and I love doing stuff for Scout." He frames the choice as between being called an expert "reluctantly, but carry on doing it" or not doing it at all—and the former obviously won.

The criticism has been severe enough that "there's a lot of people are being blocked now on Twitter," and Az has been called various names. However, he reveals that he "just can't really be bothered" to engage with the negativity, emphasizing that he has "a full-time job" and does FPL content "for fun" as "a passion that I've had since 2007/08." He loves making content and "get[s] great opportunities" through the work, and "for the moment," has "no plans" to stop despite the abuse.

The Draft Discussion

After this substantial detour into self-analysis, Az turns to his actual FPL draft for the upcoming season—though the transcript provides limited detail on his specific team selections. He indicates he will be "looking at the fixtures and the reasoning behind some of those players" and examining "some of the other players that might make it into the draft."

Given that this video was recorded just over a week before the season start, Az's draft represents a snapshot of his thinking at a crucial moment in the preseason period. The emphasis on fixtures suggests a tactical approach focused on favorable early-season scheduling—a common FPL strategy that can provide a foundation for points accumulation in the opening gameweeks.

Detailed Takeaways

Takeaway 1: Transparency About Performance Builds Credibility Az's decision to openly discuss his three consecutive poor seasons (143K, 445K, 265K) rather than hiding them demonstrates a refreshing approach to content creation. By acknowledging his failures directly and even joking about losing to his wife Yelena "three years in a row," he transforms what could be a liability into trust-building. FPL managers who respect honesty will find this approach more credible than creators who only showcase their successes.

Takeaway 2: The Template Exists for a Reason Az's self-criticism that he was "too slow" to include players like Salah and "tried to go against the template too much" serves as a warning about the dangers of contrarianism for its own sake. While differentiation can yield advantages, ignoring proven premium assets simply to be different represents a fundamental strategic error. The lesson: respect the template until you have strong evidence to deviate from it.

Takeaway 3: Data Models Should Be Incorporated, Not Ignored Az's admission that he and Mark "pushed too much against" analytical models represents a significant shift in his thinking. He now acknowledges that "models and things like they need to be incorporated a little bit because they are so good at synthesizing that data." This suggests that even qualitative analysts should find ways to integrate statistical insights into their decision-making rather than relying solely on intuition or "watching more football."

Takeaway 4: Tilt Decisions Destroy Seasons The 2023/24 season serves as a case study in how tilt—making desperate, high-risk decisions after a poor start—compounds problems rather than solving them. Az's decision to "ignore Haaland, home to Wolves, Captain Palmer" and make "lots of tilt decisions" after falling to 250K resulted in his catastrophic 445K finish. The lesson: maintaining discipline during bad runs is crucial, as gambling to recover often makes things worse.

Takeaway 5: FPL Should Remain Fun Despite the criticism, Az emphasizes that he continues creating content because he genuinely enjoys it: "This is like a passion that I've had since 0708. Um, I love making content." This perspective serves as a useful reminder that FPL, at its core, is a game meant to enhance the enjoyment of football—not a source of stress or abuse. Managers struggling with the pressure of FPL might benefit from adopting Az's stated attitude: play for the love of the game.

Who This Is For

This video provides value to several distinct audiences within the FPL community. First, managers who appreciate behind-the-scenes honesty will find Az's candid discussion of his recent failures genuinely refreshing—a counterpoint to creators who only showcase successful outcomes. Second, managers who have experienced their own poor seasons will find solidarity in Az's acknowledgment that even "experts" can struggle, potentially alleviating some of the shame associated with bad results.

Third, managers interested in strategic analysis will benefit from Az's self-diagnosis of why his results declined—particularly his insights about template adherence, data incorporation, and tilt avoidance. Finally, managers who follow the FDL Blackbox channel will appreciate the context Az provides about his working relationship with Scout and the Premier League, understanding better how content creators are positioned within the FPL ecosystem.

For anyone building their FPL team in the final days before the season, Az's discussion of fixtures and player reasoning provides tactical insight. However, the more lasting value lies in his lessons about strategic humility, data integration, and maintaining perspective—lessons that apply far beyond any single FPL season.
