Az Discusses His Latest Draft & Rank History | Pre Season | Fantasy Premier League Tips 2025/26

Introduction

As the 2025/26 Fantasy Premier League season approaches—now just over a week away—content creators across the FPL community are unveiling their drafts and strategizing for the campaign ahead. In this installment of the FDL Blackbox channel, Az sits down to walk viewers through his current team selection, examining the fixtures and reasoning behind his player choices. This video marks the second in a series of team reveal content, following Andy's contribution earlier in the week, with Luke expected to deliver his own draft in the near future.

The timing of this video carries particular significance given the broader context surrounding FPL content creators in recent years. Az begins by directly addressing what he calls "the elephant in the room"—the considerable negativity directed toward content creators on platforms like Twitter, with specific criticism aimed at his own performance over the past three seasons. Rather than deflecting, Az offers an unusually transparent breakdown of his FPL history, revealing his ranks from 2007 through the present day, acknowledging his recent struggles, and explaining the lessons he hopes to apply to his new draft approach.

The Transparency Moment: Az Addresses His Critics

Az opens up about the criticism he's received, acknowledging that some of it is justified given his recent results. He explains that while he works for the FPL Show and Scout—organizations that use marketing terms like "expert" to describe their contributors—he never personally adopted that label for himself. "That's obviously the marketing term they use," Az states. "Never something I would ever call myself with anything really, let alone FPL."

The creator addresses the tension between his professional work with the Premier League-affiliated platforms and his personal performance, noting that he wouldn't tell the Premier League how to market him. "If they don't want to use me, if they don't think I'm an expert, then they can not use me. That's absolutely fine," he explains. "I have no say in what they choose to call me, and I'm not going to turn down work because, you know, I love working for the Premier League and I love doing stuff for Scout."

Az reveals that the criticism has become substantial enough that he's blocked several people on Twitter, though he emphasizes that he doesn't intend to stop creating content. "I've got a full-time job. I do this for fun. This is like a passion that I've had since 0708," he says. "I love making content. I get great opportunities. I really enjoy doing Blackbox, you know, with Mark and now with Andy and Luke. So, I'm not going to stop that while I'm enjoying it."

A Deep Dive Into FPL Rank History

Rather than simply dismissing the criticism, Az provides a comprehensive overview of his FPL career dating back to 2007, offering viewers a clear picture of his track record. His first three years were essentially a learning period, but he achieved a strong finish that launched his subsequent opportunities.

From 2010 to 2012, Az experienced his first major breakthrough, securing his first top 5,000 finish with a rank of 3,800 in the 2012/13 season. The following year brought a 175,000 finish, which Az describes as "wasn't that great," but he bounced back with a 10,600 finish in 2014. "I remember that season because I finished one point outside the top 10k and I was gutted, determined to get back in again," he recalls.

The period from 2013 to 2016 represented Az's golden era, with an average rank of approximately 20,000 across those seasons—including another 5,000 finish and a remarkable 2,000 rank in 2014. "That was a good couple of years," Az acknowledges. "I had a pretty decent run between 13 and 15."

The 2016/17 season marked a turning point when Az first joined the Scout Cast team, finishing around 59,000 that year. However, 2017/18 became his best-ever season, culminating in an 800th-place finish. He followed that with a 62,000 finish the next year, representing an average rank of 40,000 across that two-year span.

When he launched Blackbox in 2020, Az admits his first season was "pretty lousy," finishing 135,000. Yet he reminds viewers that his rank was substantially lower during the actual season before he mounted a comeback. The 2021/22 season proved to be another strong campaign, with an average rank of 52,000 across that three-year period. "So I mean over that four-period, I mean I did well," Az reflects. "My three-year average was—you know, the lowest was 63k. So you know, I had pretty good—I was quite high up in some of the um, you know, the hall of fame tables and things."

The Recent Struggles: Three Years of Disappointment

Az doesn't shy away from discussing his recent performance, calling the last three seasons "a complete disaster." The 2022/23 season saw him finish 143,000, though he notes he was around the one-million mark with six weeks remaining before managing to turn things around—including a dramatic last-day finish where he beat Mark by just one point. "Um, so actually I'm overall quite happy with how that season went," Az explains. "I managed to turn things around. It could have been a hell of a lot worse."

The 2023/24 season proved far more damaging. Az finished 445,000—a result he describes as "embarrassing" and "really bad." He owns that his own decision-making contributed significantly to this outcome: "The one justification I've got for that is I was around 200-250k and I was like, 'Okay, I can sort of settle for a pretty rubbish finish or I can really push and try and get into the top 100k.' And I did push. I ignored like Harland, home to Wolves, Captain Palmer. I think I went for like Romero over Porro. I made lots of like tilt decisions and hope some of them would pay off and absolutely none of them paid off."

The 2024/25 season brought more struggles, with Az unable to find any consistent form. He identifies his slowness to react to popular picks—particularly Mohamed Salah—as a central problem. "I've just been too slow to react," he admits. "I've tried to go against the template too much. I've completely ignored things like review. Always thought, you know, this isn't the way to play the game. I want to pick players based on what I've done before."

The Strategic Shift: Embracing the Template

Perhaps the most significant insight from this video is Az's frank assessment of his strategic philosophy and what he believes needs to change. He acknowledges that previous seasons had fewer analytical tools available, meaning his approach of watching more football and making decisions based on personal observation provided a meaningful edge. However, he now believes the models and data analysis tools have become so sophisticated that ignoring them entirely puts him at a severe disadvantage.

"I think the models and things like they need to be incorporated a little bit because they are so good at synthesizing that data," Az explains. He specifically calls out himself and Mark (his Blackbox co-host) for "pushed too much against that over the last few years," noting that neither has performed well during this period. "Neither of us have had particularly good years," he admits.

This self-reflection leads Az to indicate that his new draft will likely be more aligned with popular template selections rather than deliberately contrarian choices. He's essentially conceding that the old approach of identifying value based on personal intuition no longer provides sufficient edge in an era of advanced analytics.

Az also addresses the role of luck and variance in FPL results, noting that factors like the bench boost chip and the controversial 11-point gameweek in 2022/23 didn't help his cause—though he quickly adds that these don't excuse his overall performance. He's clear that his focus now is on implementing what he's learned from these difficult seasons.

Channel Updates and Community Engagement

Beyond his personal performance, Az provides brief updates about the FDL Blackbox channel's direction for the new season. He confirms that Luke will be contributing weekly videos to their Instagram platform, where they'll be sharing graphics and statistical tables. The main YouTube channel will continue providing extensive preseason content, with the weekly streams (at least two per week) resuming throughout the campaign.

For viewers new to his content, Az offers this context: if they've wondered about the credentials of "this guy who's been called an expert and finished 265K and 440K, 440K," they now have the full picture. "I've done all right. I've done all right in the past," he summarizes. "And I guess the journey with me now is to see whether I can get back to the old—the old ranks, the ranks of yesterday year, which seem like a long distant memory."

There's also a personal element to Az's motivation: he reveals that his partner Yelena has beaten him in their personal rivalry for three consecutive years, and he's determined to change that trajectory. "The main thing is not to lose to Yelena again," he says with evident determination. "Really, she beat me three years in a row, and it's just getting a bit embarrassing."

Detailed Takeaways

Takeaway 1: Transparency Builds Trust Even When Results Are Poor Az's decision to openly address his recent rank history—including specific finishes like 445K—rather than ignoring the criticism demonstrates a commitment to authenticity with his audience. While many content creators might deflect or minimize poor performance, Az owned his struggles completely. This level of honesty, particularly about the 2023/24 season where he "tilted" his team with risky decisions, creates a foundation of trust that makes his subsequent advice more meaningful. For viewers, this demonstrates that even experienced FPL managers go through difficult periods and that acknowledging mistakes is the first step toward improvement.

Takeaway 2: The Analytical Tools Have Changed the Game Az's admission that he's been "too slow to react" to template picks and that he's "completely ignored things like review" represents a significant philosophical shift. He's explicitly stating that the advanced models and data synthesis now available have reached a level where ignoring them constitutes playing at a disadvantage. This insight is particularly valuable for experienced managers who pride themselves on independent analysis—the message isn't that intuition is worthless, but that it must be combined with statistical evidence rather than used as a replacement for it.

Takeaway 3: Chasing Rank Can Be Self-Destructive The 2023/24 season provides a cautionary tale about the dangers of "pushing" when already in a comfortable position. Az was sitting around 200-250k—an acceptable if unspectacular finish—yet chose to make risky, contrarian decisions in pursuit of a top 100k finish. The result was a catastrophic drop to 445k. This illustrates a common psychological trap in FPL: the desire to improve a position often leads to worse outcomes than simply accepting a reasonable finish. The lesson applies broadly: once you've secured a usable rank, the marginal gains from aggressive moves rarely justify the variance risk.

Takeaway 4: The Template Exists for a Reason Az's indication that his new draft will likely be more template-aligned reflects an important truth about FPL: popular selections become popular because the data supports them. While being contrarian can yield advantages in specific scenarios, consistently fighting the consensus without sufficient analytical backing puts players at risk of missing obvious value. This doesn't mean blindly following the crowd, but it does suggest that deviations from the template require clear, data-supported justification rather than mere intuition.

Takeaway 5: Longevity Requires Enjoyment, Not Just Results Az explicitly states he's continuing FPL content creation because he enjoys it: "This is like a passion that I've had since 0708." Despite facing ridicule, blocked accounts, and genuine performance issues, his motivation remains intrinsic rather than outcome-dependent. This perspective offers a model for managing long-term engagement with competitive hobbies—recognizing that the activity itself should provide satisfaction, not solely the results. If the criticism ever becomes unbearable, Az notes he would consider stepping away, but that threshold hasn't been reached.
