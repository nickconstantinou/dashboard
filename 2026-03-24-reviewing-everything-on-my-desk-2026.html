Reviewing Everything on My Desk: A Deep Dive into KHC's 2026 Workspace Setup

Introduction

In the world of tech content creation, few things reveal more about a creator's priorities, workflow, and philosophy than a detailed look at their workspace. YouTuber KHC has spent years producing high-quality tech reviews and tutorials, and in this comprehensive desk tour video, they offer viewers an intimate look at the gear that has served them throughout their career. The video isn't about unboxing new products or providing first impressions—instead, it's a retrospective review of the equipment they have used extensively, some for many years, some for over a decade. This approach provides a uniquely valuable perspective: these aren't products tested in controlled review environments but tools that have been battle-tested in real-world, professional production environments.

The timing of this desk review is particularly interesting given the rapid pace of technology evolution. When KHC acquired much of this gear, some of it represented the absolute pinnacle of what was available—Apple's most powerful computer, professional-grade reference monitors, premium ergonomic seating. Now, several years later, the landscape has shifted considerably. New chip generations have emerged, newer monitor technologies have proliferated, and some of what was once cutting-edge is now showing its age. This creates a fascinating tension between long-term investment value and the inevitable march of technological progress that makes this desk tour especially valuable for anyone building or upgrading their own creative workspace.

The Desk: Next Desk Air Pro

The centerpiece of the workspace is the Next Desk Air Pro, a sit-stand desk that KHC has owned for nearly a decade. The desk measures an impressive 96 inches across by 38 inches deep—essentially an 8-foot by 3-foot surface that provides massive workspace for multiple monitors, equipment, and workspace organization. What makes this desk particularly remarkable is its construction: the surface is just a quarter inch thick, consisting of a single piece of aluminum that somehow manages to support all of KHC's equipment without bending, rocking, or shaking. The desk is described as "rock solid," a description that conveys both its physical stability and its reliability as a long-term workspace solution.

KHC notes a fascinating detail about this desk: they purchased it when the company was still called Next Desk, but the company has since rebranded to Xesk. The original "Next Desk" branding remains visible on the small controller unit used for height adjustment, which KHC identifies as a kind of "OG" status marker. This longevity speaks to the desk's fundamental quality—it has remained functional and excellent throughout a complete company rebrand and years of daily professional use.

The only minor complaint KHC mentions relates to the height adjustment mechanism. Unlike most modern sit-stand desks that allow you to press a button once to reach a saved height position, this desk requires holding the button continuously during adjustment. While KHC describes this as "such a minor complaint," it's worth noting as the one area where the desk's age shows compared to more recent competitors. Despite this, the overall assessment is overwhelmingly positive: a nine out of ten rating, with KHC declaring this their "forever desk"—a workspace solution they plan to keep for the foreseeable future.

The Chair: Herman Miller Embody

Sitting at the desk is the Herman Miller Embody, a chair that KHC describes as "one of the best investments I've ever made." This assessment is particularly noteworthy coming from someone who spends significant portions of their day seated while editing videos, producing content, and managing their creative business. The Embody has long been considered one of the premier ergonomic office chairs available, and KHC's long-term experience confirms its reputation.

The chair offers substantial adjustability, allowing users to customize the fit to their specific body dimensions and preferences. However, KHC notes that after years of use, they no longer need to make those adjustments—the chair has essentially "molded around my back," creating a personalized seating experience that requires no fine-tuning. This speaks to one of the key benefits of investing in premium ergonomic seating: while the initial setup may require some configuration, the long-term payoff is maintenance-free comfort that adapts to the user rather than requiring ongoing user adaptation.

KHC's history with the Embody extends beyond their current setup—they had one during their college years as well, suggesting a long-standing appreciation for the brand and model. The decision to repurchase the same chair speaks to product satisfaction and the value of investing in long-term comfort. The rating reflects this satisfaction: a perfect ten out of ten. KHC explicitly notes that "the investment in your comfort and your back makes it worth it," framing the premium price tag as a worthwhile investment in health and productivity rather than an unnecessary expense.

The Computer: Mac Pro Tower

The computing heart of the setup is the Mac Pro Tower, specifically the M2 Ultra configuration that KHC purchased approximately six years ago. This was no modest investment—at the time of purchase, the system cost approximately $13,000, representing Apple's most powerful computer at that moment. The specifications were indeed impressive: an M2 Ultra processor with 24 CPU cores and 76 GPU cores, paired with 192 gigabytes of RAM and 8 terabytes of storage. This was, and in many respects still is, an extraordinarily powerful workstation.

However, KHC is candid about the computer's current status: "This is probably going to be one of the first things to go. I'm not giving up on it yet, but I'm pretty sure Apple already has." The rationale for this assessment is straightforward: the Mac Pro has not been updated to any of the subsequent chip generations. The M3 generation came and went without a Mac Pro update. The M4 generation similarly passed without revision. The M5 generation is now on the horizon, and the Mac Pro remains unchanged. This lack of updates has created a situation where a once-top-of-the-line workstation is now demonstrably behind Apple's current technology curve.

Despite this, the computer remains functional for current workflows. The decision to purchase the Mac Pro rather than a Mac Studio was driven by the need for expansion capabilities—specifically, a PCI card slot that allows for additional storage. KHC has installed an OWC SSD with 64 terabytes of storage for live backups, ensuring that all content produced is backed up in real-time. This backup strategy reflects the priorities of professional content creation where losing source footage or projects would be catastrophic.

The Mac Pro sits on the desk rather than underneath it, a deliberate choice related to the sit-stand desk's design. Because the desk moves up and down for height adjustment, placing the computer underneath would require cables that stretch and reach during movement. By keeping the computer on the desk surface, cables can be routed and hidden more cleanly. The ultimate assessment is pragmatic: "It's fine. I say four out of ten"—a clear acknowledgment that while the machine remains serviceable, its best days are likely behind it, and an upgrade is anticipated in the near future, potentially to an M5 Ultra Mac Studio with a Thunderbolt OWC SSD for backup.

The Displays: Pro Display XDR

Behind the desk sits a dual-monitor setup featuring two Pro Display XDR monitors from Apple. These professional reference displays represent Apple's premium offering for color-critical work, and KHC's setup includes two of them for a comprehensive multi-monitor workspace. Each display is a 32-inch, 16:9 LED backlit LCD operating at 60 Hz, featuring Apple's Pro Display XDR technology designed for high Dynamic Range and color accuracy work.

The specifications reveal this as a niche product designed for professional workflows. There is no integrated webcam and no integrated speakers—these are displays designed purely for visual reference work, with the expectation that users will add such peripherals separately. The pricing reflects this professional positioning: each display costs $5,000, the stand adds another $1,000, and the nano-texture glass finish—which provides superior anti-reflection properties for brightly lit environments—adds yet another thousand dollars. The total cost per display reaches approximately $7,000, meaning the dual-monitor setup represents a $14,000 investment in display technology alone.

KHC addresses the nano-texture finish with a nuanced perspective. In their brightly lit studio with a wall of windows, the nano-texture is "extremely effective" at managing reflections and maintaining image clarity. However, this effectiveness comes with a significant tradeoff: "it also makes them impossible to clean." Once touched or marked, the special coating makes it extraordinarily difficult to remove marks or clean the surface. KHC admits to being extremely careful about keeping the displays clean and notes that everyone in the studio who does color work uses the same displays, ensuring consistency when matching and comparing reference colors across different workstations.

For daily use, KHC notes some limitations. While the displays excel at video editing and color work, for general computer use and non-color-critical tasks, they find themselves wishing for a higher refresh rate and additional features that would make the displays more versatile for general productivity. The rating of eight out of ten reflects this balanced assessment: exceptional for their intended purpose but not perfect for every use case.

Additional Workspace Elements

Beyond the major equipment pieces, KHC's workspace includes several additional items worth noting. A Groveade shelf provides additional storage for smaller items like cables, SIM card removal tools, pens, flash drives, and similar accessories. KHC acknowledges these are "pretty pricey" but notes they serve their purpose effectively while adding to the workspace aesthetic. The black version matches the overall setup, and while KHC acknowledges similar functionality could be achieved with cheaper alternatives, they give the shelf a perfect ten out of ten for meeting its intended purpose.

An audio interface serves as the digital-to-analog converter for the entire audio system, handling the conversion of digital audio signals from the computer into analog signals that the speakers and headphones can reproduce. This external unit connects via Thunderbolt and functions as what KHC describes as "a giant glorified volume knob"—a satisfying, tactile way to control audio levels. The unit includes various software features and plugins, though KHC admits to rarely using most of them. The software experience is described as "really buggy," requiring frequent resets and firmware updates that sometimes cause the device to "forget what it is." Despite these frustrations, the unit continues to function and serve its core purpose after years of use, earning a four out of ten rating—functional but not without significant drawbacks.

The speakers are a pair of Yamaha HS8 active near-field studio monitors, which KHC describes as "iconic at this point to just the look of my desk." These 8-inch powered monitors have been a fixture of professional audio and video production setups for years, and KHC's long-term use confirms their versatility. The speakers handle general reference listening, video editing, audio editing, and music listening equally well, with the 8-inch woofer capable of reaching down to approximately 40 Hz—eliminating the need for a separate subwoofer in this setup. One minor quirk involves RF interference: placing a phone too close to the speakers can cause audible buzzing, and KHC notes they can sometimes predict incoming calls or messages by hearing the speakers react to the phone's signals.

A wireless charger sits next to the speakers, providing a convenient charging option for compatible devices.

Key Insights and Takeaways

The Value of Long-Term Investment in Quality Tools KHC's workspace demonstrates a philosophy of investing in quality equipment that lasts. The Herman Miller Embody chair, the Next Desk Air Pro, and the Yamaha HS8 speakers represent purchases made years or even over a decade ago that continue to serve their purpose excellently. This approach emphasizes that the "best" tool isn't necessarily the newest or most feature-rich, but often the one that continues to perform reliably year after year. For viewers building their own setups, this suggests prioritizing core furniture and infrastructure that won't need frequent replacement over incremental technology upgrades.

Professional Workflow Requirements Differ from Consumer Needs The Mac Pro and Pro Display XDR choices reveal how professional workflows shape equipment decisions. The need for PCI expansion for live backup storage drove the Mac Pro choice, while color-critical work requiring precise reference monitoring justifies the substantial investment in professional displays. These aren't simply "better" versions of consumer products—they're tools designed for specific professional requirements that most users would never utilize. This distinction is valuable for anyone building a workspace: understanding your actual requirements prevents overspending on features you'll never use while ensuring you don't underspend on capabilities that matter for your specific work.

The Inevitability of Technology Aging Even premium equipment eventually shows its age. The M2 Ultra Mac Pro, once Apple's most powerful computer, is now demonstrably behind current chip generations—not because it became bad, but because the industry progressed. This creates a realistic expectation for technology investments: even the best equipment will eventually be superseded. The key is investing in equipment that serves your current needs well while it remains relevant, rather than waiting indefinitely for better options that may never arrive.

Practical Considerations Override Ideal Specifications The decision to keep the Mac Pro on the desk rather than underneath illustrates how practical considerations often override theoretical ideal configurations. Cable management for sit-stand desks creates real challenges that affect equipment placement decisions. Similarly, the ongoing frustration with the audio interface's software bugs is weighed against the fact that the core functionality continues to work. These nuances remind viewers that real-world usage involves tradeoffs that no amount of planning can anticipate.

The Importance of Backup and Data Protection The elaborate backup system—with a 64TB SSD for live backups of all content created—reflects the critical importance of data protection for content creators. Losing source footage, projects, or assets would be catastrophic for a channel that produces ongoing content. This investment in backup infrastructure represents a hidden but essential component of any professional creative workspace that often gets overlooked in favor of more visible equipment.

Who This Is For

This desk tour and equipment review will be most valuable for several distinct audiences. First, anyone building or upgrading a professional video editing or content creation workspace will find detailed insights into how an experienced creator prioritizes their equipment investments. The reasoning behind each choice—what drove the decision, what has worked well, what has proven disappointing—provides a valuable framework for making similar decisions.

Second, technology enthusiasts and Apple ecosystem followers will appreciate the candid assessment of equipment that has aged. Seeing how an M2 Ultra Mac Pro performs after six years of daily professional use, and understanding the expectation that it will soon be replaced, provides realistic insight into technology lifecycle expectations that marketing materials rarely offer.

Third, anyone considering major investments in professional-grade equipment—particularly the Herman Miller Embody chair or the Pro Display XDR displays—will benefit from long-term user assessments rather than just initial reviews. The distinction between the nano-texture's effectiveness in bright environments versus its cleaning challenges is exactly the kind of practical knowledge that only emerges through extended use.

Finally, viewers interested in understanding how professional content creators structure their workspaces will find value in seeing how all the pieces connect: the cable management approach, the audio routing through the interface, the dual-monitor arrangement, the backup strategy. This holistic view of a functioning professional workspace provides templates that viewers can adapt to their own situations.

The video succeeds because it offers something rare: honest, experienced-based evaluation from someone who has genuinely used this equipment extensively in a professional context. There are no sponsorships being acknowledged that would compromise the honesty, no need to artificially praise products, and no pre-release access creating artificial enthusiasm. Just a creator sharing what has worked, what hasn't, and what they would do differently—exactly the kind of practical wisdom that makes building a workspace slightly less uncertain.
