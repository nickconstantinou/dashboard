<p># The Science of Signs: A Neuroscientist's Journey Through Grief, Consciousness, and Communication with the Departed

## Introduction

Dr. Tara Swart is no ordinary grief counselor. As a neuroscientist and psychiatrist with a career built on scientific rigor and evidence-based practice, she spent years studying the brain, consciousness, and the limits of human perception. But nothing in her academic training prepared her for the death of her husband Robin in 2021—a loss so devastating that she describes herself as "totally lost and broken" despite her professional expertise in mental health. This profound personal tragedy became the unlikely catalyst for a journey that would challenge everything she thought she knew about the boundaries between life and death, the nature of consciousness, and humanity's hidden capacity for perceiving beyond the five traditional senses.

In this candid and deeply personal conversation, Dr. Swart shares her remarkable story of widowhood, spiritual awakening, and scientific investigation into what she calls "the signs"—the meaningful coincidences and sensory experiences that she believes represent genuine communication from deceased loved ones. Now communicating with Robin on what she describes as a daily basis, she has written a book called *The Signs* to share what she has learned with others navigating the painful terrain of loss. What makes her account particularly compelling is that she approaches this deeply personal journey with the same scientific discipline that defined her previous career—not blindly accepting experiences that challenged her worldview, but rigorously examining them through the lens of neuroscience and consciousness research. The result is a perspective that bridges the gap between hard science and spiritual possibility, offering a framework that many grieving people may find both intellectually credible and emotionally transformative.

## The Shock of Loss and the Dawn of Signs

The interview opens with Dr. Swart recounting the circumstances of Robin's death and the immediate aftermath. He was given two weeks to live but survived for three and a half weeks, finally passing away just two days before their fourth wedding anniversary. The cruelty of timing meant that she found herself reading condolence cards on what should have been a celebration of love. She describes this period with raw honesty, acknowledging that without what she calls the "fortress" of supportive people around her, she does not believe she would have survived the emotional devastation. "Never having had that experience before," she says, "it was just so, so devastating. And even though I'm a neuroscientist and a psychiatrist, I was just totally lost and broken."

What followed was equally unexpected. In the weeks after Robin's death, she began seeing robins everywhere—both at their home in Hampshire and in London. She had "never, ever seen so many robins in my life, like not before or since." While she acknowledges that she had no idea what this meant, if anything, she also noticed it. This pattern of attention to meaningful coincidences would become central to her journey of discovery.

Six weeks after Robin's passing, she experienced what she describes as an extraordinary sensory event. At approximately 4 a.m., she was asleep when she heard a distant noise. She went to check the garage, assuming it might be birds or an alarm, but could not identify the source and returned to bed. Moments later, she was jolted awake by what she describes as a "massive thump to my shoulder" so forceful that she says she would not demonstrate it on the interviewer because it would be too painful. When she opened her eyes, she saw next to her bed "a very vague, hazy version of Robin as if he was pushing himself through treacle to be seen." She describes watching transfixed as he became clearer, eventually able to see "the outline of his hair in his face." Then, she watched as he "dissolved from the top down," with her eyes following his form until she could see his shins and feet before he was entirely gone. She gasped aloud in that moment of witnessing.

## The Frustration with Mediums and the Decision to Go Within

In her desperation, Dr. Swart did what many bereaved people do: she consulted a couple of mediums. But she also experienced a profound internal conflict about this decision. "I had that dual conversation," she recalls. "I said to myself, this is the kind of thing that crazy, desperate people do. And within the same breath, it's OK for me to be crazy and desperate right now. I've lost my best friend, my life partner. Like everything I thought about how the world worked has crashed around me." Ultimately, she was not impressed by the mediums she consulted, finding their communications unconvincing.

This disappointment became the turning point. She realized that if optimizing her brain and expanding her consciousness were indeed possible—and she had spent her career studying these topics—then she should be capable of attempting direct communication with Robin herself. "If it's possible to communicate with someone that's passed away, and he was my husband and my best friend, and I am all about optimising my brain and expanding my consciousness, then I should be able to do it myself." This marked the beginning of what she describes as a journey of discovery that she has since documented in her book *The Signs*.

The scientific part of her mind immediately went to work, seeking research to back up what she was experiencing and learning. Central to her investigation became the question of whether the mind, psyche, or soul can exist separately from the body. But she also describes a pivotal moment at the actual moment of Robin's death, when he died in front of her. Once he had passed, she looked at his body and had an overwhelming certainty: "I remember a really strong feeling of looking at his body and just knowing that wasn't him. And that the essence of who he was, I didn't know where it was, but it was not there lying in that bed."

## Understanding the 34 Senses and Non-Ordinary Perception

One of the foundational concepts Dr. Swart discusses is her belief that human beings possess far more than the traditional five senses. She references research suggesting that we have approximately 34 senses, many of which operate below the threshold of conscious awareness. These include what she describes as non-conscious senses such as the pH of the blood or the balance of oxygen and carbon dioxide in the bloodstream. While we cannot consciously perceive or directly control these processes, activities like breathwork can influence them indirectly.

This framework becomes crucial to understanding her theory of communication with the deceased. If there are senses operating outside our conscious awareness, then it follows that some people might develop sensitivity to dimensions of reality that others dismiss or cannot perceive. This is not supernatural in her view, but rather an expansion of what was always biologically possible—similar to how some individuals develop extraordinary musical ability or athletic talent through a combination of natural predisposition and dedicated practice.

The interviewer raises an important question: can these expanded senses help us communicate with the living as well as the dead? Dr. Swart affirms that this is indeed the case. The work of developing these senses begins with self-awareness—recognizing that one possesses 34 senses, many of which are operating non-consciously. The more one cultivates awareness of these senses, the more effectively one can utilize them in various contexts, including both interpersonal connection and what she describes as communication with those who have passed.

## Somatic Trauma: The Body's Memory of Loss

One of the most striking aspects of Dr. Swart's account involves her experience with what she calls somatic trauma in the period surrounding the first anniversary of Robin's death. Approximately two months before October 26th—the anniversary date—she began experiencing severe physical symptoms. Starting on October 4th, she developed "aches and pains all over my body" that lasted for six or seven weeks, accompanied by profound depression. So severe was her physical suffering that she went for a massage, only to find it so painful that she did not return for a year. She even went so far as to examine herself against the clinical criteria for depression, though she concluded she did not technically meet all those criteria.

It was only later, when she looked back through her phone calendar, that she made the connection: October 4th was the day she had taken Robin home from the hospital to die. "I didn't remember that date," she admits, "but clearly my body had, and the trauma was just reemerging as like physical pain." This realization led her to understand that some trauma cannot be processed through talking therapy alone and requires what she terms "somatic work"—body-based therapies that address trauma held physically rather than merely psychologically.

Dr. Swart explains the neuroscience behind this phenomenon. There is an area in the brain, located in the interior, that is responsible for articulating speech. This region—which she identifies as likely Broca's area—can become functionally shut down by trauma. This explains common expressions like "I'm speechless" or "I'm dumbfounded." When someone has experienced overwhelming shock or grief, the body may hold trauma that cannot be articulated and processed through verbal therapy alone. Somatic approaches that work through the body—massage, dance, art, craniosacral therapy, or practices like Tai Chi—are necessary to address these residual traumatic memories. This was a crucial lesson she learned through her own healing journey.

## The Temperature Connection and Sensing the Departed

Another remarkable experience Dr. Swart shares involves what happened in the days between Robin's death and his cremation, approximately ten days later. Each morning during that period, she would wake up "absolutely freezing cold, like shaking and shivering," despite it being October and not the depths of winter. She would blast up the heating, but when someone else was in the house, she would realize the temperature was like a sauna, leading to embarrassment and the need to open windows.

Looking back, she connects this experience to Robin's comfort preferences. "Robin actually hated being cold," she notes. "And he would have been in the morgue in a refrigerated drawer that whole time." She theorizes that her sense of temperature was somehow "on the same wavelength" as Robin's physical location, even though she was not consciously thinking about where his body was. This example illustrates her broader point that some forms of communication or connection with deceased loved ones may manifest through the body before they become conscious or verbal.

## Thought Insertion and the Science of Grief

Dr. Swart also opens up about experiencing what psychiatrists call "thought insertion"—a phenomenon typically associated with schizophrenia, where one has thoughts in their head that they recognize are not their own. This experience was particularly intense for her, and she describes the strange cognitive position of experiencing this symptom while simultaneously being a psychiatrist who could name what was happening. She recognized intellectually that she was displaying a "psychotic symptom" while also understanding that grief itself creates neurochemical and electrical changes in the brain that parallel those seen in psychosis.

This led her to what she describes as profound empathy for those who go through grief without the resources she had available. "I just have so much empathy for people that have to go through that and don't have the wherewithal or the resources that I did." She notes that grief changes neurotransmitter levels, alters electrical and chemical signaling in the brain, and can create experiences that might be clinically labeled as symptoms of mental illness when they are actually normal responses to extraordinary loss.

## Cultivating the Ability to See Signs

When asked whether the ability to perceive signs requires cultivation—like building a muscle at the gym—Dr. Swart responds with an emphatic yes. She describes this as a skill that took her years to develop, noting that Robin himself had apparently worked on developing his own sensitivity over years before his death. "It's like learning a language," she explains, "but you're right, it's like going to the gym."

What does this cultivation involve? For Dr. Swart, it always starts with belief. She addresses the common barrier that prevents many people from accessing these experiences: doubt and skepticism. While she acknowledges that some people simply do not believe in these phenomena, she suggests that a larger group "secretly do, but they're scared to talk about it because they think people ridicule them." This fear of judgment creates silence around experiences that may be more common than assumed.

Her own relationship with belief is nuanced. She describes how her brain demands scientific evidence before accepting propositions: "I think sometimes I worry that if I don't have scientific rigor around my beliefs, then I'm susceptible to believe anything and I'll believe in a spaghetti monster at the bottom of the garden and I'll believe every religion in the world and everything and then I'm unanchored and then I blow around like a plastic bag in the wind and then I have no orientation." This rigorous approach actually drove her investigation rather than preventing it. "My entire career has been based on that," she says of scientific rigor, "but I just, you know, I was pushed up against a wall so I had to think differently."
