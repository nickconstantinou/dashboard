Overwatch 2 was a big mistake - Overwatch creator explains | Jeff Kaplan and Lex Fridman

Introduction

In a rare and candid conversation that has sent ripples through the gaming industry, Jeff Kaplan—the legendary game designer who led Overwatch from its inception through its meteoric rise and subsequent turbulent years—sat down with Lex Fridman to deliver what amounts to a comprehensive autopsy of one of modern gaming's most controversial releases. The conversation, which spans the origins of Overwatch 2's development in 2015 to Kaplan's eventual departure from Blizzard, represents an unusually honest reckoning from a creative leader who has spent decades at the forefront of online multiplayer game design.

The timing of this discussion carries particular weight. Overwatch 2 launched in 2022 to mixed reception, with longtime fans feeling betrayed by the gap between what was promised and what was delivered. Behind the scenes, the game had been in development for over seven years, surviving corporate restructurings, executive turnover, and the collapse of the esports ambitions that originally drove much of its direction. Kaplan, who left Blizzard in 2021, had never before spoken so openly about the internal struggles that plagued the sequel's development—until now. His admission that Overwatch 2 represents "one of my biggest mistakes I made as a creative leader in my career" serves as the thesis for a conversation that dissects everything from the dangers of corporate roadshows to the fundamental misalignment between what players wanted and what the business demanded.

This conversation matters because it illuminates the invisible forces that shape the games we play but rarely understand. Kaplan's reflections offer a window into the tension between creative vision and corporate execution, between what developers want to make and what publishers demand they deliver. For anyone interested in game development, business strategy, or the human costs of ambitious projects, this interview provides essential lessons that extend far beyond the world of Overwatch.

The Genesis of Overwatch 2: A Vision Before the Original Even Shipped

The story of Overwatch 2 begins in 2015, a full year before Overwatch itself would launch to the public. Kaplan, alongside colleagues Chris Metson and Michael Chu, began brainstorming what a campaign mode for Overwatch could look like—specifically, a cooperative PvE experience that would allow players to engage with the game's beloved characters and universe in a fundamentally different way than the competitive multiplayer that would become the original's identity. "We had this idea of like a cooperative PVE shooter," Kaplan recalls, and they pitched this vision to the team before Overwatch even launched, attempting to create runway for the project that would ultimately work against them.

What makes this timeline significant is that Overwatch became a "gigantic success" almost immediately upon its 2016 release. The game captured the cultural imagination, spawning a passionate community, lucrative esports competitions, and a universe that players desperately wanted to explore more deeply. Yet even as the original Overwatch was winning awards and attracting millions of players, a faction within the development team was chomping at the bit to work on something else entirely. According to Kaplan, there were people on the dev team "who didn't like PvP or competitive shooters and they really loved the Overwatch universe and wanted to play these characters and heroes, but they wanted to kind of do it on their own terms in a PvE setting." This internal tension—developers who saw the massive success of the PvP game but felt excluded from it—would become one of the two critical pressure points that shaped Overwatch 2's troubled development.

The other pressure came from above—from the executive suites at both Blizzard and, more significantly, Activision. Kaplan reveals that corporate leadership "started really putting the heat on" because "you said Overwatch 2 was going to be out in 2019." These commitments traced back to presentation slides that Kaplan now acknowledges were essentially meaningless at the time they were created. "It was you never want to put a PowerPoint deck in front of a corporate executive like you might as well etch it in stone and come down from the mountain on it," Kaplan explains with self-deprecating honesty. "So you just threw some dates... this is just in the same way we put like the tablet, you know, we just put Overwatch like put Tracer on a tablet and say we have a mobile strategy." The lesson here is brutal: corporate presentations have a way of becoming binding contracts, even when they're created with the same casual optimism as a mobile strategy slide.

The Summer Games Dilemma: Success as a Distraction

As Overwatch 1 dominated the competitive gaming landscape in 2016, the team found themselves at a crossroads that would define the next several years of development. Kaplan remembers the moment clearly: "We were in like May of 2016 and that year the Olympics were going to be in Rio, I think." The opportunity seemed obvious—create a limited-time event tied to the Olympics, give players something new to engage with, and capitalize on the cultural moment. The result was Summer Games, featuring the beloved Lucio Ball mode created by developers Mike Hyberg and Dave Adams. "It was extremely successful," Kaplan notes, and it spawned a pattern of seasonal events that would keep the live game vibrant for years.

The problem was that these events became a source of internal conflict. While fans loved the new content—Halloween events, seasonal celebrations, new maps and heroes—another faction within the development team felt the patches were "overscoping" resources that should be devoted to Overwatch 2. Kaplan describes this tension: "Why are we doing this Halloween event? We should be doing we should start work on Overwatch 2. We shouldn't be this focused on the live game, which was [expletive] nuts." The irony is striking: a live service game that was generating enormous revenue was being criticized by some on the team for not abandoning its audience in pursuit of a sequel that may never have delivered what was promised.

Kaplan's instincts, he admits, were different. "My instincts at that point were like, let's just keep how many more of these live events can we do?" This perspective aligned with what he calls the "wave" theory of game development: "There's this phrase of catch the wave, ride the wave. Most games fall off the back of the wave. They don't catch the wave. No one plays it or plays it for two weeks. If you're lucky enough to have caught the wave, write it till the end." Overwatch had caught a wave, and Kaplan wanted to ride it as long as possible through continuous live content. But the coalition of team members who wanted Overwatch 2 built instead, combined with the mounting executive pressure, made this vision increasingly difficult to execute.

The Overwatch League Catastrophe: When Ambition Outpaced Reality

If the internal team tension and executive pressure created the conditions for Overwatch 2's troubled development, it was the Overwatch League that became the primary derailment—a force that consumed resources, redirected priorities, and ultimately left both the original game and its sequel in worse shape than they would have been otherwise. Kaplan expresses genuine belief in the League's original vision: "We thought it was like the future of esports and doing regional-based teams, ensuring minimum player salaries and player protections." The concept was ambitious and, in many ways, noble—transforming esports from a Wild West of exploitative teams into something resembling traditional professional sports with actual player protections.

But what followed was a classic case of corporate overreach. "There was a lot of excitement about Overwatch League like too much so and then it got over marketed to the people buying the teams," Kaplan explains. The roadshow that sold franchise spots to investors was, in Kaplan's words, essentially "selling the Brooklyn Bridge"—promising that Overwatch League would be "more popular than the NFL." This absurd claim attracted billionaire investors who purchased teams across the world, from London to Shanghai, expecting returns that could only be justified by impossible viewership numbers.

The technical obligations alone were staggering. In early 2018, the League signed a massive media rights deal with Twitch that created cascading commitments for the development team. "We signed this huge deal with Twitch for streaming of Overwatch League... and that means that here's all these commitments we made for Overwatch League of like in-game stuff that had to exist," Kaplan recalls. This included integration with Twitch, camera controls for spectating, and perhaps most resource-intensive of all, team skins and uniforms for all the franchise organizations. "There was huge technical challenges to like how all that worked and was efficient and hit the right memory footprint and all that kind of stuff."

The business model collapsed almost immediately. The original plan assumed robust in-person events with ticket sales and merchandise revenue at levels that would rival traditional sports. "The merch was good, but it wasn't going to be making NFL level money, whatever insanity anybody thought that was going to be," Kaplan notes with evident frustration. When the financial reality kicked in, these same investors who had been promised NFL-level returns began demanding more from the game itself. "We had all these people who basically invested in the game and then they started to express their opinions." The pressure to monetize—to extract more revenue from the live game to compensate for the League's underperformance—redirected resources away from content development and toward systems designed to extract maximum value from players.

The Reckoning: What Went Wrong and What Could Have Been

The convergence of these forces—internal team pressure for PvE, executive pressure for a 2019 release, and the Overwatch League's resource demands—created what Kaplan describes as a situation where "all your plans at that point kind of go out the window. Like you're not going to work on new world events. You're not really even focused on Overwatch 2. You're just kind of treading water." The game that eventually emerged in 2022 was a shadow of what had been promised: aPvP-focused sequel with a reduced PvE component that failed to deliver on the cooperative campaign that had been the original justification for the entire project.

Kaplan's retrospective analysis is remarkably self-aware. "What would have been correct was to do more world events like keep it going, but the major derail was Overwatch League." This single factor, which he describes as "a house of cards waiting to fall," consumed the attention and resources that could have been devoted to either improving the live service or actually building the promised PvE experience. "It got to the point... my exit at Blizzard. I believed in Overwatch, too. I think we could have made a great I have a lot of hindsight of like how I would have designed that game differently with what I know now versus what ultimately we didn't ship."

The most poignant moment comes when Kaplan clarifies that when he refers to Overwatch 2 as a mistake, he's talking specifically about "the PvE version, which by the way, I would have loved to play. I'm one of the people that were Overwatch is great, but the PvP, but I would have loved to play the PvE version." This personal connection to the unrealized vision makes his critique more than just professional second-guessing—it's the regret of a creator who knows what could have been but watched it slip away due to forces beyond his control.

Key Takeaways: Lessons from the Overwatch Collapse

Corporate presentations become contracts. Kaplan's most blunt advice concerns the danger of putting dates in front of executives: "You never want to put a PowerPoint deck in front of a corporate executive like you might as well etch it in stone." Once those slides leave the room, they become binding commitments regardless of how casually they were assembled. For anyone in business development, product management, or any role that involves executive presentations, this serves as a cautionary tale about the permanent weight of temporary projections.

Live service games require sustained commitment, not sequel distractions. The "wave" analogy Kaplan uses deserves serious consideration from any developer working on a successful live game. Overwatch caught lightning in a bottle, and the correct strategic response was to continue delivering content that maintained and grew that audience—not to divide resources by building a sequel. The pressure to create "Overwatch 2" came from business assumptions about monetization, not player needs.

Esports ambition must match business reality. The Overwatch League story illustrates how noble ideas can become corrupted when investors expect returns that require impossible growth. The original vision—regional teams, player protections, legitimate esports infrastructure—was sound. But the marketing promises made to sell franchises created expectations that could never be fulfilled, and those broken promises eventually came due in the form of pressure on the development team.

Internal misalignment can be as damaging as external pressure. The team members who wanted to work on PvE rather than maintaining the live game weren't wrong about their preferences—they simply had different priorities than what the business required. Managing such misalignment requires intentional leadership, not just allowing factions to compete for resources.

Creative leaders must protect their teams from corporate pressure. Kaplan's reflection that "there was too much focus on let's make lots of money really fast and a lot of people got dragged into it" suggests a failure of leadership to push back against unrealistic demands. The cost was borne by developers who worked long hours on a project that could never meet its promises, and ultimately by players who received a diminished product.

Who Should Listen to This Conversation

This conversation carries exceptional value for several distinct audiences. Game developers and producers will find Kaplan's behind-the-scenes account invaluable for understanding how corporate pressure can derail ambitious projects, and how internal team misalignment can compound external challenges. The lessons about managing stakeholder expectations, protecting development resources, and recognizing when a "sequel" is actually a distraction apply broadly across the industry.

Business leaders and executives—particularly those in gaming, entertainment, or any industry involving creative products—should pay close attention to the Overwatch League case study. The story illustrates with painful clarity how aggressive revenue projections can create perverse incentives that ultimately damage the product they're meant to support.

Investors and analysts interested in esports will find this conversation essential viewing. Kaplan's honest assessment of what went wrong with Overwatch League provides crucial context for understanding the sector's challenges and the gap between hype and reality in competitive gaming.

Finally, longtime Overwatch fans will find a kind of closure in Kaplan's reflections. Understanding that the game's troubled history resulted from specific decisions and pressures—rather than simple incompetence or lack of care—provides context for evaluating both what was lost and what might have been.

The conversation concludes with Kaplan expressing hope that "everybody would have loved to have played" the PvE version that never materialized.
