# Cheap, Unloved, Profitable: The Case for Value Investing Today?

Key Insights

  • Temple Bar Investment Trust has delivered standout performance since 2020, demonstrating that disciplined value investing—buying fundamentally cheap companies with recovery potential—remains viable even in challenging market conditions
  • Value investing success hinges on focusing on long-term earnings power rather than getting distracted by short-term market pessimism or negative sentiment surrounding certain stocks
  • Ian Lance's approach involves deliberately owning controversial or "unloved" companies that the broader market has dismissed, positioning the trust to benefit when sentiment eventually improves
  • The strategy requires patience and conviction, as positions are held for extended periods while waiting for the market to recognize intrinsic value
  • This approach fundamentally differs from growth investing, which prioritizes future earnings potential over current valuation metrics

    • ---

    Introduction

    In an investment landscape dominated by flashy technology stocks and momentum-driven strategies, value investing has spent much of the past decade in the wilderness. The strategy—built on finding cheap, unloved companies with solid fundamentals and waiting for the market to recognize their true worth—fell dramatically out of favor as growth stocks surged to unprecedented heights. Yet for investors willing to look past the noise, significant opportunities have persisted in the forgotten corners of the market.

    This episode of the Money Week podcast features Merryn Somerset Webb in conversation with Ian Lance, portfolio manager at Temple Bar Investment Trust. Temple Bar represents one of the most disciplined implementations of the classic value investment philosophy available to UK investors, and the trust has attracted considerable attention for its performance in recent years. The conversation explores the mechanics of why value investing works, the psychological challenges it presents, and why Lance believes the approach remains as relevant today as it has ever been.

    What makes this episode particularly compelling is its frank examination of what actually drives investment returns over the long term. Rather than chasing the latest market fads, Temple Bar's approach demands something far more difficult: the willingness to buy assets that everyone else has abandoned, hold them through periods of sustained underperformance, and maintain conviction when conventional wisdom suggests doing otherwise.

    ---

    Key Concepts & Arguments

    The Philosophy of Buying What's Broken

    At its core, value investing rests on a simple but powerful premise: markets periodically over-react to bad news, creating opportunities to purchase high-quality businesses at steep discounts to their intrinsic worth. Ian Lance explains that the Temple Bay approach centers on identifying companies that have fallen out of favor not because their underlying businesses have fundamentally deteriorated, but because sentiment has overwhelmed fundamentals. These businesses often possess durable earnings power, strong market positions, and experienced management teams—but their share prices have been crushed by short-term thinking and investor bias.

    The critical distinction here involves the difference between a cheap business and a value trap. Lance emphasizes that Temple Bay's process focuses specifically on companies where the underlying earnings stream remains intact or recoverable, rather than businesses that appear cheap because they are genuinely declining. This requires substantial analysis to distinguish between temporary setbacks and structural decline—a distinction that separates successful value investors from those who simply chase low prices.

    Holding Through the Wilderness

    Perhaps the most challenging aspect of value investing is the patience it requires. Lance discusses how the strategy often means holding positions for extended periods while waiting for the market to recognize value that is not immediately apparent. During these periods, portfolio companies may continue to trade below their intrinsic worth, and other investment styles may outperform dramatically. This testing of conviction represents where many investors abandon value approaches, crystallizing losses and moving to whatever strategy is currently working.

    Temple Bay's disciplined approach means resisting the temptation to chase relative performance. Instead, when a position is conviction held, the trust maintains its allocation regardless of short-term headwinds. The payoff, when it arrives, typically comes as sentiment rapidly reverses—a company beats earnings expectations, a strategic initiative delivers results, or simply as the market recognizes what fundamental analysis had identified long before.

    Embracing Controversy

    One of the more provocative aspects of Temple Bay's approach involves its willingness to hold controversial stocks—companies that the broader market has actively avoided. Lance argues that this controversy often creates the very discount that makes a position attractive. When everyone is selling a stock, price gets pushed down to levels that more than compensate for the genuine risks involved. The key, however, lies in distinguishing between controversy that masks an attractive opportunity and controversy that signals a genuinely broken business model.

    This willingness to stand against consensus requires both analytical rigor and psychological fortitude. It means accepting that positions may underperform not just for months but for years before the thesis plays out. It means reading analyst reports that dismiss your holdings and questions from clients who wonder why the portfolio owns such seemingly problematic businesses. The reward, as Temple Bay's track record demonstrates, can be substantial returns from positions that the market once considered beyond redemption.

    The Role of Long-Term Earnings Focus

    Throughout the conversation, Lance emphasizes that value investing is ultimately about earnings—the cash a business generates over time that ultimately determines its worth. While growth investors pay close attention to future earnings potential, value investors focus on what a business currently earns and what it could earn if operating conditions improve. This involves building detailed financial models that project earnings under various scenarios, identifying the catalysts that might unlock value, and calculating the margin of safety between current price and estimated intrinsic worth.

    This focus on earnings rather than sentiment distinguishes value investing from pure contrarianism. Simply buying whatever everyone else hates accomplishes little if those companies genuinely cannot generate sustainable profits. The Temple Bay approach requires identifying unloved companies where the market has over-reacted, but where the underlying business can still produce attractive returns over a full economic cycle.

    Detailed Takeaways

    1. Value opportunities arise from sentiment dislocations, not just price discounts. The most attractive value opportunities exist when a company's share price has fallen due to temporary factors that will resolve, not when the business model itself has become obsolete. Understanding this distinction requires deep fundamental analysis and patience to wait for market recognition.

    2. Controversial positions often offer the largest potential returns. Companies that the broader market has actively avoided frequently trade at discounts that vastly overstate their true risks. The Temple Bay approach deliberately seeks these opportunities, accepting short-term criticism in exchange for the potential upside when sentiment eventually improves. However, this requires distinguishing between temporary controversy and genuine business decline.

    3. Holding through periods of underperformance is essential to value investing success. By definition, a value strategy will underperform growth during periods when growth stocks dominate. The key is maintaining conviction in positions where the underlying thesis remains intact while avoiding the temptation to chase whatever is working. Successful value investing requires accepting that short-term underperformance is the price of long-term outperformance.

    4. Long-term earnings power determines ultimate value, regardless of current sentiment. Market prices fluctuate based on investor sentiment, but underlying businesses either generate earnings or they do not. Ian Lance's approach focuses on the fundamental question of what a company can earn over a full economic cycle, using this to anchor value estimates regardless of what the market currently believes.

    5. Diversification across multiple value opportunities manages risk without sacrificing potential returns. While individual value positions may be controversial, spreading exposure across a portfolio of unloved companies reduces the risk that any single position's thesis fails to materialize. The combination of multiple discounted positions offers a way to benefit from the value premium while managing individual stock risk.
