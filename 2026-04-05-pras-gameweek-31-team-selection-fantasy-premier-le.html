Pras' Gameweek 31 Team Selection: Navigating the Blank Gameweek and Chip Strategy Maze

Introduction: The Season's First Blank Arrives

The Fantasy Premier League landscape has shifted dramatically with the arrival of blank gameweek 31—the first void of the season—and with it comes the inevitable scramble among managers trying to navigate fixture chaos while keeping their long-term plans intact. Pras, the voice behind the FPL Wire, returned with his gameweek 31 team selection video to guide managers through this turbulent period, and the timing couldn't be more critical. With the Friday deadline looming and less than 24 hours for managers to finalize their squads, the strategic decisions made in this window could define the remainder of the season.

The atmosphere around this blank gameweek carries a particular tension that Pras captured perfectly: it's the moment when "things start to take shape" and when managers begin making moves that might contradict their long-term vision simply because circumstances demand it. The psychological pressure of blank gameweeks often forces otherwise disciplined FPL managers into reactive rather than proactive decision-making, and understanding how to avoid that trap is central to Pras's guidance.

What makes this particular blank gameweek especially significant is the aftermath of European competition action. Three English teams progressed through their respective European ties, and as Pras noted with evident satisfaction, "more teams progressing in Europe means more carnage"—which, paradoxically, is exactly what the FPL community wants to see. Crystal Palace advancing in the Europa League carries particularly heavy implications for the fixture schedule, Nottingham Forest won on penalties to progress, and Aston Villa's advancement in their competition adds another layer of complexity to an already intricate fixture puzzle. All of this European progression, according to Pras, constitutes "good news" for those preparing their FPL teams.

The Crystal Palace Effect: Why Gameweek 32 Is No Longer a Double

The most consequential development discussed in this video centers on Crystal Palace's advancement in the Europa League and its direct impact on the fixture schedule. Pras pulled up a chart—similar to one he presented in previous videos—that he'd updated to reflect the latest European results, with the Crystal Palace row now showing midweek commitments clearly boxed out. This visual representation makes immediately clear that Palace's fixture against Manchester City cannot be rescheduled into gameweek 31, confirming it as a guaranteed blank.

For managers who had held onto Eze or other Palace assets in hopes of securing a fixture reschedule, Pras offered reassurance: "Don't need to worry people who have sold Holland, don't need to worry that oh is he going to get a fixture in gameweek 31." The anxiety around whether Palace assets might suddenly become active again has been eliminated by their European commitments.

However, the implications extend further than gameweek 31. Pras explained that his original concern—that if Palace had been eliminated, the Manchester City fixture would have fit into gameweek 32—has also been rendered moot. Because Crystal Palace now face the second leg of their next European round, "gameweek 32 confirmed will not be a double gameweek now." This represents a significant adjustment to the strategic landscape that managers had been planning around.

The confirmed picture emerging is that gameweek 33 will be "the double that we've been talking about" with Palace, and there are strong indications that gameweek 36 will see another Palace double. Pras emphasized that this "base case" scenario—developed in collaboration with FPL.team and discussed in previous podcasts—remains intact and is actually "even more likelier than it was 2 hours ago" because the likelihood of a double in gameweek 32 has now dropped to zero.

For a double to occur in gameweek 33 as anticipated, several conditions must align: Manchester City must defeat Liverpool, Chelsea must win their FA Cup fixture, Arsenal must win their FA Cup fixture, and television schedulers must implement the predicted fixture changes. "Lots still up in the air" as Pras put it, but the foundation is more solid than it was before Palace's advancement.

Pras's Current Standing: A 500K Manager Eyeing the Summit

Before diving into strategic analysis, Pras provided an honest assessment of his own FPL position—a useful frame of reference for understanding the perspective from which he's advising. His previous gameweek proved "very good," with a nice gameweek rank driven primarily by his Bruno Fernandes captaincy decision. He acknowledged that he could have alternatively captained Mohamed Salah, having discussed that possibility, but ultimately chose not to make that move. The Bruno captaincy delivered as hoped, while "Thiago and Dango did very well" and the double Arsenal clean sheet provided additional points—though Pras noted this clean sheet was somewhat exclusive because "luckily for Ryan owners, Timber went off as well," meaning the clean sheet effectively only rewarded those with Ryan Gabriel in defense.

James Hill, whom Pras has consistently highlighted, "keeps doing very well," further contributing to the positive gameweek. Despite this strong showing, Pras finds himself "still around 500k"—a rank that has fluctuated significantly this season. He traced his journey: "once I did that 2 million to 500k jump, then I went back up to 700k, now I'm back to 500k." This volatility, rather than discouraging him, has reinforced his optimism about what's achievable.

Pras revealed that he's approximately "50 points from top 100k"—a distance he considers "very much doable," though he acknowledges the need for some fortune along the way. "These jumps make me more positive and optimistic that it's possible to catch," he explained, offering a philosophy that should resonate with any manager currently outside their target rank. The message is clear: in FPL, significant rank movements remain possible throughout the season, and a 500k standing is hardly a death sentence for ambitious managers.

The Three Pillars of Chip Strategy: A Framework for the Run-In

Perhaps the most valuable content in this video concerns chip strategy—the decisions that will separate successful managers from also-rans in the crucial run of double gameweeks. Pras wasted no time in establishing that the core chip strategies have been thoroughly discussed elsewhere, but he provided a clean breakdown of the three viable approaches that managers will likely employ.

Strategy One: The Popular Route

The first and most popular strategy involves playing the wild card in gameweek 32, bench boost in gameweek 33, and free hit in gameweek 34. With the Friday deadline for gameweek 32 approaching, managers on this path face an immediate decision: press the button now or wait? Pras has already given his definitive view on this matter. "You should not hold off," he advised. "Press it when the FA Cup quarterfinals have happened. You're not going to miss out on much in terms of prices." The key insight is that waiting through the international break provides crucial additional information—specifically, which fixtures will fall into gameweek 33 versus gameweek 34—and this information comes at essentially no cost because price changes over that period will be minimal. This represents the textbook approach for managers wanting maximum flexibility, and it's likely to be the most commonly executed strategy across the FPL community.

Strategy Two: The Alternative Path

The second strategy—which Pras indicated he's "looking to do if the fixtures look like this eventually"—involves a free hit in gameweek 33, bench boost in gameweek 36, and wild card in gameweek 35. "I just like it because my team looks nice in 32 and 34," he explained, identifying the appeal of this approach for managers whose squads are already well-constructed for the non-double weeks. This strategy assumes that the manager's current team doesn't require significant surgery for gameweeks 32 and 34, making it unnecessary to burn a wild card earlier. It's "semi-popular" according to Pras but likely less common than the first option because it requires more confidence in the existing squad's fixture coverage.

Strategy Three: The Niche Approach

The third strategy is distinctly "niche" and involves wild carding in gameweek 33, taking a hit in gameweek 34, and potentially forgoing bench boost entirely if the manager has already used that chip. Some managers on this path "will look at the 32 team and say, you know what, I'll just wild card in 33 instead" rather than forcing a wild card into a week where it might not be optimally deployed. These managers might bench boost in gameweek 35 or 36 instead, depending on their specific squad construction and the fixture schedule they face.

Pras's assessment was that "all three are viable," which provides important reassurance to managers uncertain about which path to follow. However, he offered a meta-level observation that reveals why waiting might be the prudent choice: "the fact that there are three strategies and only one of them has a wild card in gameweek 32 makes me think that why don't you just wait. Like within this international break, we will know 100% which fixture is going into 33 and which is staying in 34." This crystallizes the fundamental argument for patience—additional certainty costs virtually nothing in this window, making it foolish to commit prematurely.

The Solio Fixture Ticker: A Strategic Decision-Making Tool

To help managers navigate the strategy selection process, Pras introduced a sophisticated fixture analysis tool from Solio (acknowledging he should have included their logo). This spreadsheet presents projected goals for attacking and defensive options across different chip strategies, providing a data-driven foundation for player selection decisions.

Free Hit 33 Analysis

The first column of the Solio analysis applies to managers planning a free hit in gameweek 33. When this column is examined, "Liverpool is the team you need to target." For managers on this strategy, Mohamed Salah becomes the obvious premium asset to pursue, though his fitness remains uncertain pending Arne Slot's press conference. Beyond Salah, Liverpool's appeal stems from their favorable fixtures: "they have Fulham at home and Crystal Palace at home in 32 and 34." This double home fixture against relatively favorable opposition makes Liverpool assets particularly valuable for those planning a free hit in gameweek 33.

Manchester United appears as the second-highest projected team across both gameweeks 32 and 34, meaning "Man United attack also works in that strategy." The consistency of their fixture profile makes United assets particularly reliable for managers targeting this approach. Fulham attack also scores well in this analysis, explaining "why Wilson is popular this week" and why "Rogers is also fine" as a more budget-friendly alternative to premium Fulham options. The analysis shows that between the fourth and fifth highest options, "there's really nothing much in it"—meaning managers could reasonably choose between West Ham, Villa, Fulham, or other options based on factors like goal share and penalty involvement rather than pure fixture difficulty.

In defense for the free hit 33 strategy, the teams showing up prominently include Liverpool, Manchester United, West Ham, Tottenham, and Nottingham Forest. These defensive options represent the backbone of a free hit squad targeting gameweek 33 doubles.

Free Hit 34 Analysis

For managers considering a free hit in gameweek 34 instead, the analysis shifts dramatically toward Chelsea's assets. "Xiao Pedro, Palmer all are good for their horizon," Pras noted, identifying the Chelsea attack as the primary target. Newcastle also features prominently as "good for their horizon," making their assets relevant for this strategy.

The Newcastle versus Fulham debate represents one of the most pressing questions for managers not on a gameweek 33 free hit. Pras provided a nuanced framework for this decision: "My answer is right here. Depends on what your strategy is." For managers planning to free hit in gameweek 34, Newcastle becomes the primary entry point. However, for those who might not actually execute that free hit, Fulham becomes the safer choice because "if you want to play them in 34 then go for Fulham."

The practical implementation matters here. If a manager can bring in a Newcastle player this week and potentially bench them if their strategy changes, Newcastle offers higher upside. But if the plan is to commit fully to gameweek 34 as a free hit week, Fulham provides more flexibility since they're already being considered for that gameweek anyway. This represents a classic FPL optimization problem where the answer depends entirely on the manager's conviction about their ultimate strategy.

Special Considerations for Different Squads

Pras addressed managers currently on a wild card who are contemplating their chip deployment. For those who have "already wild carded and are thinking about free hitting in one or the other week," the Solio analysis provides direct guidance. These managers need to assess whether their current squad is better suited for gameweek 33 or gameweek 34 coverage, then build their wild card team accordingly.

For managers still deciding on Free Hit 33 targets, Pras highlighted Andreas Pereira (Seabos) as an interesting option: "there's nothing wrong with him. He should be on penalties if Salah misses out. Good for minutes. Good pick." This represents a middle-ground option that might not be the absolute premium choice but offers solid value and minutes consistency.

The analysis also touched on Manchester United midfielders as potential additions. "If you wanted to add a Man United midfielder like a Kuna, like an Ibumo, I think that's good as well," Pras suggested, noting this might be something he considers "next week." This forward-looking observation indicates that while this gameweek might be too soon for such moves, the strategy is sound for those planning ahead.

The Arsenal Situation: Value in the Blank

One of the more counterintuitive observations in the video concerned Arsenal assets despite their gameweek 31 blank. "Look at that Arsenal blank, but still are the highest because obviously they double as well," Pras noted, explaining that Arsenal's projected value remains elevated because their double gameweek potential (presumably in gameweek 33) makes them worth holding or targeting despite the immediate blank. For managers who had Arsenal coverage, taking the blank now in exchange for a likely double later represents the mathematically sound approach.

This highlights a crucial principle for blank gameweek management: the blank itself isn't the enemy. The enemy is making panicked decisions that sacrifice future value for temporary relief. Arsenal owners suffering through gameweek 31 should view this as an investment rather than a loss.

Detailed Takeaways: Actionable Insights for FPL Managers

Takeaway One: The International Break is Your Friend

The most strategically valuable insight in this video is Pras's argument for waiting until after the international break to commit to chip usage in gameweek 32. With the FA Cup quarterfinals providing definitive information about which fixtures will fall into which gameweeks, and with minimal price movement expected during the break, managers lose virtually nothing by waiting. "Why don't you just wait?" Pras asked, his exasperation barely concealed. "You're not going to miss out on much in terms of prices." This represents a rare opportunity in FPL: additional information with negligible cost. Managers who can exercise patience will make better-informed decisions than those who panic before the Friday deadline.

Takeaway Two: Crystal Palace's Path Defines the Double Landscape

With Crystal Palace now confirmed to blank in gameweek 31 and unable to provide a double in gameweek 32, the double gameweek landscape has clarified considerably. Gameweek 33 emerges as the primary double for Palace assets, with gameweek 36 increasingly likely to feature another Palace double. Managers holding Eze or other Palace assets should plan for gameweek 33 as their breakout opportunity rather than hoping for earlier returns. This certainty, gained from European progression results, represents actionable intelligence that should inform current transfer decisions.

Takeaway Three: Strategy Dictates Player Selection, Not Vice Versa

The Solio fixture analysis demonstrates a crucial principle: your chip strategy should drive your player selection, not the other way around. Managers planning a free hit in gameweek 33 should prioritize Liverpool and Manchester United assets because their fixture profiles align with that strategy. Those planning a gameweek 34 free hit should focus on Chelsea and Newcastle assets. Trying to force a squad built for one strategy into another strategy creates inefficiency and wasted transfers. The framework Pras provided—assess your chip plan first, then build your squad accordingly—offers a logical hierarchy for the decision-making process.

Takeaway Four: The 500K Rank Is Not a Prison Sentence

Pras's own journey from 2 million to 500k, then back to 700k, then to 500k again demonstrates that rank is fluid throughout the season. The approximately 50 points separating him from top 100k represents "very much doable" in his assessment. For managers currently outside their target ranks, this should provide both hope and perspective. Significant rank movements remain possible if managers execute well during the crucial double gameweek run. The blank gameweeks and fixture chaos that stress other managers represent opportunities for those who've prepared correctly.

Takeaway Five: Newcastle Versus Fulham Depends on Commitment Level

The specific question of whether to target Newcastle or Fulham players this gameweek cannot be answered without understanding your broader strategy. If you're confident about a gameweek 34 free hit and can afford to bench a Newcastle player temporarily if plans change, Newcastle offers higher upside. If you're less certain about your ultimate strategy or want players who can function in multiple scenarios, Fulham represents the safer choice. "Depends on what your strategy is," Pras concluded—this conditional answer is frustratingly accurate but represents the correct analytical approach.

These managers have enough quality in their squads to be competitive but haven't executed perfectly, leaving room for significant rank improvement through strategic chip deployment. The content is less essential for managers in the top 100k, who likely already have their strategies locked in, or for casual managers far outside the top million, who may not be invested enough to act on the advice.
