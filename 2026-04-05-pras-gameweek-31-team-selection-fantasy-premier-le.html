Pras' Gameweek 31 Team Selection: Navigating the First Blank Gameweek of the Season

Introduction: The Blank Gameweek Has Arrived

The moment FPL managers have been dreading—and secretly anticipating—has finally arrived. Blank gameweek 31 marks the first official blank of the 2025/26 season, and as PRA opens his team selection video on the FPL Wire, he acknowledges that this is the time when things truly start to take shape. The pressure of the blank forces managers into decisions they might not make under normal circumstances, pushing people toward moves that contradict their long-term planning. It's a nerve-wracking but ultimately exciting period, and Pras frames it as something to look forward to rather than fear.

The timing of this video is particularly significant. Pras录制 after witnessing three English clubs advance in European competition, with Crystal Palace's progression being the most consequential for FPL purposes. Nottingham Forest also advanced on penalties, and Aston Villa secured their advancement as well. While more English teams in European competition traditionally spells "carnage" for FPL fixture planning—meaning more blanks and doubles—it represents good news for the sport overall. Pras admits he's quite pleased with how the evening turned out, and this sense of controlled optimism permeates the discussion that follows.

Given that the deadline falls on Friday, managers have under 24 hours to absorb information, make decisions, and finalize their squads. Pras understands this urgency and promises to keep the discussion efficient while still covering everything necessary for sound decision-making.

Last Gameweek Recap: Bruno's Captaincy Delivers

Before diving into blank gameweek strategy, Pras briefly reflects on gameweek 30, which proved to be a highly successful week for his team. The gameweek rank was strong, and the Bruno Fernandes captaincy decision ultimately delivered as expected. Pras notes that he had considered switching to Mohamed Salah but ultimately decided against that move, choosing instead to stick with Bruno. That decision proved correct when Bruno delivered the expected points.

Thiago and Dango performed exceptionally well, contributing significantly to the strong overall showing. The double Arsenal clean sheet—which came as a surprise to many—added further value, though Pras observes that not many managers were positioned to benefit from this outcome. Rya owners who had invested in Arsenal's defense were particularly fortunate, especially given that Timber went off during the match, effectively making the clean sheet an exclusive reward for those with Ryan Gabriel defensive assets. James Hill also continued his excellent run of form.

Despite these gains, Pras remains around the 500,000 overall rank mark. He experienced a dramatic climb from 2 million to 500,000 in a previous gameweek, then slipped back to 700,000 before recovering. He's now back in that 500k range, and rather than viewing this as frustrating, he sees it as encouraging. Pras calculates he's approximately 50 points away from the top 100k, which he considers very achievable with some luck and smart decisions over the coming weeks. This mindset—that these ranking jumps prove catching up is possible—keeps him motivated through the volatile swings.

Crystal Palace's European Progression: The Fixture Calendar Changes

The most significant development affecting Pras' analysis is Crystal Palace's advancement in European competition. Using an updated version of the fixture chart he presented previously, Pras walks viewers through the new landscape. The Crystal Palace row now shows the midweek boxes clearly marked as "busy," which has direct implications for the fixture schedule.

The key takeaway is that Palace's fixture against Manchester City cannot fit into gameweek 31—it's now a guaranteed blank. For managers panicking about whether to hold or sell Eze or other Palace assets, this removes one variable from the equation. However, the implications extend further than gameweek 31 alone.

Pras had previously considered the possibility that if Palace were eliminated from European competition, their Man City fixture might slot into gameweek 32. That option has now evaporated because Palace have the second leg of their next European round scheduled. This means gameweek 32 is confirmed not to be a double gameweek, at least not with Man City involved. The expected double for Palace in gameweek 33 remains intact, and there's a strong likelihood of another Palace double in gameweek 36 as well.

This crystallization of the fixture picture represents "good news" in Pras' assessment. The base case scenario that he and others have been discussing based on data from FPL.team remains the most likely outcome. As more information has become available, the probability of this scenario has actually increased rather than decreased. Crystal Palace's continued European involvement makes the anticipated doubles more rather than less likely.

However, Pras is careful to note that chaos could still disrupt this base case. For Man City to be involved in gameweek 32 as part of a double, several things need to happen: Manchester City must beat Liverpool, Chelsea must win their FA Cup match, Arsenal must win their FA Cup fixture, and the television broadcasters must make the predicted fixture selections. All of these variables remain "up in the air," meaning managers should stay flexible and monitor the situation closely.

The Three Main Chip Strategies: A Comprehensive Breakdown

Having covered the fixture landscape, Pras pivots to chip strategy—the most consequential decision managers face around blank gameweeks. He identifies three distinct approaches that managers are employing, each with its own logic and trade-offs.

The first and most popular strategy involves wildcarding in gameweek 32, bench boosting in gameweek 33, and free hitting in gameweek 34. This approach requires pressing the wildcard button in gameweek 32, which has a Friday deadline—essentially happening immediately or very soon. Pras has already shared his views on this strategy, which he believes managers should have heard or will hear: don't rush the decision unnecessarily. He recommends waiting until after the FA Cup quarterfinals have concluded before pressing the button, as managers won't miss out on significant price changes during that short window. The logic is sound—why commit to a wildcard before you have complete information about which fixtures will move between gameweeks 33 and 34?

The second strategy, which Pras admits he's personally inclined toward if the fixtures confirm the expected pattern, involves free hitting in gameweek 33, bench boosting in gameweek 36, and wildcarding in gameweek 35. This approach appeals to Pras because his current team structure looks reasonable for gameweeks 32 and 34, meaning he doesn't need the wildcard immediately. This strategy will likely be adopted by a significant minority of managers—popular but not as widespread as the first option.

The third strategy is more niche and involves wildcarding in gameweek 33, accepting a hit in gameweek 34, and potentially forgoing bench boost entirely. Some managers who have already used their bench boost earlier in the season—or who simply look at their gameweek 32 squad and decide it doesn't require major surgery—will take this path. They might instead bench boost in gameweek 35 or 36 if the opportunity arises later.

Pras considers all three strategies viable, which is refreshing given that some FPL content creators treat their preferred approach as obviously correct. However, he makes an observation that subtly favors patience: the fact that only one of the three main strategies involves wildcarding in gameweek 32 suggests that managers who want maximum flexibility should wait. During the upcoming international break, 100% clarity will emerge about which fixtures belong in gameweek 33 and which stay in gameweek 34. Why commit early when perfect information is just days away?

This patience-first philosophy represents Pras' personal approach. He's waiting for the international break results before making his final chip decisions.

The Solio Fixture Ticker: A Visual Planning Tool

For managers who want to think ahead about player selection—whether for immediate transfers or for positioning toward future gameweeks—Pras introduces a sophisticated visualization tool created by Solio. He acknowledges the tool looks complicated, joking that it might remind viewers of the information-dense screens from the film Minority Report, but promises that the underlying concept is straightforward once explained.

The tool displays fixture projections in a matrix format, with different columns representing different chip strategies. Column one shows what the fixture ticker looks like for managers planning to free hit in gameweek 33. The data has been processed to remove gameweek 33 itself, since those playing a free hit can select from any team regardless of their blank status. The remaining data shows projected goals for various teams across gameweeks 32 and 34—the weeks when free hitters in 33 will still be playing regular fixtures.

For attack in a free hit 33 strategy, Liverpool emerges as the clear priority target. Pras notes that Salah's fitness status remains uncertain—he'll need to be monitored following the international break—but managers should be bullish on Liverpool regardless, as they have favorable home fixtures against both Fulham (gameweek 32) and Crystal Palace (gameweek 34). Manchester United appears as the second-highest projected team across both those gameweeks, making their attack a viable secondary option. Fulham rounds out the top tier, which explains why Callum Wilson has become such a popular pickup this gameweek.

Pras also highlights Andreas Pereira and Morgan Rogers as reasonable fourth-tier options, noting that between the 4.4 million price points, there's "really nothing much in it." Managers choosing between West Ham, Villa, and Fulham attack options should base their decisions on factors like goal distribution and penalty involvement rather than marginal fixture differences. For example, if Salah misses any games, someone like Mac Allister could be an excellent differential play because he'd likely step into penalty duties.

The right-hand column of the Solio tool addresses the alternative strategy: managers planning to free hit in gameweek 34 rather than 33. In this scenario, Chelsea's attack becomes the primary target, with Cole Palmer, Pedro Neto, and Noni Madueke all presenting excellent options. Newcastle emerges as the secondary target for gameweek 34 coverage.

This is where the Fulham versus Newcastle decision becomes particularly relevant. Both teams have favorable fixtures this gameweek, but the optimal choice depends on your long-term strategy. If you plan to free hit in gameweek 34 and want to get Newcastle exposure now while potentially benching them until then, Newcastle makes sense. If you want to play your chosen assets immediately and throughout the gameweek 34 window, Fulham is the better choice. The Solio data gives managers the framework to make this decision systematically rather than arbitrarily.

Detailed Takeaways

Takeaway 1: Blank Gameweeks Require Strategic Thinking, Not Panic

The fundamental message from Pras is that blank gameweeks, while stressful, represent opportunity rather than catastrophe. The key is understanding your chip strategy in advance and making transfers that position you optimally for the double gameweeks that will follow. Managers who panic-sell Palace assets or other blank-week players often find themselves chasing points and spending valuable transfers playing catch-up. Instead, view blank gameweek 31 as a temporary inconvenience that sets you up for potential massive point returns in gameweeks 33 and potentially 36.

Takeaway 2: Patience on the Wildcard Is Likely Rewarded

Given that the international break provides near-certainty about which fixtures will move between gameweeks 33 and 34, waiting until after the FA Cup quarterfinals to press the wildcard button represents the most information-efficient approach. The price changes during those extra days are unlikely to be significant enough to justify committing to a team before you know exactly which teams will double. Managers should use gameweek 31 to make minor adjustments—selling obviously redundant players like Palace assets or players with no immediate fixtures—but avoid major reconstructive surgery until the wildcard window opens.

Takeaway 3: Your Chip Strategy Should Match Your Current Team Structure

Pras' personal inclination toward the free hit 33, bench boost 36, wildcard 35 strategy stems from the fact that his current team already looks reasonable for the blank and non-blank gameweeks surrounding it. Managers who already own Salah, Bruno Fernandes, and other players with good fixtures in gameweeks 32 and 34 have less urgent need to wildcard immediately. Conversely, managers whose squads are badly mismatched with the upcoming fixtures should strongly consider the earlier wildcard. The optimal strategy is personal—it depends on where you are, not where someone else thinks you should be.

Takeaway 4: Use Fixture Tickers to Make Data-Driven Decisions, Not Gut Feelings

The Solio fixture ticker demonstrates that optimal team selection isn't about finding hidden gems through intuition but rather analyzing projected goals across relevant gameweeks and selecting players from teams that appear most frequently at the top of those projections. For a free hit 33 strategy, Liverpool and Man United attack are clearly the priorities. For a free hit 34 strategy, Chelsea and Newcastle attack deserve attention. These aren't arbitrary recommendations—they're the logical output of systematic analysis. Managers should seek out similar tools and resist the temptation to pick players based on reputation or recent form alone.

Takeaway 5: European Results Create Both Blanks and Doubles—Monitor Continuously

The relationship between European competition and FPL fixtures is a double-edged sword. More English teams progressing means more blank gameweeks in the short term but also more double gameweeks as those teams continue their European journeys. Palace progressing eliminates their gameweek 31 fixture but strengthens the case for gameweek 33 and gameweek 36 doubles. Managers should track European results religiously during this period and update their fixture charts accordingly. What looks like bad news today (another blank) might be great news tomorrow (confirmed double).
