Europe's Borders Under Threat from Russia: Baltic States Gear Up for War

A Comprehensive Report from FRANCE 24

---

Introduction

In the heart of Vilnius, Lithuania's capital, a unsettling reality has settled over residents along the eastern edge of the European Union. The border with Belarus lies just thirty kilometers away—a proximity that has taken on entirely new significance since Russia's full-scale invasion of Ukraine in February 2022. Across the city, Ukrainian flags hang from buildings in a visible display of solidarity, yet these flags serve a dual purpose: they are also a constant reminder that armed conflict remains perilously close. That reminder became dramatically concrete last summer when a Russian combat drone, carrying two kilograms of explosives, flew directly over the Lithuanian capital, waking residents in the middle of the night with what one witness described as "a very loud motorbike in the sky."

The incident sent shockwaves through the Baltic nation. For five days, the drone vanished from radar before eventually being discovered at a military training ground—an unsettling demonstration of how vulnerable European airspace has become. The questions that followed were uncomfortable: Why wasn't this drone stopped? How exactly do defense systems work when faced with such threats? These inquiries have catalyzed a dramatic transformation in how the Baltic states approach their defense, spawning what European officials now call an ambitious initiative to build an anti-drone wall stretching three thousand kilometers from Finland to Poland.

---

The Drone Incident That Changed Everything

The Russian drone that penetrated Lithuanian airspace last summer represented more than a technical failure—it exposed a fundamental vulnerability in European border defense. The aircraft was armed with explosives and capable of reaching civilian populations, yet it navigated undetected through radar systems designed to identify traditional military threats. One resident who witnessed the drone passing overhead described the experience with chilling clarity: "It flew right over my building and followed that path. It sounded like a very loud motorbike in the sky. At the time, I didn't realize the drone was carrying explosives."

What made this incident particularly alarming was not merely the drone's presence, but the five-day gap between its flight over Vilnius and its eventual discovery. During that time, authorities had no knowledge of the aircraft's location or its intentions. The drone eventually landed at a military training ground, where its payload of explosives was confirmed. This episode forced military planners to confront an uncomfortable truth: existing defense infrastructure was inadequate for the modern threat landscape, where inexpensive drones can penetrate deep into sovereign territory with relative ease.

The incident ignited public debate across Lithuania regarding the effectiveness of current defense systems and the government's responsibility to protect its citizens. Questions emerged about coordination between military and civilian authorities, the capabilities of existing radar systems, and the adequacy of response protocols. More broadly, the event crystallized what many Baltic officials had been warning about for years—that Russia's willingness to use military force against its neighbors extended beyond Ukraine's borders.

---

Building Europe's Anti-Drone Wall

In response to these emerging threats, European nations have embarked on an ambitious defensive initiative that represents the most significant strengthening of the continent's eastern flank since the Cold War. The proposed anti-drone wall, stretching three thousand kilometers from Finland through the Baltic states to Poland, aims to create a comprehensive air defense network capable of detecting and neutralizing the new generation of low-flying unmanned aircraft that traditional systems struggle to identify.

The strategy involves multiple layers of defense. New radar installations are being deployed specifically designed to detect aircraft flying at low altitudes—precisely the flight profile favored by combat drones that seek to avoid traditional air defense systems. These radars will fill a critical gap in existing coverage, as most current installations were designed to track high-altitude military aircraft rather than the small, slow-moving drones that have become increasingly prevalent on modern battlefields.

Beyond detection, European planners are investing heavily in offensive capabilities to bring down hostile drones. Advanced electronic jammers will attempt to disrupt drone navigation systems and communications, forcing them to the ground or causing them to crash. Mini-missiles, specifically designed to intercept smaller aircraft, are also being deployed. The combination of improved detection and multiple engagement options represents a comprehensive approach to a threat that has proven difficult to counter with any single method.

The scale of investment reflects the seriousness with which European officials view the drone threat. NATO members along the eastern border have committed substantial resources to this initiative, recognizing that the lessons learned in Ukraine regarding drone warfare have profound implications for their own security. The three-thousand-kilometer frontier represents a vast area to defend, but military planners argue that gaps in coverage anywhere along this line could provide an adversary with opportunities for infiltration or intimidation.

---

Latvia's Military Base: A Glimpse into the Future of Drone Warfare

At a military base in Latvia, journalists were given a demonstration of one of the technologies destined for deployment along these borders. The scenario: a suspicious aircraft has just been spotted, and a defensive drone is immediately launched in pursuit. Operators at the base track the target, with artificial intelligence systems keeping the threat firmly in the camera's sights. Within seconds, the defensive drone closes the distance at speeds up to 250 kilometers per hour.

What distinguishes this system from traditional air defense is its degree of automation. "It's fully automated," one operator explained during the demonstration. "I'm not doing anything. I can do some tiny adjustments if needed. It locks the target on center of the camera and steers itself into the target. It will explode before the target and hit it down—basically intercepting." The system represents a significant advancement in autonomous defense capabilities, allowing rapid response to threats that might otherwise evade human operators.

The drones demonstrated at the Latvian base carry a price tag of less than ten thousand euros each—a relatively modest cost compared to traditional military aircraft or missile systems. This affordability is intentional, reflecting the reality that modern drone warfare favors volume over sophistication. As one military analyst noted during the demonstration, Russia currently produces hundreds of drones daily, with the capacity to scale to thousands in the near future. European defenses must be equally scalable to meet this challenge.

Within months, these defensive drones could be deployed along the Latvian border, providing a tangible capability against the Russian drone threat. The technology is moving from concept to deployment with remarkable speed, driven by the urgency of the security situation and the demonstrated effectiveness of similar systems in the Ukraine conflict.

---

Lithuania's Citizen Army: Preparing the Population

While high-tech defensive systems grab headlines, Lithuania has embarked on a parallel initiative that speaks to a deeper transformation in how the Baltic states approach national defense. The country has opened training centers where citizens—including children as young as eight—can learn to pilot drones. The program, led by the Ministry of Defense, represents an unprecedented effort to prepare civilian populations for potential conflict.

The training centers have experienced overwhelming demand. Six additional locations are planned to open soon, with the ambitious goal of training fifteen thousand adults and eight thousand children. The scale of this initiative reflects Lithuania's determination to create a society capable of resisting aggression, drawing on the lessons of Ukrainian resistance while preparing for what many officials view as an inevitable future confrontation with Russia.

When asked about the connection between the training program and the Russian threat, instructors were careful to emphasize the educational aspect rather than the military dimension. One instructor explained that "our program was designed to give children the basics of aviation and if they enjoy it, they can choose to continue later on." This framing, while technically accurate, obscures the clear strategic rationale: Lithuania is building a pipeline of drone operators who could be mobilized in the event of conflict.

The involvement of children as young as eight has generated some discussion, but Lithuanian officials argue that early exposure to drone technology is essential in an era when such systems have become central to modern warfare. The training is voluntary and recreational, but the skills developed could prove invaluable in a defensive scenario. This approach represents a philosophical shift away from professionalized military forces toward broader societal preparation for conflict.

---

The Strategic Calculus: Why the Baltic States Are Bracing for War

The aggressive military buildup along Europe's eastern border reflects a fundamental reassessment of the threat posed by Russia. For years, Baltic officials had warned that their countries could be potential targets for Russian aggression, but these warnings often fell on skeptical ears in Western European capitals. The invasion of Ukraine changed everything, demonstrating that Moscow was willing to use military force to redraw borders and subjugate neighboring nations.

The proximity of the Belarus border to Vilnius—just thirty kilometers—makes this threat particularly immediate for Lithuania. Russian forces in Belarus could potentially launch operations into Lithuanian territory within hours, and the flat terrain along the border offers few natural obstacles to military movement. The concrete barriers now being deployed along the border represent a physical manifestation of these concerns, creating obstacles that would slow any potential invasion while giving Lithuanian forces time to mobilize.

Beyond the immediate military threat, Baltic states are grappling with the reality of Russian hybrid warfare. The drone that flew over Vilnius may have been intended as a test of Western resolve rather than a genuine attack, but its psychological impact was significant. Such incidents demonstrate that Russia can project power into NATO territory while maintaining plausible deniability, forcing Western nations to respond without the clear justification that would accompany an overt military attack.

European officials acknowledge that they remain behind Russia in drone technology and production capacity. "Europe is still sleeping," one military expert observed, contrasting the continent's current efforts with the massive investment Russia has made in unmanned systems. The three-thousand-kilometer anti-drone wall represents an attempt to close this gap, but experts suggest it will take years of sustained investment to achieve parity with Russian capabilities.

Detailed Takeaways

The Drone Threat Is Already Here: The Russian combat drone that flew over Vilnius was not an isolated incident but part of an escalating pattern of intimidation. With Russia capable of producing hundreds of drones daily—and potentially thousands in the near future—the threat to European airspace is immediate and growing. The five-day gap between the drone's flight and its discovery reveals fundamental gaps in surveillance capabilities that the new anti-drone initiative aims to address.

Technology Alone Cannot Provide Security: While the automated intercept drones demonstrated in Latvia represent impressive technological achievements, they are part of a broader ecosystem of defense that includes physical barriers, human training, and societal preparation. Lithuania's decision to train thousands of citizens, including children, reflects an understanding that modern conflict requires mobilization beyond professional military forces.

The Financial Dimension Matters: Each defensive drone costs less than ten thousand euros—a fraction of traditional military systems. This affordability enables deployment in volume, but it also means that adversaries can saturate defenses with inexpensive drones. The economic aspect of drone warfare favors attackers, creating challenges for defensive planners who must find cost-effective solutions to protect vast territories.

The Timeline for Implementation Is Urgent: European officials speak of deploying new systems "within months," but the full three-thousand-kilometer defensive network will take years to complete. During this transition period, the Baltic states remain particularly vulnerable to the kind of drone incursions already witnessed over Vilnius. The urgency of the threat makes rapid deployment a priority even at the cost of imperfect solutions.

Public Awareness Is Being Deliberately Cultivated: The Ukrainian flags hanging from buildings in Vilnius serve as visible reminders of the conflict, and the drone incident has clearly raised public awareness of security threats. Lithuania's citizen training program, while framed as educational, clearly aims to prepare the population for potential conflict. This represents a cultural shift toward treating defense as a societal responsibility rather than solely a government function.
