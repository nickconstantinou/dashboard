# If You Have Career Regrets... Watch This | Bill Gurley

## Introduction

Bill Gurley, the renowned venture capitalist and general partner at Benchmark, sits down for a candid conversation about one of life's most universal yet rarely discussed experiences: career regret. With a career trajectory that has included engineering, Wall Street analysis, and eventually becoming one of Silicon Valley's most influential investors, Gurley brings both personal experience and professional observation to a topic that touches nearly everyone in the workforce.

The conversation emerges from research Gurley conducted while writing his book, which revealed a striking statistic: when asked if they could start their career over again, seven out of ten people would make different choices. This number held even when validated through Wharton People Analytics, which confirmed that six out of ten people—still a remarkable majority—would alter their professional paths if given the chance. What makes this conversation particularly compelling is not just the data, but Gurley's honest exploration of whether even successful people escape this phenomenon of doubt and wondering "what if."

The discussion ventures into territory that most career advice glosses over: the weight of inaction, the importance of curiosity, and the critical role that support systems play in enabling bold career moves. Rather than offering generic motivation, Gurley and his interviewer dissect the psychological mechanisms behind career satisfaction and provide concrete frameworks for making better decisions.

## The Psychology of Career Regret

The conversation opens with a deep dive into the nature of regret itself, drawing from Daniel Pink's book *The Power of Regrets*. Gurley explains that research reveals a crucial distinction between two types of regret: those stemming from action and those from inaction. Humans, he notes, are remarkably good at making mistakes and moving forward—the regret of having done something often feels lighter, more resolvable. But the path never taken, the door never opened, carries exponentially more psychological weight.

This insight has profound implications for career decision-making. The "what if" questions that haunt professionals tend to cluster around opportunities not pursued, risks not taken, and passions not followed. Gurley acknowledges that his own research may have been capturing exactly this phenomenon—the survey questions about starting over likely activate the brain's tendency to ruminate on roads not traveled, particularly as people age. Longitudinal studies cited in the conversation show that regrets of inaction become increasingly heavy as individuals grow older, accumulating like sediment in the mind.

The interviewer pushes further, admitting a personal vulnerability: despite having sold a previous company and currently running a successful venture, he still questions whether he followed the right path. Gurley's response is particularly valuable—he Normalizes this experience, suggesting that even those who appear successful frequently harbor these doubts. The question becomes not whether we experience regret, but how we navigate it and whether we can make decisions that minimize its long-term weight.

## The Framework for Better Career Decisions

Gurley outlines several practical frameworks for evaluating career choices, the first being a question he asked himself repeatedly throughout his career: "Do I see myself doing this thirty years from now?" This deceptively simple inquiry served as a clarifying lens during two major career transitions. After earning his engineering degree, Gurley spent three years on Wall Street as a sell-side research analyst—performing well but constantly asking himself whether this would be his life's work.

In both his Wall Street position and later roles, he looked around at the people who had done the same work for decades. This wasn't meant judgmentally, he emphasizes, but rather as a mirror: if someone had dedicated their entire career to this path, was that the life he wanted? The exercise provided clarity. After approximately two or three years in each position, the answer became clear—he did not want to be doing this work at sixty, and that realization enabled him to make decisive changes.

The conversation introduces Jeff Bezos' "regret minimization framework," which approaches decisions from the perspective of an eighty-year-old self looking back. This reframing technique recognizes that our present-day risk aversion often leads us toward safe choices that we later regret. The eighty-year-old version of ourselves, having experienced more of life's brevity, would likely encourage more risk-taking and pursuit of meaningful goals. Gurley notes that this framework aligns with his own intuitive approach of thinking about decisions in terms of a complete life rather than immediate comfort.

## A Story of Radical Career Change

The interview takes a personal turn when Gurley shares the remarkable story of how he joined Airbnb—a narrative that exemplifies the principles being discussed. As a college student in Nashville, Tennessee, Gurley ran a hot dog stand called "Southern Sam's Wieners" (described as "as big as a baby's arm") to pay for his education. While working on his degree, he became obsessed with the internet and Googled "where do internet companies live?" The answer was San Francisco.

Without connections or guarantees, Gurley researched which internet companies were "cool" at the time—Uber (then called UberCab) and Airbnb. He cold-emailed Brian Chesky, Airbnb's CEO, and when that received no response, found another employee named Justin and simply stated: "I'm going to be there on Monday. Can I interview for this company?"

There was one small problem: Gurley lived in Tennessee, not the Bay Area. He lied and said he lived in the Bay Area, flew out the next day, interviewed, got the job, returned to Tennessee, dropped out of college, and sold everything he owned to pursue this opportunity. The chutzpah is remarkable, but the story doesn't end there.

Gurley's parents, entrepreneurs in Missouri who had started their careers owning a fruit stand, provided crucial support when he told them he was leaving school to join a startup. Their response perfectly illustrates the role of parental support in enabling bold career moves. When Gurley asked if the startup had health insurance and mentioned it was paying $22 an hour, his parents simply said "Okay, cool. We'll come pack up your stuff." No lectures about the stability of completing college, no fear-based warnings about startup risk. They had taken their own flyers in life—his father had left Virginia to become one of the first employees at NASA in Houston—and they understood the value of supporting a child's ambition.

## The Systems That Enable Bold Action

This story becomes a pivot point for discussing what actually enables people to take career risks. Gurley observes that he has met many immigrants who fall into two distinct categories: those who came with nothing and are willing to do anything to win, and those who came to create a big life but feel pressure to take the safe path. His parents, he suggests, were clearly in the first category—and that orientation enabled his own risk-taking.

The conversation expands to critique systems that don't encourage this kind of exploration. Gurley points out that while we visit doctors for physical checkups and attend school to get good grades, at no point does anyone ask us whether we're happy doing what we're doing. There is no formal education in how to find meaningful work, how to discover one's passions, or how to evaluate whether a career path aligns with long-term values.

This gap inspired Gurley's interest in the Japanese concept of *ikigai*—a Venn diagram with four intersecting circles representing what the world wants, what the world will pay for, what you're good at, and what you're passionate about. The goal is to find something in the center where all four elements overlap. Yet despite its importance, this kind of self-knowledge is rarely taught formally. Gurley mentions that his book includes multiple exercises designed to help people discover these answers, borrowing techniques from various sources to provide pathways for self-discovery.

## Key Insights for Career Decision-Making

The conversation offers several actionable takeaways for anyone navigating career decisions.

First, the "thirty years from now" test provides immediate clarity. When facing any career choice—whether to accept a position, stay in a role, or pursue a new opportunity—asking whether you want to be doing this work at sixty or seventy acts as a powerful filter. The answer may not come quickly, but the question itself forces long-term thinking that short-term compensation discussions often obscure.

Second, the regret minimization framework offers a reframe for risk-averse decision-makers. By imagining your eighty-year-old self looking back on the decision, you access a perspective that values experience and fulfillment over safety. The person you will become is more likely to regret the risks not taken than the failures experienced while trying something meaningful.

Third, curiosity itself is a pathway, not a preliminary. Gurley's book includes a chapter titled "Chase Your Curiosity" specifically because he believes the hardest part of finding meaningful work is the discovery process itself. Rather than waiting for a fully formed passion to emerge, actively pursuing curiosity leads to information, connections, and eventual clarity. The interviewer himself found Airbnb not by knowing what he wanted, but by investigating what seemed interesting.

Fourth, the support system matters enormously. Gurley explicitly states that the emotional support of parents who encourage risk-taking rather than safe choices "is one of the biggest things that separates what allows people to win versus allow people to lose." For those in advisory roles—whether as parents, mentors, or leaders—the willingness to support others in taking calculated risks may be the most valuable gift they can offer.

Finally, the data suggests that most people would change their careers if they could. Rather than viewing this as discouraging, Gurley seems to suggest it should be liberating: the uncertainty you feel is nearly universal, and the fact that six out of ten successful professionals would do things differently means that questioning your path is not a sign of failure but a recognition of how much possibility exists.

## Who Should Watch This Conversation

This interview speaks most directly to professionals in their twenties and thirties who are navigating early and mid-career decisions, particularly those who feel pulled between stability and meaning. The frameworks offered provide concrete tools for making decisions that resist the pressure to simply optimize for immediate compensation or safety.

However, the conversation also holds significant value for parents, mentors, and advisors. Gurley's story about his parents' response to his leaving school offers a model for how to support young people in taking career risks—a lesson that applies whether you're guiding a child, a mentee, or a junior colleague. Anyone in a position of influence over another's career decisions will benefit from considering whether they're encouraging safety or enabling exploration.

Finally, anyone experiencing career regret or questioning their current path will find both validation and practical guidance. The central message—that most people would make different choices, that doubt is normal, and that frameworks exist for making better decisions—offers relief alongside strategy. The conversation Normalizes the experience of wondering "what if" while providing concrete tools for answering that question more confidently.
