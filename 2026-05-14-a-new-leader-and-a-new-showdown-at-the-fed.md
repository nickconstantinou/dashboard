# Introduction

On a single afternoon in May, the United States Senate executed one of the most consequential personnel changes in American financial history — confirming Kevin Warsh as the new chair of the Federal Reserve — while simultaneously creating one of the most constitutionally ambiguous situations the central bank has ever faced. Jerome Powell, the man Trump had spent a year publicly vilifying, did not leave. He would not go quietly into retirement as the president had demanded. Instead, Powell announced he would remain as a governor on the Fed's Board, a position that grants him significant ongoing power even without the chairmanship.

This arrangement breaks with nearly eight decades of precedent. The last time a departing Fed chair stayed on as a governor was at President Harry Truman's explicit request in the late 1940s. No modern occupant of the position has voluntarily remained after their term ended while the president who appointed them explicitly wanted them gone. The decision, Powell explained in his final press conference as chair, was not about him. It was about the institution — and the broader threat that Trump's sustained attacks posed to the Fed's hard-won independence.

The weeks leading to this moment had been extraordinary, even by the standards of an administration that had already upended countless norms. Trump had called Powell "a fool" and "grossly incompetent" on multiple occasions, openly mused about firing him, and ultimately weaponized the Justice Department to investigate him over mundane construction cost overruns. That investigation ultimately backfired, derailing the confirmation process and forcing a retreat. But it also steeled Powell's resolve, convincing him that leaving now — with the institution under siege — would be a dereliction of duty.

---

# The Campaign Against Powell

The conflict between Trump and Powell had been building since early in Trump's second term, driven by the president's intense frustration that the Fed chair would not lower interest rates to fuel economic growth. Trump had openly mocked Powell's intelligence, questioned his competence, and repeatedly suggested he should be removed. But these were merely the public salvos in a much larger pressure campaign.

The turning point came when the Justice Department, under the direction of US Attorney Jeanine Pirro, launched a criminal investigation into Powell's oversight of renovations at the Federal Reserve's Washington headquarters. The project had run millions of dollars over budget — a serious administrative issue, but hardly a prosecutable crime. Nevertheless, federal prosecutors made unannounced visits to the Fed's active construction site, and the investigation became a focal point of the administration's campaign.

This move, however, immediately drew widespread criticism. Federal judges and legal observers noted that the investigation appeared designed not to uncover wrongdoing but to harass and pressure Powell into resigning. The timing was particularly suspect: launching a criminal probe just months before Powell's term was set to expire looked less like law enforcement and more like political theater. As one commentator noted, the scenario was akin to "designing a system to cause interest rates to go up and not down" by forcing the Fed and the executive branch into open conflict.

The investigation quickly became a significant impediment to Trump's own goals. Republican Senator Tom Tillis of North Carolina announced that he would not support any of Trump's Fed nominees until the legal threats against Powell had ended. Tillis occupied a pivotal position on the Senate Banking Committee, the body that oversees the confirmation process for Federal Reserve appointments. With the Republican majority in the Senate slim and fragile, his opposition created a de facto blockade.

---

# The Political Calculus Shifts

The standoff placed Trump in a painful position. He had long wanted Powell gone — not merely replaced as chair, but thoroughly humiliated and removed from any position of influence. The criminal investigation represented the sharpest instrument in achieving that goal. Yet the political cost of maintaining that investigation was blocking his ability to install Kevin Warsh, his chosen successor, in the chairmanship.

The clock was ticking. Powell's term ended on May 15, and the Senate was running out of time to confirm Warsh before that deadline. Each passing day without resolution increased the risk that the Fed would enter a period of leadership limbo at the worst possible moment — with high prices, economic uncertainty, and markets watching closely for signals about the direction of monetary policy.

Throughout this period, the signals from the administration were contradictory and confusing. Pirro insisted the investigation was proceeding in earnest. Federal prosecutors visited Fed facilities. Yet at the same time, there were indications that the legal foundation was crumbling and that the administration might eventually need to capitulate. Powell himself began speaking more openly about his intentions, signaling that he would not be browbeaten into submission.

At a press conference in April, Powell delivered a pointed statement that reframed the entire conflict. He described the investigation as part of a broader pattern of pressure and intimidation aimed at compromising the Fed's independence. The central question, he argued, was whether the institution would continue to set interest rates based on economic evidence and conditions, or whether it would bow to political pressure and become an extension of the executive branch.

---

# Powell's Decision to Stay

On April 24, Pirro finally announced that she was dropping the criminal investigation. The announcement came just three weeks before Powell's term expiration, and it was enough to unlock the Senate confirmation process. Senator Tillis ended his blockade, and the path to confirming Kevin Warsh was suddenly clear.

But it was not enough for Powell. In his final press conference as Fed chair, Powell explicitly addressed the announcement, welcoming it while noting its inadequacy. The US attorney had said she would not hesitate to restart the investigation "should the facts warrant doing so." To Powell, this conditional language meant the threat remained alive. He had demanded the investigation be "well and truly over with transparency and finality" — a threshold clearly not met by Pirro's announcement.

His decision to stay, he explained, was not what he had planned. He had long intended to retire, to move on to the next chapter of his life. But the events of the preceding three months had made that impossible. "I'm literally staying because of the actions that have been taken," he said. "I had long planned to be retiring, and, you know, the things that have happened in, really, in the last three months have, I think, left me no choice but to stay until I see them through at least that long."

The deeper reason, Powell emphasized, was the threat to the institution itself. "I worry that these attacks are battering the institution and putting at risk the thing that really matters to the public, which is the ability to conduct monetary policy without taking into consideration political factors." He framed this as existential: "It has risked the Fed becoming more politicized, and it has risked the US economy being undermined."

---

# The Strategic Logic of Staying

By remaining on the Board of Governors, Powell accomplishes something that resignation would not: he limits Trump's ability to reshape the Federal Reserve in his preferred image. The Board of Governors is a seven-person body that exercises enormous influence over the direction of American monetary policy. These seven individuals make the key decisions on interest rates, bank regulation, and staffing across the entire Federal Reserve system, including the regional bank presidents who sit across the country.

Trump had successfully appointed a new chair in Kevin Warsh. But with Powell staying on as a governor, the president could no longer add another ally to the board. The seat Powell occupies remains blocked. This effectively reduces Trump's influence over the institution, preserving a counterweight to the new chair's agenda.

From a purely legal standpoint, staying on offers Powell limited additional protection. A sitting governor cannot be removed by the president at will, which provides some shield against arbitrary dismissal. But the deeper protection is institutional: by remaining at the table, Powell ensures that any future attempts to overhaul the Fed or subordinate it to political demands will face resistance from someone with inside knowledge, institutional credibility, and the legitimacy that comes from having led the institution through the pandemic and the subsequent inflationary crisis.

---

# The Uncertain Coexistence

The installation of Kevin Warsh as Fed chair thus unfolds in deeply unusual circumstances. Warsh served as a Fed governor between 2006 and 2011, overlapping with the global financial crisis, and was instrumental in crafting the central bank's emergency response. His tenure there, however, also seeded his deep dissatisfaction with the institution and specifically with Powell, whom he blames for mishandling the post-crisis period and subsequent years.

Warsh has been a vocal critic of the Fed under Powell's leadership, going so far as to call publicly for regime change. The question now is how aggressively he will push that agenda with Powell lingering in the background. Powell has said he intends to keep a low profile as a governor, but the markets, Congress, and the media will be watching his every move. Any speech he delivers, any statement he makes in rate-setting meetings, will be parsed for signals about whether he is actively working against the new chair's priorities.

The situation creates genuine ambiguity about where power truly resides. Warsh holds the chairmanship, but Powell retains the authority and knowledge of a sitting governor, with a platform to shape decisions on interest rates, regulatory policy, and institutional direction. This is "a central bank in a crucial moment" — one where nobody is quite certain who is really in charge.

# Detailed Takeaways

**The Federal Reserve's independence is more fragile than commonly assumed.** Powell's decision to stay was framed as a defense of institutional autonomy, but the fact that it required a former chairman to remain in a subordinate role to prevent political capture reveals how easily the Fed's structure can be leveraged against it. The president's ability to install a loyalist as chair, combined with the power to fill vacant governor seats, could over time reshape the institution into an instrument of monetary policy tuned to political rather than economic considerations.

**The criminal investigation was a strategic miscalculation.** The Trump administration's decision to launch a criminal probe into Powell backfired precisely because it created a visible conflict of interest. Senator Tillis's blockade showed that even members of the president's own party were unwilling to participate in what looked like an explicitly political targeting of a Fed chair. The episode illustrates that attempts to use law enforcement as a tool of political pressure carry reputational and procedural costs that can derail the intended outcome.

**Institutional staying power matters in the executive branch.** Powell's ability to remain on the Board of Governors demonstrates that formal departure from one position does not mean departure from influence. By leveraging a technical entitlement — the right of former chairs to continue serving as governors — he created a structural obstacle to full presidential control. This tactic echoes historical precedents where officials used remaining authority to constrain successors, and it suggests that the architecture of government agencies can be weaponized defensively by those reluctant to leave.

**Political pressure on central banks is a global pattern, not a US anomaly.** While the specifics of the Trump-Powell conflict are uniquely American, the underlying dynamic — elected officials pressuring unelected central bankers to prioritize short-term growth over price stability — appears across developed economies. The Fed's independence has historically been more robust than many peers, but the Powell episode demonstrates that even the most powerful central bank is not immune to sustained political attack when its decisions conflict with executive priorities.

**The Warsh appointment carries significant implications for monetary policy direction.** Warsh's documented skepticism of the Fed's post-crisis framework suggests he may pursue a different approach to interest rates and financial regulation than Powell favored. His history of calling for regime change at the Fed indicates a willingness to depart from established norms, and with Powell potentially resisting from the board, the institution could face open philosophical conflicts about the appropriate conduct of monetary policy in coming years.
