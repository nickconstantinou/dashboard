Anthropic Just Got Hacked by China. But Was This Illegal?

Summary

In a surprising turn of events, Anthropic recently exposed three major Chinese AI labs for conducting over 16 million fraudulent conversations with their Claude model in what appears to be an attempt to steal AI capabilities through a process called "distillation." The affected Chinese companies include Deepseek (responsible for 150,000 exchanges), Moonshot AI (3.4 million exchanges), and Minimax (13 million exchanges). This follows similar reports from Google and OpenAI, who both claimed China was attempting to hack their Gemini and OpenAI models respectively.

However, here's where the story gets interesting: the video argues that what China allegedly did may not actually be illegal. Distillation is a common AI practice where a smaller "student" model learns to mimic the outputs of a larger "teacher" model at a fraction of the training cost. Anthropic itself has used this technique, distilling Claude Opus into their Haiku model, and Google has distilled Gemini Ultra into Gemini Nano. The video highlights what it sees as hypocrisy‚ÄîAnthropic is now crying wolf about being hacked while using the exact same methodology to build their own product line.

The video raises questions about whether this is truly a security breach or simply fair competition in the AI race. Anthropic's terms of service technically prohibit using their model for distillation without permission, but enforcing this globally, especially against foreign entities, presents significant legal challenges. The broader context is the open-source AI revolution happening in China, with some suggesting that rapid Chinese AI advancements may have been fueled by access to U.S. technology.
