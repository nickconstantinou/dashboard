Iran war stifles shipping through Hormuz: ‘Unprecedented’ fallout for oil markets & global economy

Iran War Stifles Shipping Through Hormuz: 'Unprecedented' Fallout for Oil Markets & Global Economy

Introduction

The Strait of Hormuz—one of the world's most critical chokepoints for global energy transportation—has become the epicenter of a rapidly escalating conflict with ramifications that threaten to reshape international energy markets and geopolitical alliances. In the aftermath of Iran's direct military retaliation against Israel and the United States, shipping companies, oil traders, and governments worldwide are grappling with what experts describe as an "almost unprecedented" disruption to the flow of crude oil and liquefied natural gas through this vital seaway.

John Marx, chair of Crossbar Information and editorial director of the Gulf States newsletter, offered his analysis in a televised interview, providing critical insight into the multidimensional crisis unfolding across the Middle East. With decades of experience covering energy markets and Gulf region affairs, Marx drew striking comparisons to historical crises while highlighting the unique characteristics of the current situation—particularly its impact on Asian economies and the unexpected nature of Iran's immediate military response. The conversation addressed not only immediate market concerns but also deeper questions about the durability of the United States' strategic relationships with Gulf allies and the long-term trajectory of global energy dependency.

How This Crisis Differs From 2022

When asked whether the current situation feels like a replay of the 2022 energy crisis triggered by Russia's invasion of Ukraine, Marx immediately distinguished between the two scenarios—though he acknowledged very real European concerns about natural gas supplies. He explained that while Europeans have legitimate reasons for concern due to the shutting in of Qatari gas exports, the broader global oil market faces a distinctly different challenge this time around, one that is "almost unprecedented" in its scope and implications.

The key difference, according to Marx, lies in the geographic orientation of global energy markets. Whereas the 2022 crisis primarily affected European dependence on Russian gas, the current situation threatens supplies flowing toward Asia—the axis around which global energy markets increasingly revolve. This fundamental shift in the crisis geography means that the economic impact will be distributed differently across the globe, with potentially severe consequences for major Asian economies that have become increasingly reliant on Middle Eastern oil and gas shipments.

Marx noted that the Strait of Hormuz has experienced risky periods in the past, including during the Iran-Iraq war of the 1980s when American and British warships escorted merchant vessels through contested waters. However, what makes the current situation extraordinary is that an actual closure or severe disruption of this vital seaway has never actually occurred—even during the most acute geopolitical tensions of the past four decades. This places the crisis in a category alongside the 1970s oil shocks, Marx suggested, though the ultimate long-term consequences remain uncertain.

Qatar's Critical Role and Force Majeure Declaration

The interview highlighted Qatar's extraordinary importance to global energy markets as the world's largest single exporter of liquefied natural gas. Marx emphasized that Qatar's strategic position and immense wealth have been built entirely around expanding LNG production and exports—a decades-long national strategy now suddenly interrupted by force majeure declarations on output due to the current conflict.

This development carries profound implications for European energy security in particular. Following Russia's invasion of Ukraine in February 2022, Europeans experienced a jarring realization about their deep dependence on Russian gas supplies. Qatar emerged as a critical alternative supplier, with European nations racing to secure long-term LNG contracts and build the necessary import infrastructure. The sudden disruption of Qatari exports—however temporary—represents a significant setback to these efforts and forces European economies to confront uncomfortable questions about energy vulnerability once again.

The Asian Axis: China and India Face Maximum Pressure

Marx explicitly identified the major Asian economies—particularly China and India—as "the biggest losers" from the current disruption to shipments through the Strait of Hormuz. This assessment reflects the fundamental reorientation of global oil trade flows over the past two decades, as Asian nations have become the primary consumers of Middle Eastern crude.

The situation for India appears particularly dire. Marx noted that India has placed itself in a vulnerable position by dramatically increasing imports of Russian fuel since Putin's invasion of Ukraine—from minimal levels to over one million barrels per day. This strategic shift, while economically rational in the short term, has created new dependencies just as regional tensions threaten to disrupt alternative supply routes. The United States recently granted India a thirty-day sanctions waiver on some Russian oil exports, underscoring the delicate diplomatic balancing act required during this crisis.

China, while in a somewhat better position than India, will nonetheless begin feeling the pain of disrupted shipments. Marx mentioned that China has accumulated the equivalent of approximately 200 days of oil imports in strategic reserves—a buffer that will provide some breathing room but cannot indefinitely substitute for steady flows through Hormuz. South Korea, another major Asian economy heavily dependent on Middle Eastern energy imports, was described as being in a "very bad situation" by the analyst.

European Economic Fallout: Fuel Prices Surge

The interview touched on immediate impacts visible in European fuel markets, where diesel prices in France recently climbed above €2 per liter—a psychologically significant threshold that signals further pain for consumers and businesses already grappling with elevated inflation. Marx confirmed that diesel is rising slightly more than unleaded petrol, reflecting the particular vulnerability of diesel-dependent industries such as transportation and manufacturing.

The timing, while challenging, could have been considerably worse. Marx noted that Europe is emerging from winter, when energy demand for heating would have compounded the crisis dramatically. Nevertheless, economic pressures across the continent remain substantial, with southern England, France, and Germany all experiencing significant challenges. The analyst suggested that European consumers should prepare for more expensive fuel in the coming weeks and months, with the ultimate extent depending on how quickly and completely shipping through Hormuz normalizes.

The US LNG Opportunity and New Dependencies

One of the striking ironies of the current conflict, as Marx observed, is that American liquefied natural gas exports stand to benefit significantly from the disruption to Qatari and other Middle Eastern supplies. The United States has emerged as the "newish boy on the block" in global LNG markets over the past decade, and European nations seeking alternatives to Russian pipeline gas have increasingly turned to American suppliers.

This dynamic raises uncomfortable questions about whether Europe is simply substituting one dependence for another. When pressed on whether critics are exaggerating concerns about swapping Russian gas dependency for American LNG dependency, Marx confirmed that such a transition is already well underway. He cited Morocco as an example of a country now importing both American and Qatari LNG through Spain, illustrating how European and North African energy infrastructure is being repurposed to deliver American gas to continental markets.

Marx also pointed to an interesting political question that the crisis is surfacing: how much pressure will emerge to reopen some of the gas pipelines that formerly brought Russian gas into Europe? This is precisely the kind of calculation that European governments "really didn't want to have to confront," he noted, but which the current crisis now makes unavoidable.

Gulf States and the US Alliance Under Strain

A significant portion of the discussion addressed the political and strategic fallout from the conflict, particularly regarding the relationship between Gulf States and the United States. The journalist noted that Gulf nations have poured billions of dollars into the US economy in exchange for American defense commitments—and asked how this arrangement would appear "when the guns go silent."

Marx offered a nuanced assessment, suggesting that the American offering will remain attractive both strategically and economically—for the foreseeable future, Gulf States "can't actually live without the US." He noted the interesting convergence between Gulf nations, particularly the United Arab Emirates and Saudi Arabia, and the technology-focused businesses surrounding Donald Trump, indicating that commercial ties beyond traditional energy trade will continue to bind the economies together.

However, Marx was clear that trust has been severely damaged. Drawing on conversations with sources in Gulf capitals, he described how American officials had essentially assured their Gulf partners that any military action would be "extremely efficient" with no real consequences for the energy industry. The Iranians' immediate and forceful response—which differed markedly from their restraint during the June 2024 Israel-US attacks on Iranian nuclear facilities—caught Gulf States by surprise. "The trust that has now been literally bombed out between those states will be hard to bring back," Marx observed, though he added that diplomatic protocol would require all parties to maintain polite appearances in the months ahead.

Why Trump Hasn't Struck Kharg Island

The interview explored an intriguing question: why has Donald Trump not targeted Kharg Island, the primary export terminal for Iranian oil? A significant Financial Times report the week of the interview focused specifically on this facility, which represents Iran's most critical energy infrastructure.

Marx characterized this as a difficult calculation even for Trump's military planners, noting that Iranian energy targets have been "in the crosshairs" but thus far spared. He suggested that there remains "an awful lot that hasn't happened yet" on both sides, indicating that the conflict could still escalate significantly. The analyst mentioned that the International Institute for Strategic Studies in London had been discussing assets the Iranians have not yet brought into play—including additional cruise missiles and drone capabilities.

Particularly significant is the question of whether the Houthis in Yemen—Iran's main allies in the region—will enter the conflict. Marx noted that his colleagues had been watching closely to see if, under pressure in Lebanon, the Houthis would be mobilized. Their track record against shipping and energy infrastructure "is major," and if fighting expands, "the Houthis will be there."

Key Takeaways

The Strait of Hormuz disruption represents a category of crisis not seen since the 1970s. Unlike the 2022 energy shock focused on European gas dependence, this crisis threatens the Asian economies that now dominate global oil consumption. The unprecedented nature lies not in theoretical risks that have existed for decades, but in the actual implementation of disruptions affecting real shipments through this vital chokepoint.

Gulf States' confidence in American security guarantees has been fundamentally shaken. The failure of US assurances that energy markets would not be disrupted—and the unexpected Iranian response—has created a trust deficit that will be difficult to repair regardless of how the immediate crisis resolves. This could have lasting implications for regional security arrangements and America's strategic influence in the Gulf.

European energy transition just became more complicated. The crisis simultaneously disrupts Qatari LNG (a key alternative to Russian gas) while creating opportunities for American LNG exports. This dynamic accelerates a shift toward US dependency that critics warned about, while also potentially reviving debates about Russian pipeline gas that European governments had sought to close.

Asian economies face the most acute short-term pain. China has strategic reserves providing some cushion, but India is in a particularly vulnerable position having shifted heavily toward Russian oil while still needing Middle Eastern supplies. South Korea and other Asian nations dependent on Hormuz transit face severe disruptions.

The conflict could still escalate significantly. Multiple potential targets remain untouched, and the Houthis have not yet entered the fray. Kharg Island and other Iranian energy infrastructure could become targets, potentially triggering a dramatic escalation in global oil supply disruptions.
