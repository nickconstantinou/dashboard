Introduction

When Jessica logged on for what she called a "dream come true" conversation with Conan O'Brien, the New Jersey OBGYN had no idea she'd be dispensing medical wisdom, anatomical education, and a few well-worn jokes about circumcised foreskin served as seafood. But that's exactly what made this episode of *Conan O'Brien Needs a Fan* so memorable.

The episode spotlights Jessica, a practicing obstetrician-gynecologist from Monmouth County, New Jersey, who confesses to being a longtime fan of Conan's comedy. She reveals she attended multiple tapings during her high school years, even preserving a ticket stub from a 1999 show where her mother encountered Fabio—the actor and model famous for butter substitute commercials. What makes this detail remarkable isn't just the nostalgia, but the serendipitous fate that followed: Fabio allegedly kissed Jessica's mother on the mouth while dipping her backward, only to be struck in the face by a rogue goose on a roller coaster approximately one month later. Jessica's father, a Brooklyn native she suspects possesses supernatural powers, allegedly placed a hex on Fabio in retaliation.

Beyond the celebrity encounter, the conversation ventures into territory that blends genuine medical insight with the irreverent humor expected from a Conan podcast. Jessica discusses the realities of delivering babies, the procedures that follow birth, and educates Conan—somewhat unsuccessfully—about basic female anatomy. She also explains the circumcision process in surprisingly clinical detail, treating it as routine rather than sensational.

Key Concepts & Arguments

The Miracle of Birth and Professional Renewal

Jessica's relationship with her profession reveals something profound about maintaining passion in demanding medical careers. Despite being in her 40s and describing herself as "a little old, a little schleppy," she insists that the moment she stops finding birth miraculous is the moment she should quit. She finds genuine awe in every ultrasound showing a first heartbeat and every infant's inaugural cry. This perspective offers insight into how medical professionals sustain emotional engagement with repetitive work—viewing each case as unique rather than routine.

Interestingly, Jessica expresses mild frustration with the timing of deliveries. "I wish it happened during normal business hours," she admits. "It's pretty rude" that babies insist on arriving at inconvenient hours, including bank holidays. This practical complaint humanizes a profession often portrayed as purely noble, revealing the real scheduling challenges obstetricians face alongside their life-saving work. She jokes about colleagues accusing her of trying to induce labor specifically to avoid nights and weekends, though she clarifies she doesn't even play golf—perhaps an outdated reference she acknowledges.

The conversation also briefly acknowledges that pregnancy doesn't always end in vaginal delivery. Jessica mentions Gourley, someone on the podcast team, recently enjoying paternity leave after welcoming his second child, indicating cesarean sections as a common alternative she manages.

Post-Birth Procedures: What Actually Happens

When Conan admits he was "very present" for both his children's births but struggles to remember details, Jessica provides a detailed explanation of standard post-delivery protocols. She emphasizes that mothers aren't immediately separated from their infants—babies go "right on mommy's abdomen" for skin-to-skin contact lasting that crucial first hour, which hospitals increasingly recognize as important for bonding and infant regulation.

Delayed cord clamping represents another significant practice Jessica highlights. Rather than immediately cutting the umbilical cord, medical teams allow blood to flow back into the baby before clamping, a procedure supported by research showing benefits for iron stores and early development. Jessica notes her hospital prioritizes "mom and baby bonding" as part of this approach.

The process of stimulating the baby to cry—a sign of healthy lung function—has evolved. While older methods involved somewhat粗暴 approaches, Jessica explains they now simply "flick its little foot" to provoke a response. The humor here masks genuine information: getting that "good first cry" indicates the baby is breathing properly and clearing fluid from airways.

Regarding fathers specifically, Jessica doesn't soften her assessment of their reliability during delivery. "The dads black out a lot. They literally pass out a lot," she states, adding that medical staff's typical response is essentially dismissive: "We don't have time for you right now. Oh, you little bitches." She mentions keeping smelling salts available specifically for this purpose. The cutting of the umbilical cord—often portrayed as a bonding moment for fathers—receives particular scrutiny. Jessica notes she always warns fathers that "it's thicker than you think," comparing it to cutting through chicken tissue and encouraging them to commit fully rather than hesitating.

Anatomical Education and the Clitoris Question

Perhaps the most出人意料 portion of the conversation involves Jessica's attempt to educate Conan about basic female anatomy. She approaches this diplomatically, explaining that "knowing anatomy is really important, erogenous zones, things that could"—pausing diplomatically.

When she mentions that women have more than two openings, Conan's confusion prompts clarification. Jessica enumerates: "You urinate through one hole... baby comes out of the vagina... that's three." The conversation reveals Conan's discomfort with medical terminology—he refers to anatomical structures as "the spud hole" and uses childish terms like "peepees" and "poo-poos." Sona, the podcast's co-host, gently suggests using "doctor words."

The clitoris discussion escalates into one of the episode's recurring jokes. When Jessica mentions the organ, Conan declares himself "a clitoris denier," comparing it to Sasquatch: "You hear a lot of talk, but I've seen no evidence." Jessica's concern about spreading "misinformation online" parallels real-world concerns about health literacy, though framed comedically. Conan insists he has "a blurry photo of a clitoris" from 1938, supposedly taken by a Scottish hiker, prompting Jessica to lament that they now have "clitoris deniers" alongside flat earthers.

This extended bit, while clearly humorous, touches on genuine concerns about anatomical education. Jessica's frustration with Conan's reluctance to acknowledge basic anatomy reflects real frustrations many medical professionals experience when patients—or in this case, celebrity hosts—lack fundamental bodily knowledge.

Circumcision: A Clinical Perspective

Switching to male anatomy, Jessica explains the circumcision procedure she performs, primarily for Jewish families observing the brit milah (bris) tradition. She describes her preferred tool: "I like to use something called a Morgans. So it looks like a cigar cutter, and you just pull up the foreskin, and you go across, and then you clamp it down, and then you lop it off with a scalpel."

Contrary to popular assumptions about infant pain, Jessica details a surprisingly gentle approach: "We actually do dorsal nerve blocks of injecting lidocaine. They get a sugar pacifier. We play music. It's like a spa experience." When Conan asks if the child cries afterward, Jessica admits "they can't tell me" but emphasizes the effort to avoid trauma. She firmly states that any circumcision provider who claims circumcisions are their "favorite thing to do" should be considered a "red flag."

The conversation includes a recurring joke Jessica uses at brises: after the ceremony, she tells the family the calamari was excellent. When they respond that they didn't serve calamari, she explains—"Oh, I thought the foreskin was calamari." The punchline works because the removed tissue resembles the appetizer. Jessica acknowledges stealing this joke but it's clearly her contribution to the bit.

The Fabio Incident: Serendipity and Entertainment

Beyond medical content, Jessica shares the story that provides the episode's emotional anchor. As a teenager, she and her mother attended Conan's taping in 1999 when Fabio—an intern at the time, before becoming the butter substitute pitchman—approached them. The Italian model "grabbed my mom, dipped her backwards, kissed her on the mouth in front of my dad, and then walked away."

Jessica connects this event to Fabio's famous accident exactly one month later, when a goose struck him on a roller coaster, drawing blood across his famously pristine face. She half-jokingly attributes this to her Brooklyn father's supernatural retribution: "I think my dad has some sort of superpowers where he put some sort of hex like on Fabio."

Conan marvels at the narrative quality of this anecdote, declaring "What an incredible piece of writing that is" when Jessica recounts the sequence of events. He notes that at the Harvard Lampoon, they couldn't have composed such a perfect series of "images cascading into each other."

Detailed Takeaways

Birthing professionals need deliberate strategies to prevent burnout. Jessica's insistence that she'll quit the moment birth feels routine demonstrates a professional self-awareness that could benefit anyone in emotionally demanding fields. Recognizing when passion has eroded—and having predetermined criteria for that recognition—provides a safeguard against merely going through motions.

Fathers require expectations management around delivery. The repeated jokes about fainting fathers reveal genuine patterns. Medical teams should prepare fathers for what they'll witness, frame potential lightheadedness as normal rather than shameful, and ensure fathers understand their supporting role takes priority over any need for medical attention themselves.

Medical humor serves genuine bonding and stress relief. Jessica's willingness to joke about circumcision—comparing foreskin to calamari, calling circumcisions "a spa experience"—demonstrates how humor functions in medical contexts. Making light of sensitive procedures helps both provider and patient navigate anxiety, and laughing together establishes rapport that improves care.

Anatomical education remains surprisingly deficient. While framed as comedy, Conan's confusion about basic anatomy reflects real knowledge gaps that affect sexual health and communication. Jessica's patient attempts to explain the clitoris despite Conan's jokes demonstrate the persistent need for accessible anatomical education.

Delayed cord clamping and skin-to-skin contact represent evidence-based practice evolution. These now-standard practices differ significantly from older protocols that immediately separated mother and baby. Understanding why hospitals implement these changes—improved iron stores, better thermoregulation, bonding benefits—helps parents appreciate rather than question new approaches.

Who This Is For

This episode primarily appeals to fans of Conan O'Brien's comedic style who enjoy celebrity conversations with unexpected depth. Listeners interested in medical procedural reality shows or birth-focused content will appreciate the authentic glimpse into obstetric practice, while those with general curiosity about what happens during and after delivery might find the explanations illuminating.

Medically curious audiences without direct experience of childbirth or circumcision procedures will gain practical knowledge, though the conversational framing means information comes wrapped in humor rather than clinical precision. Parents-to-be considering birth plans might benefit from understanding standard protocols like delayed cord clamping and skin-to-skin contact, seeing how practitioners describe these practices conversationally rather than through hospital paperwork.

Anyone sensitive about anatomical terminology or religious circumcision practices may find certain segments uncomfortable, as the conversation treats medical procedures with irreverent humor. However, those who appreciate medical professionals who maintain levity alongside competence will find Jessica's approach refreshing and relatable.
