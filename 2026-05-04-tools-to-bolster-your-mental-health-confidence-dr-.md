## Key Insights

- **Start from a position of strength**: Dr. Conti emphasizes that "there's far more going right in any of us, in all of us, than there is going wrong" — this isn't merely optimistic framing but a truth-aligned approach that makes examining areas for change more effective than deficit-based models.

## Introduction

Mental health advice is everywhere in the modern landscape, yet most of it directs people to examine what's broken within themselves — their deficiencies, disorders, and dysfunctions. Dr. Paul Conti, a psychiatrist with decades of clinical experience and expertise in trauma recovery, approaches the subject from an entirely different angle. In this pivotal conversation on the Huberman Lab Podcast, Conti and host Andrew Huberman — a professor of neurobiology and ophthalmology at Stanford School of Medicine — explore a revolutionary framework: instead of cataloging what's wrong, what if we began by examining what's already going right?

The timing of this discussion is significant. Contemporary life bombards individuals with curated representations of others' existence through social media, creating unprecedented pressure to perform a version of oneself rather than discover and embody an authentic one. The average person experiences dozens of context shifts daily, from work environments to family obligations to digital social spaces, and rarely pauses to observe how these contexts shape their thoughts, feelings, and behaviors. Conti's clinical observations suggest that this frenzied pace creates a kind of psychological scattering, where people lose touch with a stable sense of self that transcends any single situation.

This conversation serves as a conceptual foundation for Conti's forthcoming book, *What's Going Right? A Powerful New Method for Optimizing Your Mental Health* — a resource that Huberman describes as having read "from front to back" and recommends enthusiastically. The discussion moves from individual self-exploration to the complexities of authentic self-presentation in an overconnected world, offering listeners both a philosophical reframe and practical starting points for genuine personal development.

## Key Concepts & Arguments

### The Malleability of Self-Concept

Huberman opens with a question that lies at the heart of personal transformation: "How malleable is our self view?" This inquiry speaks to a universal human concern — the sense that we've accumulated固定 patterns of thinking and behaving that feel immutable, that we're somehow locked into who we've always been. Conti's response cuts through this fatalism with both compassion and clinical precision.

According to Conti, our self-concepts are "very malleable" and offer "a lot of flexibility," but two conditions must be met. First, we must be willing to actually look at ourselves, which sounds simple but represents a significant hurdle for most people. "Very often we're not looking at ourselves," Conti observes. "We're afraid of what we're gonna find, or we don't know how to understand or how to bring change." Second, we must approach that self-examination with what Conti calls "compassionate curiosity" — neither the harsh self-criticism that reinforces shame nor the avoidant numbness that prevents growth, but a genuine interest in learning about oneself the way one might learn about an fascinating subject.

This reframing addresses a fundamental problem Conti sees in conventional mental health approaches: the tendency to begin self-examination by cataloging deficits. When someone walks into a psychiatrist's office or reads a list of symptoms online, the implicit message is that something is broken and must be fixed. While this medicalized model has value in certain contexts, Conti argues it misses something crucial about human nature. "We should start from a position of strength," he states flatly. This isn't positive thinking or motivational platitude — it's grounded in the reality that any functioning human possesses vastly more competencies, strengths, and functional patterns than dysfunctional ones.

### Two Foundational Pillars of Self

Conti references a previous conversation with Huberman in which he articulated "two foundational pillars" of human psychology that apply universally. While the specific framework was developed through their collaborative dialogue, Conti describes it as emerging from the recognition that "there's a structure of self and a function of self" that all humans share by virtue of possessing human brains and human minds.

The structure of self refers to the underlying architecture that makes selfhood possible — the capacity to maintain a sense of identity over time, to recognize oneself as the same person who experienced yesterday and anticipates tomorrow. The function of self refers to how that structure operates in daily life — how we think about ourselves, talk to ourselves, present ourselves to others, and navigate the gap between our internal experience and external behavior.

Understanding these pillars creates a map for self-examination. Rather than flailing around in confusion about why we feel stuck or unhappy, we can locate our inquiry within this framework: Are we examining our self-talk? Our life narrative? Our behavior across different contexts? Our relationship between our observed behaviors and our underlying values? This structure transforms vague intentions like "know thyself" into concrete investigative pathways.

### The Art of Self-Talk Examination

One of the most accessible entry points Conti offers is examining one's self-talk — the constant stream of commentary we generate about ourselves, particularly during quiet moments when external distractions fade. "What are you saying to yourself in quiet moments when no one else is listening, or when there's a pause in the action in your life?" Conti asks. "What messages are you giving yourself?"

The critical insight here is that most people are entirely unaware of this internal dialogue. We replay critical messages so frequently that they become invisible background noise, their content normalized to the point of unconscious acceptance. Someone might carry a persistent inner voice declaring them unworthy, incompetent, or fundamentally flawed without ever consciously registering that this voice exists or examining whether its claims bear any relationship to reality.

Bringing awareness to self-talk creates the possibility of change. Once we notice "I'm telling myself I can't do anything right," we can ask whether this message is accurate, where it came from, whether it served us in the past and continues to serve us now, and what alternative self-talk might be more grounded and constructive. This process requires the compassionate curiosity Conti emphasizes — meeting our own harsh inner critic not with defensive denial but with genuine interest in understanding where it came from and what it needs.

### Life Narrative as Reflexive Story

Beyond moment-to-moment self-talk lies the larger narrative we tell ourselves about who we are across time. Conti invites listeners to examine this narrative directly: "If you just tell yourself about yourself, or if you're telling someone else about you, what is it that you say? What is it that you say in a reflexive way? And does it match what's real and true about your life?"

This question addresses a universal human tendency toward narrative construction. We don't simply experience life; we story it, weaving our experiences into a coherent tale that explains who we are, why we do what we do, and what the future holds. These narratives often contain significant distortions — childhood stories that no longer fit adult realities, self-concepts formed during periods of trauma that persist long after circumstances have changed, comparisons to others' trajectories that ignore our own unique path.

The power of examining one's life narrative lies in the recognition that these stories are constructions, not facts. They felt true when we formed them, and they may have served protective functions in the past, but they aren't immutable reality. A person who formed the narrative "I'm not good with people" during awkward adolescence might discover, upon examining that narrative with curiosity, that it no longer matches the evidence of their adult life. This realization doesn't erase the old story but creates space for a new one that better accommodates who they've become.

### State Dependence and the Observing Ego

One of the most clinically rich concepts Conti introduces is the distinction between state-dependent behavior and the observing ego. Huberman poses the question directly: "Do you think there's tremendous variation in how state dependent people are?" He describes individuals who feel and think completely differently when relating to others versus when alone — "almost like it's two different lives."

Conti's response acknowledges this phenomenon while reframing it within a broader psychological structure. When life moves quickly and contains numerous stressors, "we become very state dependent as opposed to being able to observe ourselves." The rushing from situation to situation, the constant responsiveness to external demands, prevents the kind of reflective distance that allows for self-knowledge.

The alternative to being swept along by state dependence is cultivating what some psychologists call an "observing ego" — a part of the self capable of witnessing one's own thoughts, feelings, and behaviors from a slight distance. "To be able to observe ourselves is how we knit together oneself across situations," Conti explains. This observing function allows someone to notice, "I'm here, and this is what I'm doing, and this is the people I'm with, and how I'm feeling, and how I'm behaving." Importantly, this observation doesn't negate state dependence; it exists alongside it, providing continuity and coherence even when moment-to-moment experience varies dramatically across contexts.

This concept has profound implications for self-understanding. Rather than feeling fragmented by the obvious differences between how we show up at work versus home, or with different friends versus alone, the observing ego allows us to recognize these variations as normal human experience while maintaining a sense of underlying unity. "We can be aware I'm different in one situation than another," Conti notes. "But there's a whole self that's riding above all of it."

### Curiosity as the Crucial Ingredient

When Huberman asks about practical logistics — when and how should someone engage in this self-examination work — Conti's answer strips away unnecessary complexity. "I think all we need to bring is curiosity," he says. "That's all."

This answer might seem too simple, but Conti immediately elaborates on its depth. Curiosity "doesn't have to be overly serious or worried. It doesn't have to have a gravity to it." The same quality of interest that drives us to learn about subjects that fascinate us, to explore new places, to understand how things work, can be directed inward. And crucially, "it's great for our brains and it's great for our health to be curious and to want to learn."

The problem is that we consistently leave ourselves out of this curious exploration. "So often what we leave out of that equation is being curious about ourselves," Conti observes. "And that can be a sort of high-spirited thing to do: what is there in me that runs through all the things that I do? How is it that I feel so different doing one thing than another? What are the common threads of me that run throughout my life?"

This framing transforms self-examination from a dreaded chore into an engaging inquiry. We don't need special tools or techniques; we need only the willingness to apply our natural human curiosity to our own existence. The questions themselves become the methodology — questions about what we value, what energizes us versus what depletes us, what we avoid and what we're drawn toward, what feelings keep recurring across situations, what we want from our lives and what we fear about them.

### The True Self Versus False Self in Modern Life

The conversation takes an especially contemporary turn when Huberman raises the phenomenon of social media presentation and the broader cultural shift away from contextual etiquette. "There seems to be at least a kind of partial erosion of etiquette," Huberman suggests. People now share information about every facet of their lives — their children, their meals, their wins and losses — in a way that would have been unthinkable in previous generations. "This is very unusual," Huberman continues. "Processing time alone has become a very, very different thing altogether."

Conti responds by distinguishing between two uses of social media and public self-presentation. The first involves the "falseness of self" — presenting externally in ways that differ from internal reality, often to hide something. "Wanting everyone to see what's best in my life, to think that I'm doing really well... maybe I'm doing that to hide something," Conti describes. When we notice this impulse in ourselves, curiosity becomes essential: "What am I trying to protect against? Why is it that I want people to see me in a certain way that might be different from how my life actually is?"

The second use involves authentic connection across distance — finding kindred spirits who share our interests or sensibilities even when they're not present in our immediate physical environment. This can represent genuine self-expression rather than performance. "Someone who has an interest or a passion that it's hard to find people right around them, but they can find that more distantly," Conti offers as an example.

The distinction matters because it determines whether our use of technology strengthens or weakens our sense of self. "We can use it to be closer to ourselves and to have a stronger sense of self," Conti explains. "Or we can use it to distract from who we really are, to maybe find solace somewhere else, or find accolades somewhere outside of us because we're protecting against something."

### The Sweet Spot of Connectedness

Huberman presses further into the psychological implications of modern connectivity, asking whether genuine happiness with oneself requires the ability to explore different ways of being without the impulse to share or compare. "The moment we see something else, there's more sensory input, or the moment that we think what we're doing needs to be shared, it changes the experience," Huberman observes. "It's not truly an alone experience."

Conti agrees and articulates what he calls "a sweet spot of connectedness." The research on human well-being clearly demonstrates that isolation and loneliness correlate with poor outcomes, suggesting that some degree of connection is essential for mental health. However, the modern world has tipped toward the opposite extreme. "Where the modern world has gone is it offers us too much the opposite — not enough aloneness."

The problem with insufficient solitude extends beyond mere preference. When we're constantly connected and receiving external input, "in order to decide what it is we even like or prefer, how we feel about things, we're looking for external cues." This creates a subtle but profound dependency on others' responses to determine our own inner states. We don't know how we actually feel about something until we see how others reacted; we don't know what we want until we see what others have; we don't know who we are until we see how we compare.

The sweet spot involves maintaining what Conti describes as "external check-ins" — gathering information about how the world feels, how people we trust and respect feel, how people similar and different from us feel — while preserving "enough aloneness that I am still thinking about myself and the questions of my own life, thinking about on my own before I'm pinging outside." This balance allows us to benefit from the wisdom of others while maintaining access to our own inner compass.

## Detailed Takeaways

**1. Reframe Self-Examination from Deficit to Asset-Based Thinking**

The conventional approach to mental health improvement begins by asking "What's wrong with me?" and works backward toward solutions. Dr. Conti's framework inverts this, beginning with "What's going right?" This isn't positive thinking or denial of real problems — it's a recognition that any functioning human possesses vastly more strengths than weaknesses, and leveraging those strengths creates more sustainable change than obsessing over deficiencies.

The practical application involves explicitly identifying what's working in your life before examining what isn't. Before cataloging your failures or shortcomings, write down three to five things you do well, that energize you, or that represent genuine competence. This isn't to ignore areas for growth but to establish a psychological foundation of capability. When you later examine problematic patterns, you'll do so from the position of someone who already possesses the resources for change, rather than someone fundamentally broken.

**2. Cultivate the Habit of Self-Observation Across Contexts**

Modern life encourages unreflective reaction. We move from demand to demand, stimulus to stimulus, rarely pausing to notice how we're being shaped by each context. Conti's concept of the observing ego offers an alternative: the capacity to witness yourself in action, across situations, and recognize patterns that would otherwise remain invisible.

This cultivation doesn't require formal meditation or extended retreat. It begins with simple pauses throughout the day to ask: "How am I feeling right now? What's my internal state? What am I noticing about my thoughts and emotions?" You might do this during a commute, before a meeting, or while waiting in line. The key is regularity — making observation a consistent practice rather than an occasional discipline. Over time, this builds the capacity to notice yourself in the moment, creating options for choice rather than automatic reaction.

**3. Interrogate Your Internal Narrative with Compassionate Curiosity**

Every person carries a story about who they are — their strengths, weaknesses, potential, limitations, and destiny. These narratives often form during formative years and persist unexamined into adulthood, shaping behavior even when they're no longer accurate or helpful. The practice of examining your life narrative involves asking whether the story you tell yourself matches the actual evidence of your life.

When you notice persistent patterns of self-criticism, limitation, or negative prediction, approach them with curiosity rather than either accepting them as truth or feeling ashamed for having them. Ask: "When did I form this belief about myself? What evidence supported it at the time? Does that evidence still exist? What would I tell a friend who held this same belief about themselves?" This compassionate inquiry often reveals narratives that were protective once but are now simply constraining.
