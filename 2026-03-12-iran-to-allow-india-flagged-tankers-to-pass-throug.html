Iran Grants Passage to Indian Tankers Through Strait of Hormuz Amid Ongoing Regional Tensions

Introduction

The Strait of Hormuz, one of the world's most critical maritime chokepoints through which roughly a fifth of global oil shipments pass, has become a dangerous war zone since hostilities involving Iran began on February 28th. The narrow waterway connecting the Persian Gulf to the Gulf of Oman has seen burning oil tankers and mounting fears among maritime crews worldwide, with the images of blazing vessels striking terror into those who must navigate these waters. However, amid the chaos, ships from certain countries have continued to secure passage, revealing the complex diplomatic maneuvers unfolding behind the scenes of this escalating conflict.

Iran's Deputy Foreign Minister Majak Ravanchi has confirmed that some countries have engaged in direct negotiations with Tehran and have been granted passage for their vessels through the contested strait. This development comes as India flagged its first vessel arriving from the Middle East since the war began—an oil tanker named Shenlong carrying Saudi crude that docked in Mumbai on Thursday. While Iran has denied that a formal "deal" exists with India, New Delhi has confirmed that high-level diplomatic conversations have taken place between the two nations' foreign ministers, specifically addressing the safety of shipping. The situation represents a delicate balancing act for major oil consumers like India and China, who together account for approximately 50% of all petroleum shipments from the Gulf and have enormous stakes in maintaining flows through this strategic corridor.

The Passage Problem: Ships Getting Through

The Strait of Hormuz has transformed into a precarious passage for world shipping since the conflict commenced, with Iran's new Supreme Leader vowing to continue throttling traffic through this vital waterway. Yet despite the rhetoric and the visible attacks on vessels, ships from certain nations have continued to navigate the strait successfully. Iran's deputy foreign minister confirmed that countries engaging in direct dialogue with Tehran have received guarantees for their vessels, though the specific terms and scope of these arrangements remain unclear.

The situation has spawned creative—and some might say desperate—tactics among ship operators seeking safe passage. In the past week alone, at least 30 vessels have abruptly claimed to have Chinese ownership or to be carrying Chinese crews, attempting to associate themselves with Beijing, Iran's most important economic partner. A Panama-flagged ship managed to pass through the strait just two days after mysteriously changing its destination to China and its owner information on its transponder. China has not publicly acknowledged or reacted to these maneuvers, though it has called for an end to the hostilities and stated that maintaining security and stability in the region "serves the common interest of the international community."

India's Delicate Position

The arrival of the Shenlong tanker in Mumbai marked a significant milestone—it was the first vessel to reach India from the Middle East since the war began. According to reports from Reuters, India had reached some form of understanding with Iran that would allow its ships to pass through the strait. While Tehran officially denied that any formal deal existed, New Delhi confirmed that substantive talks had occurred. India's External Affairs Minister and Iran's Foreign Minister have held three conversations in recent days, with the most recent discussion focusing specifically on issues pertaining to shipping safety.

However, India has not escaped the consequences of this conflict unscathed. The country's shipping ministry reported that three Indian sailors have been killed and four others injured in maritime incidents involving foreign-flagged vessels since the Iran war began. Despite these losses, India has provided safe harbor to nearly 200 Iranian sailors, allowing their ships to dock on India's southwestern coast shortly after the conflict broke out—a humanitarian gesture that underscores the complex relationship between the two nations.

Expert Analysis: The Geopolitical Chess Game

Professor Jack Maghrani from the Paris School of International Affairs provided critical analysis of the situation, emphasizing the considerable uncertainty surrounding what exactly is happening with shipping through the strait. He noted that observers need to determine whether only a few trapped tankers have been allowed to pass or whether this represents genuine freedom of passage for all Indian vessels. Key questions remain unanswered: Does this apply to Indian-flagged tankers specifically? Does it cover vessels carrying oil heading to India? The answers to these questions will significantly shape understanding of the arrangement's scope and implications.

Maghrani emphasized that if Indian and Chinese ships are indeed getting through while others are not, this sends a clear diplomatic message about which side of the conflict these nations align with—or more precisely, which side they do not align with. Both China and India are major customers of Gulf oil and have enormous economic interests at stake. They could reasonably argue that their economies are being undermined by the ongoing disruption and that they should have the right to access the oil they need, having had no role in starting the war. China has long supported the Iranian regime, never showing interest in regime change and defending Tehran against international pressure and sanctions. India, meanwhile, has always been a significant customer of Iranian oil and never accepted the sanctions against Tehran.

The professor also addressed speculation about whether Iran might plant mines in the strait, calling such a move "rather foolish." He noted that the navigation corridors lie entirely in Omani waters, meaning Iran would need to mine Oman's territory as well—a significant diplomatic consideration given that Oman has served as a mediator between Iran and other parties. While Iran did recently bomb a port in Oman, causing considerable consternation, Tehran's president subsequently called the Omani sultan to apologize and announced an inquiry into the incident. Mining the waters would be difficult to characterize as accidental, making it an escalation Iran would likely avoid, particularly since Iran itself needs passage through Hormuz for its own trade.

India's Role: Moral Complexity or Pragmatic Realism?

The broadcast addressed a provocative question: whether India's actions in facilitating oil flows constitute moral corruption, particularly given New Delhi's well-documented role in purchasing and reselling Russian oil that has supported Moscow's war effort in Ukraine. Maghrani drew a crucial distinction between the two situations. Before Russia's invasion of Ukraine, India imported very little Russian oil but then significantly increased purchases while reducing imports from the Gulf to profit from discounted Russian crude, subsequently exporting refined products worldwide, including to Europe.

With Iran, however, the situation is fundamentally different. Iran has always been a supplier to India, and New Delhi never accepted the sanctions against Tehran. "It is something that has not changed significantly over the years," Maghrani observed. "India cannot be really accused of profiting from the situation at the moment. Perhaps it has profited from the situation throughout by not adhering to the sanctions. But that's a problem of the countries that have created the sanctions." This framing positions India's approach as consistent long-term policy rather than wartime opportunism.

Key Takeaways

The Strait of Hormuz remains functionally open to strategic partners, undermining Iran's ability to pressure global oil markets. If China and India can indeed receive oil freely through the strait, it will be extremely difficult for Iran to sustain a prolonged crisis or drive oil prices to the $200 per barrel level some analysts have predicted. The very fact that Tehran is granting passage to these major customers suggests Iran recognizes the limits of its leverage and the potential costs of alienating its remaining economic partners.

Shipowners are employing creative workarounds to avoid attacks, including false claims of Chinese ownership. The dramatic spike in vessels suddenly claiming Chinese ownership or crew represents a telling indicator of how market participants perceive Beijing's influence over Tehran. This shadow fleet phenomenon highlights the intersection of economics and geopolitics, as operators seek to exploit perceived diplomatic protections.

The India-Iran arrangement is not a formal "deal" but rather a diplomatic understanding reached through high-level engagement. The careful semantic distinction between a "deal" and ongoing diplomatic discussions reflects the politically sensitive nature of any arrangement that could be perceived as supporting one side in the conflict. The three conversations between the two countries' foreign ministers in just a few days demonstrate the priority both nations place on resolving this issue.

Oman's waters complicate any Iranian mining scenario, making large-scale mining operations unlikely. The geographical reality that the navigation corridors lie entirely in Omani waters creates diplomatic obstacles tomining the strait, as Iran would be attacking a neutral country's territory. While Iran has already struck Omani territory once, systematically mining the waters would represent a different order of escalation that would be difficult to explain away as accident or rogue action.

India's history with Iran differs fundamentally from its recent Russian oil transactions, complicating moral condemnation. India's long-standing trade relationship with Tehran predates the current conflict and represents consistent policy rather than wartime profiteering. This distinction matters for understanding the broader framework of how nations navigate competing pressures from sanctions regimes and economic necessities.
