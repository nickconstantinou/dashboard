    Introduction

    In the fall of 2025, the United States experienced what can only be described as a political violence cascade. A state legislator and her husband were murdered in Minnesota in what authorities called a politically motivated attack. An arson was set at the Pennsylvania Governor's residence while the governor and his family slept inside. Charlie Kirk was shot. And the country witnessed what appeared to be the third assassination attempt against former President Donald Trump. Behind each of these acts lay a suspected perpetrator who, according to reports, had authored manifestos outlining specific political targets.

    The question that kept surfacing was both simple and profoundly troubling: How did we get here, and how much worse could it really get?

    To answer that question, *The Daily* turned to University of Chicago Professor Robert Pape, one of the country's leading experts on political violence who has advised every White House from 2001 to 2024. Through his work directing the University of Chicago Project on Security and Threats (CPOST), Pape has conducted twenty nationally representative surveys of Americans over the past five years, tracking attitudes toward political violence with unprecedented precision. His research reveals findings that should alarm anyone who cares about American democracy: tens of millions of Americans on both sides of the political aisle now find political violence acceptable, and this acceptance is actively fueling the very acts of violence we are witnessing.

    What makes Pape's analysis particularly striking is his argument that the current era of political violence represents a greater threat to American democracy than any terrorist group or foreign country—a threat rooted not in fringe extremism but in mainstream social acceptance. "Once you have tens of millions of Americans, not a fringe, not a few militia groups, who see violence as acceptable, this changes everything about the risks of attack," Pape explained. This social acceptance doesn't just exist in isolation; it actively empowers volatile individuals by offering them the prospect of social approval for their acts, while simultaneously making it harder to identify and stop potential attackers before they strike.

    ---

    Understanding Violent Populism

    Pape has developed a framework he calls "violent populism" to describe the current moment in American political life. This is not civil war—America does not have organized armies confronting each other. Nor is it merely the nasty but ultimately manageable polarization that has characterized American politics for decades. Violent populism, in Pape's definition, occurs when tens of millions of citizens come to see political violence as an acceptable tool for achieving their political goals.

    The distinction matters enormously. A handful of extremists plotting in basements can be monitored, investigated, and stopped. But when tens of millions of people express tolerance for political violence—even if they never intend to commit it themselves—they create an ecosystem that emboldens those who are already predisposed toward impulsive, angry action. "It makes the individuals who are volatile for their own psychosocial reasons—it pushes those volatile individuals over the edge, because what they see is the prospect of social approval for their acts," Pape explained. "And when you have tens of millions of Americans who support political violence, it makes tips to the FBI very, very difficult to get, because the more support there is for political violence, the more people will simply look the other way. They'll discount what they see."

    This dynamic explains why the recent spate of violence has been bipartisan rather than unilateral. We have seen attacks targeting Trump administration figures and conservative commentators alongside attacks targeting Democratic leaders. What we are witnessing, according to Pape, is "clearly the rise of political violence on both sides" and, critically, "it's now starting to spiral." The spiral metaphor is deliberate: violence begets acceptance of violence, which begets more violence.

    To understand the scale of this phenomenon, Pape points to survey data that tracks public attitudes over time. During most of the Biden administration—roughly 2021 through early 2025—about 10% of the body politic supported political violence to restore Donald Trump to the presidency. When researchers dug deeper into what respondents meant by "use of force," they found that 55% of those who endorsed force believed it meant assassination. That is, roughly half of that 10%—approximately 20 million American adults—were essentially endorsing political assassination as a legitimate tool.

    What happened after the 2024 election was striking: that figure nearly doubled, rising to between 14% and 21%, where it has remained. Similarly, when researchers asked Americans in September 2025 whether they found it acceptable that Charlie Kirk was assassinated, 10% said yes—representing approximately 20 million American adults. The same percentage found it acceptable that Nancy Pelosi had been targeted for assassination. "Overall, what you're saying is roughly one in five people in America support some kind of political violence now," host Natalie Kitroeff observed, "which is a lot, and it's increasing on both sides of the spectrum."

    Historical Context: When America Has Been Here Before

    America is not experiencing political violence for the first time, but the current moment has distinctive features that make it particularly dangerous. Previous eras of violent populism offer instructive comparisons.

    In the 1920s, following World War I, the Ku Klux Klan underwent a remarkable revival. What had been a moribund organization with only a few hundred thousand members suddenly grew to between 4 and 6 million members who paid dues and participated in organized activities. "This was not just simply in the South," Pape emphasized. "This was in many places in the North. And what you saw was, again, millions of Americans supporting political violence." The records of the KKK from this era—membership rolls, financial records, published manifestos—allow historians to document exactly how widespread support for political violence had become.

    The 1960s offer another parallel, though with important differences. That decade saw a string of political assassinations, from John F. Kennedy to Martin Luther King Jr. to Robert Kennedy. It also saw collective political violence in the form of riots at events like the 1968 Democratic National Convention in Chicago. Opinion polling from that era reveals "a really striking correspondence of support by tens of millions of Americans for that political violence itself," according to Pape.

    What made the 1960s distinctive was that political violence was predominantly a left-wing phenomenon. What makes our current era unique is that "it's pretty much the first time it's happened on both sides at the same time." The 1920s saw right-wing violent populism; the 1960s saw left-wing violent populism. Today, we are seeing both wings of the political spectrum embracing violence simultaneously—a configuration that Pape identifies as unprecedented and particularly dangerous.

    Measuring the Rise in Political Violence

    Beyond attitudinal surveys, researchers have documented the actual increase in political violence through detailed analysis of prosecuted cases. The CPOST team at the University of Chicago conducted a study of threats to members of Congress over a twenty-five-year period from 2001 to 2024, examining threats that were actually prosecuted by the Department of Justice. The findings were stark: beginning in the first year of Trump's presidency in 2017, there was a fivefold increase in threats to members of Congress.

    Crucially, this increase affected both Democratic and Republican targets. When researchers examined the motives behind individual threats, "Trump's rise was a lightning rod on both sides." Some members of Congress were targeted "because they support Trump's agenda too much," as in the case of Marjorie Taylor Greene. Others were targeted "because they're not supporting President Trump's agenda enough." The result was "a dramatic spike" that remained elevated "year after year after year" for approximately eight years, persisting through both the first Trump presidency and the Biden administration.

    This persistence is particularly noteworthy. One might expect that as Trump left office and Biden took over, the intensity of political conflict would diminish. It did not. "So you might think it would just diminish because we've had a change of president," Kitroeff noted. "No." The sustained elevation of political violence across administrations suggests that something deeper than individual political figures is driving the phenomenon—that we are dealing with structural social changes rather than temporary political情绪.

    The Two Social Changes Driving Political Violence

    Pape's central argument is that political violence in America today is ultimately rooted in two major social transformations, neither of which shows any sign of reversing. "What we are seeing at bottom is not simply the impact of an individual, a president, although President Trump is contributing; not simply the impact of social media, although social media is contributing," he explained. "We are seeing the impact of social change. And social change, major social change, when it happens in a country, is a cause of the radicalization of politics, which can cause political violence."

    The first and most dominant social change is demographic: America is transitioning for the first time in its 250-year history from a white majority democracy to a white minority democracy. In 1960, approximately 88% of Americans were non-Hispanic whites. Today, that figure stands at 57%. "In about twenty years, it'll be 49%," Pape noted. This transition—occurring across what he calls "the tipping point generation," roughly a twenty-year period—has made immigration "probably the number one lightning rod issue in America today."

    On the political right, this demographic change creates the conditions for what might be called established-group anxiety. "When you have this major social change, you are going to see essentially the established group wanting to stop or reverse that change," according to Pape. "Because as that change happens, they will lose political power. That will mean declining economic prospects. That will have lots of impact going forward for generations." The sense that one's descendants will inherit a diminished country—politically, economically, culturally—creates existential stakes that routine policy disagreements cannot match.

    On the political left, the same demographic change is viewed quite differently. "Lots of the new group very much wants to keep this change going," Pape observed. "They're, in fact, gonna benefit from this change, and they want, if anything, not to stop it, but to accelerate it." For those who see an increasingly diverse America as a more just and equitable nation, the prospect of reversing demographic change represents a rollback of hard-won progress.

    The second major social change is economic: the shift of wealth to the top 1%, a process that began in the mid-1980s and has continued regardless of which party holds power. "Wealth is being shifted from the bottom 90% to the top 1%," according to Pape. "So if you're in the ninety-first to ninety-ninth percentile, you don't see much of a change. But if you're in the top 1%, you are gaining enormous wealth from the country, and it's coming out of the entire bottom 90% pretty much evenly at the different quintiles."

    This economic transformation compounds the political dangers of demographic change. "Both the Republican side and the Democratic side, because they're the bottom 90%, they really see their economic prospects could be clobbered even more." And critically, "nobody's solving the shift of wealth to the top 1%. Neither party has taken that on, and that is an additional fuel to the bipartisan nature of the rise of political violence."

    Who Is Supporting Political Violence? A Surprising Profile

    One of the most counterintuitive findings from Pape's research concerns the social profile of Americans who support political violence. Conventional wisdom might suggest that those who embrace political violence are economic losers, marginalized individuals with little to lose who resort to extreme measures out of desperation. The survey data suggests otherwise.

    "This is not the case in our surveys," Pape emphasized. "What you're seeing is large numbers of educated people, large numbers of middle class people, large numbers of upper middle class on both sides are supporting political violence because they're the haves, and they're worried they're gonna lose even more in the future."

    The key fear driving this support is what Pape terms "the fear of political exclusion"—the concern that as one's group becomes a minority, they will be unable to get their grievances addressed or their interests protected through normal political channels. "Once you're in the transitional ten-to-twenty-year period, both sides can fear political lockout, and then they're gonna fight even harder, because this means this may be their last chance before it's irreversible."

    This fear explains why the educated and affluent are more, not less, likely to support political violence. Those with resources and social standing have the most to lose from political exclusion. They have built lives structured around certain expectations of influence and opportunity. The prospect of their children inheriting a diminished country—politically subordinate, economically squeezed, culturally displaced—represents not merely a policy defeat but an existential threat to everything they have worked to build.

    This is why Pape distinguishes the current moment from ordinary polarization. "We're way beyond that," he argued. "What we are seeing is the rise of true existential politics." Americans are not fighting over whether Social Security should be taxed at 6% or 8%. They are fighting over whether the country they and their ancestors have known will continue to exist in any recognizable form.

    How Rhetoric Activates Violence

    Understanding that tens of millions of Americans have come to view political violence as acceptable does not fully explain how some individuals move from attitude to action. Pape identifies political leadership rhetoric as a critical activating factor that pushes already predisposed individuals toward violence.

    "When political leaders talk about literally punching people out... if those authority figures start to find violence acceptable, and again, they may not even fully understand that they're doing that, others are gonna hear that, and they're going to move in that direction as well."

    Pape provided specific examples. Donald Trump has famously declared that he would like to "punch him in the face." Beto O'Rourke, addressing Democrats in 2019, stated: "Instead of awaiting the other side to punch us in the face, we throw that punch first, and we throw it harder." Gavin Newsom, according to Pape, has similarly stated, "I'm putting a mirror up to President Trump, and I'm fighting fire with fire, and I'm punching a bully back in the mouth."

    While these examples are not identical—Trump's rhetoric is arguably more direct—"they are coming quite close to President Trump's images of actually hitting people," as Pape put it. Leaders across the political spectrum are modeling acceptable violence, and this modeling matters because leaders are authority figures whose signals are amplified and emulated.

    The mechanism works like this: some individuals are predisposed to anger and impulsive action. When these individuals hear respected leaders endorse or celebrate political violence—even rhetorically—it provides social permission and validation for acts they might otherwise restrain. "Don't think of these as iron laws," Pape cautioned. "Think of these as risk factors and the tendencies in the data." But the data do show that "there are individuals, and you can actually see them in the survey research, who are predisposed to be angry and predisposed to act out impulsively—and then it is disproportionately that portion of those folks who are already angry, already impulsive, who are far more likely to be activated."
