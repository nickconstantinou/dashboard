• 31% of liberal teen girls report using social media for more than five hours daily—a rate substantially higher than other demographic groups, suggesting a specific vulnerability linked to liberal upbringing rather than technology alone.

The Crisis of Young Women: Freya India on Gender, Technology, and the Collapse of Meaning

Introduction

Freya India is twenty-four years old. She has been writing about women and girls since 2021, and it has, by her own account, made her a target. Her book—*Angry Young Women*—has accumulated one-star reviews on Goodreads not from ideological opponents on the right, but from liberal women who opened the cover expecting a conventional progressive text and found themselves confronted with arguments they weren't prepared to entertain. The reviews, she explains, feature women warning each other that the book "is not what you expect" and that readers "might be hit with a viewpoint you disagree with." Many reviewers, she says, made it through the first chapter before encountering her arguments about trans issues or cultural trends and then abandoned the book entirely, returning to warn others.

This hostility toward her work represents precisely the dynamic India explores in her writing: the way certain truths have become so ideologically untouchable that observing them aloud invites accusations of bad faith, of grifting, of using a legitimate crisis as cover for a "right wing agenda." She finds herself, as a white, conservative, heterosexual woman, in an unusual position of intersectional illegitimacy—possessing what she calls "female privilege" that is "totally dismissed" because it is nullified by her other identities. When she recently spoke with Douglas Murray about this phenomenon, he offered a sardonic parallel: a white conservative gay man, he told her, is essentially "an honorary straight guy" within the gay community—the whiteness and conservatism overriding the protected status of sexuality.

Yet India is not alone in observing the crisis. A major feature in the *New Statesman* titled "Angry Young Women" reached many of the same conclusions she has spent years developing—that young women are more pessimistic than young men across nearly every metric, that they feel hopeless about relationships and the future, that privileged women feel these effects most acutely. The difference, India notes with undisguised frustration, is that when the *New Statesman* publishes these findings, they are "celebrated and welcomed." When she does, she faces "constant accusations of being misogynist, being far right conservative." The indignation, she suggests, is not about the substance of the claims but about who is making them—and from what political position.

---

The Pessimism Gap: What the Data Shows

The central observation driving India's work is straightforward but startling: young women are now more pessimistic than young men across nearly every measurable dimension. They are less likely to say they feel happy, ambitious, excited, or fulfilled. They are more pessimistic about the prospect of happiness over their lifetime. They express more negative views of the opposite sex than men express of them. This represents a significant departure from historical patterns, where women consistently reported higher levels of wellbeing and life satisfaction than men.

India's framework for understanding this reversal rests on a paradox she identifies as central to modern liberal womanhood: "They have everything they want and basically nothing they need." The foundations and anchors that have historically provided stability—family structures, religious communities, neighborhood connections, intergenerational knowledge—have been systematically eroded in liberal societies. And it is precisely the most privileged women, those with the most access to education, career opportunities, and material comfort, who feel this absence most acutely. They have been promised self-actualization and autonomy and have received it in abundance. What they have not received are the relational and communal structures that make those freedoms feel meaningful rather than isolating.

The *New Statesman* piece confirmed this dynamic, finding that more privileged women reported even greater pessimism than their less advantaged peers. For India, this is not a contradiction but a revelation: material resources do not substitute for existential grounding. The liberal promise that removing traditional constraints would lead to flourishing has proven to be hollow, at least for women, who seem to have been uniquely vulnerable to the specific ways that promise has played out.

---

The Product and the Person

At the core of India's argument is a distinction she considers essential: women are increasingly being encouraged to see themselves as products rather than people. The ultimate goal, as sold by both social media platforms and the broader cultural logic of self-optimization, is not to accumulate human experiences—messy, unpredictable, relationship-defining experiences—but to optimize oneself for market appeal. Your body is your brand. Your life is your content. Your value is your engagement.

This framing, India argues, explains a cascade of seemingly disconnected phenomena. It explains why young women have developed a greater aversion to having children than young men—something that would have been unthinkable a generation ago. If your goal is to be a "perfect, pristine product," why would you take the risks of motherhood? Pregnancy is unpredictable. Childbirth is dangerous. Parenting is long-term, unglamorous, impossible to document in the flattering, curated way that content culture rewards. "It's like a long term satisfaction," India observes, "and we're thinking about women previous generations of women, but I think this generation of women is very different, and their values are very different."

The product framing also explains the specific nature of the pressure young women face around relationships. India pushes back directly against the narrative, popularized by figures like Emma Watson, that young women are experiencing overwhelming pressure to "settle down" and marry. In her experience and in the experience of the women she has interviewed and observed, the pressure runs in the opposite direction: to stay single, to stay unattached, to stay available and optimized. What Watson describes as "a violence and a cruelty" of pressure to settle down, India suggests, is actually the anxiety of feeling rushed to complete all one's self-actualization before committing to another person—to heal oneself, fix one's mental health, become "the best version of yourself" and achieve wholeness and enlightenment before taking on the messiness of partnership or parenthood.

This is not pressure to settle down, India argues. It is pressure to be perfect before you meet someone—and perfection, by design, takes forever. "Your self brand, your product online, that needs to be you," she explains. "You basically need to exit the business of your public profile because, post motherhood, you're not gonna be able to go to brunch with the girls or wear cute heels in the same sort of a way." Motherhood, in this framework, is not the beginning of something. It is the end of the version of yourself that the online community values most.

The Specific Vulnerability of Liberal Girls

India is deliberate about specifying which young women she is discussing. Her book is not a universal claim about all women everywhere; it is a focused examination of women in the Anglophone world, raised in liberal households, who have grown up with specific pressures and opportunities that distinguish them from women in other contexts. "Our problems are not stigma around mental health," she explains. "They are pathologization and medicalization of all of our negative emotions. Our issues are not too much pressure to settle down. They're pressure to stay single and self actualized."

This specificity matters because the data shows that liberal girls are not merely equal to their conservative peers in social media consumption—they are dramatically more consumed. 31% of liberal teen girls report using social media for more than five hours per day, compared to substantially lower rates among conservative girls and boys. India does not view this as coincidental. She traces it to the absence of the protective mechanisms that conservative and religious environments provide: clear structures, community expectations, moral frameworks that organize experience and provide meaning independent of market value.

"Women who grew up in conservative and religious households, young girls are doing way better," she observes. "And they seem to have had some sort of protective mechanism from that." This is what initially drew her to the research—not because she was herself conservative or religious, but because she noticed a pattern in the data: liberal women raised in liberal households seemed more adrift, more addicted to the platforms, more unable to access the kinds of grounding that would make the online world less seductive.

The mechanism, as India explains it, is that social media arrived precisely at the moment when the traditional anchors had been eroded. It then offered "substitutes and simulations" of what women no longer possessed—connection, validation, community, a sense of being seen and known. The platforms exploited specific personality traits and vulnerabilities that were more prevalent among girls raised without those anchors. Girls who had strong communities, clear values, and grounded senses of self were less susceptible. Girls who had been raised in the ethos of self-optimization, who had been taught that their value was something to be performed and marketed, found in social media a natural habitat.

Growing Up Branded: The Childhood of the Optimized Self

One of the most striking elements of India's analysis concerns the age at which this self-branding begins. Girls are joining Instagram at ten or eleven years old—sometimes earlier. Every experience becomes content. Every moment is evaluated for its potential as a post, a story, a validation opportunity. "Everything is done in anticipation of an audience," India explains. "And then you're asking them to do things that are scary and have a quiet satisfaction and where they would have to give up some part of themselves."

India includes herself in this observation. She was on Instagram at eleven, and the cognitive shift required to exit that mode—to simply exist in an experience without feeling the compulsion to document it, to prove to others that she existed through content—proved surprisingly difficult. "For me to get off Instagram and then not have the constant nagging feeling of I need to take a picture, I need to document this, I need to prove to people that I exist," she says, describing the experience of stepping outside the attention economy's demands. "It was so hard to switch my mindset even though I wasn't even that addicted."

The phenomenon has extended downward in age in ways that would have seemed dystopian a generation ago. Parents are registering their children's Instagram handles before they are born, India notes. The process of raising a child has become content material: planning your baby shower for YouTube, packing your hospital bag on video, selecting your child's username before they have been born. Children enter the attention economy before they have language, before they have selves to protect from the dynamics of performance and validation.

The statistics are stark: 25% of five-to-seven-year-olds in the UK have smartphones, and 38% are already on social media platforms despite age restrictions designed to keep them off until thirteen. The minimum age for Instagram is technically thirteen, but India notes that enforcement consists entirely of asking users to enter a birthday—"you just put a birthday in. It's not like Pornhub where you do some sort of verification." This is not a technical problem; it is a cultural one. Parents are not merely allowing their young children into these spaces. They are actively facilitating it, treating the child's digital presence as a natural extension of family content creation.

The Templates of Fear

India's analysis extends beyond the individual psychological effects of social media to the broader cultural environment young women are absorbing before they have any personal experience of relationships. Young women today are learning about partnerships, attraction, and gender dynamics from multiple corrupted sources simultaneously.

First, there is the direct impact of family breakdown. Women who grew up in unstable or single-parent households lack the lived templates of stable relationships that make investment in partnership feel safe and achievable. "Having children is terrifying," India observes. "It's so vulnerable for a woman. And so I think that fear is a huge part of it. And if you think of it's not just maybe you grew up in an unstable household, but you might have also grown up online."

Second, there is the gender discourse that dominates online spaces. Young women are reading "wounded adults talk about men and women and generalize and stereotype off the back of their own hurt and heartbreak"—and they are doing so before they have had any experience of a relationship themselves. They are learning that men are, by default, dangerous, predatory, and incapable of emotional intimacy, absorbing these templates as general truths rather than the specific grievances of specific individuals.

Third, there is pornography, which teaches its consumers—often young boys and men—that women exist as objects for consumption rather than subjects capable of mutual flourishing. This dynamic, India suggests, operates differently on young men and young women, shaping their expectations of relationships in ways that make the vulnerability of genuine partnership feel increasingly alien.

The cumulative effect is a generation of young women whose templates for relationships are uniformly terrifying. They have not seen stable marriages modeled in their own homes. They have learned from online discourse that men are to be suspected and protected against. They have absorbed a view of themselves as products whose value depends on their market performance. And they are being told—by a culture that simultaneously demands self-actualization and penalizes the delay of motherhood—that they need to heal themselves, optimize themselves, and become perfect before they can responsibly commit to another person.

The Parallel Problem: Young Men and the Manosphere

India is careful to note that she is not arguing that young men are faring better or that the manosphere is a non-issue. She acknowledges that young men have their own crisis, their own vulnerabilities, their own radicalization pipelines. But she notes that the discourse around young men has been prominent for years—talk of the "lost generation of young men," of incels, of manosphere radicalization—while the parallel phenomenon among young women has been largely ignored or dismissed. "All we've heard about for the last few years is the manosphere and how men are getting radicalized," she observes. "And ultimately, the argument in their piece was that women are getting radicalized by social media and particularly by femosphere influences."

The *New Statesman* piece, she notes, reached the same conclusions she has been making for years—but from the same ideological stable as the journalists who have spent years writing about manosphere dangers. The recognition that women might have parallel vulnerabilities is apparently acceptable when framed in the right language by writers with the right credentials. When she makes the same arguments, from her own perspective and experience, she is accused of grifting, of spreading fascism, of funneling readers toward the far right.

This is the "indignation" that India finds most galling. She has spent years trying to articulate these observations "carefully and with some compassion." She has been met with "constant smears and backlash." And now, the same conclusions are being celebrated when they appear in a different venue, from a different author, with a different political framing. The argument is not that the observations are wrong. The argument is that the observer is not authorized to make them.

On Policy and Its Limits

The conversation briefly turns to the policy implications of these dynamics. India mentions Australia's under-16 social media ban, which she learned about when having dinner with Tony Abbott and the official who spearheaded the legislation. She is skeptical that the UK will follow suit. "Brits just don't like dealing with displeasure in that way," she observes. "We are a complaining country. We have the equivalent skill [to Americans' litigiousness], but at complaining. And I think that that is a weapon of mass destruction that stops progress from happening sometimes."

She is also critical of the UK's Online Safety Bill, which includes provisions for age verification requiring adults to upload ID to access certain platforms. While some elements of the bill—targeting fraud, child abuse material—are clearly necessary, the broader framework of protecting users from "bullying or hatred" raises concerns about who defines those categories and how speech restrictions will be enforced. "I think the methods we're gonna use in the UK will cause their own problems," she suggests, noting that Keir Starmer has already signaled intentions to "double down on age verification."
