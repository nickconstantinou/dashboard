# Az Discusses His Latest Draft & Rank History: A Transparent Look at FPL Success, Failure, and the Road Ahead for 2025/26

## Introduction: Setting the Stage for a New Season

As the 2025/26 Fantasy Premier League season approaches—just over a week away—content creators across the FPL community are unveiling their draft teams and sharing their strategies for the season ahead. In this video, Az, one of the personalities behind FDL Blackbox and a contributor to the FPL Show and Scout, takes his turn to reveal his current draft while also addressing what has become an increasingly prominent topic of discussion in the FPL community: his recent performance record.

The timing of this video is significant. With the new season imminent, viewers are hungry for insight, strategy, and honest assessment of who to follow and trust. Az's video stands apart from a typical team reveal because he directly confronts the elephant in the room: the substantial criticism he has received over the past three years regarding his FPL performance. Rather than deflecting or avoiding the conversation, Az chooses transparency, walking through his complete rank history from 2007 to the present day, explaining what went wrong, and offering his honest assessment of where he stands as both a player and a content creator.

This context matters enormously for anyone trying to decide whose FPL advice to follow. Az acknowledges that his recent results have been "a complete disaster," as he puts it, but he also argues that his earlier track record demonstrates genuine competence and that the story of his FPL journey is more nuanced than a single metric might suggest. Whether you ultimately trust his picks or not, understanding his reasoning, his methodology, and his self-assessment is valuable for any FPL manager looking to make informed decisions this season.

## The Criticism and the "Expert" Label

Before diving into his team or his history, Az spends time addressing the wave of negativity that has surrounded content creators on platforms like Twitter. He acknowledges that he has received "a fair bit of stick" and concedes that "some of it rightly so" regarding what he describes as "quite a poor performance over the last couple of years."

Central to this discussion is the "expert" label that the FPL Show and Scout use to describe their contributors. Az is refreshingly candid about this designation: "Never something I would ever call myself," he says, noting that it's simply a marketing term used by the Premier League's official FPL show and Scout's promotional materials. He explains that he has no control over how he is marketed—his work for the Premier League and Scout speaks for itself, and he has no intention of turning down opportunities he enjoys simply because of a title he didn't choose.

This distinction matters because it reflects a broader tension in the FPL content creation space. Viewers often place enormous trust in content creators based on titles or past performance, but Az argues for a more measured approach. He loves the work, he enjoys the opportunities, and he continues because he finds it genuinely fun—his full-time job pays his bills, and FPL content remains what he describes as "a passion that I've had since 0708." He has no plans to stop despite the criticism, though he acknowledges that if the negativity became overwhelming, he would consider stepping back.

## A Complete Rank History: From Learning to Excellence and Back Again

Az's transparency reaches its most detailed point when he walks through his complete FPL rank history, season by season. This section offers remarkable insight into the arc of an FPL player's career over nearly two decades.

**The Learning Years (2007-2009):** Az describes his first three seasons as a learning period. He finished with an average rank of 186K across these years, which he attributes to learning the game's intricacies. Even at this stage, however, he believes his performance was respectable—"probably what got me into it," he notes, suggesting this early competence helped launch his eventual content creation career.

**The Breakthrough Years (2010-2012):** This period marked his first major success. He achieved his first top 5K finish at 3,800, followed by a 175K finish, and then a 10,600 finish that he remembers vividly: he finished just one point outside the top 10K and was "gutted" as a result. This near-miss clearly motivated him for future seasons.

**The Consistent Years (2013-2015):** Az describes this as a strong period, with an average rank of 20K and "another 5K finish." Most impressively, he finished 2K in 2014, though he admits he doesn't remember that season particularly well. This was clearly his peak competitive period, and he was establishing himself as a genuinely strong FPL player.

**The Scout Cast Years (2016-2018):** Az believes he joined the Scout Cast towards the end of the 2016/17 season, finishing around 59K that year. The following season, 2017/18, brought his best ever FPL finish: 800th. He followed this up with a 62K finish the next year, bringing that three-year average to 40K. He looks back on this period as his best, and these finishes put him "up in some of the hall of fame tables," though he never broke into the top 10.

**The Blackbox Era Begins (2019-2021):** Az thinks he started doing FDL Blackbox in 2020. The 2019/20 season was his first year with Blackbox, and he describes it as "lousy," finishing 135K. However, the 2020/21 season brought a dramatic turnaround, which he rates as a "really good season." Across these three years, his average rank was 52K. He notes that his three-year average during this period showed his lowest finish was 63K, meaning he consistently placed in the top tier of FPL managers.

## The Recent Struggles: What Went Wrong

Az is unflinching in his assessment of the past three seasons, calling them "a complete disaster" with "no two ways of looking at it."

**2022/23 (143K):** While this finish looks poor on paper, Az offers important context. He was sitting around one million in the world rankings with approximately six weeks left in the season. Rather than accept this poor finish, he fought back aggressively and ultimately pipped his colleague Mark by a single point on the final day. "I'm overall quite happy with how that season went," he says, noting that he managed to turn things around when they could have been "a hell of a lot worse."

**2023/24 (445K):** This is the season Az describes most bluntly: "That is embarrassing. That is really bad." He explains his reasoning: he was sitting around 200-250K and debated whether to accept a mediocre finish or push hard for top 100K. He chose to push, making a series of aggressive decisions—he ignored Sadio Mané at home to Wolves, captained Cole Palmer, chose Romero over Porro—and he describes these as "tilt decisions" made in hope rather than strategy. "I made lots of like tilt decisions and hope some of them would pay off and absolutely none of them paid off," he admits. He acknowledges that he would have finished much better—perhaps 150-200K—had he simply coasted to the end. "That's a real blemish on the record," he says.

**2024/25 (265K):** Az describes simply struggling to find any rhythm throughout the season. He identifies his biggest mistake as ignoring Mohamed Salah for far too long. More broadly, he believes his core issue has been "being too slow to react" to the meta and template picks.

## The Analytical Failure: Why He Thinks Things Went Wrong

Beyond the specific season-by-season failures, Az offers a broader analysis of what has gone wrong in his approach over the past few years. This analysis is perhaps the most valuable part of the video for viewers who want to improve their own game.

Az believes he has been "too slow to react" to top-tier picks, often going "against the template too much." He has consistently ignored tools like the Scout review feature, believing instead that the "proper" way to play FPL is to pick players based on his own prior knowledge and intuition. He acknowledges that this approach once made sense: in earlier seasons, there were far fewer FPL players, less data available, and less tooling to help analyze the game. At that time, watching more football and making decisions based on personal observation was genuinely advantageous because everyone had to work with limited information.

However, Az now believes the models and data tools have become so sophisticated that ignoring them entirely is a mistake. "They are so good at synthesizing that data," he says. He specifically mentions that Mark, his Blackbox co-host, has also struggled over the same period, with Mark only finishing in the top 100K last year after having one strong season. "We've tried to fly the flag maybe too much against things and it hasn't really worked," Az admits.

The lesson Az draws is one of balance: the personal football knowledge he built over years of watching matches still has value, but it must be combined with—and not resist—the analytical tools now available. He wants to be more data-driven going forward while still incorporating his own observations and instincts.

## Detailed Takeaways: The Most Important Lessons

**The Danger of Chasing Rank:** Az's 2023/24 season serves as a cautionary tale about the psychology of FPL. When he found himself at 200-250K, rather than accepting a reasonable finish, he made increasingly aggressive and emotionally-driven decisions, ultimately tanking to 445K. The lesson is clear: fighting against inevitable mediocrity can produce catastrophic results. Sometimes the smart play is accepting a mediocre rank rather than gambling everything on a desperate recovery. FPL managers should set realistic expectations at the start of the season and resist the temptation to dramatically alter strategy when facing disappointing results.

**Template Resistance Has Real Costs:** Az's repeated tendency to "go against the template too much" has consistently hurt him. While being contrarian can pay off in specific situations, systematically ignoring consensus picks in favor of personal favorites or hunches has not worked for him or Mark. The takeaway is not to blindly follow the template, but to understand why certain players are template picks and to have genuine analytical reasons before deviating from them. The burden of proof for a non-template pick should be high.

**Data Tools Must Be Part of the Process:** Az explicitly states that the models and analytical tools have become sophisticated enough that ignoring them entirely is a mistake. His earlier approach—watching football and making decisions based on personal observation—was appropriate when everyone had limited data. Now, the competitive landscape has shifted. Successful FPL players need to incorporate data analysis into their decision-making rather than relying solely on intuition or tradition.

**Self-Awareness and Transparency Build Long-Term Trust:** Regardless of Az's recent results, his willingness to walk through his complete history, acknowledge failures, and explain his reasoning demonstrates a level of integrity that viewers should value. He does not hide poor finishes or make excuses; he owns them. For content consumers, this kind of transparency is invaluable for calibrating trust.

**Enjoyment and Perspective Matter:** Az repeatedly emphasizes that he does FPL content because he loves it and has no plans to stop despite criticism. He has a full-time job, and FPL is his passion project. This perspective—that FPL should ultimately be fun rather than a source of stress or livelihood—offers a healthy reminder for all players, whether they are casual participants or competitive managers.
