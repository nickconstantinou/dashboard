# Az Discusses His Latest Draft & Rank History | Pre Season | Fantasy Premier League Tips 2025/26

## Introduction

Fantasy Premier League content creators occupy a peculiar space in the FPL community—they're simultaneously relied upon for insights and subjected to intense scrutiny when those insights don't pan out. Az, one of the voices behind the FDL Blackbox YouTube channel and a regular contributor to the official FPL Show and Scout, recently sat down to address this dynamic directly in a candid video that serves as both a team reveal for the upcoming 2025/26 season and an unflinching look at his own performance history.

With the new season now just over a week away, Az joins his Blackbox colleagues Andy and Luke in sharing their draft strategies with viewers. The channel has established itself as a destination for FPL managers seeking alternative perspectives, often championing players and strategies that diverge from the mainstream template. However, Az begins his video by tackling what he calls "the elephant in the room"—the considerable criticism he's faced on social media following a difficult run of seasons.

The timing of such transparency is significant. As FPL managers across the world finalize their squads ahead of the August 16th deadline, Az offers not just tactical insights but a window into the psychological realities of being a public-facing figure in the FPL space. His honesty about both his methodology and his methodology's failures creates a foundation for understanding his draft philosophy and the lessons he's drawing from recent disappointments.

## Addressing the Criticism: Transparency and Self-Awareness

Az acknowledges the "quite poor performance over the last couple of years" with remarkable directness, recognizing that the criticism directed his way is "rightly so" in many respects. This admission sets the tone for a video that prioritizes honesty over defensiveness. He explains that while he works for the FPL Show and Scout—organizations that market him as an "expert"—he himself would never apply that label to his own abilities. "Never something I would ever call myself with anything really, let alone FPL," he states, framing the expert designation as purely a marketing term employed by his employers.

The creator addresses the awkward position of being called an expert while finishing in the hundreds of thousands of ranks, noting that some viewers have questioned why he doesn't simply ask the Premier League to stop using that terminology. His response is pragmatic: "I'm not going to tell the Premier League how to market me" and he's not about to turn down work he genuinely enjoys simply because of a marketing label he doesn't control. The choice, as he frames it, is between being called an expert while continuing to produce content he loves, or not doing it at all—and the former is clearly preferable to him.

Despite the negativity, Az makes clear he has no intention of stepping away from content creation. He has a full-time job outside of FPL, approaches this work as a passion project that began all the way back in the 2007/08 season, and genuinely enjoys the opportunities he's had working with Scout and the Premier League. While acknowledging that the criticism sometimes goes too far—people are being blocked on Twitter and he's been called various unflattering names—he's simply chosen not to engage with that aspect of online discourse. "I've got a full-time job. I do this for fun," he explains, emphasizing that he'll continue as long as the enjoyment remains.

## A Journey Through Fifteen Years of FPL History

To understand Az's current approach, one must understand the trajectory of his FPL career, and he provides a comprehensive overview spanning nearly two decades. His first three seasons from 2007 through 2009 represent what he describes as a learning period, with an average rank of approximately 186,000. "Veteran is probably what I'd call myself, or Yelen said—what about enthusiast? I think is probably more accurate to what I am," he admits, showing the kind of self-deprecating humor that characterizes his presentation style.

The period from 2010 to 2012 marked a significant improvement. Az achieved his first top 5,000 finish—placing 3,800th overall—followed by a 175,000th place finish and then a 10,600th place finish. He recalls the 2011/12 season specifically because he finished "one point outside the top 10k" and was "gutted," a memory that clearly motivated him to return and succeed in subsequent years. The following four seasons from 2013 to 2016 saw him sustain excellence with an average rank of approximately 20,000, including another 5,000 finish and notably a 2,000th place finish in the 2013/14 season. While he admits not remembering that campaign particularly well, the results speak to a sustained period of strong performance.

Az believes he came on to the Scout Cast during the 2016/17 season, finishing around 59,000 that year. The following season—2017/18—produced his best-ever result: an 800th place finish, which he describes as "my best ever year." He managed to follow that with a 62,000 finish the subsequent season, giving him an average rank of approximately 40,000 over that two-year span. These results placed him "quite high up in some of the hall of fame tables," though he never "broke into the top 10" overall.

The 2019/20 season marked a turning point, coinciding with his start at FDL Blackbox. The results that year were "lousier," with a first-year finish of 135,000—though he remembers being "a lot lower in the ranks before that," suggesting he recovered significantly over the course of the season. He then had a "really good season" in 2021/22, which, combined with the surrounding years, produced an average rank of 52,000. "Over those that four period, I mean I did well. My three-year average was—the lowest was 63k," he notes, indicating that even his "off" years during this stretch remained respectable.

## The Decline: Why Recent Seasons Have Been a "Disaster"

Az doesn't mince words when describing the last three seasons: "a complete disaster. There's no two ways of looking at it." The 2022/23 season saw him finish 143,000th overall, though he notes he was "about a million in the world with about six weeks left" before managing a recovery that included pipping his colleague Mark "by one point" on the final day. While he's "overall quite happy" with that recovery, the starting position remains concerning.

The 2023/24 season represents what Az openly calls "embarrassing"—a catastrophic 445,000th place finish. He provides unusual transparency about the decision-making process that led to this result. "I was around 200-250k and I was like, 'Okay, I can sort of settle for a pretty rubbish finish or I can really push and try and get into the top 100k.' And I did push." This push included ignoring Haaland in a home match against Wolves in favor of captaining Cole Palmer, choosing players like Romero over Porro, and making numerous other "tilt" decisions. "I ignored like Haaland, home to Wolves, Captain Palmer. I think I went for like Romero over Porro. I made lots of like tilt decisions and hope some of them would pay off and absolutely none of them paid off." The result was a spectacular tank down the rankings, and Az acknowledges that simply sitting out the season's remaining weeks would have produced a far better outcome—perhaps 150,000 to 200,000 rather than 445,000.

The 2024/25 season saw another struggle, with Az unable to "really find a groove." He identifies several recurring problems: being "too slow for like some of the top picks," which he calls "the story of my last three seasons"; ignoring Mohamed Salah "for far too long"; and "completely ignored things like review." His philosophical approach has been to "pick players based on what I've done before" and resist template plays, an approach he now recognizes as flawed.

## The Analytical Pivot: Learning from Data

Central to Az's self-critique is a recognition that his approach to FPL decision-making hasn't evolved sufficiently with the times. He reflects that in earlier seasons, he watched "a lot more football" and used that direct observation to inform his selections, a strategy that worked well when fewer tools existed. However, he acknowledges that the emergence of sophisticated models and data analytics has changed the landscape fundamentally. "Models and things like they need to be incorporated a little bit because they are so good at synthesizing that data."

This admission is particularly notable given Az's content philosophy, which has historically emphasized qualitative analysis and contrarian thinking. He acknowledges that "Mark and I, on Blackbox, have pushed too much against that over the last few years and neither of us have had particularly good years. I think Mark finished in the top 100k last year, had one good season like me, and then I think we've struggled on the other years." The correlation between their shared analytical approach and their shared poor results isn't lost on him. "We tried to fly the flag maybe too much against things and it hasn't really worked."

The lesson Az draws isn't that data should replace all other considerations, but rather that a synthesis is necessary. His upcoming draft will presumably reflect this evolved thinking, though the video focuses more on his retrospective analysis than specific tactical plans for the new season.

## Personal Stakes and Future Motivation

Beyond rank pride and professional credibility, Az identifies a deeply personal motivation for the upcoming season: avoiding another loss to his partner Yelen. "The main thing is not to lose to Yelen again," he says with audible frustration. "Really, she beat me three years in a row, and it's just getting a bit embarrassing." This humanizing detail adds context to his analysis—it reminds viewers that FPL is ultimately a game, even for those who make their living (or side income) from content about it.

Az frames his journey as a test of whether he can return to his previous levels of performance. "If you're new to the channel and you're wondering who the hell is this guy who's been called an expert and finished 265k and 440k—440k—that is the record. I've done all right. I've done all right in the past. And I guess the journey with me now is to see whether I can get back to the old ranks, the ranks of yesterday year, which seem like a long distant memory go away."

## Key Takeaways

**1. Transparency builds credibility more effectively than defensive posturing.** Rather than deflecting criticism about his recent performances, Az addressed it head-on with specific ranks, honest self-assessment, and acknowledgment of mistakes. This approach—admitting the 445,000 finish was "embarrassing" and explaining the flawed decision-making process that led to it—creates trust with audiences that perfection never could. For FPL managers, this demonstrates that even experienced players face significant variance and that acknowledging poor decisions is the first step toward improvement.

**2. Chasing ranks through high-risk plays typically compounds losses rather than recovering them.** Az's analysis of his 2023/24 season provides a cautionary tale about the gambler's fallacy in FPL. When sitting at 200,000-250,000, he chose to "push" toward top 100,000 rather than accept a modest finish, resulting in a catastrophic 445,000 outcome. The lesson is clear: attempting dramatic rank recovery through template-breaking captain picks and tilted decisions rarely works and usually makes things worse. Sustainable FPL success comes from consistent decision-making rather than desperate swings.

**3. Modern FPL success requires integrating data analytics with qualitative judgment.** Az's observation that "models need to be incorporated" alongside traditional methods reflects a broader truth about the game's evolution. As the FPL community has become more sophisticated—with tools like FPL Review, the OPTA stats available on official platforms, and countless Twitter analysts synthesizing data—the edge from pure intuition has diminished significantly. The best managers now synthesize multiple data sources rather than relying exclusively on either gut feeling or pure analytics.

**4. Historical performance doesn't guarantee future results, but patterns of improvement are meaningful.** Az's track record includes multiple top 5,000 finishes, an 800th place overall result, and sustained excellence across multiple seasons. While the last three years have been poor, his overall record demonstrates genuine skill. For viewers evaluating FPL content creators, this underscores the importance of looking at longer-term records rather than reacting to single-season results—both positive and negative.

**5. Content creation and playing FPL successfully require different skill sets.** Az's struggles highlight that being able to analyze FPL tactically and actually executing those analyses in your own team aren't the same thing. The pressure of public performance, the temptation to overthink for content purposes, and the psychological weight of being watched may all contribute to degraded decision-making. This doesn't absolve him of responsibility for poor results, but it does suggest that viewers should calibrate their expectations accordingly and recognize that even thoughtful FPL managers can underperform.
