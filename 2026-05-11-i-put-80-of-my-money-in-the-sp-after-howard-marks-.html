    Introduction

    This episode of *My First Million* tackles a question that haunts many ambitious people: why do some people thrive in certain arenas while others never find their footing, despite equal effort? The host—who runs Hampton Creek, the company behind Just Mayo—delivers an unusually personal and research-grounded exploration of how genetics, personality, and self-awareness shape not just investing outcomes but life trajectory itself.

    The conversation opens with a surprising research finding from Sweden that suggests nearly half of how we invest and save is predetermined by our DNA. Rather than leading to fatalism, this becomes a launching point for a broader meditation on "knowing thyself"—the idea that understanding your innate predispositions may be the single most valuable skill for finding fulfillment and success. Through the lens of legendary investor Manish Pabrai's life story and the host's own self-discovery, the episode argues that most people spend their lives in the wrong game, never realizing there was a better fit waiting for them.

    The episode weaves together big ideas from behavioral finance, personality psychology, and entrepreneurship into something surprisingly actionable: a framework for identifying what type of "game" you're built to play, and why that question matters more than any specific strategy or tactic you might adopt.

    ---

    The host begins by crediting investor Jim O'Shaughnessy for pointing him toward a landmark study that reframes how we should think about investment success. In 2014, a researcher named Heinrich published findings that emerged from one of the world's most comprehensive twin databases—Sweden's obsessive documentation of hundreds of thousands of twins, originally established for health research purposes.

    Sweden's particular combination of assets made this study possible. The country's government maintained a wealth tax until 2007, which required detailed tracking of every citizen's entire financial portfolio—stocks owned, mutual funds, savings accounts, everything. Meanwhile, the twin database allowed researchers to compare two distinct groups: fraternal twins (who share roughly 50% of DNA and were raised in the same environment) versus identical twins (who share 100% of their genetic code and also shared upbringing).

    The logic was elegant: if twins raised in the same household, attending the same schools, with the same parents speaking to them the same way, still show dramatically different financial behaviors, then environment can't be the sole explanation. The differences between how fraternal versus identical twins invest would reveal the genetic component.

    Heinrich examined six specific investor biases or behaviors: people who held too few stocks (under-diversification), those who traded excessively, people who chased performance (buying last year's winners), home bias (over-investing in one's own country), attraction to lottery-type stocks, and the disposition effect (refusing to sell losing positions to "break even"). After analyzing more than 30,000 twin pairs—and including outliers like twins separated at birth—the study concluded that 45% of savings and investing behavior patterns are genetic.

    This figure struck the host as "astounding." It suggests that something as seemingly rational and learnable as investing is, in nearly equal measure, a product of who you already are. This prompted the central question driving the episode: if outcomes are so heavily predisposed, how should we approach finding our path?

    ---

    Why We Admire Great Investors: Human Nature Over Mechanics

    Before diving into self-discovery frameworks, the host reflects on why both he and his co-host are drawn to figures like Warren Buffett and Howard Marks. He argues that world-class investing requires financial knowledge as a secondary skill—primary is an understanding of human nature.

    The reasoning is straightforward: financial instruments, market structures, and economic trends evolve constantly. SPACs are replaced by token offerings, which are replaced by whatever comes next. Learning any specific mechanism has a half-life of maybe a decade before the landscape shifts. But human psychology? "That doesn't change is thousands and thousands of years," the host observes. Understanding why people fear, why they covet, why they hold onto losers or chase winners—these insights persist indefinitely and apply across every market cycle.

    This observation dovetails with the HubSpot wealth guide promoted in the episode, which distills principles from Howard Marks, Manish Pabrai, Morgan Housel, Cathie Wood, and others. The host frames these investors not as repositories of tricks but as people who have internalized deep models of human behavior and built systems that account for those patterns.

    ---

    The centerpiece of the episode is an extended case study of Manish Pabrai—a guest whose two appearances on *My First Million* remain the most-viewed episodes in the show's history. Pabrai's story illustrates the "know thyself" principle with unusual clarity.

    Early in his career, Pabrai was running a moderately successful company generating roughly $6 million in annual revenue. He was unhappy but assumed this was normal entrepreneurial burnout—long hours, relentless pressure, the universal grind that founders accept as the price of building something. But his frustration persisted in ways that felt disproportionate to his objective success.

    A breakthrough came when Pabrai participated in a sophisticated personality assessment—a 360-degree evaluation involving interviews not just with him but with coworkers, family members, and others in his life. The results were unambiguous: he was miserable because he was playing "a game that is completely incompatible for your personality type."

    The assessment described him as someone who thrives in what the host characterizes as "solo player competitive number games." The phrase captures Pabrai's essential nature: he is most alive when working alone, when the competition is measurable, when the variables are numerical and the outcomes clear. Running a company—a business requiring networking, team leadership, stakeholder management, social navigation—was the opposite of his natural disposition. It was, in the assessment's framing, "multiplayer competitive non-numbers based."

    Pabrai took this seriously. He began investing on the side, and the results were dramatically better than his entrepreneurial track record. He discovered that the stock market was precisely the game he was built for—a single-player arena with clear scoring, where mathematical edge could be pursued methodically.

    ---

    Pabrai's Other "Games": Gambling and Philanthropy

    The host shares additional Pabrai anecdotes that reinforce the personality framework. During a visit to Pabrai's home, the host heard stories about Pabrai being banned from casinos—not for card counting, but for systematically finding mathematical edges in ways that alarmed the house. When asked about this, Pabrai was characteristically unbothered by the financial ROI: "The intellectual thrill of being able to beat the house—there's nothing like it." He had spent thousands of hours on a problem that pure economics wouldn't justify, simply because the challenge itself was irresistible.

    The same pattern emerged in philanthropy. Pabrai realized he was completely uninterested in the socialite dimension of charitable giving—galas, networking, social recognition. What fulfilled him was the "single player competitive numbers space"—figuring out how to maximize economic return per dollar of impact.

    This led to a specific initiative targeting rural India. Pabrai identified a disturbing inefficiency: millions of highly intelligent children in rural India had no access to quality education and would likely end up working farms. But if identified early, these children could be placed in competitive schools and ultimately gain admission to IIT—the Indian Institutes of Technology—which is notoriously harder to enter than Harvard or Stanford.

    For roughly $33 per year per child, Pabrai could fund the identification and education of these students. The ROI was staggering: a family's earning potential could move from $10,000 annually to $50,000—a 5x multiplier on a trivially small investment. Pabrai's admission rate to IIT among his students was around 8%—roughly 10 times higher than the institution's normal acceptance rate—because he was selecting for aptitude rather than relying on traditional school systems that often fail bright students in under-resourced areas.

    The host frames this as a perfect expression of Pabrai's nature: he found the version of philanthropy that was a competitive, solo, numbers-based game, and it happened to generate enormous human impact.

    ---

    The host reinforces the theme with a second example from James Currier, a mentor who recently told him something striking: "I wish somebody earlier in life had told me who I really was."

    Currier's story parallels Pabrai's. He played competitive soccer throughout his youth, assuming this was simply his athletic path. Only later did he realize he was actually far better suited to racket sports—he had the long wingspan and hand-eye coordination that made him excellent at racquetball, but he never discovered this because soccer was the obvious default. The same pattern played out in his career: he spent years in business types that didn't fit him before eventually finding his way to ventures that aligned with his actual strengths.

    The host draws a sobering conclusion: "There's probably billions of people walking around who never discovered what game they're actually great at and never got into that game, and therefore, like, kinda life passed them by. Isn't that sort of a tragedy?"

    The Host's Own Self-Assessment

    Shifting to first-person reflection, the host begins to articulate his own disposition—and credits a book developer (someone who ghostwrites books for famous people) with identifying his core pattern.

    Over multiple conversations spanning years, this developer observed the host cycling through various book ideas and eventually offered an observation: "The most interesting part about you is you have some weird predilection for reverse engineering businesses or reverse engineering. Everything you do, you seem to reverse engineer as a first instinct."

    The host recognizes this as accurate. His default mode when approaching any new challenge—whether in life, business, or investing—is to start by studying how others have succeeded or failed at the same thing. He seeks out conversations with people who have already done what he wants to do, extracts their principles, identifies what to ignore versus what to prioritize, and then builds his own system based on that synthesis. "I know what to ignore of what they're gonna say. I know what to focus in on. I'm gonna create my own system and my own understanding, and then I'll just do that."

    Crucially, this isn't just a professional habit—it's a source of genuine enjoyment. Researching deeply into a topic, obsessively pulling threads, arriving at a conclusion: this is where he operates at his best.

    The host observes that this pattern shapes how he runs his company, Hampton. He explicitly credits his co-founder Joe for handling the CEO role and team leadership—functions the host admits he would be less effective at. The host's "zone of genius" is most activated when preparing to launch something new: researching analogues, identifying failure patterns, talking to experts, and building frameworks. He recognizes this as a specific type of capability and notes that it likely isn't "super common."

    ---

    The host notes that this disposition shapes his approach to advising guests. When entrepreneurs appear on the podcast seeking advice, his first question is never tactical—it's not "should you raise capital?" or "should you hire?" Instead, he asks: "How would you define success in five years? What's the definition of success to you?" Only after establishing the end state can the rules of the game be determined, and only then can relevant advice follow.

    This approach is itself a form of reverse engineering: starting from the desired outcome and working backward to identify the necessary moves, rather than trying to optimize from the present state forward.

    ---

    The Host's Core Game: Research as a Solo Endeavor

    Pressed to articulate his own "game," the host identifies it as "new topic, going super deep, obsessing over one topic, trying to come to a conclusion." He describes himself as someone who finds joy in sitting alone with a book or in quiet reflection, synthesizing information into understanding. This self-knowledge shapes how he's structured his career: rather than forcing himself into conventional entrepreneurial roles that require constant team interaction and external relationship management, he's carved out a position where deep research and pattern recognition are the primary value he provides.

    The irony isn't lost on him: "You're describing sitting alone on a couch with a book and thinking, and really finding your joy. And then you're like, so I'm gonna go sit in an office with 50 other people and run another company?" He recognizes that this impulse is somewhat contradictory to the solo, introspective nature he's described, but frames his current work as an accommodation that allows him to operate closer to his natural strengths while still building something substantial.

    This content is particularly valuable for entrepreneurs, independent investors, and knowledge workers who sense something isn't clicking in their current path but can't articulate why. If you've achieved external markers of success—revenue, recognition, growth—yet feel persistently misaligned or unfulfilled, the frameworks in this episode offer a language and structure for diagnosing the problem.

    It's also valuable for anyone interested in behavioral finance or investor psychology. The Swedish twin study provides a rigorous foundation for understanding why some people naturally make better investment decisions than others—and why trying to simply "learn" better investing habits may be fighting against deep-seated psychological predispositions.

    Finally, the episode serves people who are early in their career exploration and want a framework for self-discovery. The idea that there are different "types of games" (solo versus multiplayer, numbers-based versus relationship-based, competitive versus collaborative) provides a useful mental model for evaluating whether a given path fits your nature.

    The core value is not specific tactics—it's the invitation to step back and ask whether you've correctly identified which game you were built to play, and whether you're actually playing it.
