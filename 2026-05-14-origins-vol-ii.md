## Introduction

Every transformative idea begins somewhere unremarkable—a cramped manga cafe in Seoul, a children's hospital ward in England, perhaps a stage waiting to be conquered or a moment of personal collapse that precedes a breakthrough. The second volume of the *Origins* podcast series by Soft White Underbelly explores this fundamental truth through four unrelated stories united by a single theme: ordinary people who made one critical decision that cascaded into consequences affecting millions of lives across the globe.

The first story, "The Debt Game," follows a Korean writer named Hwang Dong-hyuk as he navigates crushing poverty in 2009 Seoul, eventually channeling his desperation into what would become one of the most successful television productions in history. The second story, "Hospital Rhythms," traces the journey of a sickly British teenager named Rich who discovered his lifelong passion for drumming while confined to a tuberculosis ward, setting him on a path that would eventually make him a celebrated musician. Together, these narratives reveal how circumstances of profound difficulty can become the crucible for extraordinary achievement—not despite hardship, but because of it.

What emerges from these stories is a deeper understanding of creativity, persistence, and the invisible threads connecting personal suffering to universal art. The question is not whether talent matters, but rather: what happens when talent meets genuine desperation, and how long must someone wait for the world to catch up to their vision?

## The Debt Game: How Poverty Became the Fuel for a Global Phenomenon

### A Man at the Breaking Point

In September 2009, a 38-year-old man named Hwang Dong-hyuk found himself sitting in a manga cafe in Seoul, South Korea, with essentially nothing. A manga cafe differs from an ordinary coffee shop—it offers the standard amenities of snacks and socializing while maintaining extensive in-store libraries of manga, the Japanese comic books that have shaped countless lives across Asia and increasingly around the world. For Hwang, this establishment represented something essential: free access to stories.

He had no money to purchase food or beverages. Instead, he walked directly to the manga library, selected an edition of his favorite series, and settled into a booth to read. He had consumed this particular manga multiple times, returning to it again and again because, in his estimation, it represented the pinnacle of the medium.

The series in question was *Battle Royale*, a controversial manga depicting a dystopian future where students are forced by a fascist government to fight each other to the death. The series was notoriously violent—far more extreme than anything else available at the time—which made it polarizing among readers. But for Hwang, that very extremity was what made it special. Each time he read it, he experienced a complete escape from his own grim reality.

That reality was bleak. Hwang had spent his entire life pursuing a career as a professional writer and director in Seoul's entertainment industry. The Korean entertainment world, however, operated on unpredictable rhythms, and Hwang had managed to secure only scattered, small-scale writing and directing jobs that inevitably fizzled out. Time and again, he found himself in the same impossible position: unemployed, without income, and watching his dreams recede.

At the time of that September afternoon in the manga cafe, Hwang was living with his mother and grandmother. The three of them combined their limited resources but still struggled to pay rent. Recently, he had been forced to sell his laptop—the essential tool for any writer attempting to break into the entertainment industry—in order to prevent the family from being evicted. The situation had grown so dire that he was taking out high-interest short-term loans merely to purchase food and basic necessities, with absolutely no realistic means of repayment. The debt accumulated relentlessly, and the psychological weight of it became nearly unbearable.

### The Epiphany in the Booth

Yet even as his circumstances deteriorated, Hwang continued reading manga. He understood intellectually that this escape solved nothing, but the alternative—confronting his problems without distraction—felt impossible. Life was so difficult that even temporary relief through stories seemed worthwhile.

On this particular day, he sat down with *Battle Royale* once more, prepared to lose himself in its violent narrative for the twentieth time. But something unexpected happened. As he read, a thought crystallized in his mind with sudden clarity: what if he could write something inspired by this series, but grounded in his own lived experience? The idea sparked instantly, and he began recognizing other elements of his life that could fuse with this initial concept.

Within hours, Hwang had abandoned the manga book entirely. He obtained a pen and paper and began furiously writing. He stayed in that booth through the afternoon, developing an outline that would eventually encompass an entire world and narrative arc. By the time the cafe closed, he possessed a comprehensive framework for a story he knew, with absolute certainty, would be his breakthrough.

This was the one. This was the project that would lift him out of poverty and establish him as a successful, recognized writer.

### A Decade of Rejection

Over the following months, Hwang transformed that initial outline into a complete, polished screenplay. He approached potential buyers with boundless confidence, convinced that everyone would recognize the brilliance of his vision. After all, he had created something unique and powerful—a piece of intellectual property that no one else could have produced.

The market's response crushed him. Every single buyer he approached rejected the project. Their assessments were blunt and consistent: the concept was too strange, far too violent, well beyond the boundaries of what audiences would accept. One after another, they told him that no reasonable platform would ever distribute something this extreme, and that finding an audience would be impossible.

Hwang was devastated but unconvinced. To him, the brilliance of his creation was self-evident. He understood that people who had genuinely experienced poverty—who knew firsthand the desperate lengths humans would go to when survival was at stake—would recognize the truth and power of his story. He believed an enormous audience existed for this material, even if the current gatekeepers couldn't see it.

For the next ten years, Hwang persisted. He submitted his screenplay repeatedly, approaching every potential buyer who would give him an audience. The rejections accumulated, but he never abandoned his conviction that someone would eventually recognize what he had created. His belief in the project remained absolute.

### Twelve Years Later: A Global Sensation

Then, unexpectedly, a major production company expressed interest. Perhaps the cultural moment had finally aligned with Hwang's vision, or perhaps someone at the studio simply possessed the courage to take a risk on something unconventional. Whatever the reason, they purchased his screenplay and began production.

The television series premiered in 2021—twelve years after Hwang had first conceived of it inside that Seoul manga cafe. From the moment it debuted, *Squid Game* became an unprecedented global phenomenon. It dominated every corner of the internet, sparked countless analyses and discussions, and became the most talked-about show worldwide almost instantaneously. The show's brutal depiction of desperate people competing in deadly children's games resonated with audiences who recognized, on some level, the desperate calculus of survival under economic pressure.

The financial results were staggering. The production company that had taken a chance on Hwang's rejected screenplay earned approximately $900 million from the series. Hwang himself received substantial compensation and, perhaps most meaningfully to a writer who had spent a decade being told his work was unmarketable, won an Emmy Award.

The manga that had inspired it all—*Battle Royale*—remained violent, extreme, and controversial. But Hwang had accomplished something different. He had taken the death-game concept from that manga and merged it with something profoundly personal: his genuine experience of poverty and the desperate financial decisions that accompanied it. Poor people fighting to the death for money was not merely a fictional premise; it was an artistic representation of the survival logic that had governed his own existence during those terrible years.

The lesson embedded in Hwang's story is not simply about persistence, though persistence clearly mattered. It is about the unique creative power that emerges when an artist draws directly from authentic experience. Hwang could not have written *Squid Game* if he had not lived the desperation that made its premise feel true. The suffering was not separate from the art—it was the foundation upon which the art was built.

## Hospital Rhythms: A Sick Boy Discovers His Calling

### Life in a Children's Hospital

In September 1954, a fourteen-year-old boy named Rich walked slowly down the corridor of a children's hospital in England, coughing and wheezing as he moved alongside other sick children heading toward the hospital's classroom. Rich harbored no particular expectations about what he might learn that day—anything seemed preferable to sitting alone in his hospital room, bored and isolated.

Rich was a patient because he was suffering from tuberculosis, a serious infectious lung disease that had been a major public health threat for generations. Over the previous ten weeks, he had been transferred between different hospitals as doctors attempted to treat his condition, which remained serious enough that the medical staff had informed his parents he would need to remain hospitalized for potentially another two years.

This was not Rich's first extended hospitalization. When he was six years old, his appendix had burst, leading to a severe infection that placed him in a coma for several weeks. Upon waking, he had required nearly a year of recovery in a hospital setting. The cumulative effect of these health crises had significantly impacted his educational trajectory—repeated absences meant he had fallen far behind his peers academically.

Rich's family lacked the financial resources to hire private tutors who might have helped him maintain academic progress during his hospitalizations. Consequently, his education had become a patchwork of whatever instruction the various hospitals he stayed in happened to offer—occasional classes in basket weaving or shop when the hospitals arranged temporary teachers.

### The Day Everything Changed

When Rich entered the hospital classroom that day in September 1954, he expected nothing special. He anticipated another random class that might at least provide temporary escape from his bedroom. But the instant he saw what surrounded the teacher—musical instruments arranged throughout the room—he became electrified with excitement.

Rich had always dreamed of playing the drums. As a child, he had seen a drum kit displayed in a shop window and wanted it desperately, but his family could not afford such an expense. He had never had the opportunity to pursue this interest. Until now.

When the teacher began distributing instruments to the assembled children, Rich demanded the drum set. He refused to accept anything else. The teacher acquiesced, handing him the drums. As the class progressed through several basic songs, the music was objectively terrible—everyone was clearly a beginner—but by the session's end, something fundamental had shifted in Rich.

He had not discovered hidden virtuosity or exceptional natural talent. What he had discovered was a consuming passion that consumed him entirely. From that moment forward, all he wanted to do was play the drums.

### The Long Practice

Rich understood that his tuberculosis would keep him hospitalized for the foreseeable future—potentially two more years. Rather than viewing this prospect with despair, he recognized an opportunity. He would use this time to practice relentlessly.

The following day, he explored the hospital and discovered a supply closet containing long sticks normally used for knitting. He took two of these sticks and began using them as makeshift drumsticks. For the next eighteen months, Rich practiced on virtually any surface he could find, developing his technique with improvised equipment. Whenever the music teacher returned to the hospital every few weeks, Rich attended those classes obsessively, absorbing every instruction available.

His entire existence during that hospitalization centered on drums and practicing. There were no guarantees that this investment of time would ever yield meaningful results—after all, he was a hospital-bound teenager with no formal training and no professional connections. But passion does not require guarantees. Rich simply knew that drumming was what he wanted to do with his life.

### Released but Still Constrained

In 1955, Rich was released from the hospital. At sixteen years old, he had finally overcome his tuberculosis and regained his health. But freedom from illness did not translate to freedom from hardship. His family continued to struggle financially, making it impossible for him to pursue formal music education or even return to regular schooling.

Instead, Rich took a job working full-time in a factory, contributing his earnings to the family budget. He did not resent this responsibility—he recognized that his family needed him, and he accepted the role that circumstances demanded. But he also refused to abandon his passion entirely. He continued carrying makeshift drumsticks wherever he went and practiced on any available surface during work breaks and free time. He had no expectation that drumming would ever become more than a private hobby, a personal joy that sustained him through the demands of factory labor.

He assumed this would be his life: work, family obligations, and occasional drumming for his own pleasure.

### A Christmas Transformation

Everything changed on Christmas Day, 1956—one year after Rich had left the hospital. His stepfather had been secretly saving money for months, working additional shifts and doing whatever extra work he could find to accumulate enough funds. Without telling Rich, he had finally saved enough to purchase something he knew his stepson desperately wanted and needed.

On that Christmas morning, Rich received his own drum kit. For a teenager who had spent over a year practicing on improvised knitting needles, who had never owned a proper instrument despite his consuming passion, this gift was transformative. It represented not merely an object but an acknowledgment of his dreams and a practical investment in his future.

Rich began practicing with renewed intensity. Within a short time, he started performing with local bands whenever his work schedule permitted. His reputation grew quickly—he became known in his local area as an exceptionally skilled drummer. Eventually, he caught the attention of a popular local band that had just fired their drummer. Rich was offered the position, launching his professional music career.

The hospital ward where Rich had discovered his passion, the makeshift drumsticks fashioned from knitting needles, the years of practicing while holding down factory jobs—all of it had been preparation for this moment. The stepfather who had sacrificed in secret had given his stepson not just a Christmas present but a future.

## Detailed Takeaways

### 1. Personal Suffering Creates Authentic Creative Vision

Both Hwang Dong-hyuk and Rich transformed their difficult circumstances into creative fuel. Hwang drew directly from his experience of poverty, debt, and desperation when crafting *Squid Game*. He understood viscerally what it felt like to be so financially desperate that any solution seemed worth considering. Rich found purpose and passion during extended hospitalization, turning what could have been a purely negative experience into the foundation of his career. The implication is not that suffering is desirable, but that how one responds to suffering can shape extraordinary outcomes. Authentic creative vision often emerges from artists who have genuinely lived the experiences they depict.

### 2. Persistence Without Compromise Can Eventually Succeed

Hwang spent a decade receiving nothing but rejections for *Squid Game*. Every professional gatekeeper told him his vision was unmarketable, too extreme, and too strange. Yet he never fundamentally altered his core concept to make it more palatable. His willingness to wait—while continuing to believe in his work when no one else did—eventually resulted in unprecedented success. This suggests that persistence matters, but the kind of persistence that eventually succeeds is the variety that maintains vision while remaining flexible about everything except the essential creative truth.

### 3. Recognition Often Arrives Long After the Work Begins

Both stories feature dramatic gaps between initial conception and ultimate success. Hwang conceived *Squid Game* in 2009 but saw it premiere in 2021—twelve years of waiting. Rich discovered his passion for drums in 1954 but did not begin his professional career until years later, after receiving his first drum kit. The lesson here is uncomfortable but important: the timeline for recognition rarely matches the timeline for creation. Anyone pursuing creative or professional excellence must prepare for the possibility that validation will arrive long after the initial inspiration, and possibly long after the creator has begun questioning whether success will ever come.

### 4. The People Who Believe in Us Often Do So Silently

Rich's stepfather worked extra shifts for months, saying nothing, saving in secret, until he could suddenly produce the gift that would change his stepson's life. This pattern appears repeatedly in stories of breakthrough success: the unsung supporters who invest quietly, who work extra hours without announcement, who sacrifice without expecting recognition. Hwang's mother and grandmother pooled their resources with him, sharing his poverty as best they could. Neither story explicitly emphasizes this element, yet both highlight the reality that success rarely emerges from isolated individual effort—it emerges from networks of silent support that make achievement possible even when the person doing the achieving cannot see how it might work.
