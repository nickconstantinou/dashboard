Az Discusses His Latest Draft & Rank History | Pre Season | Fantasy Premier League Tips 2025/26

Introduction

Fantasy Premier League content creator Az has returned with his second video on the FDL Blackbox channel, offering an early look at his draft strategy for the 2025/26 season while delivering an unusually candid assessment of his recent performance struggles. As the community countdowns to the new campaign—with just over a week remaining—Az joins fellow content creators Andy and Luke in the tradition of team reveals that have become a staple of the preseason period. While many creators approach such videos with polished optimism, Az chose a different path, opening his video by directly addressing the wave of criticism he has faced on social media regarding his FPL results over the past three seasons.

The timing of this reflection feels significant. With the FPL community increasingly scrutinizing "expert" picks and holding content creators accountable for their predictions, Az's willingness to lay out his complete ranking history—from his early learning years in 2007 through his best-ever 800th-place finish to his recent struggles including a 445,000th-place finish in 2023/24—offers viewers a rare glimpse into the pressures facing those who produce FPL content. His discussion goes beyond mere self-defense; it represents a thoughtful analysis of what has gone wrong, what lessons he has learned, and how he plans to approach the upcoming season differently.

Beyond the personal reflection, this video serves as a practical guide for viewers. Az walks through his current draft team, examines fixture considerations, and explains the reasoning behind his player selections. For those building their own squads ahead of the new season, this provides actionable insight into how a seasoned player approaches the formative stages of team construction.

A Candid Assessment of Recent Performance

Addressing the Criticism

Az does not shy away from the negativity circulating on platforms like Twitter, acknowledging that "there's quite a lot of negativity around content creators" and that he has received "a fair bit of stick, some of it rightly so" for his poor performance over the past few years. Rather than dismissing these criticisms, he accepts them as legitimate while providing important context about the nature of his work with organizations like the FPL Show and Scout, who market him using terms like "expert"—a designation he himself would never use.

"I'm pretty transparent about it, I mean you can all see my record," Az explains. "I do work for the FPL Show and Scout and they use terms like expert and things. Never something I would ever call myself with anything really, let alone FPL." He notes that while some have suggested he ask these organizations to stop using the "expert" label, he has no control over their marketing decisions. "I'm not going to tell the Premier League, you know, how to market me. That's up to them. If they don't want to use me, if they don't think I'm an expert, then they can not use me."

This transparency sets the tone for what follows: an honest accounting of his FPL journey without excuses or deflection.

The Three-Year Struggle

Az pulls no punches in describing his recent form as "a complete disaster" with "no two ways of looking at it." His last three seasons tell a story of consistent underperformance. In 2022/23, he finished 143,000th, though he notes he was approximately one millionth in the world with six weeks remaining before managing to overtake his colleague Mark by just one point on the final day. "I managed to turn things around," he recalls. "It could have been a hell of a lot worse and I turned things out around towards the end."

The 2023/24 season, however, was far more damaging to his record. Finishing 445,000th, Az openly describes this as "embarrassing" and "really bad." He explains that he had been sitting around 200,000-250,000 in the ranks and faced a choice: "I can sort of settle for a pretty rubbish finish or I can really push and try and get into the top 100k." He chose to push, ignoring Haaland at home to Wolves in favor of captaining Palmer, swapping out Romero for Porro, and making numerous other "tilt decisions" that backfired spectacularly. "I hope some of them would pay off and absolutely none of them paid off," he admits. "So I ended up tanking down the ranks."

Last season continued the pattern. Az describes being "too slow for like some of the top picks," particularly noting that he ignored Salah for far too long. "I think that's been the story of my last three seasons. I've just been too slow to react."

The Complete FPL Journey: From 2007 to Present

The Early Years: Learning the Game (2007-2009)

Az's FPL story spans nearly two decades, beginning in the 2007/08 season when he describes himself as "probably what I'd call a veteran or enthusiast—more accurate to what I am." During these first three seasons, his average rank sat around 186,000 as he learned the game's intricacies. These were formative years, establishing the foundation of knowledge that would eventually help him achieve significant success.

The Rise: Finding His Feet (2010-2015)

The period from 2010 to 2012 marked Az's breakthrough into elite FPL performance. He secured his first top 5,000 finish with a rank of 3,800 in 2010, followed by a 175,000th-place finish in 2011, and then a remarkable 10,600th-place finish in 2012—finishing just one point outside the top 10,000. "I remember that season because I finished one point outside the top 10k and I was gutted, determined to get back in again," he recalls.

From 2013 to 2015, Az sustained this excellence with an average rank of approximately 20,000, including another top 5,000 finish and an impressive 2,000th-place finish in 2014. "That was a good couple of years," he reflects, though he admits he does not remember those seasons particularly well. This period established him as a consistent performer in the upper echelons of FPL players.

The Peak: Scout Cast and Blackbox (2016-2021)

Az joined the Scout Cast team towards the end of the 2016/17 season, finishing around 59,000th. The following season, 2017/18, brought his best-ever result: an extraordinary 800th-place finish. He followed this with a 62,000th-place finish the next year, giving him an average rank of approximately 40,000 over that period. These were the years that would define his reputation in the FPL community.

His journey then intersected with the birth of FDL Blackbox in 2020. While his first season with the new venture saw him finish 135,000th—"the first year I did it, I was probably a lot lower in the ranks before that"—he bounced back strongly in 2021/22, describing it as a "really good season." Across those three years, his average rank sat at 52,000, with his lowest finish being 63,000. "Over those that four-year period, I mean I did well. I mean my three-year average was you know the lowest was 63k. So you know I had pretty good. I was quite high up in some of the hall of fame tables and things. Never broke into the top 10 or anything like that, but you know I did all right."

The Decline: A Three-Year Slump

The contrast with his recent performance is stark. His last three seasons—2022/23 through 2024/25—have been characterized by finishes of 143,000th, 445,000th, and additional poor results that he has declined to specify publicly, though community records indicate finishes of 265,000th and another 440,000th. The 445,000th finish in particular stands out as a "real blemish on the record," one that Az acknowledges "obviously not happy with at all."

Understanding What Went Wrong

The Anti-Template Problem

Az identifies a fundamental strategic flaw that has plagued his recent seasons: an excessive resistance to popular picks and data-driven recommendations. "I've completely ignored things like review," he explains. "Always thought, you know, this isn't the way to play the game. I want to pick players based on what I've done before." This approach, he now realizes, has become increasingly untenable as the FPL community has grown and tools have become more sophisticated.

He draws a direct comparison to his colleague Mark, noting that both of them have struggled in similar ways. "Mark finished in the top 100k last year, had one good season like me, and then I think we've struggled on the other years that we've done it as you know as shown by me beating by one point on that last day." The implication is clear: their shared philosophy of "flying the flag" against the consensus has not served either of them well.

The Changing Landscape of FPL

Az reflects on how the game has evolved since his early years. "Even though in previous seasons there's been far fewer people, um there's been less tools available. So it does kind of balance itself out a little bit. Like you had to, you know, I watched a lot more football, you know, back in the day and I used that to make more decisions than I do now." The ecosystem of FPL has transformed dramatically, with sophisticated models and analytics tools now available to the average player. His reliance on intuition and traditional scouting—skills that once gave him an edge—has become insufficient in an era where data synthesis provides powerful competitive advantages.

"I tried to carry that on, but I think the models and things like they need to be incorporated a little bit because they are so good at synthesizing that data," Az acknowledges. This represents a significant shift in his thinking, a recognition that success in modern FPL requires blending the instincts developed over nearly two decades with the analytical tools that have revolutionized the game.

The Personal Cost

Despite the frustrations, Az maintains that he has no intention of stepping away from content creation. "I've got a full-time job. I do this for fun. This is like a passion that I've had since 07/08. I love making content. I get great opportunities. I really enjoy doing Blackbox, you know, with Mark and now with Andy and Luke." He acknowledges that the negativity has sometimes become overwhelming—"there's a lot of people are being blocked now on Twitter and I get called two cents and things"—but insists he will continue while he is enjoying the work.

A personal motivation drives him forward: the desire to beat his partner Yelena, who has apparently beaten him three years in a row. "It's just getting a bit embarrassing," he admits with a laugh. "So let's see if we can turn things around this season."

Draft Strategy and Fixtures Analysis

Current Team Construction

Shifting from personal reflection to practical application, Az begins examining his current draft for the 2025/26 season. While specific player selections are not fully detailed in the transcript, he indicates he will be "looking at the fixtures and the reasoning behind some of those players" as well as discussing "some of the other players that might make it into the draft."

The approach signals a more analytical method than he has employed in recent years, suggesting that the lessons learned from his performance review will inform his team construction process going forward.

Detailed Takeaways

1. Historical Performance Does Not Guarantee Future Success

Az's career trajectory demonstrates that even players with proven elite credentials—multiple top 5,000 finishes, a best-ever rank of 800th, years of average ranks under 50,000—can fall dramatically if they fail to adapt to the evolving landscape. His three-year decline from consistent 50,000-60,000 finishes to multiple 400,000+ ranks shows that past success is not a buffer against poor decision-making. For viewers, this underscores the importance of continuously evaluating one's approach rather than relying on strategies that worked in previous eras.

2. Anti-Template Sentiment Can Be Costly

Az's explicit acknowledgment that he has "tried to go against the template too much" and that this philosophy has not worked for him or Mark provides a cautionary tale. While differentiating one's team from the pack is valuable, doing so purely out of principle rather than evidence-based conviction leads to suboptimal outcomes. The lesson is not to blindly follow the crowd, but rather to understand why certain players are template picks and to make informed decisions about where deviation is justified.

3. Data-Driven Tools Deserve Integration

"I think the models and things like they need to be incorporated a little bit because they are so good at synthesizing that data," Az states directly. This represents an important admission from someone whose early success was built primarily on football knowledge and intuition. The modern FPL player—whether casual or competitive—benefits from incorporating analytical resources into their decision-making process. The best approach combines traditional scouting with data analysis rather than favoring one exclusively.

4. Tilt Decisions Compound Quickly

Az's account of the 2023/24 season illustrates how a single bad decision can lead to a cascade of poor choices. After falling to 200,000-250,000, he made a high-risk, high-reward decision to push for top 100,000, which backfired spectacularly. "I hope some of them would pay off and absolutely none of them paid off." This pattern—where frustration leads to increasingly reckless decisions—is common among FPL players. Recognizing when to accept a mediocre finish rather than gambling on a recovery is a crucial mental skill the game demands.

5. Content Creators Face Unique Pressures

The scrutiny Az faces as an "expert" on the FPL Show and Scout highlights the gap between content creation and personal performance. He emphasizes that he does this for passion, not as his primary income, and has no control over how organizations market him. For viewers who follow multiple FPL content creators, this provides context for why some analysts might struggle despite deep knowledge—the pressure of public accountability combined with the difficulty of consistently predicting an inherently variable sport creates challenges that no amount of experience fully eliminates.

Who This Is For

This video delivers the most value to several distinct audiences within the FPL community. Competitive players looking to refine their strategy will find Az's honest assessment of his mistakes particularly instructive, as it highlights common pitfalls that even experienced managers fall into. The discussion of balancing intuition with data-driven tools offers a framework for those who may have leaned too heavily in either direction.

Casual FPL participants who follow content creators and wonder about the reliability of "expert" advice will gain perspective on the pressures and limitations facing those who produce FPL content. Az's willingness to share his complete ranking history—including his worst finishes—provides a refreshingly honest look behind the curtain of FPL content creation.

Finally, fans of the FDL Blackbox channel specifically will appreciate the context Az provides about his journey with the team, including his relationship with Mark, Andy, and Luke, and his ongoing rivalry with Yelena. Understanding the personal stakes Az has in improving his performance adds emotional depth to future content from the channel.

For anyone building their draft ahead of the new season, Az's approach of carefully examining fixtures and reasoning through player selections offers a model for thoughtful team construction—though the broader lesson is that even thoughtful construction must be paired with adaptability and a willingness to incorporate new tools as the game continues to evolve.
