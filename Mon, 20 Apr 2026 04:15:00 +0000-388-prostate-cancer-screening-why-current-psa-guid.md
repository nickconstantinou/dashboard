Despite João Pedro's non-appearance, the bench boost remained productive: Van Dijk contributed an assist and clean sheet for 12 points, Onana managed a clean sheet for eight points, and Bruno Fernandes delivered six points from the bench. ## Tottenham Hotspur: The Contrarian Differential Opportunity

This is where Harry's analysis becomes particularly interesting. Tottenham represents what he calls "the outlier of a team that we don't really talk about and don't really invest in because of the way they're playing, but have quite good projections because of the way that Wolves are playing." He acknowledges the obvious concerns: Tottenham haven't won in 2026 and "look horrible," making it difficult to justify selecting any of their players based on recent form. However, the fixture at Wolves presents a mathematical opportunity that shouldn't be dismissed entirely.

Harry describes a clear split in FPL community thinking: one camp points to Tottenham fighting for something and showing improvement against Brighton, suggesting attacking players like James Maddison or Dominic Solanke could return. The other camp emphasizes the negative recent form and argues against any Tottenham investment. Harry positions himself "somewhere in the middle," acknowledging he doesn't expect to end up with a Tottenham player in his final draft, but he expresses genuine uncertainty. As the deadline approaches and managers finalize their free hit teams, he wonders whether having one Tottenham asset might be worthwhile despite the obvious risks.

The underlying logic is straightforward: when a team faces a weak opponent in poor form, even struggling teams can exceed expectations. Wolves have been conceding goals freely, which elevates Tottenham's attacking potential regardless of their own inconsistent performances. This represents the classic FPL tension between form and fixture, with Harry suggesting fixture advantage may deserve more weight than many managers are currently giving it.

Harry transitions to clean sheet odds, revealing Arsenal at 37% for their match against Newcastle, Liverpool at 35%, and Tottenham surprisingly at 34%. The Manchester United home fixture against Brentford presents a similar puzzle: many managers might target United defenders given the fixture, but Brentford's recent defensive improvement complicates the clean sheet equation. West Ham face Everton at home, but Everton are "playing quite well" recently, which Harry suggests makes this fixture less attractive than it initially appears. The broader takeaway is that gameweek 34 presents a difficult week for reliably predicting which defenders will deliver returns, a theme Harry emphasizes throughout his analysis.

## The Free Hit Dilemma: Predictability in an Unpredictable Gameweek

Harry's central conclusion is that gameweek 34 presents unusual difficulty in identifying reliably performing players. The data suggests expected goals and clean sheet probabilities across the league are relatively low, with no fixture standing out as obviously premium. This creates a strategic dilemma: managers on free hit need to take risks to differentiate their squads, but the risks themselves carry less certainty than usual.

He frames this as a fundamental challenge in FPL strategy: when the field's choices all carry significant variance, the difference between success and failure becomes less about skill and more about luck. His own approach involves maintaining the free hit for this gameweek while accepting that the optimal draft might look quite different from what the data suggests. The acknowledgment that "picking a good Arsenal attacker feels quite difficult to predict where the attacking returns are going to be" applies equally to most other options available.

For managers still deciding on their free hit approach, Harry's analysis suggests focusing on fixtures that offer both attacking potential and defensive vulnerability from opponents, while accepting that premium players in tough fixtures may underperform regardless of their quality. Tottenham against Wolves represents the most contrarian interpretation of this principle, but the data supporting their expected returns cannot be entirely dismissed.

## Key Strategic Takeaways

**Plan chip deployments many gameweeks in advance.** Harry's 132-point bench boost didn't happen by accident—it resulted from wildcarding in gameweek 32 specifically to optimize squad depth for gameweek 33. Managers who wait until the last moment to deploy chips sacrifice the planning advantages that separate elite finishes from average ones.

**Accept variance and focus on process over results.** João Pedro's failure to play minutes cost Harry significant points, yet he remains satisfied with his decisions because the planning was sound. Similarly, Sessegnon's unlucky near-assist demonstrates that individual moments of bad fortune shouldn't override systematic approaches to player selection.

**Fixture-based differentials deserve weight even for underperforming teams.** Tottenham represent a textbook case: their form has been poor, but Wolves' defensive struggles elevate their expected returns. The FPL community's collective avoidance of a team creates genuine differentiation opportunity for managers willing to zig when others zag.

**Cheap goalkeepers with difficult fixtures may outperform expensive options with easier ones.** David Raya offers a floor but limited ceiling, while alternative options might post 10-15 points if conditions align. Free hit chips, by definition, are one-week gambles—swinging for higher ceilings often makes more sense than settling for safe floors.

**Five weeks leaves room for significant rank movement.** Harry pushed from 40,000 to nearly 20,000 in a single gameweek, demonstrating that late-season chip deployment and smart transfer strategy can still yield dramatic improvements. Managers shouldn't assume their positions are locked, regardless of current standing.
