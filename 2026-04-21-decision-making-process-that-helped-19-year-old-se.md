# The Decision-Making Framework That Guided a 19-Year-Old to a $100 Million Exit

When most college students are worried about midterm exams and part-time jobs, Blake Anderson was negotiating a nine-figure acquisition of his company. At just 19 years old, the founder of the calorie-tracking app Calorie Counter+（卡路里计数器+）completed an exit that most entrepreneurs spend decades chasing. But what makes his story particularly instructive isn't the destination—it's the decision-making process that got him there.

In a revealing conversation on My First Million, Anderson pulled back the curtain on the frameworks and mental models that guided his journey from college student with a side project to founder of a company generating millions in monthly revenue. The conversation touched on everything from the mechanics of scaling paid advertising to the philosophical approach to education that shaped his thinking. What emerged was a masterclass in how young entrepreneurs can compete with experienced operators—not by mimicking their experience, but by adopting systematic approaches to decision-making that remove emotion from the equation.

---

## The Growth Engine: From Influencer Marketing to Performance Advertising

When Calorie Counter+ began gaining traction, Anderson and his team initially relied heavily on influencer marketing to drive growth. This approach proved incredibly effective early on, carrying the business to approximately $2 million in monthly revenue through strategic partnerships with content creators who organically discussed the app with their audiences.

However, the team eventually hit a plateau with this channel. The approach that had worked so well in the early stages began showing diminishing returns. That's when they made a critical pivot: investing heavily in performance advertising across Instagram, TikTok, and Facebook. Anderson describes this shift as the moment that truly "fueling most of the growth at this point."

The learning curve was steep. Running performance ads at scale requires understanding complex targeting, creative optimization, and attribution modeling. By January, the team was spending "a little over a million dollars" monthly on paid acquisition—and Anderson notes this spending was "pretty efficient." The sophistication of their ad operations became a genuine competitive moat, allowing them to acquire users profitably while competitors struggled with rising costs.

## The Mr. Beast Bet: Making a $500,000 Wager on Brand Building

Perhaps no decision illustrated Anderson's thinking better than the company's sponsorship deal with Mr. Beast. The team paid "$500,000 for him to put us in one of his videos." The execution was memorable: a contestant was locked in a gym until he lost a certain amount of weight, filmed over an extended period (Anderson estimates "16 months ago... that's how long they were planning this video").

Anderson was given the opportunity to appear in one of the takes, speaking about the app directly. He recalls flying out to witness the filming at Mr. Beast's studio, describing it as a "wild video" that "was one of their best performing videos that like in a long time."

The immediate financial returns were tricky to measure. Anderson acknowledges that attribution is difficult when "most people are going to the app store directly and downloading. Not many people are clicking a link so you don't know exactly how many people went and downloaded." However, using promo code usage and time-overlap analysis, the team estimates they generated "$350,000, maybe $400,000" from the $500,000 spend—making it immediately unprofitable.

But this analysis misses the real value of the deal. Anderson explains: "Having the brand name Mr. Beast saying we were in a video, we were able to do way more deals afterwards. We had way more credibility with our brand and so the profit from those deals that's enabled for us has exceeded the total amount." The brand halo effect transformed what looked like a losing bet into one of their most profitable marketing decisions. This demonstrates a crucial lesson: short-term ROI calculations often miss the asymmetric upside of brand-building investments.

## The Education Paradox: Why He Chose to Be Undeclared

While managing a company generating millions in revenue, Anderson maintained a college schedule—roughly four classes per week, each lasting about an hour and a half. His approach to education reveals a philosophy that would make Steve Jobs proud.

He initially entered business school but made what he calls "a key decision": switching to undeclared. The inspiration came directly from Jobs, who famously took a calligraphy class that had no apparent practical value—until it became fundamental to the typography of the original Macintosh operating system.

Anderson applied this thinking deliberately, taking a VR class focused on the metaverse and a design class alongside his business coursework. The business professor, he notes, was "actually a really awesome guy"—but the strategic value came from deliberately seeking classes outside his normal path, ones that "just spark my curiosity."

The interviewer captures the significance: "You're a well-spoken guy, you've obviously hustled, you built a cool app. Like all of that's great, but the decision-making is actually what stands out to me... the most impressive part about you." This observation cuts to the heart of what separates good operators from great ones: it's rarely about intelligence or even effort, but consistently making better decisions than your peers.

## The Mentorship That Changed Everything

Anderson credits much of his decision-making sophistication to Blake Anderson, his co-founder at Calorie Counter+. While sharing a name, Blake Anderson served as a guiding force across "so many things, not just business life, but personal life, too." The co-founder helped Anderson "shape a lot of decision-making frameworks" and taught him "how to analyze when to make certain decisions."

This mentorship became the foundation for Anderson's systematic approach to evaluating opportunities and risks. Rather than making choices based on gut feelings or momentary emotional states, he learned to quantify decisions in ways that revealed their true expected value.

## The Expected Value Framework: Making Decisions Without Emotion

The core of Anderson's decision-making system is the EV (Expected Value) framework. He explains it simply: "It's like the percent chance of winning something and then the prize, and you multiply them together." This isn't original thinking—gamblers have used expected value calculations for centuries—but applying it systematically to business decisions represents a meaningful edge.

The framework works by forcing you to consider three variables: the probability of success, the potential payoff, and the probability of total failure. Anderson walks through a concrete example: "Let's just pretend somebody offers you $100 for your business. So you have a 100% chance of the $100. And then you say, but how— if we keep going, we could get to 500? Okay, great. Then you ask the question: Well, what do you think are the chances you could get there? There's a 10% chance. Okay, so 10% times 500 gives you 50. So it's a better decision to take the 100 expected value over the 50."

But the real power comes when you factor in downside risk. "What's the percent chance it all falls apart, goes to zero, right? There's going to be If you keep running it, you have some risk of it falling apart. Even if you think that's a 10% chance, you have to factor that in." This addition transforms the calculation from purely optimistic to realistically calibrated.

Anderson applied this framework directly to evaluating acquisition offers. He describes "what do we think the EV is of what's the chance of us getting to this? What's the chance of it falling apart?" By quantifying these variables, he could make the sell-versus-build decision based on mathematics rather than emotion, pride, or the fear of leaving money on the table.

## Why Selling at 19 Was a No-Brainer

The interviewer makes a compelling case for why accepting the acquisition was clearly the right move: "The liquid money that you have is going to compound. I mean, to have what you have—I don't know what you have, but seven or eight figures, I don't know, at that young age, it's going to compound like crazy."

This gets at something fundamental about wealth building: compounding favors those with time on their side. A 19-year-old with liquid capital can take risks with investments that would be inappropriate for someone approaching retirement. They can weather market downturns, wait for opportunities, and let their money work for decades.

Anderson's EV framework would have captured this intuitively. The certain value of the acquisition offer, discounted by the probability of closing (perhaps 90%), could be compared against the expected value of continued operations—a calculation that necessarily includes the possibility of failure, market shifts, or competitive obsolescence. When viewed through this lens, the deal wasn't just acceptable—it was attractive.

## Key Insights: Applying These Principles

**1. Brand deals should be evaluated on total impact, not immediate ROI.** The Mr. Beast partnership looked unprofitable in isolation but created a halo effect that generated far more value through subsequent partnerships and credibility. When evaluating influencer or brand investments, track secondary metrics like partnership velocity and brand perception, not just direct conversion.

**2. Transitions between growth channels require deliberate investment.** Moving from influencer marketing to performance advertising wasn't just a budget reallocation—it required "a lot more time learning and scaling ads." Teams hitting plateaus should budget time and resources for the learning phase of new channels, not expect immediate results.

**3. Educational choices compound just like financial ones.** Anderson's decision to take calligraphy-style classes (VR, design) outside his declared major demonstrates strategic curiosity. These cross-disciplinary experiences often create the conceptual combinations that lead to breakthrough products.

**4. Expected value calculations remove emotional bias from major decisions.** By quantifying probabilities and payoffs, founders can evaluate buyout offers, investment opportunities, and strategic pivots objectively. The framework is especially powerful for founders prone to either greed (holding too long) or fear (selling too early).

**5. Mentorship accelerates the development of decision-making frameworks.** Anderson credits his co-founder with teaching him to "analyze when to make certain decisions" and introducing frameworks like EV. Seeking mentors who have navigated similar decisions provides advantages that compound over time.
