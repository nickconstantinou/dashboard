# The Decision-Making Framework Behind a 19-Year-Old's $100 Million Exit

When most nineteen-year-olds are worried about midterm exams and campus social life, this episode's guest was closing a nine-figure acquisition. The entrepreneur—who built an app that reached millions of users and eventually sold for $100 million—joined the My First Million podcast to reveal the tactical decisions, growth strategies, and mental frameworks that transformed a college student's side project into a massive business success. While his story might sound like overnight achievement, the conversation reveals a methodical approach to decision-making that separates exceptional founders from the crowd.

The discussion moves beyond surface-level success stories to explore the specific moment-to-moment choices that compound into transformative outcomes. From influencer marketing strategies that drove millions in monthly revenue to the counterintuitive wisdom of dropping a business major to study virtual reality, this conversation offers actionable frameworks for anyone building a business. The host's probing questions reveal layers of strategic thinking that most people never see in successful entrepreneurs—the actual calculation methods, the mentorship relationships that shaped perspective, and the mathematical rigor applied to decisions that feel purely intuitive.

## The Growth Engine: From Influencer Marketing to Performance Ads

The foundation of this company's success wasn't a viral moment or lucky break—it was a deliberate, scalable marketing system. The founder explains that influencer marketing served as the initial growth engine that carried the business to approximately $2 million in monthly revenue. The strategy involved messaging countless potential influencers, recruiting them to create organic, native content about the app, and leveraging their audiences to drive awareness and downloads. This approach worked exceptionally well in the early stages, providing the fuel needed to gain initial traction in a crowded market.

However, the founder acknowledges that every growth strategy has its ceiling. After reaching that $2 million monthly milestone, the team experienced a plateau—a common phenomenon for businesses that over-index on any single channel. The breakthrough came when they invested heavily in learning and scaling performance advertising across Instagram, TikTok, and Facebook. By January, their monthly ad spend had scaled to just over $1 million, and this channel had become the primary driver of their continued growth. The efficiency of this spend became a competitive advantage, as the team developed sophisticated expertise in converting paid traffic into loyal users.

## Inside the $500,000 Mr. Beast Deal: Short-Term Math vs. Long-Term Value

One of the most revealing parts of the conversation centers on the company's major sponsorship with YouTube megastar Mr. Beast. The founder paid $500,000 for a placement in a video featuring someone who had been locked in a gym for 100 days, challenged to lose a significant amount of weight. The founder was given the opportunity to appear in the video and speak directly to the camera about the app—a rare moment of brand integration in one of YouTube's highest-performing channels.

The immediate financial returns were mixed. Tracking attribution for influencer campaigns is notoriously difficult because most users download apps directly from the app store rather than clicking tracked links. By analyzing time overlap, promo code usage, and other proxy metrics, the team estimated they generated approximately $350,000 to $400,000 in direct revenue from the sponsorship. On the surface, this meant the deal was unprofitable by $100,000 to $150,000 on immediate returns alone.

But the founder emphasizes that this analysis misses the true value of the deal. Having Mr. Beast's endorsement in a video created a cascade of subsequent opportunities. The credibility boost enabled the team to negotiate far more favorable partnership deals with other influencers and brands. The total profit from these downstream deals, the founder believes, has already exceeded the $500,000 investment, and this effect continues to compound. This represents a crucial distinction in business decision-making: the difference between measuring immediate ROI and calculating long-term strategic value. The Mr. Beast deal wasn't a marketing expense—it was an investment in brand equity that paid dividends far beyond what any attribution model could capture.

## Building a Team and Balancing College

The operational scale of the business required significant team investment. At the time of the conversation, the company had grown to approximately 30 employees—a substantial organization for an entrepreneur who was simultaneously managing coursework. The founder reveals that during the first semester, he carried four classes with schedules that weren't particularly demanding, typically totaling around an hour and a half of class time per week.

What makes this balance remarkable isn't just the time management—it was the deliberate approach to education itself. Rather than pursuing a conventional business school path, the founder switched from the business school to an undeclared major. His reasoning draws directly from Steve Jobs' famous Stanford commencement address, where Jobs described how taking a calligraphy class ultimately enabled him to build the elegant typography of the original Macintosh operating system. By staying undecided, the founder preserved freedom to take courses purely based on intellectual curiosity: a virtual reality class focused on the metaverse, a design course, and a business class with an instructor he found particularly compelling.

The host observes that this approach reflects a broader pattern in the founder's decision-making—a willingness to optimize for interesting experiences and long-term capability building rather than immediate practical utility. Rather than treating college as a credentialing exercise, the founder approached it as another environment for learning and growth, even while running a multi-million dollar company.

## The Expected Value Framework: Making Better Decisions Under Uncertainty

The most intellectually substantial portion of the conversation focuses on the decision-making frameworks that have guided the founder's choices. The primary mentor in this process was Blake Anderson, the co-founder of Cal-I, who taught him how to analyze decisions through the lens of expected value (EV)—a concept borrowed from gambling that quantifies the probable value of different outcomes.

The mathematical logic is straightforward but powerful. Expected value equals the probability of an outcome multiplied by its value. If you have a 100% chance of receiving $100, your expected value is $100. If you have a 10% chance of receiving $500, your expected value is only $50. According to pure EV logic, the first option is superior. This framework provides an objective mathematical structure for comparing decisions that would otherwise be made on pure emotion, mood, or the random factors of any given day.

The founder explains how this framework proved crucial when evaluating acquisition offers for the company. Rather than negotiating purely on instinct or anchoring to ego-driven valuations, he learned to systematically analyze different scenarios. What are the chances the company reaches a certain valuation if they continue building? What are the chances something goes wrong and the business falls apart? By assigning probabilities to different outcomes and calculating their respective values, the founder developed a disciplined approach to determining when selling made more sense than continuing.

The host illustrates this with a practical example. Suppose someone offers $100 for your business, with a 90% chance the deal closes (accounting for some deal risk). Your expected value is roughly $90. But what if you think continuing could eventually yield $500? If you estimate a 10% chance of reaching that outcome, your expected value is $50—making the $100 offer superior. Even if you think there's a 20% chance of reaching $500, you must also factor in the probability that the business fails entirely, potentially going to zero. Once you factor in all risks and probabilities, the mathematically optimal decision often becomes clearer than it appears when viewed through the fog of emotion and short-term thinking.

This framework doesn't eliminate uncertainty—it acknowledges that uncertainty is inevitable while providing a structure for making decisions despite it. The founder learned to stop asking "what do I feel like doing?" and start asking "what does the math suggest is optimal given what I know?"

## Key Insights: What Every Founder Can Apply

**The Long-Term Value of Brand Partnerships Defies Attribution Modeling.** When evaluating high-profile partnerships, founders must resist the temptation to measure success purely on immediate, trackable returns. A $500,000 sponsorship that generates $400,000 in short-term revenue looks like a failure by conventional metrics. But if that sponsorship creates compounding credibility that unlocks dozens of subsequent deals worth millions in aggregate, the real return is orders of magnitude higher. Founders need frameworks for evaluating brand equity effects that don't rely solely on last-click attribution.

**Expected Value Analysis Transforms High-Stakes Decisions.** Whether evaluating an acquisition offer, a major investment, or a strategic pivot, applying EV thinking provides clarity that intuition cannot match. The process requires honest probability estimates—which most people are terrible at providing—but even rough calculations reveal whether a decision has positive or negative expected value. The key insight is that EV doesn't require certainty; it requires disciplined probability assignment and basic multiplication.

**Deliberately Choosing Curiosity Over Convention Creates Unexpected Advantages.** While peers optimize for credentials and immediate career utility, the founder deliberately chose to remain undecided in college, taking courses in virtual reality, design, and other subjects purely because they seemed interesting. This counterintuitive approach echoes the history of innovation itself—Apple's typography excellence derived from a calligraphy class that seemed impractical at the time. Founders who protect space for curiosity often find that the most unexpected knowledge becomes the most valuable.

**Every Growth Strategy Has a Ceiling—Plan the Transition Early.** The company's influencer marketing strategy worked brilliantly until it didn't, creating a plateau that required a completely different capability to overcome. Successful founders understand that their current growth engine will eventually run out of steam and begin building the teams and skills needed for the next phase before they hit the wall.
