    Introduction

    In the crowded supplement market, where most brands default to clinical jargon and stock photography, Grüns has carved out a distinctive position through marketing that treats consumers as friends rather than patients. A recent conversation featuring the founder revealed the operational playbook behind one of direct-to-consumer's most talked-about brands—a company generating hundreds of monthly ad variants, running multiple funnel paths simultaneously, and building retention sequences that evolve customers over months and years.

    The discussion originated from examining Grüns' Facebook Ad Library presence, where the brand's unconventional creative approach immediately stood out. Rather than the formulaic "clinically proven" messaging dominating the supplement space, Grüns deploys humor, cultural awareness, and limited-edition drops to create genuine engagement. The founder walked through specific ad examples, explained the testing philosophy driving their output, and revealed the team structure enabling this execution—offering a rare look inside a billion-dollar D2C operation that's been widely imitated but rarely understood.

    What makes this conversation valuable isn't just the tactics themselves, but the reasoning behind them: why test multiple angles rather than converging on winners, how to build funnels that serve specific customer intents, and where most brands go wrong by treating marketing as a creative exercise rather than a systematic discovery process.

    The Art of the Angle: Why Grüns Tests Everything

    Understanding Ad Angles as Audience Segments

    The conversation began with a deep dive into Grüns' Facebook Ad Library, where the founder immediately highlighted their unconventional brand voice. One ad simply reads "Poop More"—a provocative hook that seems almost designed to make supplement industry veterans wince. But this isn't chaos; it's strategy.

    The founder explained that while competitors obsess over clinical differentiation, Grüns deliberately takes a different path. "We just don't talk about it because that's not fun," he noted, acknowledging they do conduct rigorous testing but refusing to lead with it. The brand voice is intentionally playful because it creates connection: a prospect scrolling through feeds sees the ad and "probably laughs a little bit." That moment of delight creates immediate brand recall that clinical claims rarely achieve.

    What followed in the ad library revealed the depth of their testing operation. The brand runs multiple distinct angles simultaneously—ads positioning Grüns as "GLP-1's best friend" (surfing the massive cultural momentum around weight management drugs), ads featuring "Nurse Jackie" discussing nutrient gaps, and the irreverent "Poop More" creative. Each angle targets a fundamentally different customer mindset. A person concerned about digestion responds to different messaging than someone taking Ozempic and worried about nutritional gaps, and someone interested in general wellness might connect with humor that bores the clinically-focused customer.

    The Multi-Angle Funnel Architecture

    Critically, Grüns doesn't use these angles purely for testing purposes—to find one winner and kill the rest. Instead, each angle leads to its own tailored landing page. The founder confirmed they run "five, six different angles each to a tailored landing page that continues that message."

    This represents a fundamental departure from typical D2C wisdom, which often emphasizes convergence: test quickly, find winners, concentrate spend. Grüns' approach recognizes that different mindsets exist as persistent market segments, not temporary testing phases. A customer interested in GLP-1 support isn't a "test group" to be eliminated—it's an ongoing audience that requires its own messaging framework.

    The landing pages don't simply repeat ad copy; they extend the conversation. Someone arriving from a gut health angle lands on a page dialed specifically for gut health concerns. That page's popup then asks questions to further refine understanding. If the visitor indicates interest in fiber, the subsequent email and SMS sequences deliver content around fiber and digestive wellness. The entire journey—from first impression through retention—carries thematic consistency.

    Surfing Cultural Momentum Ethically

    The GLP-1 angle received particular attention. The advertising landscape around drugs like Ozempic and Zepbound exploded in 2023-2024, with supplement brands scrambling to capture share of the conversation. Many crossed ethical lines, implying their products could replicate drug effects—a claim that invited regulatory scrutiny and consumer backlash.

    Grüns took a different approach: positioning as "the best friend to tepatide, zeppbound, whatever the sort of GP ones are." They're explicit that they "never took that approach" of implying equivalence. Instead, they recognized that patients on these medications face real nutritional challenges—appetite suppression, digestive side effects, potential nutrient deficiencies—and positioned Grüns as support for those challenges.

    This "best friend" framing accomplishes several strategic goals. It captures attention from an enormous audience researching and using these drugs. It creates topical relevance without regulatory exposure. It positions Grüns as helpful rather than competitive. And it reaches customers at a moment of high health awareness, when they're actively thinking about what they're putting into their bodies.

    The founder noted he'd seen competitors making false claims about nature and these drugs, calling such approaches "objectively wrong." Grüns' approach demonstrates that cultural momentum can be surfed without exploiting it—brands can participate in trending conversations while maintaining scientific integrity.

    The Funnel Deep Dive: From Landing Page to Lifetime Customer

    Building Complete Funnels Around Winning Angles

    The founder outlined his testing philosophy with clarity that separates operational wisdom from theory. The key principle: once an angle shows promise, the entire funnel gets built around it.

    "Don't say that you like are going to make somebody's sex better if your product doesn't actually do that like actually do the science validation if your product can help with certain things," he advised. This isn't a call to hide science—it's a call to ensure claims are defensible before building campaigns around them. The sequence matters: validate your product's capabilities, then build marketing that accurately represents those capabilities.

    When an angle passes validation, every creative type gets deployed toward it. Static ads run at the angle. User-generated content (UGC) style "hoist the camera" ads get produced for it. Cinematic production pieces target it. The team pours "all sorts of ad structures to that angle" rather than spreading thin across dozens of underfunded concepts.

    Personalization Through the Customer Journey

    The popup system demonstrates Grüns' commitment to granular personalization. When a visitor lands from a gut health ad, the popup doesn't show a generic offer—it asks questions to understand their specific gut health concerns. That data informs everything downstream.

    "Once you've identified an angle that seems to be running, then you build the entire funnel around it," the founder explained. "So you've got you've got static ads that talk to that angle. You've got UGC sort of hoist the camera angle ads that run at that. You've got um like more like cinematic type shoots that run at that angle. everything."

    This approach requires more infrastructure than simple A/B testing, but it captures value that convergence-style testing leaves behind. When Grüns serves a popup to someone who entered via gut health, they're not competing against their own message—they're deepening a connection that the ad created.

    Long-Term Retention and Expansion

    The most sophisticated part of Grüns' system addresses what happens after purchase. Most D2C marketing focuses obsessively on acquisition—the metrics are cleaner, the feedback loops are shorter, the pressure is more immediate. But the founder revealed that retention strategy is where the team is "trying to put into process now."

    The challenge: customers enter through specific angles, but products often serve broader needs. Someone who purchased for gut health might experience energy benefits they didn't expect. They might read about GLP-1 support and wonder if Grüns could help there too. The retention strategy focuses on "how do we talk to Sam long term" and "how would he how do we get Sam to understand that there's so much more to this product than just gut health."

    This represents a shift from treating customers as single-purchase transactions to viewing them as evolving relationships. The foundation is their entry angle, but the building continues over time, revealing new product dimensions as customers become ready to receive them.

    The Fun Flavor Strategy: Drops, Delight, and Defensive Positioning

    Limited Edition as Marketing Engine

    The conversation turned to Grüns' limited-time offer drops—a strategy that seems almost counterintuitive for a daily-use supplement. The team releases new flavors every month or two: Grüns' original formula, then seasonal specials like "Lollipop" (strawberry vanilla), "Granny Smith" apple, and most notably, "Grinch Punch" released in November.

    The founder challenged listeners to "tell me one other supplement brand that's doing like drops in and making it actually fun to take the product each day." This isn't a rhetorical question—it's an observation that the supplement industry has resigned itself to being boring. If everyone else focuses on clinical messaging, creating genuinely delightful product experiences becomes a defensible position.

    Why does this work? Limited editions create urgency. When a customer knows the Lollipop flavor is only available for a few weeks, they order more frequently and in larger quantities. The product becomes collectible, shareable, giftable in ways that a static monthly subscription never could be.

    Making Compliance Enjoyable

    There's a deeper psychology at work: supplements face an adherence problem. Customers buy products, use them inconsistently, and cancel subscriptions. Making the product itself enjoyable—anticipating a new flavor, sharing the experience on social media, discussing limited drops with friends—transforms a chore into a ritual.

    The founder noted the GLP-1 angle included a Lollipop image in the ad itself, creating visual consistency between marketing and product experience. When customers receive their Lollipop-flavored Grüns, the marketing isn't misleading or abstract—it's a real sensory promise that got delivered. This kind of alignment builds trust over time.

    Production Power: Scaling High-Volume Testing with a Small Team

    The 30-Person Output Machine

    One of the conversation's most practically valuable revelations concerned output volume. Grüns runs "hundreds of ads a month, cycling in and out," attempting to find "that next unlock." This comes from a team of approximately 30 people—remarkably lean for the scale of output.

    The founder broke down how this works: "It's not just about ad volume. It's actually structured around like how much of that is like business as usual ads that we already know or structure or sorry concepts themes that we know already work and how much of that is like we're trying to find new frontier um of applicability."

    The framework divides effort into two buckets. Most resources flow toward "business as usual"—scaling concepts that have proven themselves, producing variations on winning angles, maintaining the stable core of working advertising. A smaller portion goes to "new frontier" exploration—testing genuinely new angles, trying different creative approaches, looking for the next breakthrough concept.

    This structure prevents chaos while maintaining innovation. Pure exploration means most experiments fail. Pure optimization means eventual decay as markets shift. Grüns' approach balances both, with clear roles for scaling proven winners and discovering new opportunities.

    The Origin Story: Building Internally

    The founder shared his origin story, which has significant implications for how brands should think about creative development. When Sean—presumably the interviewer—reached out in December 2023, the landing page was already live. "That was me right like I was the guy that built that landing page," the founder recalled. "We didn't use an agency."

    This matters because of what followed: the buy box format that Grüns developed has been "ripped off" across the industry for the past three years. Brands copied the visual design, the layout, the pricing presentation. If the founder had outsourced creative development, the company would have no proprietary advantage in that space—or at minimum, would have been delayed while agency relationships were established.

    "We didn't use an agency," he emphasized, noting he "even said that in the message cuz you asked." The ownership of creative development enabled speed, iteration, and proprietary discovery. When you build internally, every insight becomes institutional knowledge. When you outsource, insights walk out the door with the agency team.

    The founder also revealed he didn't consider himself "an ecom guy who builds these sites" broadly across D2C—"I'm not an ecom guy who builds these sites." The site worked anyway because the team understood their specific customers deeply. This suggests that generalist agencies often lack the context-specific insight that founders bring naturally. The best creative often comes from people who know exactly who they're speaking to.

    Detailed Takeaways

    Test multiple angles to serve different mindsets, not to find single winners. Most testing frameworks treat multiple variants as a phase—the goal is to kill underperformers and concentrate on winners. Grüns' approach treats different angles as serving different persistent customer segments. Someone interested in GLP-1 support and someone focused on general gut health are both permanent market segments requiring ongoing, tailored messaging. Killing the GLP-1 angle because gut health tests better means abandoning an audience, not optimizing. Apply this by mapping your customer segments and ensuring each gets dedicated creative and funnel development, not just ad testing.
