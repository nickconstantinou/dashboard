My FPL GW32 First Thoughts! Wildcard Draft & FA Cup Impact | Fantasy Premier League Tips 2025/26

Introduction

The 2025/26 Fantasy Premier League season is approaching its critical final stretch, and managers face one of the most consequential decisions of the campaign: when to deploy their wildcard. In this video, FP Harry—renowned FPL content creator and analyst—breaks down his thought process around the much-discussed Gameweek 32 wildcard, explaining why he believes the next three weeks represent the most important period where managers should do absolutely nothing with their teams. With the international break immediately followed by a crucial FA Cup weekend, the outcomes of those cup ties will fundamentally reshape the fixture landscape for the remainder of the season, determining which teams receive double gameweeks and which face blank weeks. Harry discusses his current team situation, his ranking progress from 167k to 36k, and whether he genuinely needs to use his wildcard at all, while also examining how potential FA Cup upsets could completely alter strategic planning for the final seven weeks of the season.

The timing of this video is particularly significant because Gameweek 32 itself remains three weeks away—managers have an extended period to plan, but that planning must account for enormous uncertainty. Harry emphasises that the FA Cup results, particularly from ties involving Manchester City, Liverpool, West Ham, Leeds, Chelsea, and Arsenal, will dictate whether teams get doubles in Gameweek 33 or find themselves with blank fixtures later in the season. This uncertainty, Harry argues, makes wildcard planning impossible until those cup results are known. The video serves as both a personal team review and a broader strategy guide for the FPL community, offering insights into how professional managers approach these high-stakes decisions.

The Three-Week Wait: Why FPL Managers Should Do Nothing

FP Harry opens by establishing the timeline that makes this period so unique: Gameweek 32 is not arriving for another three weeks. The immediate calendar features an international break, followed by an FA Cup weekend, with the actual FPL deadline coming the week after that. This creates what Harry describes as "probably the most important two weeks where you don't do anything with your FPL team." The reasoning is straightforward but crucial: making transfers now means committing to a team structure without knowing which fixtures will actually exist in the coming weeks.

Harry's core advice is stark but clear: even managers who have already decided they will wildcard in Gameweek 32 should not make transfers during this period. He suggests deleting the app, logging out, and returning once the FA Cup results are known. The reason is simple—if Chelsea or Arsenal were to be knocked out of the FA Cup by lower-tier opposition, their fixture trajectories would change dramatically, potentially eliminating the need for a wildcard entirely or shifting the optimal strategy completely. Making transfers now based on incomplete information risks creating a team that becomes obsolete the moment the FA Cup results emerge.

This advice extends to all transfers, not just wildcard-related ones. Harry himself has accumulated five free transfers by rolling them over across recent gameweeks, and he acknowledges that even with transfers available, the smart move is to preserve them until the fixture landscape becomes clear. The FA Cup weekend in approximately two weeks time represents "the biggest non-FPL weekend left this season that will decide the doubles and blanks later on in the season," making it the pivotal moment around which all strategy must centre.

FP Harry's Current Team Analysis

Turning to his own squad, Harry provides a detailed assessment of where he stands entering this planning period. Having just completed Gameweek 31—a blank gameweek where several teams did not play—Harry scored 65 points, achieving another green arrow. This continues a remarkable run of four consecutive green arrows, propelling his rank from 167k, where he felt he was stagnating, up to 36k after the latest movement. He notes that he is now five points above his safety score, a position that allows him to push for a higher finish rather than merely preserving his standing.

Harry's team featured eleven players who actually played in Gameweek 31, which proved crucial given the blank nature of the gameweek. His only doubt heading into the weekend was Taroski, who ultimately "ended up being the hero of my gameweek"—though Harry, as a Chelsea fan, admits mixed feelings about his performance given Chelsea's difficult weekend. Bruno Fernandez captained the team and delivered a goal plus an assist, while Harry Wilson served as vice-captain and also scored. Defensively, Van Dyke and Anderson both returned positive scores that outperformed many alternatives, contributing to the overall green arrow.

Looking at his current asset composition, Harry highlights several key players: a double asset in defence playing at home against Bournemouth, Taroski away at Brentford, Van Dijk, Harry Wilson or possibly J Burger depending on fixture preferences, and Bruno Fernandes alongside Brian and Burma. However, Harry is "not particularly happy" with Burma as a player and acknowledges this is one position he might look to upgrade. He also notes that Echatikk suffered only a dead leg and is expected back for Gameweek 32, providing a boost to his squad.

Financially, Harry has only £0.1 million in the bank but possesses five free transfers, a wildcard, a free hit, and a bench boost remaining. This chip inventory is significant, particularly the bench boost, which Harry identifies as "the most powerful chip that I've probably got left" and something he wants to utilise in Gameweek 33.

The Wildcard Dilemma: To Use or Not to Use?

This is where Harry's analysis becomes particularly nuanced. He has been "pretty clear over the past couple of weeks that I'm definitely not locking in a wild card in Gameweek 32"—meaning he has not committed to using it and may not use it at all. His reasoning stems from looking at his current team and concluding there is "no reason to wild card this team." The squad, in his assessment, looks pretty good as a base, though there are definite areas he would look to improve.

The improvements Harry mentions include potentially upgrading one of his midfielders—likely J Burger Hall—to a better option, considering upgrading the defensive position held by Taroski who faces an away fixture at Brentford, and addressing the situation with Erling Haaland, who "hasn't been particularly great recently." However, none of these potential upgrades represent a burning need that demands a wildcard.

What makes the decision more complex is the bench boost opportunity in Gameweek 33. Harry explains that many of the teams managers would look to wildcard into for Gameweek 32—Chelsea, Manchester City, Arsenal, Newcastle—"do have a pretty good run of fixtures towards the end of the season as well." This means that a wildcard could serve not only for the bench boost but for the remainder of the campaign. However, he honestly acknowledges that using the wildcard would likely "make my team worse in Gameweek 32" compared to his current arrangement.

This creates a genuine strategic tension: the wildcard could set up the bench boost in Gameweek 33 while providing better long-term fixtures, but it would sacrifice immediate strength. Harry's current thinking is to keep his options open and make a final decision after the FA Cup results clarify the fixture situation.

FA Cup Implications and Fixture Uncertainty

The FA Cup weekend represents the hinge point for all this planning, and Harry dedicates significant attention to explaining why the cup results matter so much for FPL strategy. The first consideration is straightforward: which teams advance and which get eliminated. Chelsea face Port Vale at home and Arsenal travel to Southampton—Harry suggests most managers would predict wins for both Premier League sides, but notes that "crazier things have happened in football" and upsets remain possible.

The more complex consideration involves fixture movement. Even if the "big" teams win their FA Cup ties, the timing of their rearranged league fixtures remains uncertain. The Gameweek 34 fixtures that will be displaced by the FA Cup semi-finals can go in two places: the midweek before (creating a Gameweek 33 double) or the midweek after (remaining in Gameweek 34 as a single fixture).

Harry explains that this is not an exact science. Teams like Chelsea, Manchester City, and West Ham are all out of European competition, meaning they do not have European midweek obligations that typically force fixtures into particular slots. So even if Chelsea beat Port Vale, their displaced fixture could move into the midweek after Gameweek 34 rather than creating a double in Gameweek 33—meaning managers wildcarding to Chelsea assets expecting a Gameweek 33 double might be disappointed.

The critical insight is that the Premier League will need to announce where these fixtures move, and that announcement should come "by Gameweek 32, hopefully sooner rather than later." Until those announcements are made, wildcard planning remains based on assumptions rather than certainty. This is why Harry emphasises that the wildcard decision cannot be finalised until the FA Cup results are known and the fixture announcements follow.

Strategic Recommendations

Harry's advice for managers considering their wildcard strategy centres on patience and information gathering. The FA Cup results will reveal which teams might have doubles in Gameweek 33, which will be blank in later gameweeks, and what the overall fixture landscape looks like. If there are cup upsets—Manchester City or Liverpool being knocked out by lower-tier opposition, for example—the entire strategic picture changes.

For managers planning to wildcard in Gameweek 32, Harry's recommendation is explicit: do not make any transfers in the meantime, do not commit to any team structure, and wait for the information to become available. The temptation to use those five free transfers might be strong, but using them now rather than after the FA Cup results would be "making your team worse" in retrospect if the fixture picture changes significantly.

The bench boost chip emerges as particularly important in Harry's analysis. Gameweek 33, if it features doubles for the right teams, could be enormously powerful, and the bench boost allows managers to start eleven players from teams with double fixtures. This makes the wildcard decision about more than just Gameweek 32—it is about setting up the most impactful bench boost possible while also improving the team for the season's final seven weeks.

Detailed Takeaways

No other date on the calendar has this level of strategic consequence.

Managers should not make transfers even if they plan to wildcard. The uncertainty created by the FA Cup means any transfer made now could become obsolete within two weeks. Harry's advice to "delete the app, log yourself out, come back once the FA Cup is out of the way" is deliberately dramatic but reflects genuine strategic necessity. Making transfers now locks you into assumptions that might prove completely wrong.

Gameweek 32 is three weeks away, making this the longest gap between deadlines. The international break, FA Cup weekend, and then the actual Gameweek 32 deadline create a three-week planning period where the wise move is to plan but not act. This extended window is unusual and creates both opportunity and risk for managers.

Harry's personal wildcard decision remains open. He has five free transfers, a wildcard, and a bench boost remaining, and his current team is performing well enough that he does not "definitely need to wild card." However, he is considering using the wildcard to set up the bench boost in Gameweek 33 and to access teams with favourable end-of-season runs like Chelsea, Manchester City, Arsenal, and Newcastle.

The fixture movement from FA Cup is not guaranteed even for winning teams. Because Chelsea, Manchester City, West Ham, and others are not in European competition, their displaced fixtures from Gameweek 34 might move to the midweek after rather than creating a double in Gameweek 33. This adds another layer of uncertainty that makes premature wildcard planning risky.

Harry's rank progression demonstrates the value of patience. Moving from 167k to 36k across four consecutive green arrows, including a 65-point return in a blank gameweek, shows that a measured approach focusing on players who actually play can succeed even when the fixture schedule creates challenges.
