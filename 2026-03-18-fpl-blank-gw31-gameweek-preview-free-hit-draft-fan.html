FPL Blank Gameweek 31 Preview: Strategic Guide for Fantasy Managers

Introduction: Navigating the Most Complex Blank Gameweek of the Season

Fantasy Premier League managers find themselves at a critical juncture in the 2025/26 season as blank gameweek 31 arrives with unprecedented uncertainty. This isn't your typical blank gameweek where the fixtures are clear and the strategy is straightforward. Instead, the upcoming international break, FA Cup fixtures, and European competition results happening midweek create a perfect storm of unpredictability that could dramatically reshape the entire FPL landscape for the remaining weeks of the season.

FP Harry, the experienced FPL content creator behind this preview, begins by addressing the elephant in the room: the European fixtures taking place this midweek could have what he describes as a "massive impact on the rest of the season." The reason for this uncertainty stems from the unusual scheduling situation between gameweek 31 and gameweek 32. There is a three-week gap between these gameweeks, incorporating an international break, FA Cup fixtures, and a midweek round of European matches before the gameweek 32 deadline. This extended break creates both opportunities and complications for FPL managers trying to plan their squads.

The core challenge facing managers this week revolves around how aggressive to be with their transfers. Should you use your transfers to maximize points in gameweek 31, or should you preserve them for when more information becomes available about blank and double gameweeks? The answer, as Harry explains throughout this comprehensive preview, depends heavily on whether Manchester City and Crystal Palace both exit their respective European competitions this week, potentially scheduling their postponed fixture in gameweek 31 as late as Tuesday, April 7th.

Understanding the European Fixture Implications

The relationship between European competition results and FPL blank gameweeks is typically straightforward: teams still competing in Europe will have their fixtures rearranged around those midweek matches, creating the blank and double gameweeks that define the run-in. However, this week's European results could completely upend the conventional wisdom about where those doubles will land.

Harry explains that normally, predicting doubles and blanks is "quite simple" because all the teams predicted to double are the ones still in Europe. The usual suspects include Manchester City, Crystal Palace, Liverpool (who were facing Galatasaray), Chelsea, Arsenal, and West Ham and Leeds. Most of these teams are currently still competing in European competitions, which means their postponed fixtures would naturally be scheduled in the free midweeks when there are no European matches.

The problem is that as many as four of these teams could be eliminated from Europe this midweek. Liverpool were losing to Galatasaray in their Champions League tie. Chelsea looked likely to exit their competition. Crystal Palace could go out. Manchester City could potentially be eliminated. Only Arsenal were expected to progress against Bayern Leverkusen, and even they "could go out" according to Harry.

This creates a massive shift in the blank and double gameweek landscape. The two free midweeks without European fixtures are currently identified as gameweek 33 and gameweek 36. These were expected to be the primary landing spots for the doubles because they wouldn't conflict with Champions League or Europa League scheduling. However, if multiple teams crash out of Europe, those free midweeks suddenly become less predictable. Harry emphasizes that "the free midweeks go massively up" in value because the rearranged fixtures "could start to go into 32, 33, they could stay in the midweek of gameweek 34, it could go into gameweek 35 or gameweek 36, in theory could go into gameweek 37."

This is precisely why Harry urges managers to "please hold your transfers as much as possible" heading into this week. The fixture computer hasn't been fed the results of this week's European matches yet, and those results will fundamentally alter the strategic landscape for the remaining weeks of the season.

The Critical Importance of Fielding 11 Players in Gameweek 31

One of the clearest strategic directives from this preview is the emphasis on ensuring you have a full 11 players available for gameweek 31. Harry describes this as "quite important," noting that "the majority of people in this game are going to have 11 players this week" and that "every single team that I'm being sent that I'm looking at has a minimum of nine or 10 already with a couple of transfers available."

For managers who have already played their wildcard, the imperative to field 11 players becomes even stronger. Harry acknowledges that those who have used their wildcard should still aim to maximize their squad this week, but he notes that the majority of managers who still have their wildcard available will be using it in gameweek 32 instead.

The math behind this recommendation is compelling. Even if you have to take a -4 point hit to bring in an additional player to reach 11 starters, Harry explains that this is effectively only a -2 hit in practice. The reasoning is straightforward: that additional player will earn appearance points regardless of their performance, typically two points for a substitute appearance. So when you subtract those guaranteed appearance points from the -4 transfer cost, you're really only paying -2 net points compared to leaving the spot empty.

Harry identifies several attractive fixtures to target: "Fulham at home to Burnley, like Manchester United against Bournemouth, like Newcastle at home to Sunderland." These three teams represent the best offensive matchups of the blank gameweek, and managers should prioritize ensuring they have maximum exposure to these favorable fixtures.

Strategic Transfer Decisions: When to Upgrade and When to Hold

The question of whether to use transfers to improve your squad beyond simply reaching 11 players is where Harry draws a clear line. He distinguishes between two types of transfers: those that add an additional starter to your lineup (which he recommends) versus those that merely upgrade one player for another (which he generally discourages this week).

Using Harry's own example, a manager might have players like Taroski and Jburrough Hall in their team and want to upgrade them to players with better fixtures. However, he advises that "I don't think that transfer is worth it apart from two players which is Bruno Fernandes way at Bournemouth or Harry Wilson at home to Burnley." Even for these two specific players, he "wouldn't do either of them for a hit" unless the transfer could be made using free funds rather than taking a point deduction.

The reasoning behind this caution is multifaceted. First, there simply isn't much value in the fixture list this week. When Harry shares the projection data, the numbers reveal a relatively Goals-heavy gameweek: Fulham are predicted to score 1.83 goals at home to Burnley, which is actually the highest of any team this week, yet "no team is predicted to score two goals, which is what we often use as the benchmark of teams to target." Newcastle are second with 1.76 expected goals, Manchester United third with 1.72. These are modest projections that don't scream "must-have player."

Second, and more importantly, the information advantage available in the coming weeks is far more valuable than squeezing out a few extra points in gameweek 31. The three-week gap before gameweek 32 will see the FA Cup round complete and all European eliminations determined. By the time the gameweek 32 deadline arrives, "the doubles and blanks should be very clear." This means using transfers now to chase marginal gains could leave you with a worse squad when the big picture becomes visible.

Harry's recommendation is to "just be playing whoever it is that you've got that has a fixture and see how many points they can get and go again in gameweek 32." Unless you're specifically buying players to increase your total fixture count or to acquire one of the two standout picks (Harry Wilson or Bruno Fernandes), the transfers are better saved for when you have more information.

Projection Data Deep Dive: Identifying Value in a Low-Scoring Gameweek

The projection data shared in this video, compiled by Rob TFPL (@TFPL on Twitter), provides quantitative backing for Harry's strategic recommendations. Understanding these numbers helps managers prioritize their limited resources.

On the offensive side, the expected goals data shows a relatively anemic week for scoring. Fulham's 1.83 expected goals at home to Burnley leads the way, followed by Newcastle's 1.76 at home to Sunderland and Manchester United's 1.72 away at Bournemouth. Notably, no team in the entire fixture list is projected to score two goals, which Harry identifies as the typical benchmark for considering players "must-haves" in a given gameweek.

The clean sheet projections tell a similar story of limited opportunity. Fulham lead the way with a 42% clean sheet probability at home to Burnley, while Newcastle are second at 39%. However, "outside of that, no team gets close to it. They're about 30% and below for every other team." This means the clean sheet upside is heavily concentrated in just two teams, making it relatively straightforward to prioritize defenders from those clubs while deprioritizing goalkeepers and defenders from elsewhere.

This data reinforces Harry's overall assessment: there simply isn't enough predicted scoring volume across the gameweek to justify aggressive transfers. The smart play is to ensure you have maximum exposure to Fulham, Newcastle, and Manchester United while accepting that the rest of your squad will likely return modest totals.

Wild Card and Free Hit Strategy for the Run-In

For managers still holding their wildcard, Harry confirms that gameweek 32 is likely the optimal time to play it. This aligns with conventional wisdom that waiting until after the blank gives you maximum information about the fixture landscape.

However, he acknowledges that some managers might be on a "free hit" strategy instead. For those planning to use their free hit in gameweek 32, the approach to gameweek 31 differs slightly. You're not necessarily trying to build a team that will last 17 gameweeks; you just need 11 players who can score reasonably well in this specific gameweek before you completely reshape your squad.

For those considering whether to save their wildcard, Harry suggests that the decision depends on your current squad structure. If you have players from teams likely to have blanks in the coming weeks and you need to fundamentally restructure, the wildcard remains valuable. If your squad is already well-positioned for the expected double gameweeks, you might be better served using transfers strategically rather than burning the wildcard.

Detailed Takeaways for FPL Managers

Takeaway 1: Preserve Your Transfers Until After European Results This Week

The single most important strategic point from this preview is the recommendation to hold your transfers as long as possible. The European matches happening this midweek could eliminate four of the seven teams currently expected to double, fundamentally changing where those doubles land. Harry explicitly states that if Manchester City and Crystal Palace go out in Europe, an announcement could come "on Friday before the deadline on Friday evening." Saving your transfers until that information is known prevents you from making decisions based on incomplete data.

Takeaway 2: Always Field 11 Players, Even at a -4 Hit

The mathematical analysis of taking a -4 transfer hit to add an 11th player is crucial. Since that player will earn at least two appearance points, the net cost is only -2 points compared to leaving the position empty. When you're competing for overall rank, those two points matter far less than the green arrow you'll earn by having a full lineup in a gameweek where most managers will have 11 players. This is particularly true for managers who have already played their wildcard.

Takeaway 3: Focus on Only Two Specific Upgrades This Week

Harry identifies just two players worth actively pursuing this gameweek: Harry Wilson of Fulham and Bruno Fernandes of Manchester United. Both players have the best fixtures (Fulham at home to Burnley, Manchester United away at Bournemouth) and the best projected returns. Beyond these two picks, the expected goals data simply doesn't support aggressive transfers. Accept that most of your squad will return modest scores and save your resources for gameweek 32.

Takeaway 4: Expect Fixture Clarity After Gameweek 32 Deadline

The three-week gap between gameweek 31 and gameweek 32 exists precisely because of the international break, FA Cup, and midweek European matches. By the time the gameweek 32 deadline arrives, all European eliminations will be known and the FA Cup round will be complete. This means the blank and double gameweek picture will be much clearer than it is now. Harry emphasizes that "that big break will give us so much information," making patience the optimal strategy for all but the most urgent transfer needs.

Takeaway 5: Target Only Three Teams for Offensive Exposure

The projection data clearly shows that Fulham, Newcastle, and Manchester United are the only teams with meaningful attacking upside this gameweek. Fulham lead with 1.83 expected goals and a 42% clean sheet probability, Newcastle sit at 1.76 expected goals with 39% clean sheet odds, and Manchester United project 1.72 expected goals. Ensure your squad has maximum exposure to these three teams through starters and captain choices, while accepting lower returns from players in other fixtures.
