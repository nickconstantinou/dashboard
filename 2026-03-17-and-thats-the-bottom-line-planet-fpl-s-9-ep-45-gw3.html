And That's The Bottom Line... | Planet FPL S. 9 Ep. 45 | GW30 Review | Fantasy Premier League

Introduction

Welcome back to Planet FPL, the beloved weekly podcast where two passionate Fantasy Premier League managers dissect the latest gameweek and share their trials, tribulations, and strategic insights. In this episode, hosts Sush and James return to analyze Gameweek 30, a particularly frustrating week for many managers up and down the country. The episode was recorded on what proved to be a challenging day for James, who was battling through "man flu" while still managing to provide his characteristic analytical commentary on the state of their respective FPL teams.

The timing of this episode is crucial for several reasons. Gameweek 30 represented a pivotal moment in the season, with European competitions creating fixture uncertainties that continue to ripple through FPL strategies. Both hosts are navigating difficult periods in their own teams—Sush has experienced multiple consecutive red arrows while James is also sliding down the global rankings. The conversation provides valuable insight into how experienced FPL managers approach adversity, make transfer decisions under uncertainty, and plan for the crucial run-in ahead. Whether you're a veteran manager fighting for a top finish or a casual player looking to understand the mindset of dedicated FPL content creators, this episode offers plenty of practical takeaways.

The episode covers a remarkable amount of ground in its conversational format. From individual player performances and captaincy decisions to broader strategic questions about fixture scheduling and wildcard timing, Sush and James offer their unfiltered thoughts on what has been a chaotic and unpredictable season. Their transparency about their own struggles makes this episode particularly relatable—these aren't managers with perfect squads telling you what to do, but rather two friends working through their own problems while trying to help the community learn from their experiences.

Gameweek 30 Performance Analysis

Sush's Difficult Week

Sush opens up about his frustrating Gameweek 30, describing it as "not a great game week" for his team. At the time of recording, he was sitting on 38 points with two players still to play—Kella Utara and captain Thiago. His overall rank had dropped to approximately 19,000, a significant slide from where he expected to be. The situation wasn't helped by the fact that Tottenham, his beloved team, had finally made him "sick" after their performance the previous day, though he was quick to clarify this was metaphorical rather than the cause of his illness.

The Tottenham match against Liverpool saw the home side approach the game "a little bit more sensibly than what they've been approaching games recently," according to Sush. From an FPL perspective, this meant some reasonable returns for his assets—Gabriel delivered 9 points, James Hill contributed 8, while Fernandez scored 10 but was "obviously a loser in the book" for Sush due to his ownership. Despite having three players remaining to play (Brentford players against Wolves), Sush was realistic about his chances: "I don't think that'll end up in a green arrow for me." He humorously added that given how the gameweek had gone, he "should probably expect 11 points" as a kind of self-deprecating acknowledgment of how poorly things had unfolded.

The key issue for Sush was that his remaining players needed approximately 30 points combined to positively impact his rank, which he acknowledged was extremely unlikely. "I've been here before," he explained, referencing previous Mondays where he'd had three players to play and ended up with just one point between all of them. This realistic assessment demonstrates the emotional intelligence required to be a successful FPL manager—understanding when a gameweek is lost and pivoting to focus on the next one rather than dwelling on what could have been.

James's Wildcard Woes

James was in a similarly difficult position, though his situation had some unique elements worth examining. He was sitting on 40 points at the time of recording, also with Thiago yet to play, meaning his final score could range significantly depending on how the Brentford players performed against Wolves. His team had experienced "so many red arrows in a row" that he'd jokingly threatened to rename his FPL team "Tottenham" because they were on a five-game losing streak—though he was quick to note that he'd actually stopped the rot with a green arrow the previous day, meaning he couldn't follow through with the joke.

The most significant talking point from James's week was his decision not to use the bench boost chip. This proved to be a costly error, as his bench would have returned 14 points—Anderson with 7, Chilango with 9, Rogers with 2, and the inevitable blank from whoever filled the final slot. James was visibly frustrated when discussing this: "How many times I say to you in gameweek 30 was the out? Yeah, Buggin seven, Anderson 7, Chiao nine, Rogers, the inevitable two. Rogers was only ever going to be one or two, wasn't it?" The regret was palpable as he calculated what could have been a much better return.

When asked what he would have done differently, James explained he wouldn't have kept Malik Chow for the Chelsea away fixture but might have kept him for this gameweek had he known the returns were coming. He also mentioned looking at the Bournemouth vs Burnley game before deciding against using the bench boost: "I had Dravka and James Hill on my bench and thought I don't see a nil nil there. So um I'm not going to bother with it." As it turned out, that match delivered 14 points to his bench, and he had Alder for an additional two points—meaning he could have walked away with 16 points from the bench boost, which he would have been "happy with."

James's overall rank has now slid to 1.2 million, and he's quick to acknowledge the pain: "Gabrielle like you. Bruno Fernandez like you. Jared Bowen not like you. Nothing else." His blankers included Timber (who went off injured), Virgil, Sanchez with just the save point, and Bumho who "has been garbage for weeks." He also had blanks from Declan Rice, Enzo Fernandez, and captain Saka for just one point. The comprehensive nature of his failure in this gameweek demonstrates the variance that even experienced managers face—sometimes everything that can go wrong does go wrong.

The Liverpool-Tottenham Context

The conversation naturally drifted to the Liverpool vs Tottenham match that had concluded the previous day, with both hosts acknowledging how Liverpool "don't rate Spurs at all." Sush noted that when he saw the Liverpool team drop, he really thought "Oh, they don't rate Spurs," though James pointed out that Liverpool still had enough quality to get the result even without their strongest lineup.

This led to a broader discussion about Tottenham's trajectory and Liverpool's inconsistent season. Sush observed that "we can't rely on Liverpool. They haven't been good this season. Even though when they beat us 5-2, like it wasn't uh a stellar Liverpool performance." This reflects a common FPL dilemma—when your own team plays poorly, it affects both your enjoyment of watching them and potentially your FPL returns if you have too many of their players. The 5-2 defeat Tottenham suffered earlier in the season wasn't a vintage Liverpool performance, which makes their overall season struggles even more frustrating for neutral observers.

The hosts also touched on the psychological aspect of watching your own team play, particularly when you're managing an FPL team that relies on those players performing. James mentioned meeting David Hunter in the Arsenal outside Anfield the previous day and having "a couple of drinks" before the match—a classic fan ritual that probably didn't help his judgment when making in-game adjustments or captaincy decisions.

European Fixtures and Fixture Congestion

The Manchester City and Crystal Palace Question

Perhaps the most strategically important discussion in this episode centers on the upcoming fixture congestion caused by European competitions. Both Manchester City and Crystal Palace remain in European competition, which creates significant uncertainty for FPL managers trying to plan ahead. Sush issued an important reminder to listeners: "Just a reminder to everyone, um, hold those transfers, guys. Wait for Friday. Um, particularly if it involves your idea of selling Manchester City players."

The reasoning behind this caution is multifaceted. If Manchester City or Crystal Palace go through in Europe, and there's a projection that Crystal Palace will go through, "then obviously the fixture won't happen in 31 or 32." However, "if they both go out, then there's every chance it's going to happen. Um, 32 would be more likely than 31." This creates a complex decision tree for managers who need to decide whether to hold or sell players from these teams.

Sush explained the potential preferences: "I think there is a a feeling that maybe City would like 31 under that circumstance. They played Chelsea on the Sunday in 32. Keeps their midweek clear before the Arsenal game. Not that it matters now, I don't think." This insight into the fixture scheduling preferences of clubs adds another layer of complexity to FPL planning—it's not just about when games are scheduled, but also about how clubs might view the timing from a competitive standpoint.

The hosts then explored a hypothetical scenario: if both City and Palace go out in Europe, and the City v Palace fixture was announced for Gameweek 31 on Monday, what would happen? This question revealed the challenges FPL administrators face when dealing with fixture changes. Sush correctly identified that if the fixture was scheduled for midweek before Gameweek 32, the deadline would likely need to be moved forward—but he also questioned whether the Premier League would actually want to do this, noting that historically they've never brought deadlines forward in similar situations, preferring instead to extend them.

The Title Race Implications

The conversation then shifted to the title race, with both hosts acknowledging that Arsenal remain in control despite Manchester City's recent form. Sush pointed out: "if they beat Palace and they win at Chelsea and then they beat Arsenal they're probably what still three points behind" with the goal difference being "slightly worse." He was realistic about City's chances: "Manchester City need to be perfect and I'm telling you they're not going to be."

This assessment was informed by firsthand experience—James had watched City "on the weekend" and wasn't impressed with what he saw. While City remain a formidable team with the ability to win any match, the consensus among the hosts was that Arsenal's destiny remains in their own hands. For FPL purposes, this suggests that investing in Arsenal assets remains viable while City assets carry more risk given the uncertainty around their fixture schedule.

Transfer Strategy and Wildcard Planning

James's Immediate Plans

James outlined his immediate transfer strategy, which provides a window into how experienced managers think about the fixture list. He explained: "I've got three blankers in Timber, Gabrielle, and Rice. And I also have a blanker in Pots, who's obviously not getting into the West Ham team at the moment." This straightforward assessment of his problem players shows the importance of regular squad auditing—even when you're struggling, you need to identify which players are holding you back.

His plan was to "Pots to Harry Wilson and then log out and come back after the the games in 31 where I think I'll be wild carding into 32, but I won't be hitting the the button straight after the deadline passes." This is a crucial strategic insight—waiting until after the Gameweek 31 deadline to wildcard means he can see how the European situations resolve before committing to his wildcard team. It's a patient approach that allows for maximum information gathering before making big changes.

He also revealed that he has "no city and no palace, no rules" at the moment, which explains his relative indifference to the City European situation. However, he acknowledged that this might change: "I'm not bothered about City to be honest. Uh, no, but you might be on Friday if they announce it." This flexible approach is sensible—even managers who think they know what they're doing need to be prepared to adapt when new information becomes available.

The Wildcard Timing Question

Both hosts acknowledged that many managers still have their wildcard available, and the current fixture uncertainty makes short-term thinking more appealing than usual. Sush noted: "I know so many people still with uh wild card coming up. So looking quite shortterm in terms of who and what they're picking."

The advice to wait until Friday before making transfers is particularly important for those with City or Palace players. The European fixtures could be resolved by then, giving managers a clearer picture of what the fixture list looks like. Selling City players before knowing whether they'll have blank gameweeks could prove costly, while holding them might be the right move if their European commitments mean they don't play in Gameweek 31 or 32.

Key Player Performances and Observations

Throughout the episode, the hosts discussed numerous individual players and their FPL implications. Tottenham's approach in the Liverpool match meant "Gabriel 9, James Hill 8 was quite handy this week," though Fernandez's 10 points were somewhat offset by his ownership—everyone who owned him was essentially banking the same points. James noted that "Timber who went off injured" was a concern, while "Virgil" also blanked.

The midfield discussion revealed ongoing frustrations with Bruno Fernandes: "Bruno Fernandez like you. Jared Bowen not like you. Nothing else." The blank from Declan Rice was particularly painful given his price tag, while Enzo Fernandez and Saka contributed just one point each as captain. James summed up his goalkeeper situation succinctly: "Sanchez with the save point but still nothing."

The broader point from these discussions is that variance plays a huge role in FPL success. Even the best managers can't predict every blank or return, and sometimes you simply have to accept that the dice didn't roll your way. Both Sush and James demonstrated this acceptance while also showing they're actively working to improve their situations through transfers and strategic planning.

Final Thoughts

Gameweek 30 proved to be a frustrating one for both Sush and James, but their willingness to analyze their mistakes and share their strategic thinking provides genuine value to the FPL community. The key lessons from this episode center on patience—whether that's waiting for European fixtures to resolve before making transfers, accepting that some gameweeks are simply lost, or planning your wildcard for maximum information gathering.

The discussion about Manchester City and Crystal Palace fixture scheduling highlights the complex interrelationship between real-world football and FPL strategy. Managers who can successfully navigate these uncertainties will find themselves with an advantage over those who act prematurely. As always in FPL, the managers who succeed are those who can balance emotion with logic, short-term needs with long-term planning, and individual player preferences with broader team strategy.
