## The Soundtrack Rap Obsession

Conan opens by confessing a recent obsession that has consumed his thoughts: rappers who agree to create soundtracks for major motion pictures, only to find themselves forced into awkwardly rapping about plot details. He frames this as a kind of creative torture—executives demanding the rapper mention specific characters, plot points, and narrative elements while somehow making it sound like authentic hip-hop.

The first example Conan cites is MC Hammer's work on the "Addams Family" sequel, where Hammer had to navigate mentioning Fester, Wednesday, Gomez, and other family members while maintaining artistic credibility. "MC Hammer was getting into the weeds," Conan explains, noting that some executives had apparently insisted on specific character name-drops. Conan acknowledges this is a deep-cut observation, joking that his loved ones regularly ask him "What are you doing?" when he brings up these topics—complete with an impression that makes it sound like he's making a pizza pie.

Vanilla Ice's "Ninja Rap" from the Teenage Mutant Ninja Turtles movie represents another prime example, with Conan noting how the song has to incorporate Michelangelo, blue jewels, and other plot elements while somehow functioning as a standalone track. "They started break dancing," Conan recalls of the movie's concert scene that houses the performance. "Of course it is. Of course it is." Bobby Brown's contribution to Ghostbusters 2 rounds out this trilogy of awkward soundtrack moments, with Conan clearly delighted by how these rap interludes have aged.

What elevates this obsession into comedy gold is Conan's insistence on playing these songs for his staff—specifically Eduardo and David Hoppin—forcing them to listen and then advancing the tracks to exactly 44 seconds in to skip the preamble and get straight to the rapping. David Hoppin, driving Conan around in his Hyundai while wearing an illegal chauffeur's cap, was fooled into thinking Conan had actually recorded a new song with his band, only to discover it was Pitbull's "Men in Black 3" soundtrack contribution. "I turned and I said, you've really recorded this with the band," David recalls, admitting he thought Conan had somehow convinced a professional band to record the track.

Conan explains he heard Pitbull in a Blick art supply store while examining Japanese pens and kneadable erasers, became obsessed, and has since memorized the exact 44-second timestamp where the actual rap begins. He describes his methodology as "coming in hot" with verse two energy, demanding Eduardo play the specific portion of the song multiple times.
