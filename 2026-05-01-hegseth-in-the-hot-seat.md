## Introduction

On Thursday, May 1st, Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth appeared before the Senate Armed Services Committee for his first substantive congressional testimony in a year—a hearing ostensibly focused on approving the Pentagon's fiscal year 2027 budget request but that quickly evolved into a high-stakes confrontation over an Iran war that has stalled, management controversies that have reshaped the Pentagon's leadership, and mounting questions about accountability, authorization, and potential financial conflicts of interest within the administration itself.

The timing could hardly be more consequential. Hegseth, widely described as arguably the most controversial Secretary of Defense in a generation, has spent the past several months consolidating power at the Pentagon, removing senior military leadership—including numerous women and minorities he deemed insufficiently loyal—while simultaneously prosecuting a military campaign in Iran that has consumed tens of billions of dollars, closed one of the world's most critical oil shipping chokepoints, and produced none of the decisive victory the administration apparently expected. Compounding the accountability vacuum, Hegseth has systematically excluded reporters from the Pentagon, eliminating traditional news conferences and creating what critics describe as an unprecedented lack of public transparency for a defense secretary overseeing active combat operations.

This hearing, therefore, represented one of the few opportunities for lawmakers—and by extension, the American public—to examine under oath the decisions that have shaped the country's national security posture in the second Trump term. As New York Times reporter Eric Schmidt explained to host Michael Barbaro, the stakes were enormous: Hegseth would face pointed questions about strategy, spending, and his own conduct from Democrats on the committee, while simultaneously seeking billions in funding from lawmakers who have largely avoided challenging the administration's Iran policy elsewhere.

---

## The Budget Request and Its Stakes

The hearing opened with Hegseth making the case for what he described as a "historic" budget request. The $1.5 trillion figure—representing roughly $500 million more than the current fiscal year—reflects, in the administration's view, "the urgency of the moment" and the need to address both longstanding military readiness problems and the requirements of ongoing and future combat operations. The request encompasses everything from replenishing depleted munitions stockpiles to rebuilding the Navy's aging fleet to constructing an ambitious new anti-missile architecture that administration officials have dubbed "Golden Dome."

For Hegseth, this budget request served dual purposes. It was both a practical necessity—the military genuinely requires substantial investment after years of constrained spending—and a political validation of the direction he has taken the Pentagon. By framing the request as necessary to confront "the current and future fights," Hegseth signaled that the administration views the Iran campaign not as a temporary intervention but as part of a sustained transformation of American military posture.

Yet the Republican senators who spoke during the hearing's opening statements offered something closer to full-throated endorsement than the skeptical scrutiny one might expect when disbursing $1.5 trillion in public funds. One senator after another praised Hegseth's leadership, the war effort in Iran, and the operation that seized Venezuelan President Nicolás Maduro and brought him to the United States. The message from the committee's majority was unified: the Pentagon is in capable hands, the war is succeeding, and the budget deserves support.

This stands in marked contrast to other cabinet hearings during the second Trump term, where Republicans have demonstrated willingness to challenge officials like Homeland Security Secretary Kristi Noem and former Attorney General Pam Bondi—questionings that contributed to those officials' subsequent departures. On the most consequential policy question of the term, the Iran war, Republican senators fell firmly in line.

---

## The War in Iran: Democratic Critiques and Hegseth's Defense

The Democrats on the committee, however, came loaded for bear. They confronted Hegseth with a fundamental challenge: the war is stuck.

Senators laid out the facts systematically. After two months of military operations, Iran's theocratic regime remains in power. The Strait of Hormuz—the critical waterway through which roughly a fifth of the world's oil flows—remains closed, and Iran continues to control it, effectively "choking off the economy for the globe." Iran retains its stockpiles of highly enriched uranium and the thousands of missiles and drones that posed the original threat the war was meant to neutralize. None of the war's declared objectives have been achieved.

Senator Kirsten Gillibrand of New York pressed the political dimension of this failure, telling Hegseth that he appeared "out of touch" with the American people, whom she described as exhausted by a conflict producing no discernible benefits. She detailed the economic pain her constituents were experiencing: higher prices at the gas pump for both diesel and gasoline, increased costs for groceries and everyday goods, and a general sense that the war was being prosecuted without clear purpose or endpoint. The $25 billion already spent—averaging approximately $1 billion per day—represented real money that her constituents were feeling in their daily lives, and they did not see the upside.

Hegseth's response was to reframe the terms of the debate entirely. He insisted that when he spoke to Americans—particularly to the troops serving in the conflict—they expressed gratitude for a president willing to take on a threat that previous administrations had avoided for 47 years. He dismissed economic concerns as short-term thinking and offered a philosophical counter-argument: what price could be placed on preventing a nuclear-armed Iran? In his telling, the war was an investment in long-term security that would "elude president after president, Republicans, Democrats for decades" but was now within reach. The cost was real but justified by the magnitude of the threat.

This reasoning—essentially arguing that no price is too high to prevent a hypothetical future catastrophe—was met with skepticism by Democratic lawmakers who noted that the American people were far less convinced than Hegseth suggested. Senator Gillibrand's counter was pointed: "You don't care whether the American people support this war." She accused the administration of spin while suggesting that ordinary Americans saw clearly through it.

---

## The 60-Day Deadline and Constitutional Questions

One of the most significant exchanges of the hearing concerned the 60-day limit established by the War Powers Resolution, which requires the president to either withdraw American forces from hostilities or seek congressional authorization after 60 days of engagement. With Friday, May 1st, marking approximately that deadline, Senator Tim Kaine of Virginia pressed Hegseth on whether the administration intended to comply with the law.

Hegseth's answer stunned lawmakers. He suggested that the ongoing ceasefire had "paused" the 60-day clock, effectively suspending the legal requirement until hostilities resumed. "Ultimately, I would defer to the White House and White House counsel on that," he said, but the implication was clear: the administration believed it did not currently need to seek congressional authorization because the fighting had temporarily stopped.

Kaine and other Democrats were incredulous. "I do not believe the statute would support that," Kaine responded. Legal scholars consulted by The New York Times confirmed the skepticism: the ceasefire interpretation appears to have no basis in the text or legislative history of the War Powers Resolution. Yet the administration was staking out this novel legal position precisely at the moment when the deadline was becoming unavoidable.

Eric Schmidt observed that this episode illustrated something broader about the administration's approach to the war: officials had entered into hostilities apparently expecting a rapid victory, believing the 60-day deadline would never be triggered because the Iranian regime would quickly collapse. When that did not happen, they found themselves confronting a legal requirement they had apparently never seriously considered. The ceasefire, and the claim that it suspended the clock, appeared to be an ad hoc effort to avoid a constitutional confrontation they were neither prepared for nor particularly interested in having.

---

## Management Controversies and Insider Trading Allegations

Beyond the war itself, Democratic senators pressed Hegseth on the broader pattern of decisions he had made since taking office—most notably his dismissal of dozens of generals and admirals, many of them women and minorities who, critics allege, were removed not for performance reasons but for insufficient loyalty to the administration's political agenda.

Senator Elizabeth Warren of Massachusetts raised an entirely different concern: whether insiders were profiting from the war. She cited the recent arrest of a U.S. Special Forces soldier who allegedly used classified operational information about the Maduro seizure to make approximately $400,000 in the prediction markets—a striking example of how the opacity surrounding the administration's military operations might be creating opportunities for financial exploitation. Reports had also emerged of other individuals making significant profits from trades apparently timed to classified information about the Iran campaign.

Warren's line of questioning then shifted to a more personal target: a Financial Times report that Hegseth's broker had attempted to purchase hundreds of shares in a BlackRock fund invested in defense contractors just before the war began. Was the defense secretary himself profiting from decisions he was making in his official capacity?

Hegseth's response was emphatic. He called the story "a big fat negative," denied any personal involvement in or knowledge of his broker's investment decisions, and expressed apparent outrage that such allegations would be raised. When pressed on whether his broker required his personal sign-off on individual stock purchases, he responded with an even more emphatic "bigger fatter negative."

Schmidt noted that The Times had found no evidence supporting the allegation that Hegseth himself had sought to profit from the war—making clear that the denial was accurate as far as the newspaper's reporting had determined. Yet the episode illustrated the broader environment of opacity and potential self-dealing that Warren was attempting to illuminate: whether soldiers, officials, or anyone with access to classified operational information could exploit that access for financial gain while the administration simultaneously barred reporters from the Pentagon and avoided press conferences that might surface such concerns.

---

## Republican Support and the Limits of Congressional Oversight

The hearing ultimately revealed stark divisions within the Senate over how to evaluate the administration's conduct. Republicans offered unreserved support for both the war effort and Hegseth's management of the Pentagon, praising the "historic" nature of the budget request and the "smashing military success" of recent operations. No Republican senator raised questions about the war's stalemate, the economic costs, or the management controversies that Democrats were highlighting.

This partisan alignment had consequences for the hearing's accountability function. With the committee's majority effectively aligned with the administration, Hegseth faced limited sustained pressure from Republican lawmakers—a dynamic starkly different from other cabinet hearings where Republicans had demonstrated willingness to challenge officials whose performances they found unsatisfactory. The result was a hearing that, while superficially providing oversight, left the most consequential questions unanswered.

## Detailed Takeaways

**The War Is Not Going as Advertised, but Changing Course Seems Unlikely.** After two months of military operations and approximately $25 billion in spending, the Iran war has failed to achieve any of its publicly stated objectives. The Iranian regime remains in power, the Strait of Hormuz remains closed, and Iran's nuclear and missile capabilities are intact. Hegseth's response—that the long-term security benefits justify short-term costs—suggests an administration prepared to prosecute an inconclusive war indefinitely rather than acknowledge failure. The gap between administration rhetoric ("historic courage," "incredible success") and observable reality (stalemate, economic pain, no congressional authorization) is substantial.

**The Constitutional Crisis Over War Powers May Be Approaching.** The administration has staked out a legally dubious position—that a ceasefire pauses the 60-day War Powers Resolution clock—in what appears to be an effort to avoid seeking congressional authorization for a war that has lasted two months without producing victory. Legal scholars dispute this interpretation, and Democratic senators have made clear they intend to contest it. Friday's deadline may or may not trigger a formal confrontation, but the underlying question—when and how the executive branch must seek congressional approval for military action—remains unresolved and potentially explosive.

**Accountability Requires Transparency, and Transparency Has Been Systematically Reduced.** Hegseth's decision to exclude reporters from the Pentagon and eliminate regular press availability has created an accountability vacuum that extends beyond the obvious. Without journalists present to ask questions or document decisions, concerns about insider trading, management decisions, and the conduct of operations become harder to surface. The hearing provided one rare opportunity for direct questioning under oath, but the limitations on press access mean that the American public has limited means to evaluate the administration's national security decisions in real time.

**The Budget Request Reflects Choices, Not Just Necessities.** The $1.5 trillion request is described as necessary to address readiness problems, replenish munitions, and construct new systems like Golden Dome. But it also represents a set of choices about military posture and priorities—choices that are being made in the context of a war that is not succeeding, while economic pressures on ordinary Americans are intensifying. The magnitude of the request, and the largely uncritical reception it received from Republican senators, suggests that the political coalition supporting increased military spending remains strong even as questions about the war's wisdom and effectiveness go unanswered.

**The Broader Pattern of Management Raises Structural Concerns.** The dismissal of senior military leaders—particularly women and minorities—and the replacement of experienced officials with political loyalists has created what critics describe as a pattern of purging institutional knowledge and diminishing diversity in the armed forces' leadership. Combined with questions about operational security and insider trading, this pattern suggests an institution under significant stress, with risks that extend beyond any single controversy or decision.
