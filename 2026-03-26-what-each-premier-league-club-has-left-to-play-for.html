What each Premier League club has left to play for

📖 Read original on Fantasy Football Scout →

The 2025/26 Premier League season is hurtling toward its climax with Arsenal holding a nine-point lead over Manchester City at the summit, though the reigning champions retain a game in hand that could narrow the gap before the campaign's decisive phase. The Gunners have built their advantage on superior goal difference (+39 compared to City's +32), and their remaining fixture list offers encouragement—Arsenal only leave London once during the run-in, with away trips to Manchester City and West Ham bookending a sequence that includes home encounters with Bournemouth, Newcastle, and Fulham. City, meanwhile, face a more arduous path with away days at Chelsea, Burnley, Everton, and Bournemouth punctuating their schedule, though they possess the comfort of hosting Arsenal in Gameweek 33 in what promises to be the season's pivotal confrontation.

The arithmetic for Arsenal is stark: victory at the Etihad would effectively hand them a ten-point advantage with five matches remaining, potentially allowing Mikel Arteta's side to clinch the title before their Gameweek 36 trip to West Ham, provided they win their other fixtures. Yet City midfielder Nico O'Reilly offered defiant words after the club's EFL Cup final triumph over Arsenal, declaring: "The blood never went – we've always smelt blood. We're confident in ourselves, we know we can do it, they've got to come to our place which is a tough place to come to as everyone has seen this season. So, we do smell blood and we've got to keep going. It does a lot for us, build momentum and just push on now. Obviously, it's a big blow for them. They were going for everything, just as we were." The psychological edge swings both ways as the two favorites prepare to meet on potentially title-deciding ground.

Behind the title contenders, the race for Champions League qualification has crystallized into a compelling battle between Manchester United, Aston Villa, Liverpool, and Chelsea—with all four clubs separated by just seven points. United sit third with 55 points, Villa fourth on 54, while Liverpool occupy the fifth and final Champions League spot with 49 points from their 29 games. Chelsea, however, are mounting pressure in sixth place with 48 points, though their recent form makes for concerning reading: two successive defeats have dented Enzo Maresca's side's prospects. Liverpool's own Champions League ambitions have stuttered alarmingly, with Arne Slot's men failing to win any of their last three matches prior to the international break.

The landscape below the established contenders remains surprisingly competitive, with Brentford and Everton both sitting on 46 points—just three adrift of Chelsea's Champions League berth. That the Toffees find themselves in European contention at all represents a remarkable turnaround, with their manager recently acknowledging: "I'd love to say it was [a possibility] as I'm trying to be more positive than I would normally be, but for Everton to even be in the mix for Europe is unbelievable, whether it is Champions League, Europa League, or Conference League." The convergence of multiple clubs separated by minimal points has ensured that the race for European qualification will extend deep into the season, with no fewer than six teams harboring realistic ambitions of securing continental football.

For Arsenal, the challenge extends beyond domestic concerns. With the club remaining in contention for Champions League and FA Cup honors alongside their league title pursuit, Arteta faces the delicate task of managing squad rotation without sacrificing momentum. Should Arsenal progress to the Champions League semi-finals, Gameweek 35's home fixture against Fulham would be sandwiched by another European double-header, potentially forcing difficult decisions about player availability and minute management across all three competitions.

What makes this season's conclusion particularly significant is the near-certainty—confirmed for the second consecutive year—that the Premier League's top five will all qualify for the following season's Champions League, meaning third place effectively guarantees European football's elite competition rather than merely Europa League participation. This high stakes environment has transformed what might have been mid-table irrelevance into a genuine six-horse race for continental qualification, with clubs like Brentford and Everton demonstrating that the gap between Premier League mid-tier and elite has genuinely compressed. The final weeks promise drama on multiple fronts: can Arsenal withstand City's home advantage to wrap up an increasingly likely title triumph, and which of the half-dozen European aspirants will claim the remaining Champions League spots?
