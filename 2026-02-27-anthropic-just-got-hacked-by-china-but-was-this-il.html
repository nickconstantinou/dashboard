Anthropic's China Hack Exposed: The Irony of AI Distillation

In a major development that's sent shockwaves through the AI industry, Anthropic has published a report exposing three major Chinese AI labs for conducting what they're calling a massive "distillation attack." According to the report, these labs engaged in over 16 million fraudulent conversations with Claude, using roughly 24,000 fake accounts specifically designed to extract the model's capabilities and train their own competing AI systems. The labs named include Deepseek (responsible for around 150,000 exchanges), Moonshot AI (3.4 million), and Miniax (13 million exchanges). This follows similar accusations from Google about their Gemini models and OpenAI in recent weeks, with all three major American AI labs now pointing fingers at China for attempting to steal their AI technology.

The concept at the center of this controversy is called "distillation," and it's actually a fundamental technique in AI development. The process involves a larger "teacher" model (like Claude Opus) providing outputs to a smaller "student" model, which learns to mimic the larger model's behavior at a fraction of the cost. This allows companies to create efficient, smaller models that retain much of the intelligence of their larger counterparts. It's been described as essential for creating AI models that can run on personal devices like phones while maintaining high-level capabilities.

Here's where the story takes an interesting turn: distillation isn't just something China is accused of doing‚Äîit's standard practice across the entire AI industry. Anthropic itself has distilled Claude Opus into their smaller Haiku model, and Google has done the same with Gemini Ultra into Gemini Nano. This raises a compelling question about whether what China did is actually illegal, or simply what everyone does to stay competitive in the AI race. The video argues that the real issue may be less about illegal hacking and more about the irony of a company that built its own success on distillation now complaining when others try to use the same technique on them.

The implications of this story go beyond just these specific companies. There's a growing argument that the rapid progress in China's AI open-source ecosystem may have been largely fueled by access to U.S. model outputs through these distillation techniques. Whether this is theft or just aggressive competition remains legally unclear, since using AI models to create derivative works exists in a gray area of intellectual property law.
