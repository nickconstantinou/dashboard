Video Reveals US Tomahawk Missile Struck Iranian Girls School, Killing 168 People

New Evidence Emerges as International Outrage Grows Over Deadly Strike on Primary School in Minab

A newly surfaced video has dramatically escalated international scrutiny of American military operations in Iran, providing what investigators are calling compelling visual evidence that a United States Tomahawk missile struck near a girls' school in southern Iran, killing 168 people—most of them children between ages 7 and 12. The footage, released by Iran's semi-official Meah News agency and independently verified by the investigative collective Bellingcat, shows the moment of impact at an Iranian Revolutionary Guard base in the city of Minab, located in the direct vicinity of the primary school. This development represents a significant turning point in the ongoing controversy surrounding the February 28th strike, which occurred on the first day of US-Israel military operations against Iran, and threatens to reshape diplomatic relations between Tehran, Washington, and their respective allies.

The video emerges at a moment of profound geopolitical tension, with the Trump administration facing mounting international pressure and accusations of stonewalling. UNESCO has already condemned the bombing as a grave violation of humanitarian law, while Human Rights Watch has formally called for the incident to be investigated as a potential war crime. The footage directly contradicts statements made by US President Donald Trump, who has repeatedly asserted—without providing evidence—that Iran itself was responsible for the school bombing, claiming Tehran's munitions are "inaccurate." This assertion has been rejected by arms experts and challenges the fundamental narrative the administration has constructed around the incident.

---

The Video Evidence: What the Footage Shows

The newly released video provides the most direct visual evidence yet of what transpired in Minab on that fateful morning. According to the FRANCE 24 correspondent who analyzed the footage, the video was filmed from a vantage point overlooking the area and captures the seconds immediately before and during the missile strike on the Iranian Revolutionary Guard (IRGC) compound. The correspondent warned viewers that the footage moves very quickly, but walked through it frame by frame to illuminate its contents for audiences at home.

In the video, viewers can clearly see a US Tomahawk missile traveling through the sky before striking the IRGC compound. The moment of impact produces a distinctive black smoke plume that erupts from the targeted location. As the camera pans to the right—toward the direction of the nearby girls' school—additional smoke becomes visible in the sky. This detail has raised troubling questions among investigators: were there already impacts occurring at the school even before the Tomahawk struck the military compound? The correspondent noted that this visual evidence, combined with previous satellite imagery analysis and photographs of the aftermath, indicates that the girls' school was struck as part of the same series of US strikes that targeted the IRGC installation.

The video has been rigorously geolocated by Bellingcat, the renowned investigative factchecking collective. Bellingcat analysts matched the buildings, roads, trees, and other visual elements visible in the footage to satellite imagery of the Minab site, confirming that the missile struck precisely at the IRGC compound location. This independent verification carries significant weight in the international community, as Bellingcat has built a reputation for meticulous open-source investigative work that has been cited in numerous international contexts.

---

Identifying the Weapon: Why This Specifically Matters

Perhaps the most consequential aspect of the video evidence lies in what it reveals about the specific weapon used in the strike. Munitions experts and military analysts who have examined the footage have reached a unambiguous conclusion: the missile visible in the video is clearly a US Tomahawk cruise missile. This identification is not a matter of interpretation or inference—it is a factual determination based on the distinctive visual characteristics of the weapon.

The Tomahawk cruise missile is a sophisticated piece of military hardware that represents some of the most advanced technology in the US arsenal. According to the FRANCE 24 correspondent, these missiles are possessed exclusively by the United States military. Neither Israel nor Iran—the other primary actors in this ongoing conflict—are known to have Tomahawk missiles in their arsenals. While Trump suggested in recent comments that "numerous other nations" possess Tomahawks and "buy them from us," arms experts have uniformly rejected this claim as inaccurate. The Tomahawk is not an export item; it remains strictly within the domain of US military capabilities.

This point cannot be overstated in its significance. If the missile that struck near the school was definitively a US Tomahawk—as the video evidence strongly suggests—then the question of responsibility becomes considerably less ambiguous. The Trump administration's assertion that Iran somehow bombed its own school, presumably using weapons it does not possess, appears to collapse under the weight of this evidence. The video provides a direct visual link between American military hardware and the location of the tragedy, fundamentally undermining the counter-narrative the president has promoted.

---

The Trump Administration's Response: Contradictions and Evasion

The video footage represents a direct contradiction to the positions staked out by Donald Trump and his administration regarding the school bombing. The FRANCE 24 correspondent explicitly characterized the relationship between the new evidence and the administration's statements as one of "direct contradiction." This is not a matter of interpretation or spin—it is a factual discrepancy between what the visual record shows and what the US government has claimed.

Trump has consistently maintained that Iran is responsible for the bombing of the school in Minab, an accusation that Tehran has "loudly denounced." However, as the correspondent noted, the president has "not presented any evidence" for this claim. His sole justification has been his personal opinion that "Iran's munitions are very inaccurate"—a characterization that, regardless of its accuracy regarding general Iranian military capabilities, does nothing to explain how a US-manufactured Tomahawk missile came to strike near a school in the first place.

When pressed on this point during a press conference on Monday, Trump offered a revealing response. A reporter directly confronted him with the contradiction, noting that he was "the only person in your government saying this" and that even his own defense secretary "wouldn't say that when he was asked standing over your shoulder on your plane on Saturday." Trump's reply was striking in its ambiguity: "Because I just don't know enough about it. I think it's something that I was told is under investigation, but tomahawks are used by others. As you know, numerous other nations have tomahawks. They buy them from us. But I will certainly, whatever the report shows, I'm willing to live with that report."

This response raises more questions than it answers. If the president "doesn't know enough about it," on what basis did he previously assert with confidence that Iran was responsible? The claim that "numerous other nations" possess Tomahawks contradicts established knowledge about the weapon system. And the suggestion that he is "willing to live with" whatever the report shows offers little assurance to those seeking accountability for the deaths of 168 people, most of them children.

The correspondent also noted that in the same press conference, Trump claimed Iran possesses Tomahawk missiles—a statement that "arms experts have unanimously rejected." This represents yet another factual discrepancy between the administration's assertions and expert analysis, further undermining the credibility of the official line on the incident.

---

International Response: Condemnation and Calls for Investigation

The gravity of the situation has not been lost on the international community. UNESCO, the United Nations Educational, Scientific and Cultural Organization, has formally condemned the bombing of the school as "a grave violation of humanitarian law." This designation carries significant legal and moral weight, as UNESCO's mandate encompasses the protection of education facilities during armed conflict—a protection explicitly recognized under international humanitarian conventions.

Human Rights Watch has gone further, formally calling for the incident to be investigated as a war crime. This represents an escalation in the international response and places additional pressure on the United States to provide transparency and accountability regarding the strike. War crimes investigations carry serious implications, potentially involving individual criminal responsibility for military and political leaders who ordered or carried out the attack.

The timing of these international responses is notable. They come as new evidence continues to emerge, suggesting that the scope and character of the incident may be considerably different from the initial narratives advanced by the Trump administration. The combination of video evidence, independent verification, and authoritative international condemnation creates a powerful imperative for further investigation—something the administration has thus far been reluctant to embrace.

---

Key Takeaways: Understanding the Significance

The video evidence fundamentally challenges the administration's narrative. By showing a US Tomahawk missile striking near the school, the footage undermines Trump's claim that Iran bombed its own educational facility. Independent verification by Bellingcat adds credibility to the analysis, and the distinctive characteristics of the Tomahawk make alternative explanations implausible.

The weapon identification is dispositive. The Tomahawk cruise missile is exclusively a US system, not exported to allies or possessed by adversaries. This fact eliminates the possibility—however implausible—that Iran used American weapons against its own citizens, unless those weapons were acquired through means entirely without precedent.

Trump's denials lack evidentiary foundation. Despite asserting Iranian responsibility, the president has presented no evidence to support this claim while simultaneously acknowledging he "doesn't know enough about it." This contradiction between confident assertions and admitted ignorance undermines the administration's credibility.

International legal mechanisms are now engaged. With UNESCO condemning the strike as a humanitarian law violation and Human Rights Watch calling for war crimes investigation, the incident has moved beyond political dispute into the realm of international legal accountability.

The death toll represents a significant civilian catastrophe. The 168 fatalities, predominantly girls between ages 7 and 12, constitute one of the deadliest attacks on an educational facility in recent regional conflicts, raising the stakes considerably for questions of accountability and justice.
