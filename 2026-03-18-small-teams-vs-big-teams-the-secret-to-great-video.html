Small teams vs Big teams: The secret to great video game design | Jeff Kaplan and Lex Fridman

A Conversation with Jeff Kaplan and Lex Fridman on Leadership, Ego, and Creative Collaboration

---

Introduction

In the world of video game development, few figures carry the credibility and depth of experience quite like Jeff Kaplan. As the former game director of World of Warcraft and the creative lead behind Overwatch, Kaplan has presided over some of the most beloved and commercially successful games in industry history. Yet despite his stature, Kaplan has consistently remained为人谦虚—insisting that the credit for any success belongs entirely to his teams. In a wide-ranging conversation with Lex Fridman, Kaplan offers an unusually candid look inside the mechanics of creative leadership, exploring the fundamental tension between small, nimble development teams and the massive, institutional structures that characterize modern AAA game studios.

The conversation tackles questions that any creative leader or manager faces: How do you maintain creative energy as an organization scales? What role does ego play in stifling or enabling innovation? Why do artists and engineers so often end up at odds, and how can that divide be healed? Kaplan draws on his decades of experience at Blizzard Entertainment—first as a young lead on World of Warcraft, then as the director of the ill-fated Titan project that was later reborn as Overwatch—to offer insights that transcend the gaming industry and speak to anyone tasked with leading creative teams through periods of growth and change.

---

The Power and Privilege of Small Teams

The conversation opens with a deceptively simple question: What is the actual advantage of working on a small team when the conventional wisdom suggests that modern games require ever-larger armies of developers? Kaplan's answer cuts straight to the heart of what makes creative collaboration work—or fail.

"When you're on a small team, the way I like to describe it is you get to have a loud voice," Kaplan explains. He paints a picture of the ideal incubation period for a game: perhaps ten people in a room, all present for every decision. Kaplan, who is not a server networking specialist, nonetheless sits in on those discussions. He's not an illustrator, but he participates when the team decides what the art style should look like. This absolute proximity to every aspect of the creative process is, in his view, the secret sauce of small-team success.

The moment a team begins to grow, however, everything changes. Compartmentalization sets in—the artists get their own building, the engineers get theirs, and the natural human tendency toward alienation takes hold. Kaplan describes this as a universal phenomenon rooted in human psychology: "The less you interact with somebody, the more you sort of become alienated from them and vilify their point of view." On large teams, he observes, people begin to stereotype one another. Artists start saying things like, "The engineers just don't get what we're trying to make." Engineers roll their eyes at what they perceive as the artists' impractical demands. Kaplan is candid about how damaging this dynamic is—not just to morale, but to the quality of the final product: "That's not true. That's sort of demeaning to them. They signed up for this. This game is going to be as much theirs as it is mine."

---

The Ego Problem and the Art of Listening

From this observation about team dynamics, the conversation pivots to something more personal and psychologically nuanced: the role of ego in creative leadership. Kaplan admits that he has spent considerable time thinking about insecurity—not just as a general human phenomenon, but as a specific hazard for anyone in a position of creative authority. "Insecurity we all have, as human beings," he says. "It just manifests itself in different ways. Some people use their insecurity to rip other people apart. Some people destroy themselves through their own insecurity."

Kaplan traces his own awakening on this front to his early days as a lead on World of Warcraft. Freshly promoted and eager to prove himself, he found himself systematically dismantling the ideas of others in meetings—not out of malice, he insists, but out of an insecure need to be right, to be the shepherd of the correct vision. After one such meeting, his colleague Partardo pulled him aside and delivered a lesson that Kaplan says became foundational to his entire leadership philosophy: "You're a very smart designer, but you shouldn't do what you just did to those people. You should always listen to what people have to say and try to make their ideas work."

This simple instruction transformed Kaplan's approach. He began approaching every incoming idea with a default assumption that it could work—even ideas that initially struck him as wrong. The results, he says, were remarkable. Some of the best features in World of Warcraft and Overwatch came from developing ideas that he would have rejected out of hand in his younger, more insecure days. The key shift was moving from "that's wrong" to "how could we make this work?"

---

The Assumption of Competence

Building on this foundation, Kaplan articulates a principle he calls "the assumption of competence"—a framework for how leaders should treat the talented individuals who join their teams. At Blizzard, he argues, the company was fortunate to recruit the best talent in the industry. When hiring a prop artist to create boxes, chairs, and other environmental objects, they were getting the best prop artist in the world. The mistake many leaders make, Kaplan suggests, is starting from a place of doubt and cynicism rather than assuming they've brought in someone whose judgment is worth respecting.

He offers a concrete example from Overwatch development: when the best prop artist on the team suggests a change or proposes an alternative approach, Kaplan's instinct is to pause and ask himself why this expert is suggesting it, rather than immediately defending his own preference. "Man, the best prop artist in the industry is suggesting something. Why don't I listen to it?" He extends this thinking further, describing how he practices what he calls a "thought experiment" when speaking with new people—especially young people or interns who might feel intimidated by more experienced colleagues. "I pretend that they're the smartest person in the world in my head," Kaplan confesses. "It puts me in a mode of assuming I have a lot to learn from them. It helps you actually really listen."

This philosophy has direct implications for how creative disagreements are resolved. Rather than treating idea提案 as personal territory to be defended, Kaplan encourages a mindset where the best idea wins—regardless of who proposed it. When an idea succeeds after being developed and championed, Kaplan makes a point of ensuring the original proposer gets the credit, even if he was initially skeptical. "You've sort of elevated this person whose idea wouldn't have been championed," he reflects. "The insecure, egotistical lead of the early 2000s would have just said no. Now their idea is the thing everybody in World of Warcraft or Overwatch is just loving, and they get all the credit."

Lessons from Titan: Rebirth After Failure

The conversation briefly touches on Kaplan's experience leading Team Four at Blizzard, the team that developed Titan—a project that was ultimately canceled after years of development, only to be reborn from its ashes as Overwatch. While the transcript doesn't delve deeply into this period, Kaplan's philosophy about team dynamics and psychological safety clearly informed how he approached the difficult circumstances of a project failure and the pressure of starting over.

The principles Kaplan advocates—listening to all voices, assuming competence, checking ego at the door—become especially critical in high-stakes, high-pressure environments where the temptation to centralize decision-making and silence dissent is strongest. A team that's afraid to challenge assumptions is a team that can't iterate toward excellence. A team where artists and engineers view each other with suspicion is a team that will produce fractured, incoherent work.

---

Detailed Takeaways

Small teams create proximity, and proximity creates quality. When everyone is in the same room for every decision, the artificial boundaries between disciplines break down. Designers understand what engineers are actually doing. Artists understand what the designers are trying to achieve. This cross-pollination of knowledge and perspective is impossible to replicate in large organizations through memos, meetings, or org charts alone.

Compartmentalization is the enemy of creativity. As teams grow, the natural human tendency is to retreat into silos and view other disciplines with suspicion. Leaders must actively counteract this tendency through deliberate cross-functional communication, shared goals, and a culture that explicitly rejects stereotyping ("the artists don't understand" / "the engineers don't get it").

Insecurity manifests in destructive behaviors—and it can be overcome. The willingness to admit his own early failures as a leader gives Kaplan's advice credibility. Insecurity is universal, but it doesn't have to be destructive. The key is self-awareness and a commitment to systematic practices (like assuming competence) that redirect insecure impulses toward productive ends.

The best leaders make others look good. Kaplan's insistence on giving credit to others isn't merely谦卑—it's a deliberate strategy for unlocking creative contribution. When people feel their ideas will be heard and potentially championed rather than dismissed, they're more likely to share them. The leader who takes credit for everything ends up with a fraction of the ideas; the leader who gives credit away ends up with all of them.

Default to "how could this work?" rather than "this is wrong." The reframe from rejection to exploration is perhaps Kaplan's most actionable insight. Every idea that reaches a leader has already passed the sender's internal hurdle of seeming worth sharing. Treating it as a problem to be solved rather than an error to be corrected honors that effort and often leads to innovations that no single person would have conceived alone.
