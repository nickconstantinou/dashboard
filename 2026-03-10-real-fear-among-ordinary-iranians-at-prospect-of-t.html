'Real Fear' Among Ordinary Iranians at Prospect of Trump Deal with Regime

An Inside Look at How Iranians View the Escalating Middle East Crisis

By Ursad Ali Jani, FRANCE 24 English

---

As the conflict between Iran and the United States intensifies—now entering its eleventh day—ordinary Iranians find themselves caught between the threat of external military strikes and the ever-present repression of their own government. In a revealing interview with FRANCE 24, correspondent Ursad Ali Jani, who is Iranian himself and has been speaking directly with people inside the country, paints a portrait of a population grappling with fear, confusion, anger, and a diminishing sense of hope.

The timing of this report is significant. On the day of this broadcast, U.S. National Security Advisor Pete Hegseth declared it would be "the most intense day of strikes so far" against Iran. But beyond the immediate military developments, what emerges from Jani's conversations with ordinary Iranians is a far more complex picture—one in which the very people supposedly being "liberated" by American action feel forgotten, endangered, and deeply skeptical of what comes next.

---

The Appointment That Deepened Despair

Just days before this broadcast, Iran's religious establishment appointed Mojtaba Khamenei—son of the aging Supreme Leader Ali Khamenei—as the new Supreme Leader. For many Iranians, this appointment represented not a change but a continuation of the most repressive elements of the regime.

Jani explains that Mojtaba Khamenei, whom he refers to as "Moshaba Hami" (a common transliteration), is viewed by Iranians as "just as much of a hardliner as his father, if not worse." His name, Jani notes, has long been "linked to some of the harshest internal crackdowns over the past 20 years." Rather than signaling any opening or reform, the succession consolidates the hardline apparatus that has governed Iran with an iron fist for decades.

This development struck a devastating blow to any hope that the current crisis might produce meaningful change inside Iran. The regime that Iranians have lived under—marked by brutal suppression of dissent,限制基本自由, and violent crackdowns on protesters—appears more entrenched than ever.

The Four Emotions Defining the Iranian People

When asked to characterize how ordinary Iranians are feeling during this perilous moment, Jani offers a stark summary: "Disappointment, confusion, fear and anger."

These four emotions, while distinct, are deeply interconnected, and each reveals something critical about how Iranians perceive both the external military threat and their own government's actions.

Fear occupies a particularly prominent place in the national psyche. Jani describes "real fear that Donald Trump could strike some kind of deal with the regime or simply declare victory in the coming days, leaving Iranians facing one of the persons that we talked to." This fear is not abstract—it is rooted in decades of broken promises from Western powers and a profound sense that their interests may be sacrificed in diplomatic calculations.

One interviewee named Vahid articulated this anxiety with striking imagery, describing the regime as "a wounded beast in a cage" that Americans might simply walk away from, leaving Iranian citizens alone with that wounded beast. The metaphor captures a deep-seated terror: that Iran will be abandoned by the international community to face an even more desperate and dangerous government.

Anger has also surged, particularly following American strikes that hit fuel infrastructure in Tehran. Jani reports that several Iranians told him: "These are not the regime—they are facilities used by ordinary Iranians or historic sites." Historic sites were damaged in what many see as indiscriminate targeting. As one interviewee argued, the strikes should "only hit the military targets."

This anger reveals a painful irony: while the United States presents its military campaign as a liberation effort, many ordinary Iranians experience the bombs as yet another force harming their daily lives while leaving the regime's repressive structures intact.

Confusion stems from what Jani describes as a glaring lack of clarity from the Trump administration. "The Trump-Netanyahu coalition does not have a clear plan," he observes. "Different figures in the U.S. administration are giving different sometimes contradictory messages. Even Trump himself in a single interview outlined several very different scenarios for the week ahead."

This messaging chaos leaves Iranians unable to plan, unable to predict, and unable to understand what their country—and they themselves—are supposed to hope for. When even the President of the United States cannot articulate a coherent strategy, the people of Iran are left guessing about their future.

---

Trump's Call for Protests: A Naive Proposition

One of the more striking elements of this conflict has been Donald Trump's expressed hope that Iranians would take to the streets and attempt to topple the regime themselves. Jani directly addresses this suggestion, and his response is unequivocal: the conditions for such protests simply do not exist.

" Iranians did go out bravely and protest earlier on this year," Jani notes. "They were killed, you know, in their thousands."

The massacres that occurred in January—referring to the brutal suppression of protests that erupted after the death of Mahsa Amini in 2022 and continued into 2024—remain fresh in people's minds. Jani interviewed several Iranians who explained why a return to the streets is impossible in the current environment.

One unnamed observer told him: "The condition for going into the streets do not exist now because the regime is still firmly present in the streets. The repression machine is in place and it's working."

Another Iranian, Simin, expressed fear that "there would be another massacre, even worse than in the early January." She continued: "The conditions have to be much safer for people. The number of protesters doesn't really matter. What matters is that they are armed—the regime and the people are not."

Beyond the immediate threat of government violence, there is also the new danger posed by the ongoing air campaign. Jani reports that "if protest were to happen, people would need to stay in Tehran or other major cities, and there is a risk of being bombed." The strikes have made urban areas hazardous in ways that compound the existing risks from security forces.

One woman named Sim, whom Jani interviewed, exemplified the impossible choices facing Tehran residents. She "chose to leave Tehran like many others" because "air pollution had become unbearable"—a reference to the environmental and health hazards created or exacerbated by the conflict. For many, staying means risking both arrest and bombardment; leaving means abandoning homes, jobs, and families.

Jani's assessment is blunt: "People are afraid. They find those statements by Donald Trump are rather naive."

---

The Erosion of Hope: From Realistic Optimism to Wishful Thinking

Perhaps the most poignant dimension of Jani's reporting involves the shifting emotional landscape among Iranians over the course of just a few days.

"In the first two days of the war, especially after the news of [General] Qasem Soleimani's death—when I spoke to people—there was that I would call a kind of a realistic optimism," Jani explains. "Many felt that this time could be truly different."

That optimism has since faded. "Now when I speak to them, what I hear sounds more like a wishful optimism."

This shift is significant. In the earliest days of the current escalation, many Iranians believed that the death of prominent military figures and the pressure from American strikes might genuinely create an opening for regime change or, at minimum, meaningful political reform. There was a sense that history might be moving in their favor.

Now, that hope has curdled into something more fragile and uncertain. The appointment of Mojtaba Khamenei as Supreme Leader demonstrated that the regime's hardline structure remains unbroken. The confusion surrounding American intentions has undermined confidence that any coherent plan for Iranian freedom exists. And the strikes themselves have brought danger to ordinary citizens without demonstrably weakening the government's grip on power.

Jani quotes one of his interviewees, Vahid, who captured this ambivalence: "I hope in the midst of all this, the Iranian people who have been betrayed so many times by international powers in recent years for countless reasons will not be betrayed this time and that the real objective will truly be the freedom of the Iranian people. Even if that may sound very idealistic, I hope it will be so."

The phrase "betrayed so many times" resonates with a long history of Western engagement with Iran—from the CIA-backed overthrow of Mohammad Mosaddegh in 1953 to the broken promises of the nuclear deal, from sanctions that punish civilians to diplomatic deals that ultimately serve great power interests rather than the Iranian people. Vahid's hope, Jani suggests, is tinged with a realism born of repeated disappointment.

---

Key Takeaways

1. Ordinary Iranians feel abandoned by both their government and the powersbombing them. The regime remains the primary source of domestic repression, while American and Israeli strikes increasingly harm civilian infrastructure and create humanitarian crises. Iranians find themselves squeezed between two forces that claim to act in their interest but frequently act against it.

2. The new Supreme Leader represents continuity, not change. Mojtaba Khamenei's appointment consolidates hardline control and dashes any hopes that the current crisis might produce internal reform or political opening. The regime that has killed thousands of its own citizens shows no signs of weakening.

3. The absence of clear American strategy is causing profound confusion and distrust. Contradictory messages from different administration officials, combined with Trump's own shifting statements, leave Iranians unable to understand what the United States actually intends. This uncertainty breeds fear and skepticism about American motives.

4. Calls for Iranian protests underestimate both the regime's violence and the new dangers created by warfare. The suggestion that Iranians should simply rise up ignores the thousands who have already been killed, the armed repression apparatus still in place, and the new risks posed by air strikes in major cities. Such statements, while well-intentioned, reflect a profound disconnect from on-the-ground realities.

5. Hope among Iranians has shifted from realistic to wishful in just days. While early optimism that the current crisis might bring change has not entirely disappeared, it is now tempered by hard experience and a clear-eyed view of the regime's durability. The Iranian people want to believe in liberation but have been disappointed too many times to trust easily.

---

Who This Is For

This report is essential reading for anyone seeking to understand the human dimension of the escalating Iran crisis—particularly journalists, policy analysts, diplomats, and concerned citizens who want to move beyond simplified narratives of "liberation" or "war."

For policymakers, Jani's reporting offers a crucial reality check: military pressure alone, without a coherent political strategy and genuine concern for civilian welfare, may strengthen rather than weaken the Iranian regime's hold on power. The people most affected by this conflict are not regime elites but ordinary citizens who bear the costs of both repression and bombardment.

For journalists and observers, this piece demonstrates the importance of on-the-ground reporting that centers the voices of those directly impacted. Too often, discussions of Iran are dominated by officials and experts in distant capitals. Jani's approach—speaking directly with Iranians, conveying their emotions and perspectives—provides a counterweight to the strategic and military frameworks that otherwise dominate coverage.

For ordinary readers, this report offers a window into a world that is often discussed but rarely heard from directly. Understanding the fears, hopes, and frustrations of ordinary Iranians is essential to forming a complete picture of what is at stake in this conflict—not just for the Middle East, but for the broader question of how international power interacts with the lives of ordinary people.

---

The situation in Iran remains fluid, and the coming days may bring new developments that reshape the landscape entirely. But amid the uncertainty, Jani's reporting makes clear that the people of Iran are not passive bystanders in this conflict—they are active agents with their own perspectives, fears, and hopes, even as those hopes grow increasingly fragile.
