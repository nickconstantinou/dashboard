## The Three Main Chip Strategies: A Comprehensive Breakdown

Having covered the fixture landscape, Pras pivots to chip strategy—the most consequential decision managers face around blank gameweeks. He identifies three distinct approaches that managers are employing, each with its own logic and trade-offs.

The first and most popular strategy involves wildcarding in gameweek 32, bench boosting in gameweek 33, and free hitting in gameweek 34. This approach requires pressing the wildcard button in gameweek 32, which has a Friday deadline—essentially happening immediately or very soon. Pras has already shared his views on this strategy, which he believes managers should have heard or will hear: don't rush the decision unnecessarily. He recommends waiting until after the FA Cup quarterfinals have concluded before pressing the button, as managers won't miss out on significant price changes during that short window. The logic is sound—why commit to a wildcard before you have complete information about which fixtures will move between gameweeks 33 and 34?

The second strategy, which Pras admits he's personally inclined toward if the fixtures confirm the expected pattern, involves free hitting in gameweek 33, bench boosting in gameweek 36, and wildcarding in gameweek 35. This approach appeals to Pras because his current team structure looks reasonable for gameweeks 32 and 34, meaning he doesn't need the wildcard immediately. This strategy will likely be adopted by a significant minority of managers—popular but not as widespread as the first option.

The third strategy is more niche and involves wildcarding in gameweek 33, accepting a hit in gameweek 34, and potentially forgoing bench boost entirely. Some managers who have already used their bench boost earlier in the season—or who simply look at their gameweek 32 squad and decide it doesn't require major surgery—will take this path. They might instead bench boost in gameweek 35 or 36 if the opportunity arises later.

Pras considers all three strategies viable, which is refreshing given that some FPL content creators treat their preferred approach as obviously correct. However, he makes an observation that subtly favors patience: the fact that only one of the three main strategies involves wildcarding in gameweek 32 suggests that managers who want maximum flexibility should wait. During the upcoming international break, 100% clarity will emerge about which fixtures belong in gameweek 33 and which stay in gameweek 34. Why commit early when perfect information is just days away?

This patience-first philosophy represents Pras' personal approach. He's waiting for the international break results before making his final chip decisions.

## Detailed Takeaways

### Takeaway 1: Blank Gameweeks Require Strategic Thinking, Not Panic

The fundamental message from Pras is that blank gameweeks, while stressful, represent opportunity rather than catastrophe. The key is understanding your chip strategy in advance and making transfers that position you optimally for the double gameweeks that will follow. Managers who panic-sell Palace assets or other blank-week players often find themselves chasing points and spending valuable transfers playing catch-up. Instead, view blank gameweek 31 as a temporary inconvenience that sets you up for potential massive point returns in gameweeks 33 and potentially 36.

### Takeaway 2: Patience on the Wildcard Is Likely Rewarded

Given that the international break provides near-certainty about which fixtures will move between gameweeks 33 and 34, waiting until after the FA Cup quarterfinals to press the wildcard button represents the most information-efficient approach. The price changes during those extra days are unlikely to be significant enough to justify committing to a team before you know exactly which teams will double. Managers should use gameweek 31 to make minor adjustments—selling obviously redundant players like Palace assets or players with no immediate fixtures—but avoid major reconstructive surgery until the wildcard window opens.

### Takeaway 3: Your Chip Strategy Should Match Your Current Team Structure

Pras' personal inclination toward the free hit 33, bench boost 36, wildcard 35 strategy stems from the fact that his current team already looks reasonable for the blank and non-blank gameweeks surrounding it. Managers who already own Salah, Bruno Fernandes, and other players with good fixtures in gameweeks 32 and 34 have less urgent need to wildcard immediately. Conversely, managers whose squads are badly mismatched with the upcoming fixtures should strongly consider the earlier wildcard. The optimal strategy is personal—it depends on where you are, not where someone else thinks you should be.

### Takeaway 4: Use Fixture Tickers to Make Data-Driven Decisions, Not Gut Feelings

The Solio fixture ticker demonstrates that optimal team selection isn't about finding hidden gems through intuition but rather analyzing projected goals across relevant gameweeks and selecting players from teams that appear most frequently at the top of those projections. For a free hit 33 strategy, Liverpool and Man United attack are clearly the priorities. For a free hit 34 strategy, Chelsea and Newcastle attack deserve attention. These aren't arbitrary recommendations—they're the logical output of systematic analysis. Managers should seek out similar tools and resist the temptation to pick players based on reputation or recent form alone.

### Takeaway 5: European Results Create Both Blanks and Doubles—Monitor Continuously

The relationship between European competition and FPL fixtures is a double-edged sword. More English teams progressing means more blank gameweeks in the short term but also more double gameweeks as those teams continue their European journeys. Palace progressing eliminates their gameweek 31 fixture but strengthens the case for gameweek 33 and gameweek 36 doubles. Managers should track European results religiously during this period and update their fixture charts accordingly. What looks like bad news today (another blank) might be great news tomorrow (confirmed double).
