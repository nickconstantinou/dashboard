# Why the Vikings Refused to Turn Back: The Psychology of Great Explorers

*A meditation on the unyielding human spirit, the strange courage of medieval navigators, and what their voyages tell us about ourselves*

---

In an age before compasses, before reliable maps, before any understanding of the vast oceans stretching before them, the Vikings did something extraordinary: they refused to stop. They pushed westward from the fjords of Norway into open ocean, they reached North America five hundred years before Columbus would take credit for it, and they established trading routes spanning from England and France all the way to Russia, the Middle East, and North Africa. This wasn't reckless abandon—though it may have looked like it from the shore. It was something deeper: a philosophy of movement, a refusal to yield that historian Lars Brownworth identifies as the defining characteristic of one of history's most remarkable civilizations.

Brownworth, whose work on medieval and early modern history has made him one of the foremost explainers of the Viking Age, explores this question with characteristic insight: what drove these Norse seafarers to reject the known world in favor of the terrifying unknown? His answer, developed through a conversation that ranges from accidental discoveries to philosophical poetry, ultimately reveals as much about human nature as it does about medieval Scandinavia. The Vikings, he suggests, did not simply stumble into greatness through circumstance or violence. They embodied something universal—the flame that burns in all of us, the part we admire most about human achievement.

## The Impossible Geography of Viking Exploration

The scale of Viking navigation becomes truly staggering when examined closely. Brownworth walks his conversation partner through a map showing not just the ocean routes Vikings traveled, but the river systems they exploited, creating an interconnected network that stretched across much of the known medieval world. The fjords of Norway served as their launching points, and from there, these early Europeans pushed into territories that would remain beyond European reach for centuries.

What makes this achievement so remarkable is precisely what they lacked: a compass. Modern observers often take magnetic navigation for granted, assuming that any competent seafaring culture possessed some device to point true north. The Vikings had nothing of the sort. They read the stars, studied the birds, watched the behavior of whales, and memorized the patterns of ocean swells—but none of these methods could offer the certainty of a magnetic needle. When a Viking captain "shoved off from some fjord in Norway west," Brownworth observes, "that's your only west." There was no going back mentally, no second-guessing the instruments. The commitment had to be total.

The conversation pauses on this geographical reality. Brownworth asks his listener to truly imagine the psychological state of those early Norse explorers, standing at the prow of a wooden ship with nothing but open ocean between them and what might be the edge of the world itself. Were there sea monsters? Maybe. Was there any land, or would they simply sail off into the void? Maybe. The Vikings who pushed into the Atlantic faced genuine existential uncertainty with every voyage, and they went anyway.

## The Accidental Empire: Naddod and the Discovery of Iceland

The story of Naddod exemplifies both the randomness and the determination that characterized Viking expansion. According to Brownworth, Naddod holds the distinction of being the first Norseman to reach Iceland—though the discovery was entirely accidental. His ship, blown off course during a voyage, happened upon an island that would become a critical waypoint in the Norse world.

What makes Naddod's story "mind-blowing," in Brownworth's words, is not simply the geographic accident of his discovery but what happened when he decided to land and explore. The Viking went ashore, and his crew encountered two humans already living there. These were not fellow Scandinavians, not traders from some distant port, but Irish monks who had arrived in a skin boat—a curragh, likely, constructed from animal hides stretched over a wooden frame.

This detail reframes everything. The Irish monks, seeking escape from the world, had paddled north in a vessel far more precarious than anything the Vikings would later use and had found Iceland first. They lived there alone, in solitude, pursuing what Brownworth identifies as the monks' own unique form of exploration: spiritual rather than martial, passive rather than aggressive, yet equally daring.

When the Vikings arrived and began to settle, the monks made what Brownworth calls "a very excellent move on their part"—they ran away as soon as the Norse appeared. The image captures something essential about the two types of explorer Brownworth distinguishes throughout the conversation: the monks sought isolation and drew strength from remoteness, while the Vikings sought opportunity and drew strength from movement. Each found what they needed in the margins of the known world, even if they needed different things.

## The Penguin That Walks Toward Death: Werner Herzog and the Monks

To illustrate this contrast in exploration psychology, Brownworth reaches for what he calls his "favorite video of all time"—a Werner Herzog documentary about penguins. The scene depicts a single penguin leaving the tribe and walking determinedly toward the mountains, away from the safety of the colony and toward what Herzog intones is certain death.

Brownworth's comparison is both humorous and profound. The penguin, Herzog explains in his characteristic deadpan fashion, is simply "deranged and crazy." The penguin, as a rational creature, should stay with the group, should follow the established patterns that have kept penguinkind alive for millennia. Instead, this one penguin decides to go—just go—toward certain death without any apparent reason.

But here's the crucial observation Brownworth makes: "the penguin did look back briefly." The penguin did think about this. The act of courage was not ignorance of the danger but a choice made despite that knowledge. This is the distinction that defines both the deranged penguin and the Viking explorer and the Irish monk seeking solitude: not the absence of fear, but the presence of something stronger than fear.

This penguin, Brownworth reflects, "always reminds me of this kind of Viking spirit or the monk spirit." There is something in human nature—or perhaps in certain human natures—that responds to the call of the unknown with movement rather than retreat. These figures "just decide to go" and "not look back." The decision is almost irrational in its simplicity, and that simplicity is itself a kind of strength.

## Two Roads to the Edge of the World

Brownworth's analysis distinguishes two primary motivations for exploration, two ways of responding to the question of what lies beyond the known. The first is the "hardcore dude" motivation—the warrior, the raider, the adventurer who moves forward because forward is where the action is. Vikings who sailed to England, who attacked monasteries up and down the Frankish coast, who established trading posts in distant lands, were often driven by something closer to ambition than curiosity. They sought wealth, glory, land, and power, and they were willing to cross oceans to get it.

The second motivation is quieter but equally powerful: escape. The Irish monks who reached Iceland were not looking for plunder or conquest. They were trying to "get away from the world," Brownworth explains. They wanted to be "closer to God, closer to themselves," and the path to that spiritual destination lay in remoteness, in distance from the corrupting influences of civilization. As Brownworth notes, they sought to get away from sin.

These two motivations might seem oppositional—one pushing toward the world, one pulling away—but they share a common structure. Both the warrior and the monk are rejecting the constraints of their present situation. Both are saying, in effect, that the current arrangement of the world is not sufficient, that there must be something more beyond the horizon. The warrior goes to claim it; the monk goes to find it. But both refuse to accept the boundaries drawn by others.

This dual psychology of exploration helps explain the remarkable breadth of Viking achievement. Those who sought conquest pushed west into the Atlantic and eventually reached North America; those who sought solitude created monastic communities in Iceland and Greenland. Both groups shared the willingness to sail into the unknown, to risk everything for the possibility of something different.

## Ulysses and the Philosophy of Not Yielding

To capture the essence of this Viking spirit, Brownworth turns to Alfred Lord Tennyson's poem *Ulysses*. "It's my favorite poem," Brownworth admits, and the selection reveals something about his understanding of what makes exploration meaningful. The poem's final lines have become one of the most quoted expressions of determined forward movement in the English language:

> "To strive, to seek, to find, and not to yield."

Brownworth reads these words as a perfect encapsulation of the Viking worldview. In the poem, the aging Ulysses announces his intention to continue sailing:

> "My purpose holds to sail beyond the baths of all the western stars until I die."

The bathos of this declaration—the casual reference to "the baths of all the western stars"—contains both irony and genuine resolve. Ulysses acknowledges that he will likely die on this voyage, that he will never find the legendary lands he seeks, that the enterprise is in some sense insane. And he goes anyway. "We may die, but I'm going to do this. I'm not going to yield."

This is the spirit Brownworth identifies in the Vikings, and it is the spirit he suggests we still admire today. The name "Viking" appears not just in historical accounts but in modern contexts—satellites carry the name, football teams claim it, cruise ships bear it. Why does a medieval Norse people remain so culturally prominent, so immediately recognizable as a symbol of achievement and courage?

Brownworth's answer is that the Vikings "embody the part—the flame—that burns in all of us that we admire most about human beings." They represent "unyielding focus on going out there, of taking the leap into the unknown, into the scary, and never stopping." This is the quality we name after them, the characteristic we invoke when we need a symbol of determination against impossible odds.

## The Unyielding Flame: Why Exploration Matters

At its core, the conversation with Brownworth is not really about Vikings at all—or rather, it is about Vikings as a lens through which to examine something timeless about human nature. The question that opens the discussion, "what is it in their spirit that motivated them?" turns out to be a question about what motivates any person to step beyond the known, to risk safety for possibility, to refuse the comfortable constraint of established boundaries.

The interviewer wonders aloud whether the Vikings possessed some quality that modern humans lack, some special resilience that allowed them to sail into the unknown without turning back. Brownworth's response suggests a different understanding: the Vikings were not superhuman. They were human in the fullest sense, which means they contained within themselves the same矛盾 that we still feel today. They looked at the ocean, considered the sea monsters and the possibility of falling off the edge of the world, and they went anyway.

This refusal to yield is the defining characteristic of the explorers Brownworth discusses, and it is the characteristic he suggests we still find most admirable. The monks who paddled north in skin boats seeking closeness to God, the warriors who sailed west seeking fortune and glory, the deranged penguin walking toward death in the Antarctic—these are all expressions of the same fundamental human impulse. We are the creatures who look at the void and decide to step into it.

The Vikings remain popular, Brownworth implies, because they remind us of what we are capable of when we stop making excuses. They sailed without compasses, reached continents others would not find for centuries, and never stopped pushing toward the next horizon. They said "just go" and meant it.

## Key Insights from the Psychology of Viking Exploration

**Exploration has two parentages: conquest and retreat.** Brownworth's analysis reveals that the drive to explore the unknown can emerge from two seemingly opposite sources. The Vikings who sought wealth and glory pushed outward through aggression, while the Irish monks seeking spiritual solitude retreated into the margins. Both approaches required the same fundamental willingness to leave the known world behind, suggesting that exploration is less about destination than about the act of leaving itself.

**Courage is not the absence of doubt but the refusal to let doubt stop you.** The penguin in Herzog's documentary "looked back briefly"—a detail Brownworth emphasizes as crucial. The Viking explorers were not fearless; they simply did not let fear determine their actions. The moment of decision involved consideration of the risks and a choice to proceed regardless. This distinction matters for understanding what real courage looks like in practice: not obliviousness to danger, but commitment despite awareness of it.

**The Viking spirit remains culturally relevant because it represents an ideal we still aspire to.** When we name our satellites, football teams, and cruise ships after Vikings, we are not merely invoking historical brand recognition. We are accessing a symbol of determination, of forward movement, of refusal to accept present limitations. The "flame that burns in all of us" Brownworth describes is the desire to push beyond our constraints, and the Vikings embody that desire in a particularly vivid historical form.

**Isolation and ambition are both paths to discovery.** The monks seeking closeness to God and the warriors seeking fortune and land found different things, but both found something beyond the known world. This suggests that the value of exploration is not limited to any single motivation. Those who seek escape may find what they need in remoteness; those who seek conquest may find it in the act of moving forward. Both paths involve turning away from the present arrangement of the world.

**"To strive, to seek, to find, and not to yield" remains a viable philosophy for modern challenges.** Tennyson's poem, written in the nineteenth century about a mythological Greek hero, still speaks to contemporary concerns. The refusal to accept present boundaries, the willingness to sail toward uncertain destinations with full knowledge of the risks—these attitudes remain admirable and perhaps increasingly necessary. The Vikings did not yield; neither did Ulysses. The question for modern audiences is whether they will make the same choice.
