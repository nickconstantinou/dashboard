# Why the Vikings Refused to Turn Back: The Psychology of Great Explorers

## Introduction

In the annals of human history, few civilizations captivate the imagination quite like the Vikings. Beyond the raids and warriors depicted in popular culture lies a more profound story—one of human beings who crossed vast oceans without compasses, who established trade routes spanning from North America to the Middle East, and who possessed an almost inexplicable drive to push beyond the boundaries of the known world. This is precisely what historian Lars Brownworth explores in a compelling discussion that probes the psychological foundations of Viking exploration.

Brownworth, known for his work bringing Byzantine and medieval history to life, approaches this topic not as a mere exercise in historical trivia, but as an investigation into something fundamental about human nature. The conversation he engages in moves fluidly between historical fact and philosophical reflection, asking why certain humans possess the audacity to shove off from familiar shores and sail toward uncertainty. The map of Viking ocean routes and river systems displayed during the discussion serves as visual evidence of just how far these Norse adventurers reached—connecting England, Spain, Italy, Russia, North Africa, the Middle East, and Paris through networks of maritime travel that predate modern navigation tools by centuries. Most remarkably, they reached North America fully 500 years before Columbus would make his famous voyage.

The question that emerges, and which Brownworth tackles with evident enthusiasm, is what exactly drove these people to explore the unknown. What made them different from the countless generations before and after who remained within the comfortable boundaries of their ancestral lands? The answer, as the discussion reveals, lies in a particular quality of the human spirit that the Vikings somehow embodied more completely than almost any other civilization in history.

## The Remarkable Story of Naddod and the First Voyagers

Brownworth opens his exploration of Viking psychology with a story that immediately captures the extraordinary nature of Norse exploration. He introduces the figure of Naddod, a Viking who holds the distinction of being the first Norseman to reach Iceland—though his arrival was entirely accidental. Here, Brownworth highlights what he describes as the "mind-blowing part" of this narrative: after landing on the island and deciding to explore, Naddod and his crew encountered human beings already living there. The shock of this discovery becomes palpable in the conversation, with Brownworth's dialogue partner audibly gasping at the revelation that follows.

These early inhabitants were monks from Ireland who had somehow made the journey in a skin boat—a canvas-covered craft far more fragile than the Viking longships that would later dominate the seas. Brownworth challenges his listener to truly consider the geography: looking at a map, one sees the considerable distance between Ireland and Iceland, a stretch of open ocean that these monks crossed not in response to any navigational goal, but simply because they were trying to get away from the world. They were seeking solitude, distance from sin, and a closer connection to God—and in doing so, they inadvertently discovered one of the most significant landmasses in the North Atlantic.

The response of these Irish monks to the arrival of the Vikings speaks volumes about the nature of Viking contact with the outside world. When the Norse explorers appeared, the monks made what Brownworth describes as "a very excellent move on their part"—they simply ran away. The humor in this observation highlights a deeper truth about Viking expansion: it was not a welcomed or gradual process but often a disruptive force that pushed existing inhabitants further toward the margins of the known world.

This initial story establishes a crucial framework for understanding Viking exploration: the interplay between those who went forth to raid and conquer and those who fled toward the unknown seeking something entirely different from what the Vikings sought. Both groups, however, shared a willingness to embrace the terrifying void of the unexplored.

## The Penguin That Walked Into the Mountains

To illuminate the psychological phenomenon at the heart of Viking exploration, Brownworth invokes an unexpected source: a Werner Herzog documentary about penguins. In this darkly humorous scene, Herzog provides overdub narration explaining the behavior of a penguin that has left its tribe and begun walking toward the mountains. As Herzog intones in his distinctive German-accented style, the penguin is moving toward certain death. The penguin does pause briefly, apparently contemplating its decision, before continuing onward.

Brownworth returns to this image repeatedly throughout the discussion, finding in it a perfect metaphor for the Viking spirit—or, as he also notes, the monk spirit—that drove certain individuals and small groups to simply go. The parallel is striking: just as the penguin abandons the safety and warmth of its community to walk toward inevitable destruction in the frozen mountains, the Vikings and the monks before them made decisions that defied rational calculation. They chose to go toward the unknown despite the very real possibility of sea monsters, of falling off the edge of a flat earth, of simply dying from exposure, starvation, or encounters with unknown peoples.

Brownworth notes that Herzog does not romanticize this penguin behavior—the filmmaker describes it as deranged and crazy, the actions of an animal that has somehow malfunctioned. Yet Brownworth sees in this seemingly irrational impulse something profoundly admirable. The penguin looked back briefly, considering its choice, before pressing forward into the void. That moment of hesitation followed by continued commitment captures, in Brownworth's view, the essential psychology of great explorers throughout history.

## Two Motivations for the Journey Into the Unknown

The discussion carefully delineates what Brownworth identifies as two distinct but complementary motivations for exploration. The first is the spirit of the warrior-adventurer—the hardcore individual who possesses an unstoppable drive to raid, to conquer, to keep pushing ever further into new territories. This is the Viking of popular imagination, the raider who struck terror into monasteries across Europe and established settlements that would eventually become permanent features of the landscape from Newfoundland to the Volga River.

The second motivation is altogether different in character but equally powerful. This is the desire to escape—to flee far away from civilization in pursuit of a spiritual goal. Brownworth describes this as monks wanting to leave because they believed that distance from the corrupting influences of society would bring them closer to God and to themselves. This motivation produced remarkable feats of navigation, as demonstrated by the Irish monks who paddled their skin boats northward to uninhabited islands, seeking not conquest but solitude.

What makes this framework particularly insightful is Brownworth's recognition that both motivations ultimately stem from the same fundamental impulse. The warrior who cannot stop raiding and the monk who cannot stop fleeing are both responding to a call that most human beings cannot hear, let alone answer. They are drawn toward the unknown not despite the dangers but in some sense because of them. The edge of the world, whether conceptualized as literal geographical boundary or metaphorical separation from the sacred, exerts a pull that transcends ordinary self-preservation.

## Ulysses and the Philosophy of Non-Yielding

Brownworth reveals that he considers Alfred Lord Tennyson's poem "Ulysses" to be his favorite poem, and he turns to it as perhaps the most perfect literary expression of the Viking spirit. The poem, which depicts the aged Greek hero refusing to accept a quiet retirement and declaring his intention to continue sailing until death, culminates in a stanza that Brownworth quotes with evident reverence:

> "To strive, to seek, to find, and not to yield."

The full context of Ulysses's declaration expands this philosophy into something grander and more demanding:

> "My purpose holds to sail beyond the baths of all the western stars until I die."

Brownworth draws a direct connection between these words and the Viking psychology he has been exploring throughout the discussion. The phrase "not to yield" captures something essential about what made the Norse explorers remarkable and continues to make them objects of fascination centuries after their civilization declined. They may die—the poem's speaker acknowledges this explicitly—but they will not stop. They will not turn back. They will not accept the safety and predictability of remaining within known boundaries.

This refusal to yield, Brownworth suggests, is what we admire most about human beings. It represents what he describes as "the flame that burns in all of us that we admire most"—that capacity to commit fully to a course of action despite uncertainty, fear, and the very real possibility of failure. The Vikings did not possess superhuman courage or extraordinary technology; what they possessed was an unyielding focus on moving forward into the unknown, into the scary, and never stopping.

## The Enduring Appeal of Viking Spirit in Modern Culture

The conversation concludes with Brownworth reflecting on why the Vikings remain so culturally prominent today, even millennia after their active period. He points to the numerous ways contemporary society has adopted Viking imagery and terminology: satellites bearing Norse names, football teams drawing on Viking heritage, cruise ships invoking the legendary voyages of the Northmen. This widespread appropriation of Viking identity suggests that something about these historical figures continues to resonate with modern sensibilities.

The explanation, Brownworth proposes, lies in the "romantic hook" of a people who did not yield. In a world where most human beings live within carefully defined boundaries—geographical, professional, psychological—the Vikings represent an alternative mode of existence. They demonstrate what human beings might achieve if they surrendered entirely to the drive to explore, to discover, to push beyond the margins of the known.

This romantic appeal should not be mistaken for historical revisionism. The Vikings were, by many accounts, brutal raiders who brought violence and destruction wherever they went. But their willingness to embrace the unknown, to accept death as the price of exploration, and to refuse the safer path of staying within established limits speaks to something deeper than their specific historical actions. They embody, in concentrated form, a quality that exists potentially within all human beings—the capacity for complete commitment to an uncertain goal.

## Key Takeaways

**The psychology of great exploration combines courage with purpose.** The Vikings were not simply reckless adventurers; they possessed a clear sense of why they were going and what they hoped to achieve. Whether motivated by raiding, trade, or spiritual seeking, they maintained what Brownworth describes as an "unyielding focus on going out there, of taking the leap into the unknown, into the scary, and never stopping." This combination of commitment and clarity distinguishes genuine exploration from mere wanderlust.

**Two distinct human impulses drive exploration: conquest and escape.** Brownworth's framework identifies the warrior's drive to expand and the mystic's desire to flee as parallel responses to the same underlying call. Both involve leaving the familiar, though they seek entirely different destinations. Understanding these motivations helps explain why such diverse groups—Irish monks in skin boats and Norse raiders in longships—could achieve comparable feats of navigation.

**The unknown exerts a pull that transcends rational self-preservation.** The image of the penguin walking toward certain death, or the monk paddling away from civilization toward an ice-locked island, illustrates a fundamental truth about human nature: some individuals and groups possess an almost irresistible attraction to the edge of the map. This impulse cannot be fully explained by practical considerations but must be understood as a characteristic of certain temperaments.

**The refusal to yield represents what we admire most in human beings.** Tennyson's phrase captures something essential about heroism and achievement. Brownworth suggests that this quality—the flame that burns against the wind, the determination to continue despite the likelihood of failure—constitutes our highest ideal of human capability. The Vikings remain popular precisely because they embodied this ideal so completely.

**Modern society continues to seek connection with the Viking spirit.** The widespread use of Norse names and imagery in contemporary culture—from space exploration to sports teams—reflects a deep-seated desire to access the qualities the Vikings represented. This enduring fascination suggests that many people today feel constrained by the boundaries of ordinary life and look to historical examples of bold exploration for inspiration.
