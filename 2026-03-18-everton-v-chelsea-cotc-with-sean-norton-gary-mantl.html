Everton v Chelsea | CotC with Sean Norton & Gary Mantle | Planet FPL 2025/26

Introduction

The latest edition of Planet FPL's Clash of the Correspondents delivers a detailed tactical and team news preview ahead of gameweek 31, as Everton's Sean Norton and Chelsea's Gary Mantle join host James to dissect the upcoming battle between two Premier League clubs navigating very different pressures at this stage of the season. The fixture itself represents a fascinating subplot in the broader European qualification and relegation battles, with both teams having plenty to play for as the campaign reaches its decisive phase.

What makes this particular matchup especially relevant for FPL managers is the confluence of injury crises affecting both squads, combined with recent tactical inconsistencies that could significantly impact player selection and performance. Sean Norton, covering all things Everton, provides crucial updates on injuries to key players including the mysteriously unavailable Sarovski and the potentially long-term abscent Jed. Meanwhile, Gary Mantle offers an inside perspective on Chelsea's troubling defensive frailties, the curious case of Reece James's contract extension and subsequent hamstring injury, and mounting questions about manager Liam Rosina's tactical approach. The conversation moves beyond simple team news to explore deeper structural issues that could define both teams' trajectories through the remaining fixtures.

Everton Injury Crisis Deepens

Sean Norton opens with an honest assessment of Everton's recent fortunes, acknowledging that his side was "a bit unlucky" at the Emirates Stadium against Arsenal, where goalkeeper Pickford committed an error that proved costly in a match they had looked capable of holding on for a valuable point. This setback adds to what has been a frustrating period for the Toffees, who now face additional selection headaches heading into the Chelsea fixture.

The injury situation at Everton remains shrouded in uncertainty, with Norton admitting that not a great deal is known about Sarovski's current condition. He hasn't seen any information whatsoever regarding what has afflicted the player, making it impossible to provide a timeline for his return. More concerning is the situation surrounding Jed, where Norton reveals there were "strong rumors from some decent sources on the internet" suggesting the player would miss at least two matches, though the prognosis could extend to four to six weeks given it appears to be another hamstring issue similar to what Kenan Yebsbury has experienced. Norton admits he was "quite intrigued" when hearing David Moyes's press conference, noting that the Everton manager's approach to disclosing injuries has become considerably more guarded since the Ramweight situation earlier in the season, which reportedly left the manager "really irritated" by how that injury information was handled.

Norton explains that since that incident, Moyes has adopted a policy of not revealing much information about injuries, leading to the current scenario where supporters and media are left speculating about the true extent of the problems. The situation with Bright, another key player, is described as "a bit concerning," though Norton emphasizes they will simply have to wait and see how it plays out. On the positive side, Bright has recently signed a long-term contract, which at least provides some security regarding his long-term future at the club despite the current injury setback.

Chelsea's Reece James Conundrum

Gary Mantle shifts focus to Chelsea's injury situation, which centers on Reece James's hamstring problem that occurred "basically the day after he they announced that Rhys James had a new contract." The timing of the injury has left Mantle puzzled, as he openly questions why the player was selected to start against Newcastle on Saturday given the circumstances. "I don't know why he played personally, I don't know why he started on Saturday," Mantle admits, expressing surprise at the decision-making.

The contract situation itself has sparked significant confusion at Stamford Bridge. Mantle highlights the apparent contradiction in the club's messaging: "One of the things that surprises me is that he signed his new contract on Friday and they announced it on Friday and Rosinia comes out and he says we've signed that we've tied down the best player in his position." The problem, as Mantle sees it, is determining which position they actually mean: "But which position does he mean? Cuz he started him in center midfield and then he played him at right back for the second half. So it's like, well, if you think he's the best right back, why are we playing him in center midfield? And he's definitely not the best central midfielder."

Initial reports suggest James will be sidelined for four to six weeks, which represents the standard timeline for hamstring injuries, though Mantle notes that "they don't know" the exact extent yet as he has gone for a scan. The player will definitely miss the important Champions League tie against Paris Saint-Germain on Tuesday night, with the pre-recording of this podcast taking place on Monday night. Mantle admits he isn't giving himself "any chance" of attending that match, having passed his ticket on to someone else due to the "effort" involved, particularly given the likelihood of being "a goal or two down and going to be like I want to go home."

Tactical Problems Under the Microscope

The conversation turns to Chelsea's broader tactical issues, with Gary Mantle offering a remarkably candid assessment of the problems that have plagued the club in recent weeks. The discussion begins with their Champions League performance against PSG, where Chelsea let slip a promising position to suffer defeat. Mantle notes the irony that "pretty much everything we laughed at Tottenham about on Tuesday night, we did exactly the same things ourselves on Wednesday night, including the score line," suggesting a worrying pattern of defensive capitulation against quality opposition.

What concerns Mantle most is the recurring nature of the goals Chelsea are conceding: "We seem to be letting in goals every week at the moment where the opposition just go straight through the middle of us." He cites specific examples including the goal against Rexom, the second goal against PSG, the goal at the weekend against Newcastle, and an away match at Aston Villa where the same pattern emerged, though it was ultimately disallowed for offside. "It's like this is just happening again and again and again," Mantle observes, highlighting the systemic nature of the problem rather than isolated individual errors.

The analysis becomes particularly detailed when examining the Newcastle goal, where Mantle describes how the players themselves seemed bewildered by what had happened: "When it went in, players weren't trying to appeal for offside. They were talking to each other going, 'Who's why have we let this happen? Why has he been allowed to run into center midfield unopposed? Why is Trevor Chala ended up at left back and Reese James now center back? Why is Wes Vana not chasing Anthony Gordon?'" This suggests a fundamental breakdown in defensive organization and communication that goes beyond simple tactical instruction.

Mantle explains the underlying mechanism: "That's the danger with man-to-man that if the pieces the puzzle get moved then yeah," noting how the system breaks down when players are forced out of their natural positions. He specifically describes how Noni Madueke's "great movement moving from like left eight to like almost like a right wing position and causes confusion," leading to Chalobah following him and ultimately resulting in "Reese that ends up at Yeah. sort of center back." The discussion acknowledges that this approach "is still working on something that's quite new, right? And it's obviously very aggressive all over the pitch."

Managerial Decisions Questioned

Beyond the defensive issues, Mantle turns his attention to questions about Liam Rosina's tactical philosophy and squad management. He acknowledges that the away performance at Villa was "a really good performance" where "we exploited all of their weaknesses" and played in a way that was "really happy to see," but notes "we haven't" kicked on from that baseline. The current setup is troubling: "I don't really like the way that we're setting up at the moment. We're playing Enzo Fernandez in the sort of 10 position, but what really bugs me about this is that, okay, so we did it away at PSG and we do it away at Arsenal and away at Villa, but we also do it at home to Leeds and at home to Burnley."

This creates a fundamental tactical problem in Mantle's view: "And it's like, how are we setting up the same for a home game against Leeds and Burnley as we are away to Arsenal and away to Paris Sanain? It doesn't make sense." He suspects the underlying issue may be that "Rosinho's got a plan for this is how we're going to hurt this team and this is how we might hurt that team" - suggesting a lack of tailored approaches for different opponents.

Furthermore, Mantle believes Rosina "doesn't trust a lot of the squad" because "we're just using the same sort of 12 to 13 players all the time," and now with the injury crisis, that limited pool has been further depleted. He draws a parallel to the previous regime under Maresca: "When we spoke prior we were saying one of the reasons Moresca was getting pulled out for was for potentially playing players longer than was being recommended by say doctors or physios or whatever." This raises questions about whether similar issues might be occurring at Chelsea now, with Reece James playing significant minutes before suffering his injury.

Season Expectations and Final Assessment

When pressed on what would constitute a successful end to the season for Chelsea, Mantle offers measured expectations: "A good end is still for if we came fifth or above." He acknowledges that FA Cup success would be well-received but recognizes the difficulty of that task: "The chances are we're going to have to have beaten somebody out of Arsenal, City, and Liverpool at some point if we're going to win the FA Cup." A sixth-place finish combined with an FA Cup run would be "probably acceptable," particularly given the extenuating circumstances surrounding Rosina's appointment.

Crucially, Mantle defends the manager to some extent, pointing out that "he hasn't had like a preseason, he's not really had any time to implement anything." This context suggests that the tactical issues might partially be attributed to the manager still bedding in his methods, though it remains to be seen whether improvements will materialize in the crucial fixtures ahead.

Detailed Takeaways

Injury Management Will Define Both Teams' FPL Value

The Everton injury situation represents a critical FPL consideration, with Jed potentially missing four to six weeks and Sarovski's status completely unknown. For FPL managers, this uncertainty makes it extremely difficult to rely on Everton assets, particularly given David Moyes's new policy of withholding injury information. The Bright situation adds another layer of complexity, though his new long-term contract at least provides some reassurance about his long-term value. Managers should be cautious about investing in Everton players until there is greater clarity on the injury situation.

Chelsea's Defensive System Poses Significant Risks

The recurring pattern of opponents cutting through Chelsea's middle defense presents both a concern for the team's results and an opportunity for FPL managers looking to target defensive assets from opposing teams. Everton managers should note that Chelsea have been vulnerable through the center, which could bode well for creative players in royal blue. For Chelsea assets, the chaotic defensive setup makes it difficult to recommend any defender with confidence, though the attacking players may benefit from the aggressive approach.

Reece James's Injury Creates Significant Chelsea Void

The timing and nature of Reece James's hamstring injury raises serious questions about Chelsea's squad depth and tactical flexibility. Having just signed a new contract and been described as "the best player in his position," his absence leaves a significant gap. For FPL purposes, this potentially opens up opportunities for other Chelsea defenders to stake a claim for regular minutes, though the defensive system's issues make it a risky proposition.

Tactical Inflexibility Could Cost Chelsea

Gary Mantle's critique of Rosina's approach - using identical setups for home games against weaker opposition and away games against top teams - suggests a fundamental tactical flaw that could limit Chelsea's ceiling this season. This inflexibility might explain why they've struggled to build momentum, and it represents something FPL managers should monitor when considering Chelsea's premium assets.

Squad Rotation and Trust Issues Abound

The revelation that Chelsea are relying on only 12-13 players, combined with the injury crisis, suggests significant depth issues. For FPL managers, this means that any Chelsea player who secures a regular starting spot could be valuable, but the rotation risk is high. The situation also raises questions about whether younger or less-established players might get opportunities if the injury crisis deepens further.
