Luke's Latest Draft | Sarr's the One! | Pre Season | Fantasy Premier League Tips 2025/26

A Comprehensive FPL Strategy Guide

---

Introduction

Fantasy Premier League managers entering the 2025/26 season face a uniquely challenging landscape. With transfer windows still open, striker situations in flux across multiple clubs, and pre-season friendlies providing only fragmentary data, the typical pre-season uncertainty has been amplified. Luke from FPL Blackbox, speaking at his channel or handle @this_fpl, has emerged as a voice of caution in this environment. Rather than chasing flashy, high-risk strategies that depend on untested signings, Luke is advocating for what he describes as his "most sensible" draft—a conservative approach that prioritizes stability over spectacular potential.

In this detailed breakdown, Luke walks through his current team selection, explaining the reasoning behind each position while acknowledging the caveats that could force him to make changes. The video serves as both a practical guide to building an FPL team and a philosophical treatise on risk management in FPL. Luke explicitly frames this as his "safety net" approach, the team he might actually end up going with come the season's start. This makes the video particularly valuable for managers who prefer data-driven caution over speculative gambles.

---

Goalkeeper Strategy: The Budget Philosophy

Luke's goalkeeper selection immediately establishes the theme of his entire draft: conservative, budget-friendly choices that free up resources for more impactful positions. He has selected Keller as his starting goalkeeper, explaining that he simply does not want to spend more than 4.5 million on the position. This decision reflects a broader philosophy about resource allocation in FPL—why invest heavily in a position where the difference between the best and budget options is often minimal?

The reasoning behind this specific choice becomes clearer when Luke mentions that Dravka, priced at 4.0 million, is likely to be the starting goalkeeper for his team. This means managers can effectively run two playing goalkeepers from the 4.5 and 4.0 price points, giving flexibility without significant investment. Luke acknowledges that Brentford has changed managers and conceded many shots last season, meaning there are no guarantees about clean sheets with Keller. However, he expresses hope that Keller can replicate the performance of Flecken from last season, suggesting he views this as a reasonable floor rather than a ceiling.

One particularly interesting element of Luke's goalkeeper discussion is his aversion to rotating keepers, which he admits he "always gets wrong." He mentions West Ham as a potential complication—while typically rotating well with Areola, the club appears to be in the market for another goalkeeper and may even sign one shortly. If that happens and the new signing is ready to play, it could become difficult to ignore them. But for now, Luke is content with his 4.5-million-pound commitment to Keller.

Luke also touches on the premium goalkeeper question, noting that in previous seasons he has started with premium options like Alison or Ryan, keeping them for entire campaigns. He acknowledges that these players virtually guarantee top-three scoring among goalkeepers outside of injury—a valuable security blanket. However, he argues that at the beginning of the season, when every pound is most valuable, utilizing a 4.5 option and addressing goalkeeper later if needed makes more sense. There's always a 4.5 or 5.0 million goalkeeper who either beats the premium options or gets very close, making early investment seem premature.

The Defensive Contribution Debate

Moving to defense, Luke reveals his strategy of focusing on what he calls "defensive contribution" players—specifically mentioning two players who earn defcons (defensive bonus points). He was previously a Sky player when that platform was operational, and collecting bonus points for defenders was central to his approach. However, he immediately acknowledges a significant challenge: clean sheets have become extremely difficult to obtain in the modern Premier League, making them a premium commodity that is very hard to collect consistently.

This is a crucial admission that shapes his entire defensive strategy. Rather than chasing clean sheets—which would require spending heavily on the best defensive teams—Luke prefers to leave his defenders in and hope they can replicate previous seasons' returns. He notes that even if these defenders don't repeat their previous performances, they can still provide valuable returns of three and four points regularly, which he would "take all day" compared to the twos and ones he often received from other defenders.

Luke emphasizes that attacking returns from defenders are not guaranteed even when they appear promising. A defender might be highly attacking, positioned high up the pitch, and look attractive on paper, but there's no certainty they will actually score goals and assists. This is why he prioritizes consistent defensive contributions over speculative attacking potential.

The specific defenders in Luke's team include Taroski and Marillo (likely references to Estupinan and Malacia or similar budget-friendly defensive options). Luke admits he is not completely set on Taroski, acknowledging the possibility that they cannot repeat last season's performance. However, he argues that players generally don't change their stripes too much—these players have the same setups, same managers, and same roles. They are "giant blockheads" who love getting involved, and it would surprise him if they couldn't achieve similar returns consistently. This repeatability makes them attractive despite the uncertainty.

The Porro Dilemma

Perhaps the most interesting defensive discussion centers on Porro from Tottenham. Luke admits he has "reservations" about Porro given Tottenham's poor performance last season. The club has undergone a manager change and signed Palinia as a defensive midfielder, which might improve things. But Tottenham were simply bad last season, making any Spurs defender a risky choice.

However, Luke immediately pivots to explain why Porro is still in his team: the fixtures are exceptional. When put into virtually any model with vaguely accurate minutes, Porro comes out as a top option for the first five or six weeks. While Luke has slight reservations, he asks himself "who am I to argue with it if everyone's saying that's going to be the case?" He genuinely likes Porro as a player and describes him as "fantastic."

The key factor that convinced Luke to include Porro is set pieces. With the unfortunate injury to Maddison, Porro's stock has been slightly boosted as he might take more set pieces. Luke cites a pre-season friendly against Bayern Munich where Tottenham lost 4-0, but when checking StatsBomb, the single big chance created by Spurs was actually created by Porro—demonstrating his creative ability.

Luke acknowledges that while his core strategy involves defensive contribution players, Porro might stay in the team long term because he offers genuine attacking value and set piece involvement. He describes this as "a decent bet" and confirms he will be keeping Porro to start the season, though he may discuss other players he prefers or simply couldn't fit in later in the video.

Midfield Investment: Stacking the Money

When Luke reveals his midfield, he humorously notes that people in various Discord communities have responded with pregnant woman emojis due to the sheer concentration of expensive players. The midfield is "stacked with all the money," and while he acknowledges he might be spending too much there, he views midfield as the place where points are earned and coin is made.

This philosophy reflects a key strategic decision: rather than spreading budget across all positions, Luke prioritizes midfield because the differentiation between cheap and expensive options is most pronounced in that area. There will always be cheaper midfielders who perform well, but identifying them at the season's start is "quite tough." By stacking the midfield with proven premium options, Luke reduces his reliance on finding budget gems that may or may not emerge.

This approach becomes clearer when examining his forward line, which appears underwhelming compared to his midfield investment. The theory is that funds saved on goalkeepers and defenders can be redirected to midfield, where the points-per-million ratio tends to be more favorable for expensive players.

---

The Transfer Market Uncertainty Problem

One of the most valuable discussions in this video concerns newly signed players and their immediate FPL viability. Luke makes a crucial observation that applies broadly to FPL strategy: when players are signed just before the season and expected to be starters for their new clubs, there's no guarantee they will start immediately. Every case is unique—some managers might say they need to work the player into their system, require a few days of training, or want to introduce them slowly to get them up to speed.

This creates a specific problem for FPL managers. If you have a newly signed player in your team and other managers don't, they will likely sell that player, causing their price to drop. You then face transfers you need to make, creating unnecessary friction at the season's start when the player base is most active. While Luke acknowledges this might not matter if the player returns soon and performs well, he doesn't want to carry any "worries at the beginning of the season."

This caution stems from personal experience. Luke reveals he was one of the managers who had Barco last season—though he "didn't fall for Quana" (likely reference to specific players), he did fall for Barco, and he doesn't want to be in a similar situation again. Things "crop up" in football, and playing as secure as possible means avoiding these transitional players.

Detailed Takeaways

Takeaway 1: Budget goalkeepers are viable at the start of the season. Luke's 4.5-million-pound goalkeeper strategy recognizes that premium goalkeepers like Alison provide security but at a cost that might not be justified early in the season. With a 4.0-million-pound option like Dravka likely to start, managers can run two budget keepers and address the position later if needed. This frees up funds for more impactful positions.

Takeaway 2: Clean sheet chasing in defense has become prohibitively difficult. Rather than investing heavily in defensive teams for clean sheets, Luke recommends focusing on defenders who consistently earn defensive bonus points regardless of clean sheets. Players like Taroski and Marillo provide a floor of returns that, while not spectacular, is reliable and preferable to the volatility of chasing clean sheets.

Takeaway 3: Fixture-based planning should override team-level concerns. Porro represents a case where Tottenham's poor underlying performance is overridden by exceptional fixtures. Even if Tottenham as a team struggled last season, Porro's individual numbers and set piece involvement in favorable early fixtures make him a strong starting option. Model consensus on fixtures can outweigh concerns about a team's general form.

Takeaway 4: Newly signed players should be avoided at the season's start. The specific risk of players not starting immediately, combined with price volatility caused by other managers selling them, makes recently-signed players poor early-season investments. This applies particularly to players moving to clubs where they need to integrate into new systems.

Takeaway 5: Midfield is the position to prioritize with premium investments. The concentration of point-scoring potential in expensive midfielders, combined with the difficulty of identifying cheap performers at the season's start, makes midfield the logical place to concentrate resources. This requires accepting weaker options in other positions, but the mathematical expectation favors this approach.

---

Who This Is For

This video and summary will be most valuable for FPL managers who prefer conservative, data-driven approaches to their team selection. If you are the type of manager who struggles with the temptation to chase every new signing or who wants a stable foundation before making transfers, Luke's "safety net" draft provides an excellent template.

The content is particularly useful for managers in "mini-leagues" (private FPL leagues with friends or colleagues) where consistency matters more than taking massive risks that might not pay off. Managers who have struggled with premium goalkeepers in previous seasons might find Luke's budget approach refreshing, while those who have been burned by newly signed players not starting immediately will appreciate his emphasis on avoiding that specific trap.

However, managers who prefer more aggressive, high-upside strategies might find this approach too conservative. Luke himself acknowledges this is his "boring" draft—the one he might actually end up using, but not the most exciting option available. If you enjoy taking calculated risks on emerging players or prefer to load up on premium forwards rather than midfielders, this video offers a counterpoint rather than a definitive answer.

For those in competitive leagues where every point matters, the philosophy of avoiding transfer headaches at the season's start—even at the cost of some expected points—could prove valuable. The flexibility to react to emerging situations rather than being locked into problematic selections provides its own kind of advantage that may manifest across the season's first few gameweeks.
