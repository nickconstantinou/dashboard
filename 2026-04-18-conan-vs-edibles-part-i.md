<p># Conan Vs. Edibles Part I: FP Harry's Gameweek 33 FPL Strategy Breakdown

## Introduction: The Final Push Begins

For Fantasy Premier League managers around the world, the 2023-24 season has reached its critical juncture. With only a handful of gameweeks remaining, every transfer decision, every chip deployment, and every lineup choice carries exponentially greater weight. In this episode of "Conan Vs. Edibles," FP Harry—renowned for his strategic approach to FPL management—delivers an in-depth analysis of his gameweek 33 strategy, explaining the reasoning behind his wildcard play in gameweek 32 and mapping out his transfer plans for the crucial stretch run.

The timing of this video couldn't be more relevant. With double gameweeks and blank fixtures scattered across the remaining schedule, managers face a complex puzzle: how to maximize points in the immediate term while preserving flexibility for the season's conclusion. FP Harry finds himself in a particularly interesting position, having deployed his wildcard a week earlier than many competitors, and now sitting with five free transfers burning a hole in his virtual pocket. This strategic positioning—achieved at the cost of just six points relative to his pre-wildcard team—sets the stage for what he hopes will be a decisive surge toward the finish line.

## The Wildcard Gamble: Why FP Harry Moved Early

The decision to play the wildcard in gameweek 32 rather than holding it for later gameweeks was not one FP Harry made lightly. In fact, he admits he wasn't initially set on using the chip at all, weighing the potential points sacrifice against the strategic benefits. The turning point came when he found himself with five free transfers heading into the gameweek—a resource he recognized as too valuable to simply use on marginal improvements.

"People will think, well, Harry, if you had five free transfers, why didn't you just use loads of transfers to make your team better and then wildcard later down the line?" FP Harry explains, anticipating the logical objection. His counterargument centers on the specific timing of Manchester City's double gameweek in gameweek 33, combined with another double in gameweek 34. By wildcarding in 32, he positioned himself to completely reshape his squad around these doubles, essentially using the chip as a targeted weapon rather than a panic button.

The execution proved imperfect but ultimately vindicated the strategy. FP Harry scored 55 points in gameweek 32—a total he describes as "saved by a couple of players," specifically naming Gabriel's nine-pointer and an impressive 14-pointer from Matt O'Reilly. His wildcard selections didn't perform exceptionally well overall, but they "kept it afloat" against teams that hadn't used their chips.

## The Six-Point Sacrifice: Acceptable Losses in FPL Strategy

The emotional rollercoaster of FPL was on full display during this gameweek. FP Harry recalls how Friday evening initially looked catastrophic: Jarrod Bowen, who would have been his transfer-in if he hadn't wildcarded, delivered a 14-pointer. Then on Saturday, Thiago scored a 13-pointer and Virgil van Dijk managed a nine-pointer. The early prognosis suggested FP Harry might have lost between 20 and 30 points compared to his non-wildcard team.

The Manchester City versus Chelsea match proved to be the deciding factor. When O'Reilly came in with a 14-pointer and Liverpool assets Brennan Johnson and burom didn't start or only came off the bench without a return, the damage control was remarkable. FP Harry "actually only lost out by six points" to his pre-wildcard team—far better than the 20-30 point catastrophe that had seemed possible.

This six-point loss translated to a red arrow, with FP Harry dropping from 36,000th place to around 40,000th in overall rankings. But he emphasizes that "losing out by six points is definitely not the end of the world," especially when the strategic positioning achieved makes the sacrifice worthwhile. The ability to bench boost in gameweek 33 and free hit in gameweek 34 represents a potential swing of 15-20 points or more, which would not only recover those six lost points but potentially create significant separation from competitors.

## Transfer Strategy: The Gabriel Dilemma

With five free transfers available, FP Harry identifies his first move as selling Gabriel to a Leeds defender. This transfer carries particular nuance because Gabriel, currently at Arsenal, will be blanking in gameweek 35 when the Gunners have no fixture. FP Harry plans to "buy Gabriel straight back into my team" after that blank, effectively making this a two-transfer investment.

The math, as FP Harry explains it, is relatively straightforward: "It's effectively a cost of two transfers. Now, they may effectively be worth eight points in terms of two minus fours later down the line." However, he doesn't consider this a negative because he has five transfers available and doesn't anticipate being forced to take hits as a result. The key condition is that the Leeds defender must outscore Gabriel by at least four or five points across the double gameweek 33 fixtures. If Gabriel concedes heavily away at Manchester City, as seems likely, FP Harry will be "pretty happy" with the decision.

The recommendation isn't universal, however. FP Harry explicitly states that managers with fewer transfers—one, two, or even three available—shouldn't consider this move worthwhile. The transfer only makes sense when you have sufficient depth to absorb the two-week investment without taking hits elsewhere in the squad.

## Leeds Defender Showdown: Struijk vs. Byram and the Rotation Risk

The Leeds defender decision presents its own complexity. FP Harry is considering Pascal Struijk as the primary option, with Byram as the alternative. He's also briefly mentioning possibilities like Sam Gomeson, Leon Chelsworth, or James Justin, but keeps returning to the Struijk versus Byram debate as the core choice.

The fundamental question is about attacking returns versus defensive clean sheets. As FP Harry notes, these Leeds defenders need to accumulate "at least two assists to outweigh Struijk or Byram getting Defcon in just one or two of those games." If the Leeds defenders pick up clean sheet bonus points across three out of four fixtures while the attacking alternatives only manage one assist each, the extra attacking potential doesn't justify the switch.

There's also the rotation risk to consider. Leeds have an FA Cup semi-final against Chelsea scheduled for the Sunday following gameweek 33. If Leeds win at home to Wolves and Tottenham lose to Brighton, Leeds would be nine points clear of the relegation zone heading into the semi-final. This could lead to squad rotation in the away fixture at Bournemouth, as FP Harry explains: "I don't think it's likely, particularly for the center backs, but they are almost certainly nailed as long as Rodon remains out for Leeds going into this gameweek."

Pascal Struijk emerges as the safer long-term option because he's less rotation-prone and more likely to maintain his place even if rotation occurs. FP Harry also notes a small but interesting possibility: Struijk took a penalty in the FA Cup against West Ham. While he believes Kalvin Phillips will be the primary penalty taker, there's "a small window of opportunity that Struijk might end up on penalties" if Phillips doesn't play the full 90 minutes regularly.

## The O'Reilly Situation: Manchester City's Defensive Dilemma

The most complex decision FP Harry faces concerns Matt O'Reilly, who is currently a doubt for the upcoming fixtures. The Manchester City midfielder has been "by far probably the second-best Manchester City player to own at the moment" according to FP Harry, who goes so far as to suggest O'Reilly might be "a better pick than Erling Haaland" given the striker's recent form.

The uncertainty creates a strategic headache. O'Reilly is a doubt going into the weekend, and the press conference from Pep Guardiola is scheduled for 12:30 PM on Friday, providing approximately 24 hours to react before match deadlines. FP Harry suspects Guardiola "isn't fully transparent about that injury given how big the game is for City," referencing the importance of the upcoming fixtures in Manchester City's title chase.

The core dilemma is whether to keep O'Reilly and hope for positive injury news, or sell him now as part of the bench boost activation and buy him back later. FP Harry leans heavily toward keeping O'Reilly, even if he's a slight doubt for Arsenal and might only play against Burnley afterward. The reasoning centers on the upcoming Manchester City double against Crystal Palace potentially in gameweek 36: "I want O'Reilly in my team over one of the others."

FP Harry draws a parallel to a previous FPL season when managers faced a similar decision between Ederson and Rúben Dias for Manchester City. Many chose Ederson for perceived nailed status, only to watch Dias score multiple goals in the final matches. "I feel like that is a possibility between O'Reilly compared to the likes of Nunez and Marge," FP Harry explains, suggesting the potential swing between keeping the right asset versus selling for an inferior alternative could be massive.

## City Defender Alternatives: Nunez vs. Gvardiol

If FP Harry does need to sell O'Reilly due to injury, he's considering two main alternatives: Matheus Nunes and Joško Gvardiol. The comparison reveals interesting trade-offs in their attacking profiles.

Gvardiol's defensive clean sheet numbers "look inflated because of how he did at Crystal Palace," FP Harry notes, adding that "once he's come into Manchester City he's not done that well." However, Gvardiol represents "a massive threat from set pieces," which could be valuable against certain opponents.

Nunes offers different characteristics. His attacking returns over recent weeks "don't look quite as good once Turkishk's come into the team," but his underlying baseline bonus points suggest he's still contributing meaningfully to Manchester City's attack. The comparison is "quite close" according to FP Harry, with neither option clearly superior to O'Reilly as a defensive midfielder.

If O'Reilly isn't injured long-term—which FP Harry says "doesn't sound like it's going to be a seasonal long injury"—then neither Nunes nor Gvardiol represents an attractive sell option. The safer play is to wait for confirmation on O'Reilly's status before making any defensive transfers from Manchester City.

## Looking Ahead: The Road to the Season's Conclusion

FP Harry's strategy reveals a manager thinking multiple moves ahead. His immediate plans include activating the bench boost in gameweek 33, using the free hit in gameweek 34, and navigating the blank in gameweek 35 before potentially bringing Gabriel back into his squad. Further down the line, he's considering bringing in Anthony Gordon from Newcastle, though "after their performance in gameweek 32," he's less certain about that particular investment.

The broader philosophy centers on using the five available transfers aggressively rather than conservatively. With "loads of transfers" available and not many squad spaces that need urgent addressing later in the season, FP Harry feels comfortable making moves like selling Gabriel even though they'll require future transfers to undo.

The bench boost represents the week's primary opportunity. FP Harry is counting on his strongest lineup to deliver "somewhere between 15 and 20 points on the bench boost" that he can bank while competitors who didn't wildcard face difficult choices about who to start from their squads. Combined with the free hit the following gameweek, the wildcard investment in gameweek 32 could generate a 30-40 point swing over the next two weeks.

## Detailed Takeaways

**The Wildcard Timing Trade-off Is About Opportunity Cost, Not Just Points**

FP Harry's decision to wildcard in gameweek 32 despite having five free transfers demonstrates a key FPL principle: chips have strategic windows based on fixture congestion. Using the wildcard earlier to target specific double gameweeks can be more valuable than saving it as emergency insurance. The six-point sacrifice was acceptable because it positioned him to extract maximum value from City defenders and Leeds assets across gameweeks 33 and 34—value that would far exceed the initial cost.

**Rotation Risk Demands Context Beyond Surface-Level Fixtures**

The Leeds defender analysis shows how fixture lists alone don't determine transfer value. The FA Cup semi-final creates a potential rotation scenario that could affect Bournemouth availability even though the double gameweek looks attractive on paper. Managers should always consider the broader matchweek context—including cup competitions, league position, and manager tendencies—when evaluating assets from clubs with meaningful external commitments.

**Two-Transfer Investments Can Be Worthwhile With Sufficient Resources**

Selling Gabriel to a Leeds defender while planning to repurchase him in gameweek 35 costs two transfers but could net eight points of advantage in the double gameweek. This strategy only works when managers have enough transfers to absorb the double cost without taking hits elsewhere. The key question isn't whether the transfer costs points, but whether the manager has the depth to execute it without compromising other positions.

**Injury Doubts Require Weighing Certainty Against Opportunity**

O'Reilly's status creates a genuine dilemma: selling him means guaranteed bench boost points but potentially missing out on massive returns if he plays. Keeping him ties up a squad spot but preserves access to one of the season's best assets. FP Harry's conclusion—keep him unless the injury is confirmed as serious—reflects a preference for option value over certainty, recognizing that the second City double could be decisive.

**Chip Combinations Create Compound Advantages**

The bench boost and free hit sequence represents a compound opportunity. Activating bench boost in gameweek 33 allows FP Harry to field his strongest possible lineup while potentially leaving high-value but bench-prone assets like Gabriel on the bench. The free hit in gameweek 34 then provides flexibility to completely reshape the squad for whatever fixtures follow. This sequencing—made possible by wildcarding in gameweek 32—creates multiple layers of strategic advantage over managers who used their chips differently.
