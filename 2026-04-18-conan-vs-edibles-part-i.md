## Introduction

Fantasy Premier League managers are approaching the business end of the 2024-25 season, and for serious competitors, the decisions made in the final stretch can mean the difference between a memorable finish and a forgettable campaign. In this episode of his ongoing FPL series, content creator FP Harry walks viewers through his meticulous thought process heading into gameweek 33—a crucial double gameweek that could significantly impact his rank and overall season outcome.

Harry's situation is unique and worth examining closely. He played his wildcard chip in gameweek 32, a decision that initially appeared questionable when key assets like Jarrod Bowen (14 points), Thiago (13 points), and Virgil van Dijk (9 points) all delivered strong returns on the bench. ## The Wildcard Decision: Timing and Rationale

When FP Harry initially considered playing his wildcard in gameweek 32, he wasn't entirely convinced it was the right move. The prevailing wisdom among many FPL managers suggests holding chips as long as possible, but Harry recognized that his specific circumstances demanded a different approach. Having accumulated five free transfers entering GW32, he faced a strategic fork in the road that required careful consideration of the broader fixture landscape.

The reasoning behind his eventual decision to wildcard was rooted in targeting the double gameweeks 33 and 34. Harry understood that by playing the wildcard in GW32, he could construct a squad optimized for the enhanced fixture schedule while preserving his transfers for the crucial period beginning in gameweek 35. This approach would give him approximately four to five transfers to work with once the double gameweeks concluded and the blanks began appearing more frequently.

The alternative—saving the wildcard and using transfers to improve his team—would have left him with limited flexibility once the season's final stretch arrived. Most managers who wildcarded in GW32 would find themselves with depleted transfer resources when the closing weeks demanded active squad management. Harry's strategy essentially front-loaded his wildcard to capture the double gameweek value while positioning himself to make informed, unpunished moves once the full picture became clearer.

## Gameweek 32 Results: Damage Assessment

The actual gameweek 32 proved to be a rollercoaster of emotions for Harry. His pre-wildcard team featured Jarrod Bowen, who delivered an exceptional 14-point haul on Friday evening. On Saturday, both Thiago Silva (13 points) and Virgil van Dijk (9 points) returned strongly, making Harry's decision to wildcard look increasingly costly. By Saturday evening, models suggested he had potentially lost between 20 and 30 points compared to his non-wildcard team—a devastating scenario that threatened to derail his season.

However, fate intervened through the Manchester City versus Chelsea match. O'Reilly, who Harry had transferred into his wildcard team, delivered a magnificent 14-point performance. Meanwhile, Ben White and Bukayo Saka—who remained in Harry's squad from before the wildcard—failed to return, effectively saving him from even greater losses. The overall damage amounted to just six points lost to his pre-wildcard alternative, which still included the Bowen transfer Harry would have made.

This narrow margin allowed Harry to maintain his rank around the 40,000 mark, having dropped from approximately 36,000. While a red arrow is never ideal, losing a mere six points when expecting to drop 20-30 represents a fortunate outcome that vindicated his strategic thinking. More importantly, the wildcard enabled him to bench boost in gameweek 33—a chip activation he calculates could yield between 15 and 20 points if executed correctly. Even accounting for the six-point deficit from GW32, a successful bench boost could transform his position entirely, potentially pushing him into positive territory and setting up an advantageous run-in.

## The Gabriel-to-Leeds Defender Transfer

Among Harry's five available transfers, the most straightforward involves selling Arsenal's Gabriel to acquire a Leeds United defender. This move targets Leeds' favorable double gameweek 33 fixture, which includes matches against Manchester City and potentially another favorable opponent. Harry acknowledges this isn't a transfer he would recommend if he had fewer transfers available, as it essentially costs two transfers: one to sell Gabriel and another to bring him back in gameweek 35.

The reasoning behind this move stems from Leeds' defensive fixtures and clean sheet potential during the double gameweek. If Gabriel's Arsenal face challenging fixtures while Leeds have a double, the point differential between the two defenders could exceed the four-point hit taken for the two transfers. Harry estimates that even a four or five-point advantage for the Leeds defender would make the move worthwhile, provided Gabriel concedes away at Manchester City.

Harry explicitly recognizes that without multiple available transfers, this strategy becomes less attractive. The luxury of having five transfers transforms what could be a risky proposition into a calculated opportunity to maximize double gameweek returns. This illustrates an important FPL principle: chip timing and transfer strategy must align with your available resources, and having excess transfers enables aggressive plays that wouldn't make sense under constraints.

## The Leeds Defender Dilemma: Struijk vs. Bijol

Selecting the appropriate Leeds defender presents its own complexity. Harry identifies Pascal Struijk and Kristoffer Olsson (Bijol) as the primary candidates, with Jordan Bogle and James Justin as less-preferred alternatives. The decision between Struijk and Bijol requires balancing several factors: defensive clean sheet potential, attacking returns, rotation risk, and long-term value.

The mathematical threshold for choosing the more attacking option is stringent. Harry explains that for Bogle or Goodmanson to outweigh Struijk or Bijol, they would need to register at least two assists across the double gameweek. If the Leeds defenders accumulate three or four clean sheets between them while the attacking alternatives manage only one assist, the mathematically superior choice is clear: stick with the defensive option.

However, rotation risk complicates this calculation. Leeds face an FA Cup semi-final against Chelsea immediately following their league fixtures, and if Leeds accumulate a comfortable points buffer over the relegation zone by beating Wolves at home while other results go their way, manager pressure might prompt squad rotation for the Bournemouth match. Harry doesn't believe this is particularly likely for center-backs, but it's a factor worth monitoring.

Rodon's injury status looms large over this decision. The Leeds defender has been absent, and his return would threaten Bijol's starting position. Harry prefers Struijk as the safer long-term option precisely because his place in the starting eleven appears more secure if Rodon returns in gameweek 35. Additionally, there's a small possibility that Struijk could take penalty duties if Calvin Bassey doesn't play the full ninety minutes every week—a potential bonus that adds marginal value to the Struijk selection.

The attacking threat between Struijk and Bijol appears roughly equivalent, making safety and long-term nailedness the deciding factors. Harry concludes that unless there's a long-term setback to Rodon meaning Bijol would be guaranteed starting from GW35 onwards, Struijk represents the slightly better choice for his squad structure.

## O'Reilly's Fitness: The Most Critical Decision

Manchester City's Rúben Dias remains unavailable, making O'Reilly—Manchester City's young phenom—the most valuable City defender to own. Harry considers O'Reilly potentially better than both Erling Haaland and other premium options, describing him as "by far probably the second-best Manchester City player to own at the moment." This assessment reflects O'Reilly's exceptional underlying statistics and his involvement in both defensive and attacking phases.

The complication enters through O'Reilly's injury status heading into gameweek 33. Harry reports no substantial update from Manchester City, with Pep Guardiola's press conference scheduled for Friday afternoon—approximately 24 hours before the deadline. This timing provides a narrow window to assess O'Reilly's availability before finalizing team selection.

Harry expresses concern that Guardiola may not be fully transparent about the injury given the magnitude of City's fixtures. This uncertainty creates a genuine dilemma: does he transfer O'Reilly out if there's a chance he's unavailable, or does he hold and risk losing a valuable chip activation slot?

The historical parallel Harry draws is instructive. He recalls a previous season when managers debated between Ederson (for guaranteed minutes) and Kyle Walker or other Manchester City defenders. Many chose the safer option, only for Walker to deliver massive returns in the final matches while the conservative choice underperformed. Harry sees echoes of that situation in the current O'Reilly dilemma.

His inclination is to keep O'Reilly unless there's definitive confirmation of a significant injury. The reasoning extends beyond the immediate gameweek: City have a second double against Crystal Palace potentially in gameweek 36, and with the title race likely going down to the wire, O'Reilly's involvement will be critical. Selling him now could mean missing out on significant points while paying additional transfer costs to reacquire him later.

## City Defender Alternatives: Nunez vs. Mares

If forced to transfer O'Reilly out as part of the bench boost activation, Harry identifies two primary alternatives: Matheus Nunes and Jeremy Doku (Mares). This decision requires evaluating their recent form, underlying statistics, and set-piece involvement.

Nunes, by contrast, has shown better attacking returns in recent weeks, though his underlying baseline bonus points look less impressive since Erling Haaland's integration into the team. The comparison becomes nuanced: Mares offers set-piece guaranteed value but may not replicate his earlier-season defensive numbers, while Nunes provides more consistent underlying returns but lacks the explosive potential of set-piece involvement.

Harry's chip deployment follows a logical sequence that his wildcard decision in GW32 enabled. Following the double, gameweek 34 presents a blank gameweek where many teams have no fixtures. Harry will deploy his free hit chip to construct a temporary squad of players with matches, then transfer back to his primary team for the run-in. This approach represents standard optimal play for the period between double gameweeks and the season's conclusion.

The key insight is Harry's plan to preserve four to five transfers for gameweek 35 onwards. This represents a significant advantage over managers who wildcarded later and will find themselves with limited resources when making crucial decisions about squad composition for the final matches. The strategy demonstrates how chip timing creates ripple effects throughout the season—the wildcard in GW32 wasn't just about that gameweek but about setting up favorable conditions for every subsequent decision.
