Introduction

Gameweek 32 represents a critical inflection point—a week where managers must commit to their chip strategy for the remainder of the season, or risk leaving valuable weapons unused at the most crucial moment.

FPL Harry, a prominent FPL content creator known for his strategic analysis and transparent approach to team management, finds himself in an enviable but complicated position. With his wild card, free hit, and bench boost all still available, he possesses significant flexibility heading into the season's business end. Key Concepts and Arguments

The Chip Strategy Dilemma

Harry opens by acknowledging that gameweek 32 is "probably the biggest week left of the entire season for our decision-making" because it's the moment managers must finalize their chip deployment strategy for the final seven weeks. His original plan had been to play his free hit in gameweek 33, his wild card in gameweek 35, and his bench boost in gameweek 36—a conservative approach that would preserve his strongest chip (the wild card) for thedouble gameweeks while using the free hit to navigate a potentially tricky fixture schedule.

However, the accumulation of five free transfers has complicated this thinking. Harry explains that the chance of him wild carding in gameweek 32 has "definitely grown" from approximately 25% (1 in 4) to roughly 33% (1 in 3), though he still believes the most likely outcome is that he sticks with his original plan. The key factor driving this shift is the flexibility that five free transfers provides—he could potentially make significant improvements to his squad without sacrificing a chip, while still preserving the wild card for a future double gameweek.

Despite this growing temptation, Harry maintains that his team looks "too good" to wild card in gameweek 32, particularly because his squad is well-suited to the fixtures in both gameweeks 32 and 34. This illustrates a crucial FPL principle: sometimes the best move is no move at all, especially when your current team already matches the fixture landscape well.

Gameweek 31 Performance Review

Before diving into current decisions, Harry provides a quick recap of gameweek 31, which he describes as occurring "a very, very long time ago before all the chaos of the FA Cup, before chip strategy talk, before the international break." He achieved a Green Arrow, climbing approximately 10,000 places—a solid result that exceeded his safety score by five points, finishing with 65 points. While he admits his team "didn't look too great" on paper, the results worked out reasonably well.

His captain, Bruno Fernandes, delivered as the highest scorer, though Harry notes that "everyone seemed to captain him on Monday night," meaning most managers would have had similar success with the armband. Harry Wilson also returned with a score, while James Taroski—Harry's designated start—managed only six points, representing a modest underperformance. The takeaway here is that gameweek 31 was "pretty good" rather than exceptional, demonstrating the sometimes random nature of FPL scoring.

Gabriel, having played for Arsenal in the Champions League on Tuesday evening, is expected to be available for the Bournemouth match with no real concerns about rotation.

However, he still favors Taroski because Richards "hasn't really been hitting clean sheets that much recently," and despite the tough fixture against Brentford's attacking play, Taroski's underlying defensive metrics (the "Defcon" rating Harry references) make him the preferred option.

The Defender Upgrade Question

With five free transfers available, Harry considers whether to upgrade his defense. He identifies two primary options: Emerson Palmieri of West Ham (at home to Wolves on Friday night) or Diogo Dalot of Manchester United (at home to Leeds). However, he expresses uncertainty about whether either represents a significant enough upgrade over Taroski to justify using a transfer: "I don't think the clean sheet probabilities combined with that much attacking threat are really that beneficial." He concludes his transfer is "probably better used elsewhere," indicating he'd rather invest in upgrading his midfield or forward positions.

Midfield Evaluation

Harry's midfield features Bruno Fernandes and Alejandro Garnacho in a Manchester United double-up, facing Leeds at home. He admits slight concern about Garnacho's minutes, expressing some doubt about whether the young winger will play more than 70 minutes, but he's keeping him in the side for now.

The interesting decision comes in Harry's cheap midfield options. He's benching Elliot Anderson at home to Aston Villa (a favorable fixture) while starting Harry Wilson away at Liverpool—the more difficult fixture but "probably the best pick" according to Harry. He explains this choice by noting Liverpool played in the Champions League on Wednesday night and have another Champions League match next Tuesday, meaning Wilson's team (presumably Nottingham Forest) might catch Liverpool fatigued. Additionally, "Wilson [is] in very good form" and "Liverpool [are] not looking great defensively."

Harry acknowledges he might "bench Harry Wilson" in favor of "Drewsbury Hall" (the transcript likely means Dwight McNeil of Burnley), but he's gone with Wilson because both Wilson and McNeil have away fixtures, and Wilson's form and Liverpool's defensive vulnerabilities make him the better option. He's also benching Chris Richards, meaning he's not overexposed to the Brentford vs. Everton fixture where he already has Taroski starting and would potentially add more players.

The Forward Dilemma

Harry's front three features Erling Haaland away at Chelsea, along with Ekit and Dominic Solanke (the transcript appears to have "Ecat" which likely means Ekit Hale or similar). He identifies Ekit as "the one that comes with the most amount of risk," explaining that Manchester City play Champions League football on Tuesday (after playing Wednesday night against Real Madrid) with a Saturday fixture at home to Fulham in between, followed by the Manchester derby against Everton in gameweek 33. While Harry expects Ekit to start all these games, there's a risk that rotation could impact his minutes, particularly with the Champions League implications.

He notes that Alexander Isak is "back in contention for Liverpool" but doesn't think Isak will start any games "over the short-term horizon," making this less relevant to immediate decisions.

The Likely Transfer

Harry has to make a move with his five free transfers, and he identifies the most likely outcome as replacing Ekit with Jarrod Bowen of West Ham. His reasoning combines multiple factors: West Ham have a favorable fixture at home to Wolves in gameweek 32, while in gameweek 34, Liverpool host Crystal Palace whereas Bowen has Everton at home—Bowen's fixture is "slightly better" than Ekit's. Additionally, Harry notes that once "Alexander Each comes closer to being back to full fitness in a few weeks," Ekit will "continue to be a minutes risk," making Bowen the safer long-term option that also provides immediate fixture advantage.

He acknowledges other options he's considered: selling Taroski (unlikely given Wolves' improved recent form), selling Anderson or Richards to bench Wilson, or selling Ekit for a different premium forward. But Ekit to Bowen represents "the most likely" move given the fixture swings and injury timeline.

Holding Haaland

One key strategic element Harry highlights is his decision to retain Erling Haaland, who faces Chelsea away this week. He notes that "most people or quite a lot of people actually sold Haaland a couple of weeks ago before the blank gameweek to buy MoSalah in order to sell him then to Anthony Gordon." By holding Haaland, Harry maintains "a bit of shield against the people playing their wild card in gameweek 32"—essentially, he's protected against the differential success of managers wildcarding this week who might have sold Haaland for other assets.

Detailed Takeaways

1. Preserving the Wild Card for Double Gameweeks Remains Optimal—Usually

Harry's analysis reinforces a fundamental FPL principle: the wild card is most valuable when deployed during weeks with double fixtures or particularly favorable fixture swings. His reluctance to use the wild card in gameweek 32 despite having five free transfers demonstrates that sometimes accumulated transfers can solve problems without sacrificing your biggest weapon. The exception, which Harry acknowledges exists at roughly 33% probability, comes when your team is so poorly matched to upcoming fixtures that even multiple transfers can't solve the problem. For most managers, sticking to a planned chip strategy through the double gameweeks remains the most mathematical approach.

2. Fixture Fatigue Is a Real Differential Factor

Harry's decision to start Harry Wilson over other options specifically references Liverpool's Champions League schedule—midweek European fixtures followed by quick league turnarounds create fatigue that can impact defensive performance. This represents sophisticated thinking beyond simple fixture difficulty ratings: managers who identify teams playing multiple competitions can find undervalued assets facing tired defenses. Wilson's good form combined with Liverpool's fixture congestion made him the choice despite the tougher underlying fixture.

3. Form and Attacking Threat Outweigh Pure Clean Sheet Probability

When evaluating his defensive options between Taroski and Richards, Harry doesn't simply choose the team with better clean sheet odds. Instead, he considers "Defcon" ratings—underlying defensive metrics that capture more than just whether a team kept a clean sheet. Richards' recent struggles with these metrics, combined with Crystal Palace's likely prioritization of the Conference League, made Taroski the preferred option despite the theoretical difficulty of playing Brentford away. This suggests managers should look beyond basic defensive rankings and consider recent underlying performance and fixture congestion.

4. Transfer Value Depends on Future Fixtures, Not Just Current Ones

Harry's reasoning for Ekit to Bowen isn't just about gameweek 32—it's about the entire remaining schedule. Bowen has Everton at home in gameweek 34, which is "slightly better" than Liverpool's fixture against Crystal Palace. Additionally, with Alexander Isak approaching fitness, Ekit will become a "minutes risk" as Manchester City manage their striker rotation. This demonstrates that successful FPL management requires thinking multiple weeks ahead, not just reacting to immediate opportunities.

5. Holding Premium Players Provides Protection Against Wildcarders

By retaining Haaland while others sold him to buy Mohamed Salah before subsequently moving to Anthony Gordon, Harry maintains "a bit of shield against the people playing their wild card in gameweek 32." This is a subtle but important strategic element: if wildcarders have invested in differential picks, holding the premium assets they might have sold provides a floor against their potential success. It's not about Haaland being the best pick every week, but about maintaining portfolio balance against manager populations making different decisions.
