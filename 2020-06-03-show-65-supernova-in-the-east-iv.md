## Introduction

In the fourth installment of his sweeping "Supernova in the East" series examining the war in Asia and the Pacific between 1931 and 1945, Dan Carlin of *Hardcore History* turns his meticulous attention to questions that have haunted military strategists since ancient times: What separates the measurable from the immeasurable in warfare? Can a nation's spirit be broken from the sky? And what does it take to rally a people through their darkest hour?

These aren't merely academic concerns. They represent the difference between understanding history and merely cataloging it. Carlin, ever the thoughtful analyst, uses this episode to explore the psychological dimensions of total war—the aspects that resist laboratory proof yet demonstrably shape outcomes on battlefields and in war rooms alike. The timing could not be more apt for American listeners, as Carlin draws direct parallels between the shock of 1941 and the psychological impact of September 11th, creating a lens through which modern audiences can viscerally understand what it meant to be an American absorbing the worst military defeats in the nation's history while wondering if the cause itself was lost.

This episode marks a shift in the series' focus from battlefield tactics to the invisible war being waged in the hearts and minds of populations, leaders, and ordinary citizens caught in the machinery of global conflict. It asks us to consider not just where the bullets flew, but where the real battles were won and lost.

---

## The Quantifiable and the Unquantifiable

Carlin opens with a fundamental distinction that runs through both world wars like a fault line: the difference between what can be measured and what cannot. In the early stages of any major conflict, prewar promises about ships, aircraft, and land-based systems face what Carlin calls "the acid test of combat"—the moment when theory meets reality. The good news about these quantifiable elements is that they provide clear evidence. If an airplane fails to perform, you know it quickly, and that failure can be used to build something better.

But Carlin argues this is only half the story. The other half concerns goals that are "much harder to measure." How do you know if morale is working or not? How do you know whether to continue doing something when you can't prove its effectiveness either way? These questions sit at the heart of understanding wartime strategy, yet they resist the kind of certainty that factory production numbers or casualty counts provide.

The tension here is not merely philosophical. It has profound practical implications for how nations wage war, allocate resources, and assess their progress. When something cannot be proven, Carlin notes, you might not be able to prove it in a laboratory, but it has "been shown to be true enough times" that you could say it reliably. Morale falls into this category—it cannot be quantified with precision, yet its importance on tactical battlefields has been recognized by military strategists since the Bronze Age.

---

## The Great Morale Question

The central question Carlin explores is whether what applies on a tactical battlefield scales up to strategic and national levels. On the battlefield, the goal is never to kill everybody—it's to kill enough people to break their opponents' will to continue fighting. Killing is a means to an end; destroying morale is the objective. This much seems self-evident. But does morale's importance translate when we're talking about entire nations?

Carlin frames this as an "open question in the second World War" with deeply divided opinions. Some strategists believed national morale could be broken. Others insisted it could not. Still others believed it could be broken, but only through specific methods. The debate had real consequences for how the war was fought, particularly in the air.

Much of the theoretical justification for strategic bombing of cities derived from this question. The idea was simple: bomb populations hard enough, and they would petition their governments for peace. They would tell their leaders, "We must have peace now. We can't be bombed anymore." This belief powered the development of bomber fleets and the expansion of bombing campaigns across Europe and Asia.

The results, however, complicated the theory dramatically. When Germany bombed London, the expected capitulation never came. Instead of breaking British morale, the bombing produced something closer to the opposite effect. Londoners weren't ready to give in; they were, as Carlin puts it, "pissed off." This created a crisis for bombing advocates: does this failure prove the theory wrong, or does it simply mean the technology hasn't advanced enough, the bombs aren't powerful enough, or enough cities haven't been hit yet?

Carlin is careful not to declare the question settled. "It means the jury is still out," he says. The same debates continued throughout the war and beyond, with some theorists believing that nuclear weapons would finally provide the means to break morale at the grand strategic level. The question of what actually breaks a nation's will—and whether such a thing even occurs—remained contested long after the war ended.

Germany's final months offered what might be seen as evidence for the bombing advocates' case. When Germany was bombed to rubble, there was "something more akin to numbness creeping into the population rather than a sense that they wanted the war to end at all costs or that they were ready to give in." But Carlin immediately complicates this interpretation: by that point, all German industry had been destroyed. The bombing wasn't working on morale; it was working on production capacity. "You didn't bomb them for morale reasons then," he observes. "You just leveled them. And if you wanna say it worked, okay, but let's be honest about why."

This gets to the heart of the problem Carlin identifies: because we know morale is important at some level, and because you can "make up anything you want about how it's important at a giant level," how much effort should be devoted to targeting it? The difficulty of measuring psychological effects leaves enormous room for theory, conjecture, and potentially misallocated resources.

## Fear, Morale, and the Complexity of Human Response

Carlin draws an illuminating connection between morale and fear, noting that the two are not the same thing. Bombing a city might create fear, but fear doesn't necessarily mean unwillingness to fight. Indeed, Carlin suggests that sometimes "scared people are the most dangerous thing you can ever run into."

To illustrate this point, he offers a memorable thought experiment about spiders. Imagine finding a small, benign spider in your hotel room—scientifically knowledge tells you it's not venomous and can't hurt you. Yet a surprising percentage of people would find it impossible to sleep until that spider was dead, regardless of rational knowledge about its harmlessness. "You never know," Carlin observes. The irrationality here is telling. Human beings are not simply calculating machines that respond predictably to threats. They are creatures of emotion, instinct, and psychological complexity that resists strategic targeting.

This spider analogy connects to darker historical realities. Carlin notes that "there have been Native American massacres predicated on little more than this idea"—the panic and irrational fear that transforms minor threats into existential dangers. Understanding this dimension of human psychology is essential to understanding both why wars start and how they unfold.

## Franklin Roosevelt and the Art of Wartime Leadership

The episode then pivots to an extended examination of Franklin Delano Roosevelt's leadership during America's darkest months of World War II. Carlin draws an explicit parallel between the post-Pearl Harbor period and September 11th, noting that from a psychological and morale standpoint, and from a "sense of injustice standpoint," Pearl Harbor was America's 9/11. But there was a crucial difference: Pearl Harbor was also "a military disaster that cripples your ability to respond to the perpetrators of the attack."

For the president of the United States, this created an impossible balancing act. Roosevelt had to bolster the nation's morale, dampen its fear, and rally it toward the war effort—all while absorbing the worst military losses in American history and knowing the worst was yet to come. Bataan was about to fall, representing "one of the biggest surrenders in US history."

Carlin is keen to distinguish Roosevelt's style from other wartime leaders. Roosevelt was "controversial" to some Americans even then, and remains so today, but "as a war leader, he was inspirational. And as a politician, he was masterful." Carlin notes that there's actually a law now limiting presidents to two terms—some of Roosevelt's detractors worried that "if his health hadn't failed, he'd still be president today at 147 years old or something."

The comparison with Winston Churchill proves instructive. Churchill was the bulldog, the leader who "you thought was ready to take off his coat and get in the mud and wrestle with Hitler." Churchill embodied aggressive, alpha-male leadership that inspired through sheer force of personality. Roosevelt couldn't compete on those terms. "This is a man in a wheelchair," Carlin observes. "He's not gonna give you that alpha male tough guy sort of routine."

Instead, Roosevelt developed a completely different persona that Carlin describes as "this sort of preacher, Yankee preacher looking down his nose and scolding while he shakes the Bible at you almost like moral indignation and righteous anger." The message was essentially: "You will get yours, sir." This approach was "very effective" and had the additional advantage of positioning Roosevelt "definitely on the higher plane in terms of morality." It was, Carlin concludes, "a presentation that in its own way works."

The proof of Roosevelt's effectiveness lies in the evidence of history. Roosevelt had a "wonderful way about him where he seems calm and sometimes funny," but he "doesn't have to change that tone and add much anger at all for him to sound absolutely livid." His mastery of communication technology—the radio—made him the first president capable of using mass communication to speak directly to the American people, "as though he was in the home with them." Carlin compares this to a modern president having his own Twitter account and communicating directly with citizens, suggesting the revolutionary impact of this approach.

## The Fireside Chat of February 23, 1942

Carlin devotes substantial attention to a specific example of Roosevelt's leadership: his fireside chat delivered on February 23, 1942. This was approximately six weeks before Bataan fell, but Roosevelt already knew it was coming. He knew the American people had been through "the worst series of bad news events that they've probably ever been through," and he knew the worst was still approaching.

The challenge was immense. How do you "get out in front of an issue" while still bolstering morale and fighting spirit? How do you prepare people for continued losses while maintaining their will to fight? Roosevelt was addressing roughly 60 million listeners—more than a Super Bowl audience relative to the population of the time. And this wasn't a brief statement; the speech ran approximately 35 minutes. Roosevelt literally asked his audience to "get out your maps and follow along," couching his remarks "as a report to the American people."

The speech addressed conspiracy theories claiming the government was hiding the true extent of losses. Roosevelt also acknowledged that America had "most certainly suffered losses from Hitler's U-boats in the Atlantic, as well as from the Japanese in the Pacific," and that they would "suffer more of them before the turn of the tide." Yet he reframed these losses within a larger narrative of inevitable victory: "We Americans have been compelled to yield ground, but we will regain it. We and the other United Nations are committed to the destruction of the militarism of Japan and Germany. We are daily increasing our strength. Soon, we and not our enemies will have the offensive. We, not they, will win the final battle. And we, not they, will make the final peace."

Carlin analyzes the psychological buttons Roosevelt chose to push. It was "not the Rambo button," not the "lead from the front" approach of Churchill. Instead, Roosevelt pushed what Carlin calls "the schoolyard button"—the assertion that America's enemies "think we're weak. Are you gonna take that?" He was masterful, however, in immediately incorporating "the rest of the United Nations" rather than making it sound like an "all American show." Every nation had its role to play; everyone was included. Carlin describes this as "the great politician makes sure not to make it sound like it's an all American show."

The speech included this memorable passage: "Ever since this nation became the arsenal of democracy, ever since enactment of Lend-Lease, there has been one persistent theme through all Axis propaganda. This theme has been that Americans are admittedly rich, that Americans have considerable industrial power, but that Americans are soft and decadent, that they cannot and will not unite and work and fight. From Berlin, Rome, and Tokyo, we have been described as a nation of weaklings, playboys who would hire British soldiers or Russian soldiers or Chinese soldiers to do our fighting for us. Let them repeat that now. Let them tell that to General MacArthur and his men. Let them tell that to the sailors who today are hitting hard in the far waters of the Pacific. Let them tell that to the boys in the flying fortresses. Let them tell that to the marines."

Carlin notes that this ending "you could almost hear in today's audience, everybody going USA, USA with that crazy chant."

## The Japanese Submarine Attack

Carlin then introduces what he describes as a "legendary incident" from his own region—the Japanese submarine attack on the California coast that occurred while Roosevelt was delivering his fireside chat. A Japanese submarine, one of the large ones the Japanese built (similar to the French in this respect), surfaced off Santa Barbara at approximately 7:15 PM Pacific time and opened fire on refinery installations with a 140mm deck gun.

This incident, which Carlin grew up hearing about, is "legendary" in his part of the country but largely unknown to the rest of America. Its timing may not have been coincidental: it "may have been timed to coincide with Roosevelt's speech as a way to put the lie to all of his fancy and inspirational talk." The message would have been essentially: "Sure, you say you're going to fight back, but we can shell your own coastline while you're making speeches."

Carlin frames this as "a pretty classic sort of attack against the morale of the enemy intended to sow fear in a sense of vulnerability." It was a low-risk, high-reward endeavor. "Worst case scenario, you lose a sub. Best case scenario, who knows?" It was "free money," a gamble worth taking.

Whether or not the submarine accomplished anything to American morale, it "sure already added a few more sticks of kindling to the fire that was burning on the West Coast in terms of fear." History books have labeled this period "the West Coast war scare" or "the West Coast war panic," and the Japanese submarine attack only intensified those fears. Carlin leaves the listener with this image of American soil under fire, psychological warfare intersecting with actual military strikes, and the impossible task facing leaders like Roosevelt who had to maintain national resolve in the face of seemingly endless bad news.

## Detailed Takeaways

**The Limits of Quantification in Understanding War**

Carlin's exploration of quantifiable versus unquantifiable elements of warfare offers a crucial lesson for anyone studying military history: the aspects of conflict that can be measured and tested are not the only aspects that matter. Equipment can be evaluated, tactics can be drills, production can be counted—but morale, will, and psychological resilience resist such neat analysis. This doesn't make them less important; it makes them harder to understand and target. Decision-makers must grapple with both dimensions, even though one is far more tractable than the other. The failure to recognize this distinction leads to overconfidence in some strategies (like strategic bombing) and underestimation of others (like the resilience of populations under attack).
