The Unreleased Rollable Smartphone!

The tech world is full of companies that pushed boundaries, took wild risks, and ultimately disappeared—but few did it with as much creative abandon as LG. The South Korean electronics giant was once a major player in the smartphone market, but by 2021, the company had shuttered its mobile operations entirely. The reason, as this video explains, was a perfect storm of ambitious experimentation without the financial backbone to sustain it. LG made phones that the industry still talks about today—the kind of devices that made reviewers and enthusiasts weak in the knees—but none of them became the consistent revenue generator a company needs to stay alive. This video dives into one of LG's most audacious final projects: a fully functional rollable smartphone that never made it to store shelves. Thanks to dbrand, who somehow obtained one of these rare units, the reviewer got his hands on what might have been LG's craziest creation yet.

The video begins by acknowledging this bittersweet reality. LG died because they made a bunch of crazy phones without also having a steady moneymaker. That's the same reason we loved them, the reviewer notes with a mix of admiration and sadness. But just before the end came, LG had one more trick up their sleeve—one more shot in the barrel. That final shot was a rollable smartphone that represented the culmination of years of experimentation with alternative form factors. The device was fully functional, complete with working software, special features, and an engineering team that had clearly thought through countless edge cases. They simply didn't survive long enough to actually ship it.

LG's History of Experimental Phones

To understand why the rollable phone was such a big deal, you first need to appreciate just how unconventional LG was willing to be. The reviewer walks through some of LG's most memorable experiments leading up to this point. The LG Wing was perhaps their wildest mainstream attempt—a device that looked like a normal phone at first but was split so you could swivel the spring-loaded top layer up and over into a horizontal orientation, revealing a second smaller square display underneath. The result was a T-shaped screen experience that somehow felt intentional despite its absurdity. All the engineering and software tricks that went into making this work, to make it feel deliberate rather than gimmicky, were described as "just insane."

Then there was the LG G8X, which arrived right at the beginning of the folding phone era but took a completely different approach. Instead of building a foldable display into the phone itself, LG created a case with a built-in display. You could plug your phone into it and have a dual-screen experience at a fraction of what a true foldable would cost. The engineering didn't stop there—LG added a fully folding 180-degree hinge and pass-through magnetic charging adapters so you could still fast charge even while the USB port was occupied by the case. These were the kinds of solutions that made LG beloved by tech enthusiasts, even if they never became massive commercial successes.

The pattern here is clear: LG was willing to try anything and everything, from swiveling displays to clip-on second screens to fully rollable interfaces. They were essentially a mobile computing R&D department that occasionally shipped products. But without a single breakout hit—a phone that sold consistently year after year—the company couldn't fund its experimental division indefinitely. By 2021, the money ran out, and the mobile division closed its doors.

The Rollable Phone: Concept and Design

The concept behind the rollable phone is elegantly simple, even if the execution is extraordinarily complex. When closed, the device looks like a regular smartphone with approximately a 6.7-inch display measured diagonally. It resembles a standard slab phone from most angles, giving it everyday portability and normal ergonomics. But with a simple three-finger swipe gesture across the display, the entire phone expands. It rolls open to become a slightly more squarish 7.4-inch mini-tablet, giving users a larger canvas whenever they needed it.

The reviewer points out that the 6.7-to-7.4-inch jump doesn't sound dramatic on paper, but it's the change in shape that makes all the difference. When you're working with a rollable display, you're not just getting a bigger screen—you're getting a fundamentally different aspect ratio and usable area. The reviewer describes it as having a "scroll or parchment or something" quality when it unrolls, which captures both the novelty and the elegance of the form factor. It's the kind of transformation that feels magical the first time you see it.

Inside the Mechanism: Motors, Springs, and Hidden Screens

The real engineering marvel becomes apparent when you examine how LG actually stored that extra screen real estate. When the phone is closed, it appears to have a single screen that simply bends over both edges, similar to some of Samsung's curved-edge phones from a few years ago. But the extra display panel is hidden in plain sight, tucked away to the right where the display dips off behind a rail mechanism. If you flip the phone over, you can see the rest of the display curving around the back of the device.

LG made a particularly interesting choice here: they used clear glass on the back, which means you can actually see the hidden portion of the display even when the phone is closed. This wasn't just an aesthetic decision—LG leveraged this exposed back section for software features like displaying messages or even showing the camera UI for taking selfies with the rear cameras. The primary camera setup includes a 64-megapixel main sensor and a 12-megapixel ultrawide, both positioned on the back. This design philosophy clearly echoes the Xiaomi Mi Mix Alpha, a concept phone that was also all screen all the way around, prioritizing screen-to-body ratio above all else.

The implications of this design extend to the physical buttons. With the sides of the phone consumed by rails and the back partially covered by display, there's not really enough room for traditional hardware buttons. So LG got creative: the volume buttons on the left side are pressure-sensitive areas positioned where the buttons normally would be, while the power button lives on the back of the phone, doubling as a fingerprint reader underneath the camera module. It sounds别扭 at first, but the reviewer notes it's surprisingly reachable in practice.

Software Integration: The Little Tricks That Make It Feel Real

What really sells this device as something LG was serious about shipping is the software integration. LG clearly got far enough into development that they created special features to make the rollable experience feel smooth and intentional. The lock screen animations are particularly memorable—when the phone expands, it displays a blooming flower animation that feels almost organic. When you shrink it back down, the animation reverses, with the flower closing back to a single petal. It's the kind of thoughtful detail that transforms a tech demo into a product.

The Settings app demonstrates another layer of polish. When you transition from closed to open, there's a subtle slide-over animation that adds a column to the Settings interface, properly utilizing the additional screen real estate. When you go from open to closed, that column smoothly slides away as the interface adapts. These aren't just functional changes—they're visual confirmation that the software knows exactly what's happening and is designed to respond gracefully.

Motor Strength and Physical Reality

One of the most surprising aspects of the rollable phone is just how strong the motors are. The reviewer was impressed by their power, noting that if you try to open the phone and give it a little resistance, it pushes surprisingly hard against that resistance. In one memorable test, the reviewer demonstrates the phone actually pushing a 16-inch MacBook Pro across a table—with rubber feet intact, no less. That's approximately five pounds of force. The motors are clearly designed to handle real-world use and the physical demands of unrolling against fabric pockets or other resistances.

LG also built in protective behaviors. If the phone is open and you squeeze it really hard in your hand, it can actually trigger itself to finish closing, presumably to prevent damage from excessive pressure. After completing this self-protective closure, it flashes an error message. This kind of thinking reveals just how much the engineering team had considered real-world scenarios and edge cases.

The phone does have a pinch risk, though it's mostly a concern for smaller items. Your fingers are mostly too big to get caught in the back or bottom mechanisms, but the phone can easily pinch smaller items and then pick them up—like microfiber cloths, for instance. It's not dangerous for human use, but it's something you'd need to be aware of.

Sound Design: Hiding the Motors

One detail that the reviewer finds particularly amusing is LG's approach to masking the sound of the motors. Every time you open or close the phone, it plays a sound through the speakers. The reviewer is fairly certain this sound was designed to cover the auditory signature of the motors, though he acknowledges it's "about as effective as coughing to cover up a fart." Still, the effort is appreciated, and it's clearly a deliberate choice by the engineering team.

There's even a specific person who had to sit in a room and design the perfect sound to match the frequencies of the motor, trying to mask it effectively. The reviewer finds this idea hilarious in the best possible way—it's exactly the kind of obsessive detail that makes LG's engineering culture so fascinating. The user can also choose between different sounds or turn the sound completely off if the motor noise doesn't bother them.

Durability and the Challenge of Rollable Screens

The video doesn't shy away from the obvious durability concerns with this form factor. When the phone is open, you can see the teeth of the gears at the top and bottom. There are visible air gaps throughout the mechanism. The reviewer notes that between this mechanical exposure and the fact that the soft, flexible display is constantly exposed to the elements—unlike a modern folding phone where the display is protected inside when closed—this phone was probably never going to have impressive IP ratings for water or dust resistance.

This is actually one of the fundamental trade-offs of the rollable form factor. The whole point is that there's no crease like you'd find on a folding phone, because the display doesn't fold in half around a tight radius. Instead, it rolls over a larger radius. But this means the display is always exposed, always vulnerable. There's no clamshell protection when you're not using the phone. LG never got to the stage of marketing this device or giving it an official IP rating, let alone a name, so these durability questions remained unresolved.

The reviewer also notes that while there is less of a crease than a folding phone, it's not "no crease" at all. The display still has some curve as it rolls over to the side, creating just a larger radius of curvature with a little wavering. You can see this more clearly when the display is off. It's not really a big deal compared to some of the creases we've seen on folding phones, but it's worth noting that a rollable display isn't completely smooth either.

What's Under the Hood

Digging through the settings reveals some of the internal specs: a 4,500 milliamp-hour battery and 12 gigabytes of RAM. These are respectable numbers for the era, though the reviewer quickly dismisses them by noting that none of this really matters because it's not a real phone that you can buy. It's a prototype—a proof of concept that LG got remarkably far with before the company shut down.

For those curious about just how over-engineered the inside of this phone is, the reviewer recommends checking out Zack from JerryRigEverything's teardown video. According to that video, the phone doesn't have just one motor doing the opening and closing—it has two. There are also three spring-loaded arms pushing the whole chassis over to the side while it unrolls. The internals are described as "ridiculous" and "wild," the kind of mechanical complexity that reveals just how seriously LG took this project.

The Broader Implications: What LG's Failure Means for Innovation

The reviewer ends the video by giving LG their flowers while also acknowledging the bittersweet nature of this story. They clearly got insanely far in the development process—functional hardware, working software, special features, and all the little polish details that make a product feel ready for market. They simply didn't survive long enough to actually ship it. The reviewer speculates that LG might have been the only company willing to actually try to ship a phone like this, given their history of experimental devices.

But there may be hope yet. The reviewer mentions that there are rumors Samsung is also working on a rollable type of smartphone, possibly for release in the near future. If that device does come to market, the reviewer promises to get their hands on it. LG's unreleased concept might just become the foundation for the next generation of rollable devices, carrying forward ideas that the Korean company pioneered but couldn't bring to market themselves.

Detailed Takeaways

The rollable form factor represents a genuine alternative to folding phones, but it comes with significant trade-offs. Unlike a folding phone where the display is protected when closed, a rollable phone exposes its flexible display constantly. In exchange, you get no visible crease and a different kind of transformation. The concept is compelling for users who want a larger screen in their pocket without the折痕 (crease) that plagues current foldables, but the durability implications are substantial. This trade-off might be worth it for some users, but it's not a clear upgrade in every dimension.

LG's failure wasn't about lack of innovation—it was about lack of business fundamentals. LG made incredible, forward-thinking devices, but they never had a consistent moneymaker to fund the R&D. The Velvet, the Wing, the G8X, and the rollable phone were all interesting, but none became a flagship success year after year. This is a cautionary tale about the importance of balancing experimentation with profitability. You can love a company for being innovative, but investors and executives need a return on investment.

Software polish can make even unconventional hardware feel intentional. The lock screen animations, the Settings app transitions, the sound design to mask motors—these details reveal how far LG got in making the rollable experience feel cohesive. This matters because early adopters and reviewers judge new form factors partly on how "finished" they feel. LG understood this intuitively, investing in software features that made their unconventional hardware feel designed rather than hacked together.

The mechanical complexity required to achieve rollable displays is staggering. Two motors, three spring-loaded arms, and precision-engineered rails all working together to smoothly unroll a display while protecting it from damage. The fact that this device exists at all, let alone functions reliably, is a testament to LG's engineering capabilities. It also explains why such devices are expensive and difficult to manufacture at scale—each one is a miniature engineering project.

Innovation often dies before it reaches consumers, but that doesn't mean the work was wasted. LG got far enough to create functional hardware with working software and special features. While the company didn't survive to ship it, the ideas and engineering solutions they developed will likely influence future devices from other manufacturers. Samsung's rumored rollable phone, if it materializes, may owe a debt to LG's pioneering work. The lesson here is that even failed experiments advance the state of the art and provide valuable knowledge for the industry.
