If You Have No Customers & No Revenue You Need This | My First Million

Introduction

In the world of entrepreneurship, the gap between having a great idea and having paying customers often feels insurmountable. Most founders struggle for months—sometimes years—trying to figure out how to generate their first revenue, often running out of runway before they crack the code. But what if the secret to rapid growth wasn't about building a better product or spending more on advertising? What if it was about mastering the art of storytelling?

This episode of *My First Million* features an entrepreneur who has lived this journey twice. As the second employee at Morning Brew, they helped build the fastest-growing newsletter in the world, taking the company to $75 million in revenue before it exited. Then, applying the same principles they'd learned, they launched a new venture and scaled it to $30 million in annual revenue within just four years—going from zero customers to millions in a remarkably compressed timeframe.

The host Sam Parr opens the conversation by referencing Austin Kleon's book *Steal Like an Artist*, which argues that most seemingly original ideas are really remixes of what came before. This guest's story, Parr suggests, is the perfect embodiment of that principle: taking the growth playbook learned inside Morning Brew and remixing it into a product that anyone can use. The interview dives deep into the specific tactics, mindsets, and strategies that made this possible—and more importantly, how anyone can copy these approaches regardless of their starting point.

The Foundation: Founder Market Fit

The conversation begins with a concept the guest calls "founder market fit"—the idea that the most successful entrepreneurs typically don't just stumble into industries they know nothing about. Instead, they develop expertise in a low-risk way as employees first, then apply those hard-won learnings to build their own businesses.

The guest illustrates this with a candid story from their own past that they've "never actually told before." Between 2017 and 2020, crypto was exploding everywhere. Despite having no background in cryptocurrency—not owning any Bitcoin, not knowing anyone who did, not being in any Discord channels—they decided they wanted to be a "crypto guy." They found an open-source 3D model of a cold storage wallet, found a manufacturer in China, bought a thousand units, shipped them to New York, and started selling crypto wallets on day two.

"I thought I could well myself into being a founder in the space that I had no credibility whatsoever," they admit. "I had no connections." The result? They sold exactly three wallets and still have 997 sitting in their basement. The lesson was brutal but invaluable: you can't manufacture credibility in a space where you have no track record.

In complete contrast stands their Morning Brew experience. When they joined as the second employee, they built the referral program and growth mechanisms from the ground up. They saw what success looked like from the inside—the exact tactics, the specific plays, the internal tools that made it work. When newsletters eventually became the hottest thing in media, they had earned the credibility to be "the newsletter person" through actual experience, not self-promotion.

"For the founders that I've backed and seen become extremely successful, there's typically a through line between them doing that previously and then launching again," the guest explains. The path isn't just about having a good idea—it's about having earned the right to tell a compelling story in that specific domain.

The Marketing Killshot: Crafting Your Credibility Story

Sam Parr then introduces what he calls "the marketing killshot"—a concept he developed after countless conversations with founders who fail to communicate their value effectively. He describes watching a founder pitch their marketing agency for CPG companies, and the experience was painfully typical: they threw out acronyms like "D2C CPG," listed services including "copywriting, packaging, design," and ended with the bland proclamation that they "could do it all."

Parr asked this founder a simple question: "If you couldn't tell me all that junk, and you only could say one sentence, but off that one sentence, I had to want to work with you, what would that be?" The founder started with "We do marketing for consumer companies," to which Parr responded: "You and any thousand other companies out there didn't do it for me."

Then came the pivot. "What's the most impressive thing you've done?" the founder was asked. They named a huge consumer brand like Poppy and admitted they'd done all of Poppy's initial branding, marketing, and positioning. "Why didn't you say that?" Parr asked. "Because that's the kill shot."

The power of this approach cannot be overstated. If you're a new consumer brand meeting this agency and they tell you they helped launch Poppy—that's immediate credibility. You don't need to hear about their services or capabilities. You see proof, you want that same result, and you're ready to sign up. General marketing claims can never touch specific, impressive proof.

The guest's own kill shot is perfect: "I ran growth for the fastest-growing newsletter in the world. Now I'm building a tool for you to grow your newsletter. Sign me up." It's simple, specific, and impossible to ignore. It communicates expertise, track record, and a clear value proposition in a single sentence.

How to Build Credibility When You Don't Have It

For listeners thinking "well, I didn't work at Morning Brew," the conversation immediately addresses this objection. The guest offers multiple paths forward.

Path One: Go Get It First. Join a fast-growing company with high potential and kick ass there. Learn the playbooks, build the relationships, and earn the credibility before you launch your own thing. This is the low-cost, low-stakes way to develop founder market fit before going solo.

Path Two: Manufacture Credibility Through Study. The guest gives an example: even if you didn't work at Morning Brew, you could say, "I spent 1,000 hours studying how Morning Brew grew, and all these tools they built internally, I've built them now so that anybody can use them." That would also work. The key is finding a way to tell a compelling story that establishes your authority, even if you didn't get it through traditional employment.

The guest emphasizes that story is "so massively underrated" and that they think about it constantly—whether pitching investors or acquiring customers. When pitching VCs, the story is simple: "I did this at Morning Brew, and I believe I can do it again and do it for more people." That's the entire pitch. Everything else is just supporting evidence.

Anti-Credibility: The Power of Admitting Failure

Perhaps the most counterintuitive strategy discussed is what the guest calls "anti-credibility"—the technique of deliberately sharing your biggest failures and mistakes to build trust and connection. They used this approach to launch Milk Road, their crypto newsletter that became the biggest in the world in just one year and sold for millions.

"One of them was like anti-credibility," they explain. "So I told this story: 'Can I tell you about the stupidest moment of my entire career?' And people will lean into that story. You're going to tell me about this huge mistake you made."

The story they told was this: in 2017, their technical co-founder—the smartest, most technical person they knew—was supposed to come to a meeting but was five minutes late. When they went to get him, he was on eBay buying Ethereum during the pre-sale. At the time, the guest thought Ethereum was a "weird name" and told his co-founder to come on, they had real business to do in the meeting room. Of course, that co-founder later made hundreds of millions from that investment, while the guest made nothing.

"I basically vowed to not make that mistake again—that when there's an explosive new industry where there's lots of growth potential, I might start as the dumbest guy in the room, but I'm going to ask a lot of questions. I'm going to learn really fast." That story became the foundation for Milk Road. By admitting they were the "dumbest guy in the room," they created a relatable, authentic narrative that resonated with an audience hungry for genuine voices in the crypto space.

The Growth Trajectory: What Numbers Actually Look Like

The conversation concludes with concrete numbers that demonstrate what's possible when these strategies work. The guest's current company took about a year to reach $1 million in revenue. By year two, they hit $5 million—a 5x increase. And in year four, they reached approximately $30 million in annual revenue.

Parr describes their investor updates as ones he "likes to open up"—a testament to the consistency of their growth story. The trajectory isn't accidental; it's the result of systematically applying the principles they'd mastered: building credibility, crafting compelling narratives, and leveraging founder market fit to accelerate growth from day one.

Key Takeaways

1. Founder Market Fit Before Product-Market Fit. The most reliable path to rapid growth isn't just building a great product—it's building expertise first as an employee in a high-potential company, then applying those learnings to your own venture. This gives you credibility, domain knowledge, and often your first customers through the network you build.

2. Your Story Is Your Most Valuable Asset in the Early Days. When you have no customers and no revenue, all you really have is your story. Whether pitching investors, acquiring customers, or recruiting talent, the ability to articulate a compelling narrative about your background and why you're the right person to solve a specific problem is the difference between struggling and scaling.

3. The Marketing Killshot: Specific Proof Beats General Claims. Rather than telling people what you do, show them what you've already done. "We helped launch Poppy" is infinitely more powerful than "we do marketing for consumer brands." Every founder should be able to articulate their "kill shot"—one sentence so impressive that anyone hearing it immediately wants to work with you.

4. Anti-Credibility Creates Authentic Connection. Sharing your failures, mistakes, and embarrassing moments often builds more trust than boasting about successes. People connect with vulnerability and authenticity. Your biggest mistake can become your most compelling origin story.

5. You Can Manufacture Credibility If You Don't Have It Organically. If you haven't worked at a famous company, you can still build credibility through intensive study, public writing, building in public, or finding other ways to demonstrate expertise. The key is finding a story that establishes your authority—even if it's "I spent a thousand hours studying this" rather than "I did this at a famous company."

Who This Is For

This episode is essential listening for early-stage founders who are struggling to generate their first customers or revenue. If you're currently at ground zero—building in public, pitching investors, trying to convince your first paying customers—this conversation offers a fundamentally different mindset than the typical "build a better product" advice.

It's also valuable for aspiring entrepreneurs who haven't yet launched but are considering joining a high-growth company first. The discussion around founder market fit provides a strategic framework for career decisions: rather than jumping straight into entrepreneurship, deliberate experience-building can dramatically increase your odds of success.

Finally, anyone in marketing, sales, or business development should pay attention to the "marketing killshot" concept. The principle—that specific proof always beats general claims—applies whether you're pitching a startup, selling a service, or even negotiating a salary. The ability to tell a compelling story about your value is a skill that compounds across every area of business and career.
