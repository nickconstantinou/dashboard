Mohamed Salah: The Story of an All-Time FPL Legend

The Full Story

Mohamed Salah's unprecedented reign as Fantasy Premier League's greatest asset is nearing its conclusion, with the Egyptian wizard set to leave Liverpool at the end of the 2025/26 season. The announcement, made on Tuesday evening, marks the end of an extraordinary eight-year chapter in FPL history that has seen Salah redefine what it means to be a premium asset in the world's most popular fantasy football game. Having arrived at Anfield from AS Roma in June 2017 for an initial fee of £34 million under Jurgen Klopp, Salah has accumulated a staggering 2,216 FPL points—good for third place on the all-time list behind only Wayne Rooney (2,338) and Frank Lampard (2,318). His haul includes 191 goals, 104 assists, and 232 bonus points across his Liverpool career, with 34 of those points having come during a brief, largely forgettable spell at Chelsea between 2013 and 2015.

The 33-year-old's season-by-season output reads like a fantasy manager's dream, with consistency that no other player in FPL history has come close to matching. Salah's 2017/18 debut season saw him finish with 303 points—the first time anyone had ever exceeded 300 in a single campaign—alongside 32 goals and 44 returns. He would repeat that feat in 2024/25, accumulating another 344 points with 29 goals and 18 assists across 18 haul matches of 10 or more points. Remarkably, in each of his eight full seasons at Liverpool, Salah has never finished below 211 points, demonstrating an almost supernatural reliability that has made him an essential pick for managers across millions of fantasy squads. His record-breaking 29-point haul against Watford in 2017/18 remains the highest single-match score in FPL history, a record that stood even through last season's Double Gameweek 24, which produced another 29-point explosion.

Certain Premier League clubs have developed a particular dread when Salah's name appears on the fixture list. The Egyptian has been particularly devastating against Manchester United, accumulating 136 points across 16 meetings—an average of 8.50 per game—alongside 13 goals and six assists, with four separate double-digit hauls. Newcastle United and West Ham United have also felt his wrath repeatedly, yielding 131 and 127 points respectively with prolific goal contributions. Interestingly, Salah has shown a peculiar weakness against Burnley, scoring just twice across 11 meetings with the Clarets, and has managed only five goals in 16 appearances against his former club Chelsea—a stat that seems almost shocking given his otherwise prolific record.

Why This Matters

For the millions of FPL managers who have built their seasons around Salah's consistent excellence, his departure represents the end of an era that fundamentally shaped how fantasy football is played. His combination of goal threat, creativity, penalty duties, and near-guaranteed 90-minute appearances made him the ultimate "set and forget" asset—a player whose price rarely deterred managers despite the opportunity cost of fitting him alongside other premium picks. The Egyptian's value extended beyond his own points returns; his presence in a squad often dictated budget navigation strategies, captaincy choices, and overall team architecture for managers competing at all levels of the game.

The broader implications for the Premier League and Liverpool cannot be understated either. Salah's 197 goals in 326 Premier League appearances for Liverpool place him among the club's greatest-ever strikers, and his departure—alongside the likely exits of other Anfield legends in the coming years—signals the end of Klopp's golden era. For FPL, his departure creates a significant void in the premium forward category, likely triggering a scramble among managers to identify the next dominant asset while forcing difficult decisions about how to reallocate the substantial budget previously locked into Salah's £14.0m price tag. The timing, coming during the 2025/26 season, means managers must now navigate a truncated run-in without their established captaincy anchor.

    • Context and Background

    Salah's journey to FPL immortality began somewhat humbly, with FPL managers initially pricing him at just £9.0m for the 2017/18 season—a figure that seems almost absurd given his eventual output. His transformation under Klopp from a talented but inconsistent winger at Roma into one of the Premier League's most lethal goal scorers was reflected perfectly in his fantasy returns. Where other premium forwards have shown susceptibility to rotation, injury layoffs, or form slumps, Salah maintained an almost machine-like reliability that made him indispensable across competitive seasons.

    His Chelsea spell, during which he scored just twice in 564 Premier League minutes across 2013/14 and 2014/15, had convinced many that he was ill-suited to English football's physical demands. That narrative has been comprehensively dismantled by his Liverpool career, which has seen him become the eighth-highest Premier League scorer at Old Trafford in the 2020s—a testament to his continued dominance at one of football's most intimidating venues.

    What's Next

    As the 2025/26 season enters its final stretch, FPL managers face the unenviable task of squeezing maximum value from their Egyptian talisman while simultaneously preparing for a post-Salah landscape. His next fixture against Manchester United in Gameweek 35 presents one final opportunity to capitalize on his remarkable record against the Red Devils, though managers must weigh the risk of investing heavily in a player with uncertain future minutes as Liverpool potentially rotate ahead of his departure.

    For the broader FPL community, the focus will soon shift to identifying which Liverpool attackers—Darwin Núñez, Cody Gakpo, or Luis Díaz—might absorb Salah's creative and goal-scoring responsibilities, and whether any can approach the consistency that made the Egyptian an auto-include for nearly a decade. The upcoming summer will likely see intense debate about who deserves the mantle of "premium forward" in a Salah-less FPL universe, with managers already strategizing how to navigate a new era in which no asset can match the Egyptian's historic combination of output and reliability.
