Why are ships linked to China getting through the Strait of Hormuz? • FRANCE 24 English

The Mystery of the KLS Hangyang

In the volatile waters of the Strait of Hormuz—one of the world's most critical maritime chokepoints—where Iranian threats have terrified international shippers and reduced traffic to a trickle, one vessel managed to pass through without incident when so many others have come under fire. The KLS Hangyang represents a fascinating case study in how international shipping, geopolitics, and strategic deception intersect in one of the most dangerous shipping lanes on Earth. Officially registered under the flag of the Marshall Islands in the Pacific, this cargo vessel appears to have leveraged hidden connections to China to secure safe passage through waters that have become increasingly perilous since the outbreak of regional conflict. The question that FRANCE 24 investigates is straightforward yet profound: how did this ship manage to transit the 212-kilometer strait when the number of vessels making the crossing has collapsed from an average of over 130 per day before the war to just a handful now?

The stakes could not be higher. The Strait of Hormuz represents a vital artery for global oil shipments, with roughly a fifth of the world's petroleum passing through its narrow waters. Iranian officials have made their intentions abundantly clear, with representatives from the Revolutionary Guard and the army explicitly threatening to set ships on fire if they attempt to pass through without permission. Yet despite these threats, certain vessels continue to move through, prompting questions about what deals, exemptions, or strategic calculations are at play behind the scenes. The KLS Hangyang is not an isolated case—rather, it represents a pattern that international relations analysts say points to a deliberate arrangement between Beijing and Tehran.

The Mechanics of Maritime Deception

The KLS Hangyang illustrates a sophisticated approach to navigating hostile waters, one that relies on the strategic manipulation of vessel registration and identification systems. When the ship departed Saudi Arabia on March 4th, something remarkable happened with its automatic identification and tracking system—the AIS transponder that broadcasts a vessel's position and details to surrounding ships and coastal authorities. While the official registry still showed the Marshall Islands flag, the tracking system transmitted that the vessel was Chinese-owned and crewed by Chinese personnel. That night, the KLS Hangyang passed through the Strait of Hormuz without incident.

This was no mere coincidence, according to international relations analysts quoted in the report. The timing and specificity of the identification change suggests coordination at the highest levels—a deliberate signal to Iranian authorities that this particular vessel should be treated differently from the many others transiting the region. The phenomenon extends beyond this single ship. At least nine vessels were observed waiting at the entry point of the strait, and analysis of their registration records reveals a telling pattern. These ships were originally registered in nations like Panama, Liberia, and Kuwait, but closer examination shows they have significant ties to China in their ownership or operational structures. The key insight is that ownership or destination details are frequently changed for specific periods of time, or explicit messages are sent indicating that a particular vessel is Chinese-owned.

The Beijing-Tehran Deal

Behind this maritime chess game lies a broader geopolitical arrangement that analysts describe as a mutually beneficial exchange between China and Iran. The compensation, according to the report, involves China supplying the Iranian regime with goods and support that it desperately needs but cannot easily obtain through other channels. This includes military infrastructure, intelligence-sharing arrangements, radar systems, and other defense-related materials that strengthen Iran's regional capabilities. In exchange, Iran provides China with petroleum—a commodity that remains strategically valuable despite international sanctions regimes designed to constrain Tehran's nuclear and military ambitions.

This arrangement explains why Chinese-linked vessels appear to enjoy safe passage while others face harassment, seizure, or attack. The relationship between Beijing and Tehran has deepened significantly in recent years, transforming what was once a cautious diplomatic partnership into a substantive strategic alliance. China's willingness to provide military and technical assistance to Iran represents a significant shift in Middle Eastern power dynamics, one that the United States and its allies have watched with growing concern. The petroleum-for-technology exchange creates a relationship of dependency that both sides have strong incentives to protect and preserve.

Beyond China: The Turkish Exception

The pattern extends beyond Chinese-flagged vessels, though through a different mechanism. According to reports from AFP cited in the investigation, at least two Turkish ships have also successfully navigated the Strait of Hormuz despite the heightened tensions. Their strategy was notably different but equally effective: these vessels announced that their crews were Muslim. This declaration appears to have provided them with a measure of protection, presumably because targeting ships with Muslim crews would carry significant political and diplomatic costs for Iran in the region.

This Turkish example illustrates that the calculus governing safe passage through the strait is not purely about military capability or alliance structures—it also involves careful consideration of reputational costs and regional sensitivities. Iran must balance its strategic objectives against the potential fallout from actions that might be perceived as aggression against Muslim-majority nations or violations of Islamic principles. The Turkish ships demonstrate that there exist pathways through the crisis for those who understand and can navigate these complex political calculations.

The Collapse of Hormuz Traffic

The broader context for these developments is the dramatic decline in commercial shipping through the Strait of Hormuz. What was once one of the world's busiest shipping channels, with an average of over 130 vessels passing through daily, has been reduced to a ghost waterway where only a handful of ships dare to venture. This collapse represents a significant disruption to global energy markets and international trade, forcing companies to reroute shipments around the Cape of Good Hope at dramatically increased costs and transit times.

The Iranian threats have proven extraordinarily effective at deterring commercial traffic, even without direct military action against every vessel that ignores the warnings. The mere perception of risk has been sufficient to push most shippers to seek alternative routes, creating a self-fulfilling prophecy where the absence of traffic validates the threats themselves. Against this backdrop, the continued passage of Chinese-linked vessels and the occasional Turkish ship stands out as an exception that proves the rule—and that demands explanation.

Key Insights

The Strait of Hormuz has become a geopolitical flashpoint where economic coercion intersects with military threats. Iran's campaign to disrupt international shipping represents a strategic attempt to demonstrate its regional power and to pressure Western nations supporting Israel in the ongoing conflict. The near-complete collapse of commercial traffic through the strait demonstrates just how effective these threats have been, even without direct attacks on most vessels.

Vessel identification systems have become tools of geopolitical strategy. The deliberate manipulation of ship registration data and tracking information represents a form of maritime signaling that allows certain vessels to receive preferential treatment while maintaining plausible deniability. This "gray zone" tactic exploits the gap between official registry records and operational reality.

The China-Iran relationship is deeper than commonly recognized. The exchange of military technology for petroleum represents a strategic partnership that bypasses Western sanctions and creates mutual dependencies. This relationship appears to include explicit guarantees regarding the safety of Chinese shipping through contested waters.

Regional and religious considerations still matter in strategic calculations. The Turkish ships' success in obtaining safe passage by emphasizing their Muslim crews demonstrates that Iran cannot act with complete disregard for regional opinion and Islamic solidarity, even as it pursues aggressive policies against Western-linked shipping.

The analysis also provides valuable context for investors, policy analysts, and journalists seeking to understand the complex interplay between military threats, economic interests, and diplomatic arrangements that govern one of the world's most strategically important waterways.

The deeper value lies in understanding how gray-zone tactics and strategic deception have become central to modern geopolitical competition. The KLS Hangyang's journey is not merely a story about one ship—it's a window into how nations and corporations adapt to an increasingly hostile international environment, exploiting legal ambiguities and strategic relationships to maintain flows of critical resources even amid open conflict.
