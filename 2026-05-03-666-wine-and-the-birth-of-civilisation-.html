    Introduction

    In this episode of the popular history podcast, hosts Tom and Dominic embark on a journey through one of humanity's most enduring and unexpected companions: wine. Spanning approximately 8,000 years of human civilization, the episode traces wine's emergence from a regional curiosity in the mountains south of the Black Sea to its current status as a $500 billion global industry. The narrative weaves together archaeology, ancient texts, economics, and personal anecdote to demonstrate how wine has shaped - and been shaped by - the rise and fall of empires.

    The episode opens with a characteristically humorous examination of wine snobbery, citing Pliny the Elden's encyclopaedia from the first century AD, which catalogues wine by region with what the hosts describe as a remarkable similarity to Alan Partridge's fictional *World Book of Wine*. This sets the tone for a discussion that balances scholarly rigor with accessible entertainment, exploring everything from the science of grape domestication to wine's unexpected role as a instrument of Roman imperial conquest.

    What emerges is not merely a history of a beverage, but a meditation on how a single agricultural product became intertwined with the very fabric of human civilization - influencing trade routes, philosophical traditions, military strategy, and the formation of cultural identity across continents and millennia.

    ---

    The Science of Domestication: How Wine Was Born

    The story of wine begins not with human innovation, but with an astronomical catastrophe. Following the asteroid impact that wiped out the dinosaurs at the end of the Cretaceous period, young forests regenerated across the planet, creating conditions apparently ideal for wild grapevines. These early vines thrived by curling around young trees and filling gaps in the post-catastrophe ecosystem.

    But the true revolution came with human intervention. According to Kathleen Burke's forthcoming book *Wine: The Global History*, which Tom cites as an essential reference, wild grapevines presented a fundamental biological challenge: they were either male or female. The female vines could produce fruit, but the male vines could not, meaning pollination had to occur through bees or wind. What early humans discovered - whether through accident or deliberate experimentation - was that crossbreeding could create hermaphroditic vines capable of producing fruit entirely on their own.

    This genetic breakthrough occurred in what Tom describes as the great mountain ranges south of the Black Sea - the Taurus Mountains, the Caucasus, the Zagros in Iran - a region encompassing Mount Ararat, where the biblical Noah supposedly planted his vineyard. The Republic of Georgia, as distinct from the American state, claims with considerable archaeological support to be the birthplace of wine production. Chemical analysis of pottery shards found within approximately 100 miles of Tbilisi has revealed traces consistent with wine dating to 6,000 BC, lending scientific credence to biblical tradition.

    Two thousand years after the Georgian origins, a village called Areni in Armenia yielded the oldest known winery - discovered in a cave containing a large winepress with preserved traces of withered grapevines, animal skins, and grape seeds. The Areni winery operated around 4,000 BC, suggesting wine production had already achieved sufficient scale for organized, community-level output. Today, Areni continues producing wine, including a Zulal Areni dry red described as "balanced, pure, and lush."

    The Canaanites and the Birth of Global Trade

    From these mountain origins, wine began its spread through ancient trade networks, and no people were more instrumental in this expansion than the Canaanites. Centered in the Beqaa Valley of Lebanon - now famous for Chateau Musar and other robust reds produced in what the hosts note is technically Hezbollah-controlled territory - the Canaanites established the first mass wine export operation beginning in the sixth millennium BC.

    The critical innovation that enabled this trade was the amphora. This remarkably functional vessel featured two handles, a pointed base designed to be embedded in sand or mud, and an extremely narrow neck sealed with a clay stopper. The design was so effective that amphorae remained in continuous use for over five thousand years, persisting through the Bronze Age collapse that destroyed Canaanite civilization itself and continuing until the Islamic conquests of the seventh century AD. Dominic notes from personal experience that wine aged in clay amphorae retains a distinctive, not unpleasant clay taste quite different from wine stored in steel or oak.

    The Egyptians, by 2,000 BC, had become enthusiastic importers of Canaanite wine, describing the region as a land where wine was more plentiful than water. Beyond Egypt, Canaanite wine flowed to Cyprus and Crete, establishing wine-drinking cultures throughout the eastern Mediterranean. The discovery of wine amphorae at Cadiz (ancient Gadir) in southern Spain, dating to around 800 BC, suggests the Phoenicians - successors to the Canaanites - had extended this trade network to the Iberian Peninsula.

    The archaeological evidence for Phoenician wine commerce is extraordinary. In 1997, two Phoenician ships were discovered approximately 50 miles off Gaza by a US submarine. The cargo included 781 amphorae - equivalent to roughly 20,000 modern wine bottles. Other discovered wrecks have contained even larger quantities. These merchant vessels exported wine across the entire Mediterranean and beyond, transforming wine from a local agricultural product into an internationally traded commodity.

    Greek Civilization and the Rules of Wine

    While the Phoenicians dominated maritime wine trade, the Greeks developed something equally significant: a complete cultural framework for wine consumption that elevated the beverage from mere drink to civilizing practice. The classic literary account appears in Homer's *Odyssey*, where Odysseus and his men carry twelve jars of precious wine from Maron in Thrace to the Island of the Cyclops.

    The encounter between the Greeks and the Cyclops Polyphemus serves as a parable about wine culture versus barbarism. The Greeks diluted their wine - approximately 19 parts water to one part wine - following established rules of civilized consumption. Polyphemus, encountering this practice for the first time, drank the wine undiluted and became so intoxicated that he fell asleep, enabling Odysseus to blind him with a heated stake and escape. The lesson, as Tom explains, was that proper wine consumption required restraint, dilution, and adherence to cultural norms.

    This philosophy manifested in the Greek symposium - the institution where citizens would recline, drink wine according to established rules, and engage in philosophical discussion. Wine was not simply consumed; it was the catalyst for intellectual exchange, social bonding, and the transmission of civilization. When the Greeks established the colony of Massilia (modern Marseille) around 600 BC, they brought this entire cultural package to what would become France, planting the seeds for one of the world's great wine-producing nations.

    Rome's Imperial Vintage

    By the time the Roman Empire emerged, wine culture had spread from its Near Eastern origins across the entire Mediterranean world. The Romans inherited this tradition but transformed it into something unprecedented: the first truly international wine culture operating within a unified political framework.

    Initially, the Romans viewed themselves as cultural newcomers, regarding Greek wines as the finest available. Kathleen Burke's research reveals that Greek wines commanded premium prices in Rome, reflecting a kind of wine snobbery remarkably similar to nineteenth-century Americans visiting Bordeaux or contemporary connoisseurs disparaging domestic productions. The Romans were, as Tom notes, "very conscious of themselves as parvenus."

    A crucial early influence came from Carthage. When the Romans destroyed Carthage, they distributed or destroyed most of its famous libraries, but they preserved one volume by the agronomist Mago - a celebrated writer on viticulture. This work was translated from Carthaginian into Latin and became a foundational text for Italian viticulture, demonstrating how even military enemies could shape Roman agricultural practice.

    However, wine's most unexpected role in Roman history came through imperial expansion itself. As Rome extended its legions into Gaul (modern France) in the second century BC, merchants traveled northward along rivers carrying wine. The Gauls, encountering Roman wine for the first time, became, as Tom describes, "completely addicted." Like Polyphemus, they did not dilute their wine, instead engaging in the kind of drunken binges that one Greek historian described as leaving Gauls either asleep or mad.

    The Romans exploited this addiction systematically. Merchants created dependencies, inflating prices as demand soared. To ensure that only Romans could supply the market, the Senate passed an official decree prohibiting the sale of vines to Gauls. The result was a form of economic colonization - the Romans controlled production while the Gauls provided ever-greater quantities of slaves and tribute to obtain wine.

    The exchange rate by Julius Caesar's time was stark: one amphora of wine equaled one slave. This extraordinary valuation reveals wine's status as a luxury commodity of immense value. But the consequences extended far beyond commerce. Because Gallic tribes competed fiercely to capture slaves to exchange for wine, they fought among themselves, making unified resistance to Roman expansion impossible. Meanwhile, the Romans used these captured slaves in their own vineyards, producing more wine to export to Gaul, creating what Tom calls "a virtuous circle" for imperial conquest.

    Wine, in this telling, was not merely an accompaniment to Roman civilization - it was an instrument of Roman power, deployed strategically to weaken enemies, create dependencies, and generate the slave labor that sustained further expansion.

    Detailed Takeaways

    The Origins of Wine Are Scientific, Not Mythical: While the Bible credits Noah with planting the first vineyard, archaeological science has now traced wine production to the Republic of Georgia and Armenia approximately 8,000 years ago. This matters because it demonstrates that human civilization developed alongside viticulture - wine is not simply a byproduct of settled agriculture but an active participant in the formation of human society. The domestication of the hermaphroditic grape represents one of humanity's earliest deliberate genetic modifications, with profound implications for food security, trade, and cultural development.

    Technology Determines Trade: The invention of the amphora was not merely a packaging innovation but a prerequisite for globalized commerce. Without a container capable of preserving and transporting wine across seas, wine would have remained a local luxury. The amphora's design - with its pointed base for stability, narrow neck for sealing, and handles for handling - remained essentially unchanged for over five millennia. This teaches us that foundational technologies often have extraordinary lifespans when they solve fundamental logistical problems elegantly.

    Wine Export Was Always Cultural Export: When the Phoenicians, Greeks, and Romans exported wine, they were not merely shipping a beverage but an entire way of life. The Greeks exported their dilution practices, their symposia, their philosophical traditions. Wine became synonymous with civilization - the Greeks literally called non-wine-drinking peoples "barbarians" (a term possibly derived from the onomatopoeic sound of non-Greek speech, but reinforced by their drinking habits). Understanding wine as a cultural technology helps explain why it spread so rapidly and why it became embedded in religious practices worldwide.

    Economic Dependency Enables Political Control: The Roman strategy of creating wine dependency in Gaul represents a sophisticated form of economic warfare that predates modern colonialism by two millennia. By controlling a desired commodity and prohibiting local production, Rome created a captive market that financed its own expansion while preventing indigenous industrial development. This pattern - create dependency, exploit it commercially, prevent local alternatives - recurs throughout history, from the British opium trade in China to contemporary technology dependencies.

    Personal Taste Is Historically Contingent: Pliny the Elden's story about the freedman who declared a wine "vinegar" while predicting Caesar would love it reminds us that wine expertise is partly performance, partly training, and partly cultural construction. What counts as good wine varies dramatically across time and culture. The Roman preference for aged, resinated Greek wines seems strange to modern palates; modern preferences for oak-aged Bordeaux would have seemed equally peculiar to ancient consumers. This suggests that expertise in any domain is always partly historical accident rather than objective truth.
