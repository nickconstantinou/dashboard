FPL's Biggest xG Underachievers: Who Fantasy Managers Should Watch

The Full Story

Fantasy Premier League managers seeking to capitalize on regression to the mean should pay close attention to players significantly underperforming their expected goals (xG), according to analysis from Fantasy Football Scout covering the 2025/26 season. While some players like Antoine Semenyo, Harry Wilson, and Bruno Guimaraes are exceeding their xG projections, an alarming number of high-profile attackers are failing to convert their chances at rates that suggest their fortunes must eventually turn around.

Crystal Palace emerges as the most troubling team in front of goal, posting a staggering -12.75 xG differential that dwarfs any other side in the league. At the individual level, French striker Jean-Philippe Mateta tops the underperformance charts despite being largely out of favour at Selhurst Park—he's managed only 30 minutes across the club's latest seven matches. Mateta has squandered 17 of his 24 big chances, none of which were penalties, putting him among the worst converters among players with at least 10 such opportunities. Among that group, only Harvey Barnes (81.8%), Tolu Arokodare (80.0%), and Mohamed Salah (76.9%) have missed a higher proportion of their golden chances. Though notably, Mateta's underlying shot quality has been solid—his shots on target should have yielded 7.48 goals rather than just five, suggesting considerable misfortune rather than poor finishing technique.

Over the most recent eight Gameweeks (GW24-31), Newcastle's Marcus Tavernier has become the individual focal point of frustration. Despite starting only the latest four matches, Tavernier managed to top the underperformance table, hitting the woodwork three times while failing to take Bournemouth's Gameweek 31 penalty despite the expectation he would assume spot-kick duties. Three players have earned the unwanted distinction of being the week's worst individual underperformer twice this season: Evanilson, Thierno Barry, and Dominic Calvert-Lewin—with Barry and Calvert-Lewin both recording their double-underperformances in recent Gameweeks 28 and 30. Calvert-Lewin's reputation as a big-chance squanderer is particularly entrenched, with data showing that across his Leeds United spell combined with his final four seasons at Everton, the England international should have accumulated an additional 16.66 goals beyond his actual tally of 27.

Why This Matters

For Fantasy Premier League managers, xG differential represents one of the most powerful predictive tools available. When high-quality players consistently underperform their xG, the mathematics of the sport suggest they are statistically due for positive regression—meaning their goal tallies should eventually catch up to reflect the quality of chances they're creating. Identifying these players before they "click" can be the difference between a template squad and a rank-boosting differential.

The Mateta situation illustrates a different but equally important FPL consideration: usage versus ability. Despite being underachieving on conversion, Mateta has barely featured in recent Crystal Palace teams, making him a moot asset unless his situation changes. Conversely, Tavernier remains a tempting option for managers who believe in Bournemouth's upcoming fixture run and his underlying numbers, but his recent woodwork strikes and penalty miss raise questions about his point-scoring ceiling even when selected.

The Anthony Gordon subplot offers a case study in the opposite phenomenon—players who were underperforming but have recently broken through. Gordon's three consecutive goals have lifted him from third on the underperformance list, demonstrating how quickly fortunes can shift and why managers must continuously reassess their assets rather than clinging to outdated assessments.

    • Context and Background

    Expected goals (xG) has become the gold standard metric in football analytics, measuring the quality of chances a player or team creates based on historical conversion rates from similar positions. When a player's actual goals fall below their xG, analysts typically expect "regression to the mean"—the statistical principle that extreme performances typically move toward the average over time.

    FPL managers have increasingly incorporated xG analysis into their strategies, using it to identify underpriced assets whose underlying numbers suggest better returns are coming, while avoiding overpriced players whose actual output has been artificially inflated by unsustainable finishing luck.

    The Crystal Palace situation reflects broader team dynamics affecting individual players. With Mateta apparently out of favour with the manager despite his xG underperformance, the data suggests he'd be a strong pickup if his situation changes—but that "if" remains significant.

    What's Next

    Anthony Gordon represents perhaps the most compelling near-term opportunity. His recent form—ending a year-long drought in open play and scoring in three consecutive matches—combined with his penalty-taking role and apparent positional shift to centre-forward makes him an attractive option. If Newcastle receive a Double Gameweek 33, his ownership is expected to surge significantly.

    Marcus Tavernier offers a high-risk, high-reward proposition for managers confident in Bournemouth's fixture run. His underlying numbers remain strong, but recent woodwork hits and penalty avoidance suggest he needs a confidence boost more than he needs more chances.

    For managers willing to take calculated risks, Mateta should be monitored closely. If Crystal Palace's manager changes approach or Mateta regains favour, his xG numbers suggest he's been significantly unlucky and could explode if given consistent minutes.

    Dominic Calvert-Lewin, meanwhile, represents a longer-term hold—he's consistently underperformed throughout his career, and while regression to the mean suggests improvement should come, his pattern suggests some players simply don't convert at expected rates no matter how many chances they receive.
