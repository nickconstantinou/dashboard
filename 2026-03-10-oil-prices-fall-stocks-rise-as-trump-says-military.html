Oil prices fall, stocks rise as Trump says military goals ‘pretty well complete’ • FRANCE 24

Introduction

On a Tuesday broadcast of FRANCE 24's business segment, anchor Charles Pelin provided viewers with a comprehensive overview of two significant developments reshaping global energy markets and South American investment landscapes. The primary focus centered on a notable shift in financial markets following comments from U.S. President Donald Trump suggesting that military operations targeting Iran might be approaching completion. This announcement triggered a sharp decline in oil prices from their Monday highs—where both major benchmarks had approached $120 per barrel—while simultaneously fueling a rally across Asian and European equity markets. The segment also examined Venezuela's recent legislative move to open its mining sector to foreign and private investment, a development that represents a dramatic reversal of decades-old nationalization policies and signals Washington's intensifying effort to counter China's dominance in critical mineral supply chains.

The interconnected nature of these stories reflects the complex geopolitical and economic tensions defining the current global landscape. As Trump suggested military objectives in Iran were "pretty well complete," analysts cautioned that significant uncertainties remain, particularly regarding the Hormuz Strait, which remains effectively closed to tanker traffic and continues to constrict global oil supplies. Meanwhile, Venezuela's pivot toward foreign mining investment comes amid ongoing trade conflicts and represents a strategic response to U.S. pressure following the dramatic capture and removal of President Nicolas Maduro in a U.S. military operation this past January.

Oil Markets React to Trump Comments on Iran

The most significant market movement of the day centered on oil prices, which experienced a substantial decline following Trump's overnight comments suggesting progress toward ending the Iran conflict. Both global benchmarks—the North Sea Brent crude and West Texas Intermediate—had reached approximately $120 per barrel on Monday but traded significantly lower by Tuesday's session. This downward movement in energy costs translated directly into renewed optimism across equity markets, with Asian exchanges leading the recovery.

The Kospi in South Korea finished the session nearly 3 percent higher, while Japan's Nikkei index surged over 5 percent, mirroring similar gains across European markets at the open. Germany's DAX in Frankfurt climbed approximately 2 percent, and France's CAC 40 advanced over 1.8 percent. A market analyst quoted in the broadcast captured the prevailing sentiment, stating that Tuesday represented "the rebound," with "positive comments from President Trump overnight" signaling that markets were "starting to see the light at the end of the tunnel for the war." The analyst further noted that investors were "probably going to go in through a period of risk on again," suggesting that while volatility would remain a feature of trading, conditions were "certainly looking a lot brighter today."

However, the segment immediately tempered this optimism by noting that losses incurred since the outbreak of hostilities in the Middle East had "far from recovered," underscoring the depth of market disruption that would require substantial time to reverse. The analyst's emphasis on volatility as "the key word" proved particularly prescient given the competing signals emerging from the Trump administration.

Competing Narratives and Persistent Risks

The broadcast carefully highlighted the contradictory nature of Trump's messaging, which introduced significant uncertainty into market interpretations. While the president suggested that military objectives were "pretty well complete," he also declared that "we've already won in many ways, but we haven't won enough"—a statement that left open the possibility of continued hostilities or even escalation. This ambiguity was compounded by the continued closure of the Hormuz Strait, the critical chokepoint through which approximately one-fifth of global oil consumption passes daily.

The segment highlighted remarks from Saudi Aramco's CEO, who described the current situation as "the biggest crisis the region's oil and gas industry has faced," warning of "catastrophic consequences for oil markets the longer this disruption continues." The Hormuz Strait's virtual closure to tanker traffic is choking off supplies not only of oil and gas but also commodities like fertilizer, creating cascading effects throughout global supply chains. Trump has promised to intensify strikes on Iran if it further disrupts oil supplies and has discussed deploying the U.S. Navy to escort ships through the strait, though no concrete details were provided regarding implementation.

Trump Administration Considers Options to Curb Prices

The broadcast examined the range of policy options under consideration by the Trump administration to address elevated oil prices, noting that the timing carries particular political significance ahead of key midterm elections in the United States. With gasoline prices rising at the pump, administration officials face mounting pressure to demonstrate responsiveness to voter concerns about the economic impact of the Middle East conflict.

According to reporting from U.S. media cited in the segment, several options remain on the table. The administration could release oil from the country's emergency stockpiles to increase supply and stabilize markets—a classic strategic petroleum reserve intervention. Alternatively, officials have discussed temporarily suspending collection of the federal gas tax, though this would require congressional approval, introducing procedural obstacles to rapid implementation. A third approach involves the Treasury Department intervening in oil futures markets to attempt to influence price direction, though such interventions carry their own risks and uncertainties.

Perhaps most significantly, there has been discussion of waiving oil-related sanctions, with Trump acknowledging having discussed this possibility with Russian President Vladimir Putin during a phone call. Russian oil currently faces a price cap in global markets, and the two major Russian oil-producing companies are on the U.S. sanctions list. The broadcast noted that markets had already received a preview of this approach when India was permitted to purchase Russian oil once again last week. The segment emphasized that all these options remained under consideration rather than being actively implemented, but their very discussion demonstrated that the Trump administration was experiencing "mounting economic and political pressure from this military campaign in Iran."

Venezuela Opens Mining Sector to Foreign Investment

Shifting focus to South America, the broadcast reported on a significant legislative development in Venezuela, where the national parliament gave preliminary approval to a draft bill that would open the country's mining sector to foreign and private investment. Monday's vote followed a visit to Caracas by U.S. Interior Secretary Doug Burgum, making him the second cabinet-level Trump administration official to visit Venezuela since the dramatic military operation that captured and deposed President Nicolas Maduro this past January.

The broadcast explained that the United States is seeking to reopen Venezuela's vast mineral resources to foreign investment after much of the sector was nationalized decades ago under Hugo Chavez, Maduro's predecessor. Venezuela possesses significant mineral deposits, including gold, diamonds, bauxite used in aluminum manufacturing, and coltan essential for electronics manufacturing. The U.S. push to reopen Venezuela's mining sector forms part of a broader strategic effort to counter China's mineral dominance amid an ongoing trade conflict.

A lawmaker quoted in the segment framed the development as "the beginning of a new era for Venezuelan mining," emphasizing that the legislation represented "a structure that corresponds to our new needs." The broadcast noted that this legislative action followed the passage of significant hydrocarbon legislation in January, with Venezuela's legislature accomplishing this policy shift within just three weeks in what was characterized as a rapid response to U.S. pressure.

Challenges and Obstacles to Overcome

Despite the optimistic framing surrounding the mining sector opening, the segment did not shy away from detailing the substantial challenges that lie ahead. The broadcast noted that much of Venezuela's heavy industry, including its mining sector, was nationalized decades ago under the hardline left-wing Chavez administration. While the current government is described as "far more pliant to U.S. demands" following Maduro's removal, analysts warned that "massive investment will be required to revive Venezuela's mining industry"—a reflection of the country's dilapidated infrastructure following years of economic mismanagement and neglect.

Beyond infrastructure concerns, the segment identified several structural obstacles that would complicate foreign investment. Endemic corruption within Venezuela's governmental institutions presents a significant risk for international investors. Illegal mining operations controlled by armed groups continue to operate across vast regions of the country, creating security concerns. Additionally, the potential for environmental disaster in fragile jungle ecosystems raises both practical and reputational considerations for potential investors. These factors combined represent what the broadcast described as "hurdles as Washington seeks to remake the economy of what was once Latin America's wealthiest country."

Detailed Takeaways

Market Volatility Remains the Dominant Theme: While Tuesday's rally provided relief following the market turmoil of recent weeks, analysts explicitly cautioned that volatility would persist. The Iran conflict remains unresolved, the Hormuz Strait stays closed, and Trump's mixed messaging introduces unpredictable variables into market calculations. Investors should maintain defensive positioning while recognizing that energy price swings will continue driving broader market movements in the near term.

Geopolitical Events Directly Impact Energy Prices Within Hours: The speed at which oil prices declined following Trump's comments—within a single trading session from approximately $120 per barrel to significantly lower levels—demonstrates the instantaneous nature of market reactions to geopolitical developments. This underscores the importance of monitoring political communications and their potential market implications, particularly during periods of heightened tension.

Strategic Petroleum Reserves Remain a Key Policy Tool: The discussion of releasing oil from emergency stockpiles highlights that governments retain significant tools to address supply disruptions, though these come with trade-offs. Strategic reserves exist precisely for scenarios like the current one, and their deployment could provide meaningful price relief but would reduce reserves that take years to rebuild.

Sanctions Politics Create Complex Trade Flows: The consideration of waiving Russian oil sanctions, subject to discussion with President Putin, illustrates how geopolitical conflicts create unexpected shifts in international trade relationships. The precedent set by India's recent authorization to purchase Russian oil demonstrates that sanctions regimes can be adjusted when strategic considerations warrant.

Venezuela's Resource Wealth Presents Both Opportunity and Risk: The opening of Venezuela's mining sector to foreign investment represents a significant opportunity for international capital to access substantial mineral deposits. However, the combination of infrastructure decay, corruption, armed groups, and environmental concerns means that investors must carefully evaluate risks against potential returns. Washington's willingness to overlook governance concerns in pursuit of strategic mineral independence from China will be tested in the coming years.
