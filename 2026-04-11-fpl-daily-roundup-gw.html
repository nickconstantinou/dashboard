Chip Strategy: The Four Viable Frameworks

The strategic conversation around chip deployment has crystallised into several distinct approaches, each with articulated reasoning from experienced managers.

The first and most common approach involves playing the wildcard in gameweek 32, bench boost in gameweek 33, free hit in gameweek 34, and triple captain in gameweek 36. This sequence targets the fixture congestion directly, allowing managers to completely restructure for gameweek 33's doubles, then pivot using the free hit for the blank that immediately follows. The limitation is that this strategy assumes your team requires rebuilding—if your current squad already has strong fixtures in gameweeks 32 and 34, wildcarding becomes less attractive.

The second approach preserves the wildcard for gameweek 35 while using free hit in gameweek 33 instead. This fits better for managers whose teams already look strong in the current gameweek but lack exposure to the gameweek 33 doublers. Some elite managers favour this approach specifically because it allows them to see exactly which teams receive which fixtures before committing to a wholesale restructure.

The third approach involves wildcarding in gameweek 33, free hitting in gameweek 34. This represents a more niche strategy employed by managers who want to assess the gameweek 33 situation before determining their approach to the blank—essentially using the wildcard as a responsive tool rather than a proactive one. The limitation is losing access to bench boost for the biggest double gameweek.

The fourth approach applies specifically for managers who have already used their triple captain chip: wildcard in gameweek 32, bench boost in gameweek 33, free hit in gameweek 34, leaving gameweek 36 without a triple captain focus. This strategy loses some appeal because you no longer need to preserve gameweek 36 specifically, making bench boost in that gameweek potentially more valuable.

Across all approaches, gameweek 33 consistently emerges as the optimal bench boost timing because it has the most fixtures and the most doubles—a simple mathematical truth that even the most nuanced analysis cannot escape.
