Chips & Strategy Again: Navigating the FPL Minefield as Cup Competitions Collide

Introduction

The business end of the Fantasy Premier League season has arrived, and with it comes the most complex fixture chaos of the campaign. In this episode of Planet FPL (Season 9, Episode 44), hosts Sitch and James dive deep into the implications of the FA Cup quarterfinal draw and Champions League results, exploring how these competitions will reshape the remaining fixture schedule and fundamentally alter chip strategy for FPL managers up and down the country.

With Manchester City now likely to exit European competition after their defeat to Real Madrid, and multiple English clubs facing uphill battles in their Champions League ties, the fixture landscape has become remarkably fluid. The hosts explain that there are currently 3,468 possible unique blank and double gameweek fixture combinations remaining—a staggering number that illustrates just how unpredictable the run-in has become. For FPL managers, this means traditional planning strategies may need to be thrown out the window in favor of a more flexible, reactive approach.

The State of English Football in Europe

The Champions League picture has shifted dramatically over the past week, and the hosts are keen to assess exactly what this means for FPL purposes. At the start of the week, three English teams—Manchester City, Liverpool, and Arsenal—were projected to progress to the quarterfinals. Now, following the first legs, that number has been reduced to just two, with Arsenal and Liverpool still looking the most likely to advance.

Manchester City suffered a defeat to Real Madrid that has left their trajectory uncertain, and the hosts agree they are now "not likely to go through." Chelsea face an even steeper mountain to climb, requiring a three-goal comeback against a strong opponent. Tottenham find themselves in an identical situation with a three-goal deficit. Newcastle United's trip to Barcelona appears extremely daunting, leaving Sitch to note that "obviously Newcastle now have to go away to Barcelona. Not likely to."

Liverpool, by contrast, remain in a strong position, and the hosts would still back them to progress. Arsenal's tie against Bayern Leverkusen presents an interesting scenario—should Arsenal go out, their fixture would be " chucked back in" to the schedule, as they play each other in that game, making it an easy one to rearrange.

The FA Cup quarterfinal draw has thrown up some fascinating ties that will significantly impact the Premier League fixture schedule. The standout match sees Manchester City host Liverpool at the Etihad—a fixture that Sitch notes would have been "tougher to call" had it been at Anfield, but with home advantage, City remain projected to go through. Arsenal will travel to St. Mary's to face Southampton, who conquered Fulham in the previous round—a result that proved personally costly for Sitch, who owns Fulham players and a Villa player that now face different fixtures in gameweeks 33 and 34.

Chelsea face Port Vale, and the hosts are in no doubt about the outcome: "Huge favorites." The final quarterfinal sees West Ham host Leeds in what the hosts describe as "similar to the city Liverpool scenario because you're at home, you will be protected." In short, the projections expect Chelsea, Arsenal, Manchester City, and West Ham to progress, though the hosts stress these fixtures "could easily go the other way."

The Blank Gameweek 31 Crisis

The immediate consequence of Manchester City's defeat to Newcastle on Saturday night is that they will now "almost certainly blank in gameweek 31." This is a critical piece of information for FPL managers who have been holding City assets hoping for a reprieve.

However, there remains a small window of possibility. The rearranged fixture against Crystal Palace from gameweek 31 could potentially be played in either gameweek 31 or 32 if both clubs exit European competition following the second legs next week. Sitch explains the reasoning: "If they were going to try and squeeze it into late in 31, then you're talking about what a two weeks notice it would be." He notes that technically, "there's no international break" currently, which would make giving fans such short notice "impossible because it's not fair to give fans that little notice." However, with an international break approaching, this becomes more feasible.

The hosts suggest that gameweek 32 would be "marginally more likely" than 31 for this fixture to be played, though they emphasize this is far from certain. What matters most for FPL managers is that City assets now carry significant blank gameweek risk, and those with captaincy ties to City players may need to activate contingency plans.

The Gameweek 34 Puzzle

The hosts turn their attention to gameweek 34, which represents perhaps the most complex fixture situation of the entire season. Teams that reach the FA Cup semi-finals will have their scheduled Premier League fixture postponed from gameweek 34, but this does not mean those games cannot still take place during that gameweek—merely that they may involve different opponents or be rearranged entirely.

One of the key projections the hosts discuss is that "one of those fixtures will remain in gameweek 34." For clubs still in European competition after next week's quarterfinal second legs, their postponed fixtures are "highly, highly likely" to be brought forward to gameweek 33 instead. This creates a cascading effect throughout the fixture list.

As Sitch explains, the quarterfinal fixtures will be arranged before the quarterfinals actually happen, meaning clubs won't know their European fate when the rearranged dates are set. The logic dictates that the most conservative approach is to assume all four FA Cup quarterfinalists could still be in Europe and schedule accordingly.

Currently, there are four confirmed fixtures for gameweek 34. The hosts note that there will be a "minimum of six games" and that it is "not impossible that all 10 fixtures happen in gameweek 34," though Sitch describes this as "exceptionally unlikely." Their current projection sits at seven fixtures in gameweek 34, leaving significant uncertainty about which teams will blank and which will have rearranged matches.

The television selection for gameweek 33 adds another layer of complexity. Sky Sports has scheduled Crystal Palace against West Ham on Monday night in gameweek 33, which Sitch believes was done deliberately: "It also possibly blocks West Ham against Everton being brought forward from 34 to 33. Um, that's why I think Sky put that in there."

This creates a situation where the TV selectors are essentially determining FPL outcomes by choosing when matches can and cannot be rearranged—a subtle but crucial factor that managers must account for when planning their chips.

The Statistical Landscape

To illustrate just how uncertain the fixture situation remains, the hosts reference a tweet from JC, known as FPL Spaceman from Solio Analytics. The numbers reveal the sheer complexity of the situation: there are 3,468 possible unique blank and double gameweek fixture slates remaining in the season.

The most likely permutation carries just a 20% probability, meaning even what the models consider the "best guess" has only a one-in-five chance of being correct. The 11 most likely scenarios account for 50% of the sample size, while 66 permutations cover 75% of possibilities. To reach 90% confidence, you need to consider 331 different scenarios.

This statistical reality underscores why FPL managers cannot simply plan for one outcome. As the hosts demonstrate, even slight changes—such as Liverpool beating Manchester City or one variable flipping—can dramatically alter the blank and double gameweek landscape. The message is clear: flexibility is paramount, and managers should build squads capable of adapting to multiple potential outcomes rather than committing heavily to a single projection.

Key Takeaways for FPL Managers

First, Manchester City assets now carry substantial risk for gameweek 31. With their Champions League exit looking likely, City players will almost certainly blank in 31. Managers holding Pep Guardiola's players should prepare alternatives, particularly if they have the captain's armband on a City asset.

Second, gameweek 34 will feature significant disruption but remains unpredictable. The exact nature of the blanks and doubles depends on European results next week, FA Cup outcomes, and television scheduling decisions. Rather than committing to a specific plan, managers should monitor the situation and remain adaptable.

Third, chip strategy must account for multiple scenarios rather than one ideal path. With 3,468 possible fixture configurations remaining, attempting to optimize for a single outcome is foolish. The hosts recommend building squad depth and flexibility to react to developments rather than trying to predict the unpredictable.

Fourth, the international break and European results will reshape the fixture calendar dramatically over the coming weeks. Next week's Champions League second legs represent a critical inflection point that will determine much of what happens in gameweeks 33, 34, and beyond.

Fifth, television scheduling decisions have real FPL implications. The Crystal Palace versus West Ham Monday night slot in gameweek 33 was almost certainly chosen to block certain fixture rearrangements, demonstrating how broadcast deals directly impact fantasy strategy.

Who Should Listen to This Episode

This episode is essential listening for any FPL manager navigating the business end of the season, particularly those in competitive mini-leagues where marginal gains matter. The content is most valuable for managers who own Manchester City assets and need to understand the blank gameweek implications, those planning to use their chips (especially the Bench Boost and Free Hit) in the remaining gameweeks, and anyone seeking to understand how real-world football competitions directly shape the FPL fixture landscape.

The episode provides no specific player recommendations or transfer advice, instead focusing on the structural mechanics of the fixture schedule and how to think about them strategically. For managers who want to understand not just what might happen but why—and therefore how to plan for multiple contingencies—this represents required listening. The hosts' emphasis on flexibility and probabilistic thinking offers a framework for decision-making that will serve managers well regardless of how the specific scenarios unfold.
