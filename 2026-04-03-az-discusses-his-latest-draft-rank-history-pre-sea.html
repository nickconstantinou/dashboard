Az Discusses His Latest Draft & Rank History | Pre Season | Fantasy Premier League Tips 2025/26

Introduction

As the 2025/26 Fantasy Premier League season approaches—now just over a week away—content creators across the FPL community are rolling out their draft teams and sharing the reasoning behind their selections. In this video, Aziz (known as Az to viewers) delivers his team reveal for FDL Blackbox, the FPL content platform he co-hosts alongside Mark and Andy, with Luke also joining the team. The video serves a dual purpose: it showcases Az's current draft thinking while also functioning as a candid retrospective on his FPL journey, particularly addressing the criticism he has faced after a difficult run of seasons.

What makes this video particularly compelling is Az's willingness to be vulnerable about his recent struggles. Rather than simply presenting his team without context, he opens up about his FPL history—complete with the disappointing finishes, the risky decisions that backfired, and the lessons he has learned along the way. For viewers who follow the FPL content space, this transparency offers valuable insight into how even experienced creators can struggle with the game's ever-evolving landscape, and what it takes to adapt and improve.

The Elephant in the Room: Addressing Recent Criticism

Az begins by directly tackling the negativity he has encountered on Twitter and other platforms, acknowledging that content creators across the FPL space have faced considerable backlash in recent times. He admits he has received his share of criticism, some of it justified given his performance. Rather than deflecting, he chooses to present his complete FPL record transparently, allowing viewers to judge for themselves.

A significant point of contention has been the title of "expert," a label applied to Az by Scout and the FPL Show as part of their marketing. Az is quick to clarify that this is not a designation he would ever claim for himself. He explains that while he loves his work with the Premier League and Scout, he has no control over how they choose to market him. When asked whether he could request they stop using the term, he notes that he cannot tell the Premier League how to market their content—and that the opportunity to work with these organizations is too valuable to decline simply over a word choice.

This section reveals Az's pragmatic approach to content creation. He emphasizes that despite the criticism, he has no plans to step away from the work he loves. With a full-time job alongside his content work, Az frames FPL as a passion project that has sustained him since the 2007/08 season. He acknowledges the negativity but makes clear that he will not be driven away by it, particularly when he is still enjoying the creative process and the community he has built through Blackbox.

A Detailed Look at FPL History

Perhaps the most substantive portion of the video is Az's thorough walkthrough of his FPL rank history, presented as a series of era-by-era breakdowns that illustrate both his successes and failures.

The Learning Years (2007-2009)

Az describes his first three seasons as a learning curve, with an average rank of approximately 186,000. These were the years he was developing his understanding of the game, building the foundation that would later support more competitive finishes. He notes that back then, fewer people played FPL, fewer tools existed for analysis, and he relied heavily on watching actual football matches to inform his decisions—a habit he has tried to maintain but acknowledges has become less effective in the modern era.

Finding Success (2010-2016)

The 2010/11 season marked Az's breakthrough, as he achieved his first top 5,000 finish with a rank of 3,800. The following season saw a regression to 175,000, which he describes without excuse as simply not a great year. However, 2011/12 brought redemption with a 10,600 finish—made particularly memorable because he finished just one point outside the top 10,000. He recalls being gutted by that near-miss, a disappointment that drove his determination to return stronger.

Between 2013 and 2016, Az entered a consistently strong period, maintaining an average rank of approximately 20,000. He achieved another 5,000 finish during this window and notably finished 2,000th in 2014, though he admits he does not have detailed memories of that season. This era represents what he considers his most reliable stretch of performance.

The Scout Cast Era and Personal Best (2016-2018)

Az reveals that he joined the Scout Cast towards the end of the 2016/17 season, finishing around 59,000. The following year brought his personal best finish ever: 800th. He managed to build on this momentum into 2018/19 with a 62,000 finish, resulting in an average rank of 40,000 across those two seasons. This period demonstrates his ability to sustain high-level performance across consecutive campaigns.

The Blackbox Beginning and Mixed Results (2019-2021)

Az believes he started working with Blackbox in 2020, coinciding with a challenging 2019/20 season where he finished 135,000. He notes that he was likely positioned much lower in the ranks before that point, suggesting a mid-season recovery. However, 2021/22 proved to be a strong rebound, contributing to a three-year average rank of 52,000 across this period. He emphasizes that his lowest finish during these years was 63,000, meaning he maintained relatively high standing throughout—enough to place him on the hallowed tables and leaderboards that serious FPL players aspire to populate.

The Recent Struggles (2022-2025)

Az does not shy away from describing the last three seasons as "a complete disaster." In 2022/23, he finished 143,000, though he highlights that he was around one millionth in the world with roughly six weeks remaining before managing a recovery. Significantly, he pipped his colleague Mark by just one point on the final day of that season—though he acknowledges this was fortunate rather than a result of strong late-season management.

The 2023/24 season stands out as particularly painful. Az finished 445,000, calling the result "embarrassing" and "really bad." He offers context: he began the season around 200,000-250,000 before making a series of aggressive decisions in pursuit of a top 100,000 finish. These included benching Haaland at home to Wolves to captain Cole Palmer instead, selecting Romero over Porro, and making numerous other tilt decisions in hope of a breakthrough. None of these gambles paid off, leading to a catastrophic rank collapse. Az admits that had he simply coasted to the end of the season, he likely would have finished around 150,000-200,000—a much better outcome than 445,000.

Last season (2024/25), Az struggled to find rhythm once again. He had a decent start but ignored Mohamed Salah for too long and proved "too slow" to react to several premium picks that surged in value and effectiveness. He recognizes this sluggishness as a defining characteristic of his recent poor performances.

Analysis: Why Az Has Struggled

Az offers thoughtful self-analysis regarding his recent decline. He identifies several interconnected factors:

Slow reactions to template shifts. Az admits he has consistently been too slow to capitalize on emerging trends and differential picks that the community identifies as high-value. While others pivot quickly to the next breakout player, he has lingered on previous selections.

Over-reliance on personal experience over data. Az confesses that he has been resistant to incorporating statistical models and analytical tools into his decision-making. He has prioritized his own historical knowledge and instincts over the synthesization of data that modern models perform exceptionally well. He explicitly acknowledges that the models and tools available today are "so good at synthesizing that data" that ignoring them entirely is a mistake.

Too much contrarianism. Az and Mark have perhaps pushed too hard against the consensus template in recent years, attempting to differentiate their teams at the expense of missed opportunities. He notes that Mark finished in the top 100,000 last season—one relatively good year amid otherwise difficult periods for both of them.

Loss of edge from reduced football viewership. In earlier seasons, Az watched considerably more live football and used that direct observation to inform his decisions. While he has tried to maintain this approach, the reality is that modern FPL success increasingly requires integrating data-driven insights rather than relying solely on eyeball tests.

The Draft Reveal and Looking Forward

With his transparent self-assessment complete, Az transitions into discussing his current draft team for the 2025/26 season. He walks viewers through the fixture analysis, the reasoning behind his player selections, and the players who are currently on his radar as potential additions before the season begins. This practical element provides viewers with actionable FPL advice informed by everything he has learned from both his successes and failures.

Az expresses hope that Luke will also produce a team reveal video, which would complete the trio of Blackbox hosts sharing their approaches heading into the new campaign. He notes that Luke, having recently returned from holiday, has been the most-requested voice from viewers who want to see his perspective.

Detailed Takeaways

1. Transparency builds credibility more than perfection. Az's decision to openly discuss his poor recent finishes—including the embarrassing 445,000 rank—demonstrates that content creators who acknowledge their mistakes earn more respect than those who hide them. His vulnerability creates trust with his audience and provides a realistic foundation for his advice.

2. Historical performance is not a guarantee of future success. Az's detailed record shows that even players with proven track records of top finishes can struggle when the game evolves. The FPL landscape has changed dramatically with better tools, more informed communities, and faster information flow. What worked five or ten years ago may not work today without adaptation.

3. Data integration is no longer optional. Az's explicit admission that he has "completely ignored things like review" and underutilized analytical models represents a critical lesson for any FPL manager. The modern game requires balancing gut instinct with statistical evidence, not choosing one at the expense of the other.

4. Risky decisions require appropriate risk management. Az's 2023/24 season serves as a cautionary tale about pursuing dramatic rank improvement through high-risk plays. His choice to ignore Haaland, select Romero over Porro, and make multiple tilt captaincy decisions demonstrates how chasing big gains can lead to catastrophic losses. Strategic patience often outperforms dramatic gambles.

5. Enjoyment and sustainability matter. Az's repeated emphasis on doing this for fun, despite the criticism, reflects an important perspective for casual FPL players. The game is ultimately entertainment, and maintaining joy in the process is valuable regardless of final rank.

Who This Is For

This video is particularly valuable for FPL managers who follow the content creation space and want to understand the perspectives of experienced—but human—analysts who have both excelled and struggled. It offers insight for those interested in the psychological aspects of FPL decision-making, including how to handle poor performance, when to adjust strategy, and how to balance data-driven approaches with personal intuition. The draft reveal elements also provide immediately applicable team-building advice for managers finalizing their squads before the season kicks off. Viewers who appreciate candid, self-aware content will find Az's transparency refreshing, while those seeking purely optimistic tips without acknowledgment of difficulty may prefer lighter content.
