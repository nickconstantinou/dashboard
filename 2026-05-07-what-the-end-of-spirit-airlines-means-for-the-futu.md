## Introduction

In November 2025, Spirit Airlines — the ultra-low-cost carrier that had become synonymous with affordable American air travel — shut down permanently, leaving behind 17,000 displaced workers and a stunned traveling public that hadn't anticipated losing what had become, for many, the only viable path to flying.

The news sparked an unexpected wave of grief. While Spirit had spent years as a punchline — mocked for charging fees to print boarding passes, for provocative advertising campaigns, for sardine-can seating — it had also become an essential service for millions of Americans who simply couldn't afford the alternatives. When the White House's attempts to rescue the airline failed, people mourned not just a company but a philosophy: the belief that air travel could be democratized, that the sky could belong to everyone.

This episode of The Daily, hosted by Rachel Abrams of the New York Times, examines what Spirit's collapse reveals about the American airline industry, the limits of the ultra-low-cost model, and whether the forces that doomed Spirit could threaten other carriers. Through conversations with reporter Niraj Chokshi and Spirit flight attendant Colleen Burns, the episode traces the airline's improbable rise, its revolutionary business model, and the economic headwinds that ultimately proved fatal.

---

## The Origin and Philosophy of Spirit Airlines

To understand why Spirit's demise resonated so deeply, one must first understand how unusual the airline was — and how deeply it had inserted itself into the fabric of American life.

Spirit traces its corporate lineage to the 1960s, when it operated as a trucking company before eventually pivoting to charter flights. The airline officially took the Spirit name in 1992, but remained largely anonymous until the mid-2000s, when it adopted the ultra-low-cost model that would make it famous. The concept was straightforward: cut costs ruthlessly and pass the savings to consumers in the form of rock-bottom fares. This approach had been pioneered in Europe by Ryanair, which continues to thrive using similar tactics, and Spirit believed the same formula could succeed in the American market.

The key innovation was what the industry calls "unbundling." Traditional airlines bundle amenities into the price of a ticket — meals, beverages, seat assignments, checked bags. Spirit separated all of these out, charging separately for each. Ben Baldanza, Spirit's chief executive who became the public champion of this approach, articulated the philosophy clearly: "We think it gives customers more choice." If you didn't need water, didn't need a seat assignment, didn't need to check a bag, you could purchase the cheapest possible ticket and simply... fly. The airline famously charged for printed boarding passes, carry-on bags, and even water — earning it a reputation as the most nickel-and-dimed airline in America.

Spirit embraced this notoriety. Rather than apologizing for its fees, the company leaned into provocation. It ran advertisements using the acronym "MILF" — standing for "Many Islands Low Fares" — a double entendre borrowed from the film *American Pie* that generated viral attention and news coverage. Another campaign sold tickets for $69, a price point that invited obvious jokes. The company understood that controversy was free publicity. "More ads means more controversy, means more news coverage, which means more attention," as one reporter noted in covering the airline's audacious marketing.

The public philosophy was take-it-or-leave-it. "I recognize our airline's not for everyone," Baldanza said. "If you wanna be coddled in the airport, if you want an enormous amount of legroom, if you want a free Coke, we're not your airline, and we're not built to be that airline. But if you wanna save $150 round trip, we're a great deal. Come fly us."

---

## The Spirit Effect and the Airline Industry

What began as a niche product slowly became a market force. By 2011, Spirit had gone public. By 2012, it was flying approximately 10 million passengers annually. By 2016, that number had doubled to 20 million. The growth was not simply a matter of stealing customers from competitors — it was also, as one academic would later describe, bringing new people onto airplanes entirely. Ben Baldanza himself put it bluntly: "We're not necessarily competing just with other airlines. We're competing with other forms of spending." A family might choose a $49 Spirit ticket to visit relatives instead of ordering takeout that week.

More significantly, Spirit's presence in a market demonstrably lowered prices for everyone. Researchers would later call this phenomenon "the Spirit Effect." When Spirit entered an airport for the first time, competing airlines had to reduce their fares to remain competitive. This was documented in academic studies — Spirit wasn't just surviving in a niche, it was actively reshaping the economics of American air travel, making flying accessible to populations who had previously been priced out entirely.

Colleen Burns, a Spirit flight attendant who spent a decade with the company, witnessed this impact firsthand. She recalls a conversation with a passenger — a blue-collar worker with shared custody of his son — who told her Spirit made it possible for him to take trips he could never have afforded otherwise. "We're the underdog story," Burns said. "I had no idea. None of us knew. We really all thought that Spirit was gonna pull through."

The flight attendant described how Spirit attracted "first-time flyers, so many families, so many people that never would have gotten the opportunity to fly if it wasn't for us." For these passengers, Spirit wasn't just an airline — it was a bridge to experiences they had assumed were reserved for wealthier Americans.

---

## The Vulnerabilities of the Ultra-Low-Cost Model

Despite its growth, Spirit's business model contained inherent fragilities that would eventually prove insurmountable.

The first major problem emerged from Spirit's own success: it had grown too large to ignore. When Spirit began flying the same routes as major carriers, those carriers responded by introducing "basic economy" fares — essentially Spirit-style tickets with similar restrictions: no checked bags, no carry-on bags (unless paid for separately), limited seat selection. This was a direct competitive response, and it worked.

The challenge was structural. When a passenger faces the choice between a United flight and a Spirit flight at the same price, United has inherent advantages: more daily departures (Spirit might fly a route twice daily; United might fly it eight times), meaning more options if a flight is cancelled, better customer service infrastructure, and more resources overall. As one analyst put it, "If you're looking at the same cost, you might be inclined to choose them." Spirit couldn't lower its fares much further — they were already at the bone — but matching the quality of larger carriers was prohibitively expensive.

Furthermore, the big airlines had something Spirit lacked: premium cabins. "If you have a choice between United and Spirit and they cost the same, you're probably gonna go with the bigger airline," the podcast's analyst explained. "And keep in mind also, the big guys, they have more profitable seats at the front of the plane, so they can subsidize the basic economy seats. Spirit doesn't have that. Spirit does not have a $2,000 business class seat."

The pandemic years brought additional pressures. All airlines suffered during COVID-19, but the faster-than-expected recovery created its own problems. Fierce competition to capture returning travelers kept fares low, making profitability difficult. Meanwhile, many pilots had retired during the pandemic, and airlines found themselves competing intensely for a limited pool of qualified aviators. This drove up labor costs industry-wide, but for Spirit — whose entire competitive advantage rested on cost minimization — rising labor expenses were particularly devastating.

"We have to start to improve quality," one analyst summarized of Spirit's impossible position. "They have to get you to where you're going on time more often. They have to have better customer service, and that's expensive. And that puts a lot of pressure on Spirit's business model."

---

## The Human Cost and Industry Implications

When Spirit finally shut down in early November 2025, the decision came suddenly. The company had fought for survival through multiple rounds of restructuring negotiations, and the Biden administration had attempted to intervene on the airline's behalf, but ultimately the board concluded the airline could not continue. By Saturday morning, Spirit was gone.

The immediate fallout was severe. Approximately 17,000 people — both direct employees and those indirectly employed through contractors and vendors — found themselves out of work. Tens of thousands of passengers who had purchased tickets were left stranded, their flights cancelled, their plans disrupted. The airline's closure left "a gaping hole in the airline market," as one analyst described it.

For Colleen Burns and her colleagues, the end came as a shock despite the warnings. "None of us knew," she said. "We really all thought that Spirit was gonna pull through." The airline had survived so many challenges — the jokes, the criticism, the regulatory scrutiny — that employees had developed an almost cult-like faith in the company's resilience. "Spirit has been through so many changes and revivals," Burns explained. "We went from being the absolute most hated airline, to be honest, towards the end, like people loved us. And I think all of us went through that journey with Spirit, and we all had so much pride in how far Spirit had come."

Whether the forces that killed Spirit pose a threat to other airlines remains an open question. The fundamental challenges — rising labor costs, intense competition from larger carriers with deeper pockets, the difficulty of competing on price when rivals can match your cheapest offerings while cross-subsidizing them with premium revenue — are not unique to Spirit. The ultra-low-cost model that once seemed revolutionary has been absorbed, adapted, and in some ways neutralized by the industry it disrupted. What that means for the future affordability of American air travel, and whether the democratization of flying that Spirit represented is sustainable, is a question the industry has not yet answered.

## Detailed Takeaways

**1. The "Spirit Effect" Demonstrates That Disruptors Can Lower Costs for Everyone, Not Just Themselves**

Spirit didn't just win customers by offering cheaper fares — it forced the entire industry to lower prices in markets where it operated. This "Spirit Effect," documented in academic research, shows how a single low-cost entrant can benefit consumers broadly by compelling incumbents to compete. The implication is that when truly affordable options disappear from a market, the damage extends beyond the airline's own passengers to anyone who would have benefited from the competitive pressure that airline created.

**2. Unbundling Revolutionized Airline Economics, But Became a Standard Feature Rather Than a Competitive Advantage**

Spirit pioneered the separation of airfares from ancillary services, but once major carriers adopted "basic economy" tiers, this differentiation collapsed. The lesson is that innovation in business models can be quickly copied by well-resourced competitors, and that a sustainable competitive advantage requires something that cannot be easily replicated — whether that's exclusive assets, brand loyalty, or operational efficiencies that rivals cannot match at any price.

**3. The Absence of Premium Revenue Creates Structural Vulnerability**

Spirit had no first-class or business-class cabins to subsidize its budget offerings, meaning it couldn't cross-subsidize low fares with high-margin premium revenue the way United, Delta, and American could. This structural imbalance meant that when Spirit and the big airlines charged identical prices, Spirit always lost. Any carrier competing purely on price with resource-rich competitors faces this fundamental disadvantage.

**4. Labor Cost Escalation Can Be Existential for Cost-Driven Business Models**

Spirit built its entire value proposition on being the cheapest option, which required keeping costs as low as possible. When pilot shortages post-pandemic drove up labor costs across the industry, Spirit had no way to absorb those increases without either raising prices (which would destroy its competitive advantage) or degrading service quality (which would drive away customers). Companies whose business models depend on cost leadership must build in resilience against cost shocks they cannot control.

**5. Affordability Alone Doesn't Guarantee Loyalty or Sustainability**

Despite serving millions of passengers who had no other affordable option, Spirit could not sustain its business. Affection for the airline among passengers and employees was real, but it wasn't enough to override the economic pressures. This suggests that market sentiment — even strong positive sentiment — is not always sufficient to save a company when the underlying financials don't work.
