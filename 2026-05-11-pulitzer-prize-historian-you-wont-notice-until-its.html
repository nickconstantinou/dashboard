    Introduction

    Anne Applebaum is not a alarmist. A Pulitzer Prize-winning historian and journalist who spent decades studying how the Soviet Union maintained control over hundreds of millions of people—and who was present in Warsaw when that system collapsed—she has built her career on rigorous, historically grounded analysis. Yet what she describes in this conversation is not a distant historical curiosity. It is, she says, happening now, in real time, in countries she never expected to revisit.

    The conversation centers on a question that most people assume has already been answered: Can democracies fail? The conventional wisdom says no—or at least, not here, not in America. But Applebaum, who has watched authoritarian patterns resurface across multiple countries simultaneously over the last decade, offers a more sobering assessment. She argues that democracy's fragility is not a theoretical concern but an active, ongoing crisis—and that the warning signs are not what most people expect.

    What makes this conversation particularly urgent is Applebaum's specific expertise. She has studied not just the collapse of authoritarian systems but their construction—the mechanics of how neutral institutions get captured, how electoral systems get rigged, how societies transition from "somewhat open" to "one-party state" without a single election being openly stolen. That institutional knowledge, accumulated over decades, forms the backbone of her argument. She is not predicting a hypothetical future. She is mapping a process that has already begun.

    How Democracies Actually Die

    One of the most important reframings Applebaum offers is about the common misconception regarding how democracies end. Most people imagine dramatic imagery—tanks in the streets, armed coups, assassins at the palace gates. Applebaum challenges this directly, arguing that in the modern world, the actual mechanism is far more mundane and therefore far more dangerous.

    According to her analysis, democracies in the contemporary era almost always end incrementally, from within, by leaders who were legitimately elected. Someone wins an election, and then—rather than preserving the rules that allowed them to win so that their opponents might contest them fairly four years later—they begin systematically dismantling the institutions that make fair elections possible. The process is slow enough that many people don't notice until it has already gone too far.

    > "Most people think democracies end with a coup d'etat or, you know, tanks in the street or somebody shooting up the Presidential Palace. But, actually, in the modern world, they mostly end because someone who is legitimately elected begins to take apart the system and take away the things that ensure free elections can continue."

    Applebaum traces her awareness of this pattern to the last decade, during which she watched it occur in multiple countries simultaneously. What had seemed, during her years of studying the Soviet Union and its fall, like a closed historical chapter—a story about the distant past—returned with startling clarity. She began writing not only about historical systems but about present ones, as she recognized the same tactics, the same rhetorical strategies, and the same institutional capture techniques being employed in countries that had long been considered stable liberal democracies.

    The Neutral Institutions That Democracy Requires

    Central to Applebaum's framework is the concept of neutral institutions—institutions that exist not to favor any particular political outcome but to ensure that the rules of the game are applied fairly regardless of who wins. She identifies several categories of these institutions, each of which she considers essential to democracy's survival.

    Independent courts are perhaps the most commonly discussed, but Applebaum places them in a broader institutional context. She argues that courts which can be influenced or captured by whoever holds political power represent one of the first and most consequential targets for leaders seeking to consolidate control, because courts are the mechanism through which disputes about elections, laws, and rights are resolved.

    Independent electoral commissions are equally critical. These bodies determine the rules by which elections are conducted—who is eligible to vote, how districts are drawn, how votes are counted. When these commissions are captured, elections can be structured in ways that make it nearly impossible for incumbents to lose, without ever technically violating any law.

    Independent media serves the informational function necessary for voters to make meaningful choices. Without it, citizens cannot reliably evaluate the performance or character of those in power.

    A meritocratic bureaucracy—the civil service that implements government policy—is the institution Applebaum discusses with perhaps the most distinctive emphasis. She describes this as a workforce of actual experts, hired and fired based on competence rather than political loyalty, tasked with managing the concrete functions of government: measuring pollution, constructing roads, maintaining infrastructure. The danger, she argues, arises when these positions are filled with cousins, friends, and allies of the ruling party rather than with people who understand the technical requirements of the work. The consequence is not merely inefficiency but a complete breakdown in the state's capacity to function as an impartial apparatus.

    > "So people are hired and fired to measure pollution or worry about traffic and road construction who aren't cousins of the ruling party. They aren't somebody's friend, but they're actual experts who understand how to do things."

    Viktor Orban as Template: The Pioneer of Electoral Capture

    When Applebaum is asked to identify the origin point of this modern strategy—elected leaders who use their legitimate power to ensure permanent control—the name that emerges is Viktor Orban of Hungary. She describes Orban as the "pioneer" of this approach, a figure who was elected with a large legitimate margin and then systematically moved to "capture the state."

    What Orban accomplished, in Applebaum's telling, was to demonstrate that democracy could be hollowed out from within using democratic means. He did not suspend elections. He won them. But by reshaping the institutions that governed how those elections were conducted and reported, he ensured that the outcome of any future election would favor his party. The model was both subtle enough to avoid international condemnation and effective enough to create permanent single-party control.

    Applebaum is explicit that Orban's approach has since been studied and, in some cases, directly copied by leaders in other countries. This imitation is, in her view, one of the key mechanisms of democratic decline globally. Nations look to other nations and adapt strategies that have worked elsewhere. The implications for the United States are stark: if the world's oldest democracy can be eroded using techniques refined in Hungary, then no democracy is truly safe.

    The American Precedent: One-Party States in the American South

    Perhaps one of the most striking arguments Applebaum makes is that the United States has already experienced something structurally similar to what she is describing—in living memory, for nearly a century. Addressing the assumption that undemocratic systems are inherently foreign phenomena, she points directly to the American South between the Civil War and the Civil Rights Movement of the 1960s.

    In those states, she describes, the South operated as a set of effectively one-party systems. The electoral rules were rigged. Not everyone was permitted to vote—Black Americans were either barred entirely or subjected to restrictions so severe that meaningful participation was impossible. Violence was used as an additional enforcement mechanism. The outcome was a form of democracy in name only, a system in which the ruling faction had no genuine fear of losing power precisely because the system had been designed to prevent any such outcome.

    > "In the American South, before the Civil Rights Movement, you very often had, in effect, in the Southern states, these one-party states where the rules were pretty rigged, everybody knew who was gonna win, not everybody was allowed to vote."

    Applebaum draws a direct line from this historical precedent to the present, suggesting that some of the people currently in positions of power in Washington are drawing on this historical memory—of what a one-party state, operating under democratic forms, can accomplish. The parallel is not incidental. It is, in her analysis, a template.

    Financial Corruption and Self-Dealing in Office

    One of the concrete concerns Applebaum raises involves the financial dimensions of democratic erosion in the United States. She points to specific data as evidence that decisions at the highest levels of government are being made on the basis of personal financial interest rather than national interest.

    Trump's net worth at the time he entered office was approximately $2.3 billion. Within two years, she states, that figure had grown to $6.5 billion. She is careful to note the mechanism this represents: when a president maintains business interests while in office, every decision—about regulations, trade policy, foreign relations—becomes entangled with the question of what benefits the president's personal financial position.

    The example of Jared Kushner is even more pointed. Applebaum asks the question directly: Why did the Saudi government invest $2 billion in Jared Kushner's fund? The answer, she argues, is self-evident. Kushner is Trump's son-in-law. The investment was not a reflection of Kushner's investment acumen or the merit of the fund's strategy. It was a payment—or an anticipation of future payments—made on the basis of political relationship. This is, in Applebaum's framing, not merely ethically troubling but systemically corrosive to democracy, because it means that the incentives of those in power are not aligned with the public interest.

    She goes further, describing what she calls a rise in "high end corruption" as a distinguishing feature of the current moment. Presidents and those close to them, she argues, have access to mechanisms for personal financial enrichment through political activity that were not available at that scale in American history previously. This represents one component of the broader deterioration she sees occurring.

    The Map of Global Democracy

    Applebaum references a map displayed in the conversation—a map of the world showing the level of democracy in each country, produced by organizations that track democratic governance. Two features of this map concern her most.

    The first is that the United States is no longer categorized as a liberal democracy. It is classified as an electoral democracy, a designation that indicates that while elections technically occur, the system no longer meets the criteria for a fully liberal democracy—meaning the electoral rules no longer guarantee a level playing field and the neutral institutions necessary for free and fair elections are considered compromised. Applebaum notes that this classification places the United States alongside similar systems in parts of South America, rather than with the fully liberal democracies that still characterize much of Europe, Australia, Japan, and South Korea.

    The second feature is the color progression of the map over time. Compared to a decade or two ago, the map shows a dramatic retreat of blue—the color representing liberal democracy—replaced by shades of red and gray indicating autocracy or "democratic gray zones." These gray zones are states that remain formally open but are trending in either direction, neither fully consolidated democracies nor fully closed autocracies. Applebaum describes watching this retreat of democracy occur in real time, not as a theoretical projection but as an observed phenomenon documented by multiple organizations using consistent methodologies.

    > "If you'd looked at a map like this a decade ago or two decades ago, it would have been a lot bluer, the blue being democracy and the red or reddish being autocracy. So you do see an absolute process of democratic decline."

    The implications of this global decline are compounded by what Applebaum identifies as the powerful example-setting function of the United States. Because America has served as the leader of the democratic world and the largest democracy, its trajectory carries enormous weight. The message sent when America begins to backslide is not lost on other nations. For some countries, it is inspiring in the most troubling sense: proof that if the Americans can do it, the same can be done elsewhere.

    Gerrymandering, Electoral Manipulation, and the Self-Reinforcing Cycle of Corruption

    One of the most analytically sharp moments in the conversation involves Applebaum's explanation of how gerrymandered electoral districts create a self-reinforcing cycle of corruption and institutional failure. She describes this as a mechanism that is already operating at the state level in the United States and which poses a danger at the national level if left unchecked.

    The logic is precise: when electoral districts are drawn in ways that favor one party, politicians in those districts no longer face genuine electoral competition. They will win regardless of their performance, their policy choices, or their responsiveness to constituents. This eliminates the fundamental accountability mechanism that democracy relies on. Politicians who do not have to win elections do not have to worry about voters. And when politicians no longer need to maintain their constituents' goodwill, they are free to pursue their own interests—including financial interests, political favors for donors, and other forms of corruption.

    > "Because if you're going to win anyway, why do you have to worry about your constituents? Then you have worse government and worse services. Because if you don't have to have an electoral contest, then you can pursue your own interests. You can do favors for businessmen who help you in other ways."

    Applebaum connects this institutional dynamic directly to the erosion she sees happening at the state level already, arguing that the pathologies associated with uncompetitive electoral systems are not hypothetical future problems but present realities in many parts of the United States. She warns that these same dynamics could be scaled to the national level if a fixed system were put in place that guaranteed the dominance of a single party.

    She reinforces this with the observation that the United States has already experienced one attempted electoral coup—the events following the 2020 election, when the president refused to accept the result and staged what Applebaum describes as an "intended" electoral coup. It failed. But she is unambiguous about the implications: the fact that it happened once means it can happen again, and anyone who assumes that such an attempt would be blocked or that no one would dare to try again is, in her view, being naive.
