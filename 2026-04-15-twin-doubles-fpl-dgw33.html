Twin Doubles: A Comprehensive Guide to FPL Gameweek 33 Strategy

Introduction: The Calm Before the Double

The Fantasy Premier League landscape has reached one of its most critical junctures of the season. With Gameweek 33 looming as the largest double gameweek of the campaign, FPL managers across the globe are finalizing their strategies—some having already deployed their wildcards during the international break, others preparing free hit chips for maximum flexibility. In this latest episode of the popular "Who Got The Assist" podcast, hosts Tom and Sam dissect the aftermath of Gameweek 32 while offering their strategic insights for the massive week ahead.

The podcast begins with Tom welcoming listeners with characteristic self-deprecation, noting he's "still here, year older, but no new baby yet"—referring to his second child expected sometime between now and May. His co-host Sam, whose allegiances lie with Southampton (a club currently playing in the Championship, making their results FPL-irrelevant), joins the discussion while watching the Southampton versus Blackburn match at halftime. The personal dynamic between the hosts—their exhaustion from family life, Tom's recently-passed birthday that got "swallowed up" by his daughter's celebration days earlier—provides an intimate backdrop to the serious tactical analysis that follows.

What makes this particular episode essential viewing (or listening) for FPL managers is the collective realization that many competitors have converged on nearly identical strategies. Both hosts acknowledge that their teams share remarkably similar structures, a phenomenon that will shape the competitive dynamics of the season's final stretch. The episode tackles Gameweek 32 performance review, deep dives into Double Gameweek 33 opportunities and risks, explores the Nico O'Reilly injury crisis and its ripple effects, and concludes with a meta-discussion about how the end game might unfold when so many managers find themselves on nearly identical paths.

Gameweek 32 Performance: Template Wildcards and the Reality of 58 Points

The discussion opens with a frank assessment of Gameweek 32 results, which Sam describes as "a really fun game of spot the difference" between their two teams. Tom reveals his performance yielded 58 points—a "slight green arrow" that moved him up approximately 15,000 places to around 317,000 overall. While not disastrous, the score reflects what Tom calls "a pretty template wild card," acknowledging the reality that many managers who wildcarded during the international break ended up with remarkably similar squads.

Examining the point contributions reveals a tale of two narratives. On the defensive side, Tom's selections delivered unexpectedly: "Vugan [scored] nine points, O'Reilly 14, Gabrielle and Van Heck with four and five respectively." All four defensive players returned, something Tom admits was "unexpected." However, the attacking players told a different story. "Bruno at least got an assist," Tom notes, while "Palmer hit defcon weirdly enough and Gross picked up an assist for six points." The rest—Semeno, Calbert Lewin, Joel Pedro, and Harland—all blanked, though Tom suggests "bar Calbert Lewin like wall wallpaper picks"—meaning they were essentially filler players occupying bench slots rather than core selections.

Sam reveals his team mirrors Tom's almost exactly, with only the goalkeeper position differing: "You've got strike, I've got Sess." The hosts also discuss what Tom terms "strategy B"—the path he didn't take. If he had purchased Bowen as originally planned instead of wild-carding, Tom calculates his score would have reached 71 points, meaning he currently sits "13 points down on strategy B right now." However, the wildcard enabled bench boosting capability that wouldn't have existed otherwise. The calculation illustrates a fundamental FPL tension: the chip that maximizes future potential often costs present points.

Ragaba live, a respected FPL analyst, noted that wildcarders did outscore the average in Gameweek 32, though Tom acknowledges the effect of "pulled the vacuum of effective ownership towards the wild card overall." Many big accounts reportedly held their wildcard until late stages, creating compressed ownership despite the chip being widely deployed.

Double Gameweek 33: The Biggest Week of the Season

The episode's core focus shifts to Double Gameweek 33, which both hosts identify as "the biggest one of the season." This week presents unique challenges because managers who wildcarded last week are now "pretty well set up"—they know their teams and the immediate decisions have already been made. The hosts recognize this creates a different dynamic for the podcast: rather than prescribing transfers, they're analyzing what opportunities remain and how to maximize them.

Tom outlines the agenda for this DGW33 discussion: examining "the games and what interests us ahead of double gameweek 33," analyzing "what's going on with Nico O'Reilly, the one big injury doubt ahead of gameweek 33," exploring replacement options if he's ruled out, and reviewing "free hit drafts ahead of gameweek 33 because we know that this week is also a popular week for that one, as well as maybe some wild cards out there as well."

The fundamental challenge facing managers is squad construction for this double. As Tom explains: "if you did set up for a bench boost, you're picking from a pretty narrow pool as we discussed on the pod last week, barring one or two, maybe three positions." The double gameweek nature means bench options become crucial—hence the widespread wildcard-plus-bench-boost combination—but when everyone adopts the same strategy, the competitive advantage diminishes.

Sam adds perspective: "I just can't imagine there's going to be kind of Yeah, there's going to be a lot of people basically very very similar team." This concentration creates an interesting psychological dynamic: even when your team is "differential" (different from the pack), the overall strategy convergence means the differentiation becomes less meaningful.

The Nico O'Reilly Situation: Crisis and Opportunity

Perhaps the most pressing DGW33 issue involves Nico O'Reilly, who Tom describes as "the one big injury doubt ahead of gameweek 33." The discussion mentions "a blurry f photo going around that we're obviously going to pick apart to the nth degree"—suggesting managers are scrutinizing any available information for clues about his fitness. Pep Guardiola's typical reluctance to provide clear updates adds uncertainty: "I doubt Pep's going to say too much, but we'll have a look anyway."

The significance of O'Reilly cannot be overstated. As Sam notes, "Nico O'Reilly is fair to say save the wild card and without that 14 pointer, I think things do look a bit more desperate." His 14-point haul in Gameweek 32 essentially rescued Tom's wildcard investment; without that return, the chip's effectiveness would be severely diminished.

The conversation explores potential replacement options should O'Reilly be ruled out. The hosts recognize this creates both a crisis and an opportunity—managers without coverage face difficult decisions, while those with plan B might gain an edge. However, the limited pool of viable replacements (the "pretty narrow pool" mentioned earlier) means that mass transfers into the same alternative could create unwanted ownership concentration.

Tom raises an interesting point about the mental calculation many managers are making: "I did add up the points I would have got if I didn't wild card... it would have scored me 71 points. So, I'm 13 points down on strategy B right now." This mathematical approach reveals how the FPL community debates chip efficiency—not just whether you scored well, but whether the alternative approach would have yielded better results.

Meta-Discussion: When Everyone Has the Same Team

The episode's final section ventures into what Tom calls "a bit of a meta discussion towards the end of the pod." This reflection addresses the psychological and strategic implications of the current FPL landscape, where so many managers find themselves on nearly identical paths.

Sam captures the core tension: "I do feel like a bit of a sense of antilimax when it comes to how the season may unfold now that I've seen that loads of people have got 58 points this week... loads people have got a very similar team and many of us especially if you're a one-3 transfer going into this looking at a very similar set of moves into the end game."

Tom identifies himself as "pessimistic about it" while acknowledging Sam takes the "optimistic" view. Yet both recognize the paradox at the heart of competitive FPL: even when your individual picks are differential, if everyone follows the same strategic framework and transfer roadmap, the differentiation collapses. As Tom notes, "even though the team itself is differential, it feels like wild carders may not—we might do, you never know."

The discussion also touches on broader competitive dynamics. With Liverpool playing PSG and Arsenal yet to play (during the recording), there were various European competitions affecting squad availability for Villa, Forest, and Palace players. Most FPL managers have limited exposure to these assets—Sam mentions "Not a ton of FPL interest there for many of us bar Gabrielle, but I'm sure there's ownership dotted around for a few different players there."

The hosts acknowledge the "current state of the game this season" and how the "end game is shaping up." With many managers running similar strategies, the question becomes whether execution details (captaincy choices, bench management, transfer timing) will matter more than asset selection, since the latter has become largely commoditized.

Key Takeaways for FPL Managers

1. The Wildcard-Bench Boost Combination Created Massive Ownership Convergence

Tom and Sam both deployed templates that converged heavily. While this strategy was optimal for maximizing Double Gameweek 33, it simultaneously reduced differentiation. Managers should recognize that making "correct" decisions in an environment where everyone makes the same decision doesn't create competitive advantage—it only prevents falling behind.

2. Defensive Returns Can Be More Unpredictable Than Attacking Returns

Tom's unexpected clean sheets from Vugan, O'Reilly, Gabrielle, and Van Heck contrasted sharply with blanked attacking assets. This asymmetry suggests that even in template-heavy weeks, defensive picks carry higher variance—potentially either saving a week or sinking it, regardless of team construction logic.

3. The Opportunity Cost of Wildcards Can Be Quantified and Should Guide Expectations

Tom's calculation of 13 points lost versus "strategy B" demonstrates a useful analytical framework. Managers should mentally model alternative pathways—not to second-guess decisions made, but to calibrate expectations and reduce post-hoc rationalization. Knowing you're "down" on a non-wildcard strategy helps contextualize scores and maintain rational decision-making.

4. Single Injury Events Can Overwhelm Strategic Planning

The O'Reilly situation illustrates how one fitness doubt can dominate the narrative. His 14-pointer essentially validated the wildcard; without that return, the week's performance would have been drastically different. This concentration of value in single assets creates enormous variance that no amount of strategic planning can fully mitigate.

5. The End Game's Outcome May Depend Less on Team Selection Than Originally Assumed

With such high strategic convergence, the hosts suggest that "execution" factors—captain choices, bench usage, transfer timing—might ultimately determine rankings more than the specific players selected. Managers should mentally prepare for scenarios where doing everything "right" still produces underwhelming results simply due to statistical concentration.

Casual players seeking to understand FPL strategy at a deeper level will appreciate how the hosts blend tactical analysis with honest self-assessment—their discussions of missed opportunities, trade-offs, and uncertain outcomes provide an authentic view of how experienced managers approach the game.

Final Thoughts

The episode concludes with the acknowledgment that much remains uncertain—both in terms of injuries (O'Reilly and others mentioned) and in how the season's final weeks will actually unfold. The hosts' candid discussion about optimism versus pessimism reflects a broader tension in FPL: the game rewards confident decision-making, yet the underlying randomness means that confidence doesn't guarantee results.

What emerges from this hour-long conversation is a picture of an FPL community at an inflection point. The easy decisions have been made; now managers must navigate increasingly complex trade-offs with incomplete information. Whether you're pessimistic about the homogenized strategies or optimistic about finding edges through execution, one truth remains constant: the season's most consequential weeks lie ahead, and every point will matter.
