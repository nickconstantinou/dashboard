# In Plain Sight: Three Dark Secrets That Were Hiding in Plain Sight

Key Insights:

  • Rodney Alcala killed an estimated 4 victims before appearing on *The Dating Game* in 1978, and up to 130 people over two years—at a rate of at least one murder per week—using his charm and a fake "fashion photographer" identity to lure women
  • Alcala's background check would only have revealed his prior assault conviction; the check could not have exposed the nighttime predatory pattern he used to trap women by convincing them to return to his apartment under false pretenses
  • In the Mexico City case, the mother's remains showed scratch marks on the wall, indicating she was *alive* when sealed inside and desperately tried to alert her sons for help during the days or weeks before she died
  • At the Dallas surgical center, the patient's deterioration from stable vitals to cardiac arrest occurred within minutes—between the hallway encounter with colleagues and Marsden's visual observation of her skin color change
  • The Baylor Scott and White Surgicare Center incident illustrates how rapidly anesthesia complications can manifest even after standard disconnection protocols are followed, requiring constant vigilance through recovery

    Introduction

    The phrase "hiding in plain sight" takes on a chilling new meaning when examined through the lens of true crime. In this episode of *The Mr. Ballen Podcast*, listeners are treated to three unsettling stories that share a common thread: individuals whose dark secrets were concealed not through elaborate schemes, but through the ordinary, mundane surfaces of daily life. A reality television set. A childhood home. A medical operating room. Each scenario represents a space where trust is implicit, where the appearance of normalcy creates a blind spot that predators and circumstances exploit with devastating effectiveness.

    Mr. Ballen, the podcast host, frames these narratives as explorations of how deception thrives when people assume the structures around them—the screens, the walls, the medical protocols—are inherently safe. The episode, titled *In Plain Sight*, remastered audio from three separate stories originally published on the main YouTube channel, weaves together tales of serial predation, familial violence, and medical emergency. What emerges is a meditation on how thoroughly human beings tend to accept surface appearances, and how catastrophic that acceptance can be when assumptions are catastrophically wrong.

    Story One: The Dating Game

    The Setup and the Show

    Before *The Bachelorette* gave 30 men the chance to compete for one woman's affection, American audiences watched *The Dating Game*, a reality television show that began in 1965 and ran for decades. The format was simple: a bachelorette would ask three contestants questions without seeing their faces, basing her choices entirely on their verbal responses. At the episode's conclusion, she would select one contestant for a date. On the surface, it was wholesome entertainment built on the innocent premise that personality, not appearance, should drive romantic attraction.

    The September 13, 1978 episode featured Cheryl Bradshaw as the bachelorette. When she selected Bachelor Number One—a tall, good-looking, long-haired man named Rodney—the audience watched the standard reveal. Rodney came around the partition, the two contestants saw each other for the first time, everyone laughed, and they embraced warmly. It looked like any other episode. On stage, Rodney appeared no different from any other contestant who had ever appeared on the show. He was charming, confident, and immediately likeable.

    The Backstage Transformation

    What happened next would haunt Cheryl Bradshaw for the rest of her life. After the cameras stopped rolling and the two of them went backstage to continue their conversation in private, Rodney's demeanor underwent a startling transformation. According to Bradshaw's later accounts, he became "incredibly creepy" almost instantly. He began making unsettling statements, including the ominous declaration: "I always get the girl." Then he suggested she join him for a date that night, describing it as "a date you'll never forget."

    Bradshaw, trusting her instincts, declined. "No. Thanks." It was a decision that almost certainly saved her life.

    Had the show's producers conducted a thorough background check on Rodney, they would have discovered a disturbing prior conviction. Rodney Alcala had served three years in prison for assaulting an eight-year-old child and was currently on parole. But the podcast host emphasizes that even a comprehensive background check would have failed to reveal the true extent of what Rodney was doing outside of his criminal record—because what he did at night was far worse than anything that appeared in any database.

    The Fashion Photographer Predation Pattern

    Unlike many predators who operate in shadows, Rodney Alcala was shamelessly confident and possessed an unusual ability to approach any woman directly and charm her within minutes. He would enter bars, introduce himself, and propose what seemed like an incredible opportunity: he was a fashion photographer, he told them, and they were so beautiful that he wanted to photograph them professionally. Would they come back to his apartment for a photo shoot?

    Just as Cheryl Bradshaw had been charmed by his responses on the dating show, these women found themselves immediately drawn to his confidence. They said yes. They agreed to accompany him to his apartment, where he would shut and lock the door—transforming from the charming stranger into the "creepy guy" that Cheryl had glimpsed backstage.

    Unlike Cheryl, who could simply walk away when she saw this side of him, these women found themselves trapped. Some were forced to participate in the photo shoot regardless of their wishes. Then, after the session concluded—regardless of what had occurred during it—Rodney Alcala would strangle them to death.

    The Full Scope of the Horror

    Rodney Alcala was not merely a charming con artist or even a dangerous criminal with a prior conviction. He was a serial killer, and by the time he stepped onto the *Dating Game* set and was selected by Cheryl Bradshaw, he had already killed four people. In the span of approximately two years, he murdered an estimated 130 victims. The mathematics are staggering: over those roughly 730 days, Alcala was killing at least one person per week, often more.

    This case became infamous not only for the sheer number of victims but for the audacity of its setting. A nationally televised show had, inadvertently, provided a platform for a murderer to find his next victim. The entertainment apparatus that should have been safe—the romantic fantasy of *The Dating Game*—had instead become an hunting ground.

    Alcala was eventually apprehended, convicted, and sentenced to death. His case prompted ongoing debates about background checks for reality television contestants and the false sense of security that "normal" settings provide.

    Story Two: Something Scratching

    Two Brothers, A Messy Divorce, and a Vanished Mother

    On June 1, 2018, thirty-six-year-old Luis Garcia traveled into Mexico City to meet his older brother. The two men were exceptionally close, bound together by shared trauma that had shaped their entire adult lives. When Luis was fourteen and his brother fifteen, their parents had gone through a brutal divorce. During this painful process, their biological father simply walked out and never returned. The boys never saw him again.

    Shortly after the divorce, their mother remarried, and her new husband moved into the family home. Then, inexplicably, the mother vanished.

    The boys immediately suspected their stepfather. They asked him repeatedly: "Where is she? Where has she gone?" His answer never changed. She had gone out on an errand, he claimed, and simply never came back. This explanation satisfied neither of them—neither boy could believe their mother would abandon them voluntarily. She was not the type of person who would leave her children without a word.

    Their suspicions deepened when, just days after their mother disappeared, their stepfather also vanished without explanation. The two teenage boys, abandoned by both parents, were left entirely alone to fend for themselves. The childhood home that had once held their family now held only echoes and questions.

    Filing a Report, Finding Nothing

    The brothers contacted police and explicitly told them that they believed their stepfather had something to do with their mother's disappearance. They could not prove it, but their instincts told them something terrible had occurred. Law enforcement launched an investigation, searching throughout Mexico City for any trace of the missing woman. They looked for her remains, for evidence, for witnesses. They also attempted to locate both the stepfather and the biological father who had abandoned the family years earlier.

    The investigation yielded nothing. No body. No suspects. No resolution. Eventually, without leads or evidence, the case wound down. The two orphaned brothers, now essentially on their own in the world, somehow survived their teenage years without parents, learning to support themselves and each other through circumstances that would have broken many families.

    Yet they never stopped searching for answers. For nearly two decades, Luis and his older brother continued to investigate their mother's disappearance on their own, combing through Mexico City looking for any clue that might explain what had happened. The absence of closure—the not-knowing—became a wound that refused to heal.

    Returning to the Childhood Home

    The two brothers met on that June day in 2018 not to resume their search but to address practical matters concerning their childhood home. After their mother and stepfather had vanished, the boys had continued living in the house, somehow managing to survive their adolescent years there before eventually moving out and establishing their own adult lives. Crucially, they had never sold the property. They had kept it in their possession, allowing it to sit empty for years.

    Now, for the first time in a long while, they planned to return, clean the place out, assess whether to renovate or sell, and finally put the property to some practical use.

    When they arrived, the house was frozen in time. Family photographs still hung on the walls. The familiar spaces held both warm memories and painful ones—the memories of a family that had existed before everything fell apart. The brothers walked through the home, revisiting their past, experiencing the complicated emotional weight of being back in a space that held so much history, much of it profoundly sad.

    As they explored, they decided that the house required more than just cleaning. Years of vacancy had allowed the property to fall into significant disrepair. They would need basic renovations before the house could be sold or occupied. Over the next several days, they arranged for contractors to begin work on essential upgrades while they continued cleaning and packing up decades of accumulated belongings.

    The Discovery in the Wall

    On one particular day, while contractors worked in the master bedroom, the brothers were in the living room when they heard a sudden commotion. One of the contractors began shouting for them to come immediately. Something was terribly wrong.

    The brothers rushed into the master suite. There, on the ground, sat an object that neither brother initially understood. The contractor, having composed himself enough to speak, explained what it was. In an instant, decades of questions resolved into horrifying clarity.

    When the contractor's sledgehammer had struck the wall in the master suite, he had expected to find standard construction—studding, insulation, perhaps old wiring. Instead, when he pulled the hammer free, a white object came tumbling out of the freshly opened cavity. It was a human skull.

    The brothers were looking at their mother's remains.

    What the Wall Revealed

    After pulling apart the wall and examining the space within, investigators—and the brothers—found the rest of the skeleton. She had been bound and gagged. She had been placed inside the wall of her own family home, sealed in a tomb that her sons had lived beside for years.

    But the most disturbing detail was what the wall showed. All throughout the enclosed space where she had been left to die, scratch marks covered the interior surfaces. These marks were not random damage or pre-existing cracks in the construction. Forensic analysis would conclude that she had been alive when she was sealed inside the wall, and in her final hours or days, she had desperately clawed at the surfaces around her.

    She had been trying to get out. She had been trying to reach her sons. In the silence of that sealed wall, in the darkness, she had scratched and scraped and screamed without being heard—until finally, she fell silent forever.

    The brothers' suspicions had been correct all along. Their mother had not abandoned them. She had not run away. She had been murdered and hidden in the place where her sons still slept, still ate, still tried to move forward with their lives without understanding why their family had been destroyed.

    No Resolution

    The brothers immediately contacted police, who arrived at the house to investigate. They examined the scene, collected evidence, and attempted to piece together what had happened two decades earlier. Detectives strongly suspected the stepfather's involvement, given that he had vanished just days after the mother—a pattern that suggested either guilt or complicity in flight.

    But locating him proved impossible. If he was still alive, he would now be over ninety years old, and the trail had gone cold decades ago. The police could not solve the case, and it remains officially unsolved.

    For the brothers, there was finally confirmation of what they had always known and mourned—but no justice, no answers about why, and no opportunity to confront the person responsible. Their mother had been hiding in plain sight all along, in the most terrible way imaginable.

    Story Three: Be Still Their Hearts

    The Setting and the Procedure

    On May 27, 2022, Dr. Chad Marsden stood inside an operating room at the Baylor Scott and White Surgicare Center in Dallas, Texas. The room contained the standard surgical team—a surgeon, a nurse, and an anesthesiologist—and their unconscious patient, a middle-aged woman undergoing a routine liposuction procedure.

    Dr. Marsden's role that day was critical but routine: ensuring that patients remained safely unconscious during surgery and woke up smoothly afterward. He was experienced, professional, and familiar with the protocols that governed every stage of the anesthesia process.

    The liposuction was concluding without incident. The surgeon had just finished stitching up the woman's incisions, and it was now time to reverse the anesthesia and bring her back to consciousness. Dr. Marsden followed the established procedure precisely. He turned off the anesthesia machine—the rectangular device that converts liquid anesthetic drugs into vapor that can be inhaled through a mask. He removed the breathing mask from the patient's face. He changed her IV line, switching from the anesthetic drug mixture to plain saline, which would provide no sedation but would maintain the intravenous access point.

    With the drugs no longer entering her system, the patient would gradually wake up over the next half hour or so—a normal, expected process that unfolds thousands of times every day in operating rooms across the country.

    The Hallway and the Colleagues

    After completing the disconnection process, Dr. Marsden, the circulating nurse, and the surgeon transferred their patient from the operating table onto a stretcher. They began pushing her through the hallway toward the Recovery Room, where she would complete her awakening under continued observation.

    As they walked, they encountered two colleagues in the narrow hallway: another nurse and another anesthesiologist. The hallway was tight enough that Dr. Marsden needed to step aside briefly to let them pass. They exchanged brief pleasantries—small gestures of collegial recognition in a high-stress environment—and then Marsden and his team continued toward the Recovery Room.

    This was a normal interaction, unremarkable in every way. Colleagues passing in a hallway, a moment's pause, a wave. Nothing about this moment suggested anything was wrong.

    The Change

    But as they walked, Dr. Marsden happened to glance down at their patient on the stretcher. What he saw stopped him cold.

    The woman's skin had turned completely gray.

    This was not a gradual fading or a slight pallor. This was a dramatic, unmistakable change in coloration that indicated something was catastrophically wrong with her physiological state. Dr. Marsden, an experienced anesthesiologist, was utterly caught off guard by how rapidly this had occurred. Seconds earlier, everything had been proceeding normally. Now she was visibly dying.

    He looked at her vital sign monitor. Her blood pressure, which had been normal just moments before, had spiked to dangerously high levels. As he watched, her blood pressure plummeted in the opposite direction—from dangerously high to immediately life-threatening. Simultaneously, her oxygen saturation began to fall, indicating that her body was no longer oxygenating her blood properly. Then she began foaming at the mouth, a sign of severe physiological distress.
