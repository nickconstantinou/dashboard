That Show With Nico ep. 7 | Planet FPL 2025/26

Comprehensive Summary

Introduction

This episode of "That Show With Nico" marks the return of one of the Fantasy Premier League community's most entertaining podcasts after a brief hiatus. Hosted by the effervescent Nikico alongside his longtime collaborator James—formerly known as "Mr. 33k" but now residing at a humbler 14k rank—the episode serves as avictorious return to content creation following their break. The timing is particularly relevant for FPL managers who are now deep in the season's critical phases, facing crucial chip decisions that could make or break their campaigns.

The conversation balances genuine FPL strategy discussion with the unmistakable chemistry between two friends who have been producing content together for years. Rather than easing listeners back into their format with lighter fare, the hosts dive straight into the complexities of chip strategy—a decision that reflects how seriously they approach the analytical side of FPL despite their playful banter. This episode captures what makes Planet FPL content stand out: the blend of accessible strategy discussion with the unfiltered personalities that make fantasy football feel like chatting with mates down the pub rather than consuming corporate sports media.

FPL Chip Strategy and the Arsenal Result

The episode opens with a candid discussion about FPL chip deployment, specifically addressing the common dilemma of when to use bench boost and triple captain. Nikico reveals that he hadn't prepared his chip strategy in advance, choosing instead to wait until after Arsenal's result on Saturday night before making any plans. This approach proved strategic because the fixtures and projections change significantly depending on cup results, and waiting for that information gives managers a clearer picture of what the optimal strategy should look like.

The reasoning behind this patient approach becomes clear when Nikico explains that Arsenal's loss actually benefited his FPL prospects. Had Arsenal won, many managers would have been pushed toward using their wildcards in Gameweek 32 and bench boost in Gameweek 33, which would have created a much more competitive environment for those sticking to the planned schedule. By losing, Arsenal's fixtures became less attractive, meaning fewer managers would be wildcarding against him—a small but meaningful edge in a game where millions compete for every point.

James interjects with the admission that he hasn't mentioned his FPL team in months because there's "no point"—a relatable confession for anyone who has experienced a disastrous season after harbouring genuine hopes of climbing the ranks. His season went so badly that he describes the FPL gods as being "against me," listing various reasons for his struggles while maintaining the self-deprecating humour that defines his on-screen presence. Despite this, he remains committed to returning next season, displaying the same resilient optimism that characterizes many FPL managers who have endured painful campaigns.

The Saka and EZ Warning

Nikico takes the opportunity to remind listeners of advice he gave earlier in the season regarding two specific players: Bukayo Saka and Eberechi Eze. He claims he told people to "stay away" from these players, advice that was met with laughter at the time but has proven prescient given the challenging seasons both have endured. While this is clearly delivered with the benefit of hindsight and some playful self-congratulation, it touches on a genuine point about FPL decision-making: sometimes the most contrarian advice turns out to be the most valuable.

The hosts joke about James having "no joy in my football season" and how Arsenal's struggles simultaneously hurt his actual football allegiances while benefiting his FPL position—a common conflict for fans who support clubs whose players might not be performing optimally for their fantasy teams. This tension between supporting your real-life team and optimizing your FPL team is a central experience for millions of managers, and the podcast captures that internal conflict with authentic humor.

Fulham Fandom and Stadium Experiences

The conversation shifts to lighter territory as James quizzes Nikico about his recent adoption of Fulham as his team to support. Nikico admits he hasn't yet visited Craven Cottage this season but expresses enthusiasm about going, particularly for the "full lump" matchday experience. This leads to a discussion about ticket pricing at Fulham, which Nikico describes as expensive, especially for good seats in the newly rebuilt Riverside stand.

Nikico contextualizes the pricing by explaining that Fulham, as a mid-table Premier League club without a massive global fanbase, has many tickets purchased by tourists and casual viewers rather than dedicated supporters. This drives up prices in the most desirable areas of the ground, pricing out loyal fans who might prefer those sections for the facilities and viewpoints they offer. Despite this criticism, Nikico acknowledges that Craven Cottage is a "unique stadium" with "nice character," demonstrating genuine appreciation for the ground's history and atmosphere even while acknowledging its imperfections.

The hosts joke about Nikico's fickle team support, noting that he switches clubs regularly and might abandon Fulham by season's end. Nikico acknowledges this pattern, admitting he has grown "fond of Tottenham" after joining Planet FPL and also now supports West Ham—adding that he has "no choice" because viewers see his pain on Monday mornings following Tottenham matches. This segment exemplifies the show's ability to find humor in the universal experiences of football fandom while maintaining the conversational flow that keeps listeners engaged.

The Expressions Discussion

A particularly entertaining section involves James's enthusiasm for "Expressions," a Tottenham-supporting YouTube streamer known for his distinctive reaction videos. James describes watching Expressions' content after Tottenham losses, explaining that it makes him feel "worse but better at the same time"—a paradox that captures the masochistic nature of supporting a club that has historically struggled for sustained success.

The hosts debate whether they find Expressions annoying, with both agreeing that he's not irritating in the way many club-specific YouTube creators can be. James speculates that this might be because Tottenham are "so good at making fools of themselves," meaning there's less pressure on supporters to defend the team's performances when they're regularly providing ammunition for criticism. The conversation becomes briefly investigative as James attempts to discover Expressions' real name, eventually concluding that the streamer deliberately keeps his identity private—something both hosts respect as a reasonable personal choice.

This tangent demonstrates the podcast's conversational DNA, where topics naturally evolve based on the hosts' genuine interests rather than adhering to a rigid structure. Rather than forcing listeners through an agenda, the show allows the conversation to breathe, which creates the intimate feel of listening to two friends discuss their shared passions rather than a produced broadcast.

Ethnicity and National Identity

In a more personal segment, James reveals some details about his ethnic background, explaining that he has Polish, Austrian, German, and possibly Hungarian ancestry alongside Irish heritage from his mother's side. Despite these diverse roots, James states he considers himself English rather than British, acknowledging that this might not be something many people feel particularly proud of at the moment—though he notes the England football team provides exceptions to this rule.

The hosts engage in some playful banter about James's surname, with Nikico suggesting it might be "Lobovski" or similar, while James insists his name is "James Lyndon." The conversation takes on a humorous quality as they debate whether this is his actual name or whether he's using a stage name, with various ridiculous suggestions thrown out between laughs. This segment showcases the easy chemistry between the hosts, where personal information becomes material for jokes rather than serious discussion.

World Cup Qualification Chaos

The episode touches briefly on international football, specifically Italy's failure to qualify for the World Cup. Nikico describes this as "shock but not really a shock," noting that while Italy's absence was surprising on one level, their struggles in qualifying had been evident for some time. The discussion centers on Italy's match against Bosnia, where a early goal from Moyes and Keane seemed to signal the end of Bosnia's campaign before Leonardo Bonucci's sending off dramatically altered the match's trajectory.

While the specifics of this match are discussed with less energy than the FPL content, it demonstrates the breadth of topics the hosts are willing to cover, proving that the show isn't strictly limited to fantasy football despite its primary focus. This conversational flexibility keeps the episode feeling fresh rather than formulaic, allowing listeners to discover which tangent might yield the most entertaining content.

Key Takeaways

The Value of Patience in FPL Strategy: Nikico's approach of waiting until Saturday night after Arsenal's result before finalizing his chip strategy highlights an important principle in FPL management. By not committing to a plan before having complete information, managers can adapt to changing circumstances rather than forcing a strategy that no longer makes sense. This applies beyond just chip decisions—waiting for injury updates, checking team news, and monitoring form trends before making transfers all represent similar opportunities to gain advantages over managers who commit too early.

The Connection Between Real Football and FPL: The hosts repeatedly demonstrate how supporting a club creates genuine emotional conflicts when that club's players underperform in FPL. James's explanation of feeling "worse but better" after watching Tottenham content captures this paradox perfectly. Understanding this emotional dimension helps managers make more rational decisions, recognizing when personal attachments might be clouding transfer judgment.

Club Loyalty Is Complicated: Nikico's casual switching between clubs (Fulham, Tottenham, West Ham) is played for laughs but reflects a broader truth about modern football fandom. Many supporters feel connections to multiple clubs for various reasons—geographic proximity, family connections, or simply aesthetic appreciation of certain teams. The podcast normalizes this complexity rather than forcing listeners into rigid categories of "true" versus "fairweather" supporters.

Authenticity Over polish: The episode succeeds because both hosts operate in genuinely conversational mode rather than performing for an audience. When James admits his FPL season is over or when Nikico confesses to not having visited Craven Cottage, these admissions carry weight because they're clearly authentic rather than manufactured for content. This authenticity is what keeps listeners returning to the show over more polished alternatives.

Who This Is For

This episode primarily serves dedicated FPL managers who enjoy the community aspect of fantasy football—the sense of belonging to a group of people who understand the specific pleasures and frustrations of the game. The audience likely already follows Planet FPL content and appreciates the hosts' particular blend of analysis and humor.

The value here isn't primarily strategic education—though there are useful insights about chip timing and player selection—but rather the community experience of hearing two knowledgeable voices discuss the game in a way that feels like overhearing friends rather than consuming produced media. Anyone who has felt isolated in their FPL obsession will find community in this episode.

The show particularly resonates with those who support clubs with complex histories (like Tottenham) and understand the emotional weight that brings, as well as those who appreciate football analysis that doesn't take itself too seriously while still remaining genuinely informative when it counts.
