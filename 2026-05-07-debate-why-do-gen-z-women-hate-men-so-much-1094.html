    Introduction

    The conversation begins with an immediate deep dive into a New Statesman article that documented a troubling phenomenon: young women expressing bleak outlooks on life, spending increasing time online, and demonstrating stronger dislike of men than men demonstrate toward women. But rather than treating this as a cultural curiosity, the speakers systematically unpack it through an evolutionary psychology lens, arguing that these patterns are not simply products of modern technology or social media but rooted in fundamental reproductive biology and social dynamics that have characterized human females throughout our species' history.

    The discussion weaves between two perspectives—evolutionary biology and modern relationship economics—to create a framework for understanding why young women seem to be opting out of dating, becoming more politically homogeneous, and signaling in-group loyalty through increasingly explicit hostility toward men. The analysis covers everything from why women perceive themselves as less happy and healthy than men, to why political alignment has become the primary moral signifier in a post-religious culture, to how the modern mating market allows deceptive men to pursue short-term strategies at unprecedented rates without facing traditional consequences.

    The Evolutionary Framework for Women's Bleak Outlook

    The speakers open by establishing that women's seemingly pessimistic disposition isn't a modern pathology but a predictable evolutionary adaptation. Throughout human history, women faced vulnerability that made certain psychological tendencies advantageous. Women were targets of sexual abuse, smaller on average, and required substantial calories for reproduction and childcare. Data suggests that women's foraging alone wasn't sufficient to sustain themselves, let alone children—making male provisioning not just beneficial but often necessary for survival.

    This evolutionary context explains why women evolved to signal vulnerability through "looking pitiable," communicating sadness, and displaying need. These weren't weakness markers but adaptive signals that elicited help from potential mates and social allies. The pattern persists today: women globally perceive themselves as less happy, less healthy, and less safe than men do, both mentally and physically. This isn't imagined victimhood but a genuine pattern that emerges consistently across cultures.

    More striking, women's negative states spread through social networks in a contagion pattern that men's do not. When women are sad, their partners and friends become sadder as well. Depression spreads through female social networks with a transmissibility that male emotional states lack. This social contagion effect means that individual women's grievances can amplify across entire friend groups, creating collective pessimism that individual rationality wouldn't produce.

    The conversation then connects this vulnerability-signaling to political orientation. If women evolved to signal vulnerability and evoke care, it follows that they would design social worlds that transfer resources to the vulnerable—which they identify with. This creates both a material interest in progressive politics (which advocates for the vulnerable) and a signaling interest (demonstrating kindness to others is high status among women). The progressive political stance thus serves dual functions: it's genuinely in women's interest to advocate for resource redistribution to vulnerable populations, and it's also an honest signal of kindness that other women value and trust.

    In-Group Loyalty and Man-Hating as Credibility Signals

    The discussion then explores why explicit man-hating has become a marker of female in-group loyalty. The speakers reference Hannah Bradshaw's research showing that women who are "guy's girls"—those who prefer male friendships—are trusted less by other women and perceived as more provocative. This creates a pressure to signal the opposite: being a "girl's girl," having primarily female friends, and demonstrating loyalty to women.

    One way to signal this loyalty is through hating men. In patrilocal environments throughout history, women were often separated from their families and kin, relying on alliances with other women in their new communities. Signaling trustworthiness to these women was essential for survival and wellbeing. In the modern context, explicitly rejecting men as a group communicates to other women: "I am on your team, I can be trusted, I share your interests and vulnerabilities."

    This dynamic creates a competitive signaling environment where women compete to demonstrate their prosociality and kindness to other women. A woman's romantic partner becomes an extension of her own moral identity—if she dates someone who shares her political values, it signals she is truly committed to those causes. Conversely, being willing to forgo a romantic partner entirely because no man meets her moral standards becomes an honest signal of principle. The speakers note that women generally prefer partners who are kind to them specifically, but make an exception when massive status is associated with a partner who is kind to others—which describes the progressive political sphere.

    This creates what might be called a competition to be the most prosocial and kind, with progressive politics serving as the primary arena for demonstrating these qualities. Other women strongly dislike signs of cruelty, competitiveness, or unkindness, making the political sphere a way to signal virtue and earn trust within the female in-group.

    The Modern Mating Market and the Death of Traditional Male Value

    The speakers then pivot to analyzing why modern women are increasingly choosing singlehood over risky partnerships. They argue that women's status-seeking goals have become very important to them—they're crushing it in the socioeconomic arena, achieving educational and professional success at historic rates. Getting with a long-term male partner, however, represents a significant hindrance to career advancement. Partners don't want their women around other high-status rivals at work. They often prefer traditional gender roles where women stay home and provide caregiving.

    This creates a fundamental tension: "girl boss culture" and traditional relationship formation are at odds. One of the speakers quotes their mother—referred to as "Mami Kastela"—who famously observed that "women can have it all, but just not at the same time." This reflects the reality that pursuing a career takes time and energy that relationship formation and maintenance also require, and these investments often compete rather than synergize.

    The speakers then analyze the changed calculus of mate selection from an error management perspective. Throughout most of human history, women benefited from selecting men who could provision resources and provide protection. These were scarce resources that made the costs of selecting a bad mate worthwhile. But for modern women earning their own money, achieving their own status, and living in relatively safe environments, these benefits are no longer salient. Women are looking for different benefits now—shared political ideals, emotional intelligence, humor, and similar values.

    The costs of selecting a bad mate, however, remain exactly the same as they were throughout ancestral history. An unwanted pregnancy, an abusive partner, a man who won't contribute to childcare—these costs haven't diminished even as the benefits of male provision have evaporated. This means the trade-off is fundamentally unfavorable for modern women: the juice isn't worth the squeeze.

    The speakers note that women are bringing more to the table than ever before—more resources, more status, more physical attractiveness (women today look better than ever, by various metrics). Yet men are getting more and more from women while women receive less and less. The traditional benefits women provided—physical attractiveness, reproductive capacity—remain valued by men, but now women are also contributing resources and status that weren't expected historically. Meanwhile, men's contribution has become less clearly valuable in the modern economy.

    The speakers frame this as men being "a little bit lost" about how to provide value in this new context. The traditional male benefits have become obsolete, but the pathway to demonstrating new forms of value isn't yet clear to many men. This creates a mismatch where women have raised their standards and expectations while men haven't correspondingly raised their contributions in the dimensions women now care about.

    The Modern Mating Market and Deceptive Strategies

    The discussion then examines how the structure of modern dating has enabled men to pursue deceptive short-term strategies at unprecedented rates. The traditional mating market was constrained by social reputation—men who deceived women faced consequences from their kin groups, communities, and the families of women they wronged. A man who fathered children and abandoned them, or who pursued women under false pretenses, would face social sanction that made deceptive strategies costly.

    The modern dating market has eliminated most of these constraints. Anonymity is essentially complete. Men can access millions of potential mates through dating apps. They can pursue purely short-term deceptive strategies without weathering the traditional costs. Her kin and friends are no longer positioned to take revenge—they live miles away, don't know who you are, and won't encounter you again. You can simply move cities and start over.

    This creates a situation where the pathway to a long-term committed relationship requires navigating an extraordinary gauntlet. Women must deal with "fuck boys," ambiguous situations, rejection, and men pursuing deceptive short-term strategies. The dating market allows men to extract benefits (short-term sex, attention, validation) without any commitment, and the traditional social mechanisms that would have punished this behavior no longer function.

    Women recognize this pathway for what it is—a minefield of potential costs with uncertain benefits. Given that they can support themselves financially and socioeconomically without a partner, the rational calculation increasingly favors singlehood. Why risk the pregnancy, the emotional toll, the career disruption, the potential abuse, when you can simply opt out and maintain your independence?

    The speakers note that this is where the rubber will eventually meet the road for many women. Unless they pursue IVF or sperm donation, they will eventually need a partner if they want children. But the current state of the dating market makes this an increasingly distant and unattractive prospect for many young women.

    Political Alignment as Moral Signifier in a Post-Religious Culture

    The conversation then turns to why young women have become so politically homogeneous and why political disagreement has become such a dealbreaker in dating. The speakers note specific data: one in four young women say a partner having different political views would be a red flag. Six in 10 say they'd find it difficult to date someone who disagreed on the Israel-Palestine conflict or didn't share their views on Donald Trump. Seventy-four percent say they'd find it difficult to be in a relationship with someone who didn't share their views about social justice.

    The speakers argue this reflects a deeper cultural phenomenon: we've lost the ability to make objective moral judgments. Without religion providing a shared framework for right and wrong, morality has become relative—we treat men's behavior as their "personal preference" rather than as objectively good or bad. But this creates a problem: how do you evaluate a potential partner's moral character when you can't appeal to absolute standards?

    Politics has become the substitute. If you can't say "this action is wrong and this action is right," you can at least say "this person shares my political beliefs." Posting about Palestine, having strong opinions on immigration, advocating for social justice—these become proxies for moral character. They're easy to verify, easy to display on social media, and provide a seemingly objective framework for moral evaluation.

    The speakers note that this is essentially "finger-waving on social media"—identifying what can be easily advertised as moral commitment. In a culture that has rejected absolute morality, political alignment provides a convenient substitute for evaluating character.

    This creates a particularly interesting dynamic where young women will break up with someone over disagreement on the Israel-Palestine conflict (a situation not directly affecting their lives) while treating obviously antisocial behavior by men as merely personal preference. The morality is inverted: abstract political positions matter more than concrete behavioral patterns.

    The "Woke Fishing" Phenomenon and Activist Spaces

    The discussion then explores how men have responded to women's political preferences—the phenomenon of "woke fishing." The speakers note that women in activist spaces were frustrated by patterns they saw in men: a desire to give speeches, run for positions of leadership, pursue status through the activism itself. The speakers observe that this is simply "a different type of status game"—the same frustrations with men that exist everywhere else also exist in these progressive spaces.

    Men have learned to perform progressive politics as a mating strategy, adopting the language and positions that signal virtue to women, regardless of their actual commitment to those causes. This is "woke fishing"—pursuing women by appearing more woke than one actually is.

    But the women in these activist spaces were frustrated by something else: the emotional dynamics of activism. The speakers contrast how men and women process difficult information differently. Men's typical emotional response to seeing suffering—particularly Palestinian suffering—is to want a practical solution, to mobilize logistics, to solve the problem. Women, by contrast, want to sit in the emotion, to process collectively, to cry together.

    The speakers reference Joe Hudson's observation about his young daughter: when she was sad, she'd cry because that brought her sister to comfort her. When she was angry, her sister ran away. This illustrates how women's emotional expression tends toward prosocial channels (crying brings comfort, bonding, connection) while men's tends toward antisocial channels (anger, frustration, aggression push others away).

    In activist spaces, this creates friction. Women want an "outpouring of it all in the emotion" while men want to organize protests, plan logistics, find solutions. The women were frustrated that men weren't engaging with the emotional reality they wanted to experience.

    Entropy Toward Empathy: The Competition for Moral Suffering

    The speakers then introduce a concept they call "entropy toward empathy"—a dynamic where online spaces create competitive pressure to demonstrate ever-greater emotional response to suffering. They compare this to a phenomenon they've observed in conspiracy theory circles, where men compete to have the most extreme, most novel, most unusual theory. Each person tries to outdo the previous one, pushing toward increasingly bizarre conclusions.

    The empathy version operates similarly. Young women compete to demonstrate who is most emotionally affected by distant conflicts. The competition involves escalating claims: "I was so distraught I couldn't leave the house," responses that surpass previous ones, and claims to being more deeply affected than others. This creates an "entropy toward empathy" where the emotional response pushes toward increasingly extreme expressions.

    Intersectionality functions as an additional layer in this competition. If caring about one issue is good, caring about multiple intersecting issues is better. If being a feminist is good, being a feminist who also centers trans women's issues is better. The logic goes: "You think this issue is important? I've looked at it from multiple angles and I know more perspectives than you do." This creates a neurotic competition to demonstrate more comprehensive moral knowledge, more layers of intersecting oppression, more intense emotional response.

    The speakers note that this dynamic explains why progressive politics spreads so effectively online. Practical solutions and anger don't generate the same engagement as collective emotional processing. The social media algorithm rewards content that generates strong emotional responses and extended engagement—crying together, processing together, competing to demonstrate empathy—all generate these outcomes. The emotional response spreads faster than the practical solution ever could.

    The Gender Egalitarian Paradox

    The speakers then introduce what they call the "gender egalitarian paradox"—a counterintuitive finding that as societies treat men and women more equally, the sexes actually diverge more rather than becoming more similar. Men become more risk-taking, more organized, taller (the height difference between sexes actually increases in more egalitarian societies, despite claims that it's purely social). Women become more anxious, more depressed, more focused on status-seeking.

    This creates an interesting framework for understanding the current moment. As women have achieved more educational and professional success, they have also become more different from men psychologically. The equality of opportunity hasn't produced convergence in psychology or behavior—it has produced divergence. The speakers wonder if gender egalitarianism might simply function as a proxy for social competitiveness—more competitive societies create more divergent sexes as each sex specializes in different competitive niches.

    This framework suggests that the current gender dynamics—the divergence in political views, the increasing polarization in dating markets, the different emotional styles—may not be bugs in an otherwise well-functioning system but features of highly competitive societies. As women compete in professional spheres, they may be evolving different psychological profiles that make cross-sex communication increasingly difficult.

    Implications and Conclusions

    The speakers conclude by synthesizing these frameworks into a coherent picture of why young women seem to hate men more than men hate women. The evolutionary legacy created tendencies toward vulnerability signaling, negative affect, and in-group loyalty. The modern economy eliminated the material benefits that once made relationships worthwhile while leaving the costs intact. The dating market enabled deceptive strategies that have made the pathway to commitment increasingly dangerous and unappealing. The loss of absolute morality pushed women toward political signaling as the only available framework for evaluating moral character. Online spaces created competitive pressure to demonstrate ever-greater empathy and emotional investment. And progressive politics functions simultaneously as material interest, in-group loyalty signal, and moral credential.

    This creates a situation where young women rationally choose singlehood, signal their virtue through political commitments, compete to demonstrate empathy, and view men increasingly negatively—not because they've been bamboozled by social media or radicalized by feminism, but because the evolutionary and economic logic actually supports these choices.
