# How to Earn Connections to High Net-Worth Decision Makers

## Key Insights

- **Seven-sibling background**: The speaker grew up in a bottom middle-class household with seven children, and among thirteen total extended family members, zero were entrepreneurs—his entire family consisted of engineers with the highest aspiration being a six-figure job with health insurance.
- **Earned access at Stanford**: Two people close to Stanford admissions sent texts and letters of recommendation for him, not because of family connections (his father couldn't make such calls), but because he had earned their trust through delivering exceptional work for them.
- **30% fund deployment**: At Summit Partners, he sourced and deployed approximately 30% of a $5 billion fund—meaning he was responsible for investments that made the firm substantial money.
- **Exposure breakthrough at 21**: He was first exposed to entrepreneurship in a senior year "Getting Rich" class where a t-shirt company founder and a hedge fund creator spoke—the first entrepreneurs he'd ever encountered—and this single exposure "changed the trajectory of" his life.
- **Access-as-obligation philosophy**: Once someone gains privilege and access, they view it as their obligation to give it back to others who do good work.

## Introduction

The gap between where most people start and where they ultimately end up isn't primarily about talent, luck, or even money—it's about exposure and access. This is the central thesis explored in a revealing conversation between two successful entrepreneurs who dissect how meaningful connections with high net-worth decision makers are actually earned, not inherited.

The speaker, a venture capitalist and founder who has built significant wealth and influence, traces his path from a working-class childhood in a family of seven children to positions at elite institutions like Stanford and Summit Partners, where he managed hundreds of millions in investments. His story illuminates a philosophy he calls "access is everything"—the understanding that proximity to powerful people and opportunities is the true multiplier in business success, and that this access must be earned through exceptional work rather than assumed as an entitlement.

What makes this conversation particularly valuable is the speaker's willingness to examine his own journey with candid self-awareness, including an honest reflection on when he first gained admission to Stanford and felt conflicted about whether he'd "earned it." His conclusion—that he absolutely had earned it, precisely because mentors vouched for him based on his track record of delivering results for them—provides a framework anyone can replicate regardless of their starting point in life.

## The Access Is Everything Philosophy

The foundation of the speaker's approach centers on a belief that access to influential people, elite institutions, and high-stakes opportunities is the primary currency of professional success. He articulates this philosophy not as a cynical observation about networking, but as a genuine recognition of how privilege functions in business and how those who receive it can multiply its impact.

According to the speaker, access works as a cycle. When you don't have it, you must surround yourself with people who do and perform excellent work for them. Over time, this approach yields access incrementally. Then, once you possess privilege and access yourself, it becomes your obligation to pass it forward to others who demonstrate the same work ethic and quality of output.

This cyclical nature of access is central to understanding why the speaker approaches mentorship and talent development with such intentionality. He views his current position not as an endpoint but as a platform from which to extend opportunities to others, provided those others have proven themselves through deliverables. The philosophy isn't simply "give everyone a chance"—it's "give access to those who earn it by doing good work."

## The Personal Journey: From Outsider to Insider

The speaker's path illustrates how access is actually accumulated through a series of earned opportunities rather than a single breakthrough moment. He was recruited for positions at the "big three" consulting firms—McKinsey, BCG, and Bain—through a contact who connected him with the right people. He didn't get those positions, but this early networking effort planted seeds that would later bloom.

Through another mentor, he landed at Lazard, one of New York City's premier investment banks, working on mergers and acquisitions. He performed exceptionally well at Lazard, and that mentor remains someone he's close with today—he recently visited him personally. This relationship demonstrates how access compounds: the mentor gave him a chance, Chad delivered excellent work, and the mentor became an advocate who would later open additional doors.

That next door was Summit Partners, where the speaker invested approximately 30% of a $5 billion fund. He sourced the deals that made Summit significant money, and his track record there positioned him for admission to Stanford's graduate program. The speaker describes this as a pivotal moment where he grappled with whether he should feel guilty about getting in—he knew how the system worked, he understood that two people close to Stanford admissions had sent texts and written recommendation letters on his behalf.

His resolution to this moral question reveals the core of his philosophy: he shouldn't feel bad because he earned those recommendations. The people who advocated for him weren't doing him a favor out of charity—they were vouching for him because he had made their lives easier, earned their trust, and consistently delivered excellent work. The speaker draws a crucial distinction: he wasn't born into access (his father couldn't simply call Stanford and secure his admission), but he could earn access by performing at such a high level that people with connections became eager to recommend him.

## The Critical Role of Exposure

The conversation introduces a three-word framework for understanding how access is built and replicated: exposure, access, and earned access. The speaker emphasizes that exposure is the foundational step—you literally cannot imagine what you've never seen. Without exposure to possibilities, your brain won't venture toward them.

This point emerges from his own experience growing up in what he describes as a typical Indian family environment. His mother had nine siblings and his father had four, but across all thirteen relatives, there were zero entrepreneurs. Every single person was an engineer, and the highest calling in the family was securing a good job with health insurance—understood as a six-figure income with benefits. This was the totality of what he knew was possible, and it was all he was exposed to.

The breaking point came during his senior year of college when he took a class called "Getting Rich." In the very first session, the professor brought in a guest speaker—a woman who had started a t-shirt company. The next class featured another guest: a man who had started his own hedge fund. The speaker describes his reaction simply: he looked at them and thought, "Why are these guys having all the fun? This sounds awesome. Maybe I could do that instead."

He acknowledges without that single exposure, it "literally would have just not happened." This moment at age 21 fundamentally changed the trajectory of his life because it revealed a possibility he'd never encountered. He didn't suddenly become an entrepreneur—he was simply exposed to the concept and realized it was within reach.

## Applying Exposure Principles to Children and Teams

The speaker extends this understanding of exposure beyond his own career to how he raises his children and develops his team at Gruns. He notes that his children have been overexposed to comfort—his family visits the same resorts repeatedly, insulating them from the full range of human experience. He's made a deliberate shift to expose them to "adult stuff" rather than age-appropriate children's activities.

His children now go to meetings with him, work out in the gym daily, help with cooking and laundry. He observes that children actually love this exposure because they're encountering new stimuli they've never seen before. Even at ages as young as five, they're developing neural pathways that will expand their understanding of what's possible. He describes it as "breaking some fence they had around what the world is and it starts to poke holes and it lets new things in."

This approach reflects his understanding that exposure isn't just about career paths—it's about expanding the mental model of what's achievable in every domain of life. The speaker reflects on the ongoing tension between focusing his impact narrowly on the people who spend thousands of hours working alongside him at Gruns versus expanding his reach publicly through content and communication. He acknowledges that relaying these experiences in a public forum where millions can learn is challenging, but recognizes the importance of expanding exposure for others the way it was expanded for him.

## How Access Is Earned in Practice

The host speculates about what "doing good work" actually means in the context of earning access, and his guesses reveal the mechanisms at play. He suggests the speaker likely over-delivered relative to compensation, didn't get paid proportionally to what he contributed, and didn't complain about this imbalance. These behaviors, the host suggests, are the substance behind the seemingly casual phrase "I did good work for them."

The speaker confirms this framework implicitly, allowing the host's analysis to stand without correction. This suggests the earned access model works through a specific mechanism: perform at a level beyond what's required and compensated, build trust through reliability and quality, and create advocates who become eager to open doors for you.

The implications for anyone seeking access are significant. This isn't about schmoozing or networking in the superficial sense—it's about delivering such exceptional results that powerful people become personally invested in your success. The speaker notes that those with access are "so eager to help" those who have proven themselves through work. This eagerness isn't charity—it's recognition of value and a desire to support continued excellence.

## Legacy and the Obligation of Access

At 33 years old, the speaker has begun contemplating his legacy and what he wants to leave in the world. His answer centers on access: he wants to provide the access he's either been granted or earned over his career to more people, specifically those who spend their lives working with him and who put in good work.

This represents a fulfillment of the cyclical philosophy he outlined earlier. Access earned through others' generosity must be repaid by providing access to the next generation of performers. The speaker doesn't view his position as deserved in a way that justifies hoarding opportunities—he sees it as a responsibility to multiply access for those who demonstrate the same work ethic that brought him success.

The host connects this to a concept introduced by Scott Bellski, the former chief product officer of Adobe and accomplished entrepreneur, who told him he would "invest in you and you need to be a steward of my capital." The host interprets "steward" as someone who "protects and passes on like a little bit of greatness." The speaker embodies this stewardship—taking the access and capital entrusted to him and converting it into opportunities for others.

## Detailed Takeaways

**Access is a cycle, not a destination.** The speaker's philosophy centers on recognizing that access flows in one direction until you have it, then flows in the opposite direction once you've earned it. Early in your career, you must seek out people with access and provide value to them. Once you've accumulated access through demonstrated performance, you have an obligation to extend it to others. Understanding this cycle allows you to position yourself correctly at each stage—don't pretend you have access you haven't earned, but do recognize that earned access creates a responsibility to pass it forward.

**Exposure precedes possibility.** The speaker's transformation from an engineer-bound family member to a venture capitalist occurred because a college class exposed him to people running actual businesses. Without that exposure, he literally couldn't imagine entrepreneurship as an option. This has profound implications for how you approach career development: seek experiences that expand your awareness of what's possible, not just your technical skills. Take classes, attend talks, read biographies—actively curate exposure to paths you didn't know existed.

**Earned access is more legitimate than inherited access.** The speaker felt conflicted about getting into Stanford when he knew recommendation letters had been sent by influential people. His resolution was recognizing that he had earned those recommendations through performance—his work at Lazard and Summit had made his mentors' lives easier and earned their trust. This framework is crucial: the ethical concern isn't about getting help, it's about whether you've delivered value that justifies the help you receive. If you're performing at an exceptional level, advocacy from powerful people is appropriate recognition, not unfair advantage.

**Over-deliver and don't track time.** When the host speculates about what "doing good work" means in practice, his analysis centers on behaviors that go beyond compensation and formal responsibilities. The implication is clear: access is earned by consistently delivering more than you're paid for, not by tracking hours or negotiating every dollar. Those who complain about being undercompensated relative to output signal that they won't be reliable advocates for their mentors' interests—and those mentors notice.

**Children learn what's possible by watching.** The speaker's deliberate decision to expose his young children to "adult stuff"—meetings, workouts, cooking, laundry—reflects an understanding that children model their aspirations on what they observe. Parents who insulate children from professional realities limit their exposure to possible futures. The observation that children "actually kind of enjoy" this exposure because they're encountering new stimuli suggests that expanding their world doesn't overwhelm them—it awakens them.

This conversation is particularly valuable for early-career professionals who feel locked out of opportunities by lack of connections or pedigree, mid-career individuals who have accumulated some success but want to understand how to leverage it into greater influence, and founders or executives who want to build cultures that develop talent through access rather than simply credential-checking.

The specific value varies by audience. Those without existing access will learn that access is actually earnable through performance—the speaker's path from bottom middle-class childhood to Summit Partners was built on demonstrating excellence, not on family connections. Those with access but limited impact will learn how to multiply their influence by extending access to performers who will deliver results. Parents will gain insight into how to expose children to possibilities beyond their immediate environment.

The underlying message is consistent across all audiences: access is the multiplier in professional success, it must be earned through performance rather than assumed as an entitlement, and those who earn it have an obligation to extend it to others. This framework replaces the passive hope of "knowing the right people" with the active strategy of becoming the kind of person powerful people want to advocate for.
