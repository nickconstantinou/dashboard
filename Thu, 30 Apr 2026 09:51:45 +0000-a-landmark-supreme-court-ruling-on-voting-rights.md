## Introduction

The Supreme Court has once again positioned itself at the center of America's ongoing struggle over voting rights, delivering a ruling that legal experts are describing as potentially the final blow to the landmark Voting Rights Act of 1965. On Wednesday, the Court struck down Louisiana's congressional map as unconstitutional, a decision that could fundamentally reshape how American elections are conducted and who gets elected to represent minority communities.

This episode of The Daily, hosted by Sabrina Magiste, features two of The New York Times' most distinguished reporters on voting and legal affairs: Adam Liptak, the paper's chief legal affairs correspondent and host of The Docket newsletter, and Nick Corasaniti, who covers national politics with a particular focus on voting and elections. Together, they untangle the complex legal reasoning behind the ruling and explore its immediate and long-term implications for American democracy.

The ruling arrives at a moment when voting rights have already been under siege across the country. Since the 2013 *Shelby County v. Holder* decision dismantled the VRA's enforcement mechanism, dozens of states have enacted new voting restrictions, changed electoral districts, and altered voter registration procedures with minimal federal oversight. This new ruling appears to go even further, potentially eliminating the legal framework courts have used to protect minority voting power even in jurisdictions no longer subject to preclearance requirements.

## Key Concepts & Arguments

### The Legal Logic of the Ruling

Adam Liptak begins by explaining the constitutional framework at the heart of the case. At issue is a fundamental tension in American election law: how courts should balance the imperative to prevent racial discrimination in voting with the principle that the Constitution is "colorblind" in its treatment of voters. The Supreme Court's conservative majority has increasingly embraced the view that any consideration of race in drawing electoral districts constitutes a form of unconstitutional discrimination — even when that consideration is undertaken to protect minority representation.

The Louisiana case centered on the state's congressional map, which included a second majority-Black district. Civil rights groups and minority voters argued this was necessary to comply with the Voting Rights Act, which requires jurisdictions to create districts where minority voters have a realistic opportunity to elect candidates of their choice. The state, backed by conservative legal groups, countered that the map was unconstitutional because it was drawn with excessive consideration of race.

Liptak walks through the Court's reasoning, which appears to draw heavily on Justice Thomas's concurring opinion in *Shelby County*, where he declared the Voting Rights Act "racist on its face" — an extraordinary characterization of legislation that was originally passed with overwhelming bipartisan support and extended multiple times by Congress, including as recently as 2006 when it was reauthorized for another 25 years. The Court's new ruling apparently extends this logic, suggesting that not only the VRA's coverage formula but potentially the Act's protections for minority voting power are constitutionally suspect.

### The Death of the Voting Rights Act

The 1965 Voting Rights Act stands as one of the most successful civil rights laws in American history. Passed during the height of the civil rights movement and signed by President Lyndon B. Johnson, it prohibited racial discrimination in voting and employed two key mechanisms: Section 4 established a coverage formula identifying jurisdictions with histories of discrimination, while Section 5 required those jurisdictions to obtain preclearance from the federal government before implementing any changes to voting laws or district maps.

*Shelby County v. Holder* invalidated the coverage formula, effectively rendering Section 5 inoperable. Without the formula specifying which jurisdictions required preclearance, the federal government had no way to know which states and localities needed approval before changing their voting laws. The decision was a 5-4 ruling along ideological lines, with Chief Justice Roberts writing for the majority that the coverage formula was no longer justified given improvements in minority voter participation since 1965.

This new Louisiana ruling goes further by potentially eliminating judicial remedies available under Section 2 of the VRA — the provision that allows private citizens and groups to sue states for voting practices that discriminate against minorities. If the Court's reasoning holds, plaintiffs seeking to protect minority voting power through race-conscious districting may find themselves without legal recourse even when they can prove intentional discrimination.

### Real-World Consequences for American Democracy

Nick Corasaniti shifts the conversation from legal abstraction to political reality, explaining how this ruling will reshape competitive congressional races across the country. Louisiana's second majority-Black district was not drawn at random — it represented the collective judgment of redistricting experts that such a district was necessary to give Black voters a realistic opportunity to elect their preferred candidates. Without it, Black representation in Congress from Louisiana would likely diminish.

The implications extend far beyond Louisiana. States with significant minority populations that have been redistricted following the 2020 Census may now face legal challenges to their maps. Jurisdictions that assumed they were operating within constitutional bounds when drawing majority-minority districts may find those districts invalidated. The ripple effects could alter the composition of Congress and state legislatures in ways that disadvantage minority voters.

Corasaniti discusses the broader context of voting rights retrenchment in America. Since *Shelby County*, states have enacted a cascade of voting restrictions — cuts to early voting, photo ID requirements, purges of voter rolls, and reductions in polling places — with minimal federal oversight. This ruling removes yet another layer of protection, leaving minority voters more exposed to discrimination that is difficult to prove under the Court's increasingly demanding standards for showing discriminatory intent.

## Detailed Takeaways

**The Supreme Court has adopted an increasingly colorblind theory of constitutional law that treats race-conscious remedies for discrimination as inherently suspect.** This represents a fundamental shift in how American courts approach civil rights law. Rather than asking whether voting changes have discriminatory effects or were undertaken with discriminatory intent, courts must now apparently presume that any explicit consideration of race is constitutionally problematic. This framework makes it nearly impossible to protect minority voting power through districting, since proving discrimination requires acknowledging race while avoiding explicit racial classification requires ignoring it.

**The Voting Rights Act may be effectively dead as an enforcement mechanism, with only limited judicial remedies remaining.** The combination of *Shelby County* and this new ruling leaves Section 2 litigation as the last avenue for challenging discriminatory voting practices. But if the Court's reasoning extends to Section 2 jurisprudence, even that avenue may be foreclosed. Justice Thomas has long argued that the entire Voting Rights Act rests on an unconstitutional premise — the notion that jurisdictions with histories of discrimination require special federal supervision. This ruling brings that view closer to becoming the law of the land.

**This decision arrives amid a broader national reckoning over voting access and democratic participation.** The ruling is not occurring in a vacuum but rather reflects and intensifies existing tensions over who gets to vote and whose votes matter. Since 2010, Republicans in numerous states have pursued policies that disproportionately affect minority, low-income, and young voters — groups that historically vote Democratic. The elimination of VRA protections removes one of the few remaining legal tools for challenging these policies when they have discriminatory effects.

**The ruling's impact will be felt unevenly across the country, with jurisdictions that have historically discriminated most severely losing protections first.** Louisiana, Mississippi, Alabama, Georgia, and other states covered by the original VRA are now operating in a legal environment where their past discrimination provides no justification for special oversight. Courts applying this reasoning may find that historical discrimination actually strengthens the case against race-conscious remedies, creating a perverse incentive structure where states with the worst records face the fewest constraints.

**This episode represents a turning point in the debate over race and representation in American politics.** The Court's conservative majority has embraced the view that political power should be allocated without regard to race, that voters should be treated as individuals rather than members of racial groups, and that the Constitution prohibits any consideration of race even when undertaken to remedy past discrimination. Critics argue this view ignores the persistent reality of racial polarization in American politics, where voters of different races consistently support different candidates. The ruling leaves minority voters to compete in an electoral marketplace increasingly stacked against their interests.
