Planet FPL Weekender Episode 31: Manchester United's Defensive Crisis Deepens as Leeds Expose Their Vulnerabilities

The world of Fantasy Premier League is entering a crucial phase of the season, and Episode 31 of Planet FPL's The Weekender delivers exactly what FPL managers need: detailed analysis of the matches that matter most to their squads. Hosts James and David Hunter—known for their comprehensive coverage on the COOTC (Channel of the Channel) and their Patreon community—delve into one of the most dramatic Premier League encounters of the season so far: Manchester United's dramatic 2-2 draw with Leeds United on Monday night. With gameweek 33 approaching and many managers weighing up free hit strategies, this episode provides essential tactical insights and player analysis that could make or break your season.

The episode tackles several interconnected storylines: Leeds' dominant first-half performance, a controversial red card that shifted the momentum, Manchester United's unlikely comeback, and the implications for both teams' defensive structures heading into upcoming fixtures. David opens by acknowledging the emotional toll of watching Tottenham lose, before pivoting to the "thralling" spectacle at Old Trafford—though he admits he only caught the highlights rather than watching the full match live.

Leeds United's First-Half Dominance: What Went Right

The episode opens with James delivering a blunt assessment of the first half: "They battered them. That's the genuine truth." Leeds United arrived at Old Trafford with a bold mentality that caught many observers off guard. Despite sitting in a precarious league position, James emphasizes that Leeds played with "full intent to win the football match" rather than settling for a defensive approach. Their reward was a commanding first-half performance that yielded three big chances and an expected goals (XG) figure over 2.0 from ten shots.

The opening goal came from OAR—described as "Oafur" by James—after a cross from the right found Calvert-Lewin challenging Yoro in the air. James notes that "some might argue it's a foul," acknowledging the contentious nature of the goal. However, he considers it should stand, and OAR "wrapped it in very cleanly" to give Leeds the lead. The second goal arrived shortly after, with Gary Neville on commentary delivering what James describes as an ominous warning: Leeds needed to seize the moment because they were so dominant they might regret it. As Neville stopped mid-sentence, OAR capitalized on a loose ball to make it 2-0.

What struck James most was the pace at which Leeds played the game. Against a Manchester United side that had been "on a great run under Michael Carrick," Leeds refused to adopt a conservative approach. James observes that many would have expected Leeds to target a point from this fixture, yet they arrived with ambitions of all three. The speed and intensity of their play created wave after wave of attacks, leaving Manchester United's defense scrambling.

James Justin emerges as a particular talking point from the Leeds performance. James describes him as "the Leeds defender I want this week," noting his insertion into the back three after Rodon dropped out. The England international put in "one brilliant cross for Calvert-Lewin after about two minutes" that should have resulted in a goal. James is effusive in his praise: "He was really good on the eyes. He's just sorting old FPL before Defcon." For managers considering Leeds defenders for their squads, James Justin appears to be the standout option.

Calvert-Lewin himself receives strong endorsement despite his modest returns of one goal and eleven blanks. James watched the performance carefully and saw nothing to discourage FPL managers: "He looked fit. He was a handful. He's pretty much played the whole game." The striker's involvement against Wolves in Leeds' double gameweek—where the first fixture against Wolves represents the more favorable option—makes him a candidate worth monitoring.

The Tanaka Factor: Replacing Anton Stack

One of the more nuanced tactical discussions centers on Leeds' midfield situation. With Anton Stack unavailable, Tanaka stepped into the side and delivered an impressive performance. James is quick to point out Tanaka's strong record: "When he plays, he's really good dating back to obviously Championship games etc as well, but really strong percentage record when he plays." The Japanese international effectively covered for Stack's absence, providing the defensive solidity and creative spark that Leeds needed in the center of the pitch.

James notes that Tanaka's presence "is covering up fine for Anton Stack at the moment," suggesting Leeds' midfield remains functional despite the rotation. This is significant for FPL managers tracking Leeds assets because it demonstrates the squad's ability to maintain quality even when key players are unavailable. The consistent production from midfield options like Tanaka means Leeds attacker's value isn't solely dependent on Stack's availability.

The Red Card Controversy: Landry Martinez and the Laws of the Game

The second half took a dramatic turn when Landry Martinez received a red card for pulling Calvert-Lewin's hair—specifically described as his "man bun" by James. Both hosts express discomfort with the decision while acknowledging its correctness under current laws. James offers his "strong view": "I think it's soft as anything, but it is a red card according to the current laws." David agrees, calling it "a bit naff, to be honest," while conceding the officials "probably did their job."

The discussion reveals the tension between the letter of the law and the spirit of football. James draws a distinction: "This was no Romero on Cucarella hair pull"—suggesting this incident was considerably less severe than some previous high-profile hair-pulling incidents that sparked debate. The key issue is that under current VAR protocols, contact to an opponent's head—even in a hair-pull scenario—technically qualifies as violent conduct.

The consequences of the red card extended beyond the immediate sending off. Leeds, who had been dominant, suddenly found themselves with eleven men against ten. James observes that Leeds "didn't seem to have the initiative to keep driving at Manchester United in the same way." This hesitation stemmed from nerves, he explains: "Remember they haven't won away from home since September in the Premier League. Only one win prior to this at Wolves." The psychological weight of being away from home, combined with the sudden numerical disadvantage, caused Leeds to retreat rather than press their advantage.

Manchester United's Comeback: Casemiro and Fernandez's Connection

With Leeds sitting deeper, Manchester United sensed an opportunity. James draws attention to the special relationship between Casemiro and Fernandez: "I think they're the most combination of goals this year." The Brazilian midfielder scored again, with Fernandez providing the delivery, and James notes that Casemiro was "one off Defcon" at the time of the goal.

The hosts analyze the dynamic in detail. James emphasizes: "He's outstanding in the way he attacks the ball. When you see it's he's landing towards Casemiro at the back post, you think goal." David adds that Fernandez "actively looks for him on those sort of situations." For FPL managers, this connection represents a reliable route to returns. Casemiro's goal-scoring threat from midfield—especially on set pieces and crossing situations—makes him a valuable asset, particularly when facing teams with defensive vulnerabilities.

Fernandez himself attracts attention despite receiving a yellow card that cost him bonus points in the BPS (Bonus Point System). James acknowledges: "Obviously got booked for having a little bit of a winge. One off of bonus in the BPS. Um mainly because of that yellow card, otherwise he would have been in it." Nevertheless, his performance was "outstanding" according to James, particularly in how he dominated Leeds' attempts to build from the back in the final stages.

Fernandez's influence became so complete that in injury time, he essentially played "center back by himself" as Leeds "couldn't get anywhere near him." Gary Neville captured this perfectly on commentary: "Come on, man, mark him. You've got an extra player anyway. Other than Fernandez, you're not too worried about what they've got." This observation underscores how depleted Leeds were in the closing stages and how thoroughly Manchester United's creative midfielder controlled the game.

The Defensive Crisis: Who's Available for the Chelsea Game?

The hosts turn their attention to the implications for Manchester United's upcoming fixture against Chelsea. The news is alarming: both Harry Maguire and Martinez are suspended, leaving the defensive line severely depleted. James explains the unusual circumstances surrounding Maguire's additional ban: "This is because of comments he made after being sent off to the fourth official at Bournemouth back in gameweek 31." Manchester United were reportedly "well aware that he was highly likely to miss the Chelsea game as well," yet confirmation came late enough to cause confusion among managers and fans.

Martinez's suspension stems from the red card incident, which James describes as "a free game banned for pulling Calvin Lewin's man bun." David calls this "mad," highlighting the absurdity of a sending off for what both agree was a relatively minor infraction compared to more violent challenges.

The pairing that will likely feature at center back consists of Yuro and potentially Aiden Heaven. James sounds a note of caution: "Michael Carrick seems reluctant to use Aiden Heaven. Certainly, what that center back pairing is is inexperienced." David agrees, suggesting this defensive vulnerability "might draw a little bit of pressure onto them for the game."

This creates a fascinating FPL scenario. Chelsea, despite their own struggles this season, face a Manchester United side with an untested central defensive partnership. James is direct in his assessment: "I think makes Chelsea attacking assets look better for the double." David adds context about the historical matchup: "They've had some strange ones there. There was the mad 4-3 a couple of years ago where they came back from 2-0 down to go in front and then still lost 4-3 with two injury time goals from Cole Palmer."

The implication is clear: with United's defense ravaged by suspensions and organizational issues, Chelsea forwards and attacking midfielders present appealing options for managers weighing their free hit strategies.

Additional Absentees: Mainoo and the Midfield Balance

James highlights another key absence: "They really missed Kobie Maino. Really missed him." The young midfielder's energy and quality in the center of the pitch appear crucial to Manchester United's balance. James notes that "Ugate played I think first start under Michael Carrick. Him and Casemiro looked like they were really short of legs in there." The combination of Ugate and Casemiro lacks the dynamism that Maino provides, making the side more vulnerable to pressure.

There's also an intriguing subplot about Ugate's future: "Ugate's actually been linked with a move to Tottenham should Tottenham stay up. Am I not short enough of midfield players?" James questions whether Tottenham's interest makes sense given their existing midfield options. This transfer speculation adds another layer of uncertainty to Ugate's FPL value heading into the season's business end.

Strategic Takeaways for FPL Managers

The Leeds double is worth targeting, particularly for free hit managers. James identifies James Justin as "the Leeds defender I want this week," and Calvert-Lewin continues to look like a viable option despite his blanks. The first game against Wolves represents the better fixture, but the double gameweek offers substantial opportunity for managers who can find the right combination of Leeds assets.

Casemiro is a viable budget option for the run-in. With many managers focused on premium midfielders, Casemiro at his price point offers access to goal involvements through the Fernandez connection. His set-piece presence in the box makes him a threat from crosses and corners, and his price allows funds to be allocated elsewhere.

Chelsea attacking assets merit serious consideration for the double. Manchester United's defensive crisis creates an attractive fixture for Chelsea players. The combination of suspension, inexperience in central defense, and historical vulnerability at Stamford Bridge suggests multiple routes to returns for those willing to invest.

Leeds' away record deserves caution despite their Old Trafford performance. James reminds listeners that "they haven't won away from home since September," meaning the dominance against Manchester United may not translate consistently. Managing expectations around Leeds assets away from Elland Road is wise despite the impressive display.

The Manchester United midfield pairing of Casemiro and Fernandez has become the team's creative hub. With Eriksen injured, Maino absent, and the defense in disarray, the Brazilian connection provides the Red Devils' best chance of creating chances. Targeting players who can exploit the space created by their forward runs represents a viable strategy.
