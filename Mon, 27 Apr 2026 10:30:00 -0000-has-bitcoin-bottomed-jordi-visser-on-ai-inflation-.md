<p># Has Bitcoin Bottomed? Jordy Visser on AI, Inflation, and Moats

## Key Insights

- **Bitcoin is under $2 trillion market cap** while the fiat system sits at approximately $750 trillion, creating a massive reweighting opportunity as scarcity assets gain preference in an AI-disrupted world
- **SaaS companies are losing terminal value** as AI improves—when Anthropic releases new Claude versions, SaaS stocks drop 20-40% because their future discounted cash flows become uncertain, similar to how quantum computing uncertainty suppresses Bitcoin prices
- **AI destroys all abundance-based stores of value** — any digital asset created on code can be replicated infinitely, while Bitcoin's physical mining constraints and 21 million cap make it functionally scarce like DRAM, CPUs, silver, and gold
- **The three moats that survived human selection** are gold, religion, and Bitcoin — these are the only assets "decided by people that have not gone and survived the test of time"
- **Quantum is to Bitcoin what AI disruption is to SaaS companies** — both create uncertainty about future value, but AI disruption is far more present and real ("quantum is mythical"), making the AI threat to SaaS a preview of what real disruption looks like

## Introduction

In a wide-ranging conversation on the Bankless podcast, host Ryan Sean Adams sits down with Jordy Visser—hedge fund manager, macro investor, and frequent CNBC contributor—to explore what may be one of the most compelling Bitcoin bull cases of the current cycle. Unlike the many crypto cycle investors who have visited Bankless recently and remained bearish, Visser stands apart: he believes Bitcoin has already bottomed, that the current crypto winter will be the mildest ever, and that the forces converging around artificial intelligence and inflation will drive investors toward what he calls a scarcity portfolio.

Visser's thesis is not a simple prediction—it is framed as an inevitability. He argues that the digital economy has been merging with traditional finance since the Manhattan Project, and that AI now represents the final acceleration of a decades-long trend: the displacement of labor by capital, the erosion of moats, and the destruction of abundance. Within this framework, Bitcoin occupies a unique position as one of the only assets that cannot be infinitely replicated, making it a natural beneficiary as everything else loses its value proposition. The conversation also explores what Visser calls the "SaaS apocalypse," the implications of AI for traditional finance and private credit, and why the hyperscalers may have provided similar returns to Bitcoin without the perceived risk—all while the underlying forces are shifting in Bitcoin's favor.

---

## Bitcoin as an AI Trade: The Core Thesis

The conversation opens with a viral clip of Visser on CNBC, where he put his "neck on the line" to predict Bitcoin would be significantly higher the next time he appeared on the show. Visser explains that the broader context was about the world transitioning to negative real interest rates, with the next CPI print expected around 3.6%—close to the Fed funds rate—suggesting the break-even point for real rates is near. But the Bitcoin-specific comment has become a lightning rod, and Ryan pushes Visser to explain his conviction.

Visser's framework begins with a staggering comparison: Bitcoin's market cap remains under $2 trillion, while the total fiat money system approaches $750 trillion. For anyone bullish on Bitcoin, the thesis rests on some form of reweighting—capital flowing from the fiat system into the scarce digital asset. But the mechanism driving that reweighting is not simply adoption or ETF inflows. It is the structural disruption caused by artificial intelligence.

"The digital economy has been merging with the traditional finance world and the old industrial economy for a long time," Visser explains. "Since the Manhattan Project, we started to have a visual on what technology would look like." The key insight is that AI does not merely automate tasks—it fundamentally disrupts the value proposition of anything that exists as code, software, or digital content. Visser draws a direct line: "The U.S. economy is now $30 trillion. A big portion of the growth right now is semiconductors. It's artificial intelligence. It'll be robotics. It'll be all these different things. But these things don't involve labor and people as much as they used to in the past. And so you've had this massive distribution of wealth problem that's been growing literally since the beginning of the personal computer."

This wealth distribution problem—the growing gap between capital owners and labor—is what Visser argues originally drove people toward Bitcoin and decentralized alternatives. AI, in his view, accelerates this process dramatically by displacing not just manual labor but cognitive labor as well. "AI gets into another realm. It gets into the disruption of the one advantage we have over technology, which is our brain. The other advantage we have over technology is our hands and our ability to move stuff. Well, that's going to be gone with humanoids." The labor versus capital problem, which arguably motivated the initial appeal of cryptocurrency, is being supercharged by AI. But Visser does not see this as bearish for Bitcoin—he sees it as the ultimate validation.

---

## Scarcity as the Antidote to Abundance

Central to Visser's thesis is a sharp distinction between abundance and scarcity in the context of value storage. He argues that AI destroys anything that exists as digital abundance, while Bitcoin's physical mining constraints make it genuinely scarce. "I think we're in the stage, the most important stage for Bitcoin where it no longer is considered a software, a piece of code. It is considered something that is scarce. And scarcity has value like DRAM, like CPUs, like silver, like gold, like all of these different components."

This framing resolves the apparent paradox of Bitcoin as an "AI trade." While AI creates abundance of intelligence, content, and software—all of which erodes the value of existing digital assets—Bitcoin stands apart because it cannot be created infinitely. The 21 million cap is enforced by physics (energy expenditure), not code that can be updated. Visser compares Bitcoin to gold and religion as one of the "three moats in the world that have been decided by people that have not gone and survived the test of time." The implication is that Bitcoin has passed the ultimate test: human selection over extended periods.

"Digital economy has been merging with traditional finance for a long time, and so imagine something, and you can use Bitcoin for everyone paying attention, the market cap of Bitcoin is still sub $2 trillion while the market cap of the fiat system is close to $750 trillion. So for everyone who's positive on it, you're looking for some kind of reweighting of dollars transferring from one to the other."

The timing, Visser believes, is now. "So the question is when does Bitcoin get treated less like abundance and more like scarcity? And I believe that time is coming soon."

## The SaaS Apocalypse and Terminal Value Destruction

To illustrate how AI disrupts value, Visser and Ryan turn to what has become known in investment circles as the "SaaS apocalypse"—the repeated sell-offs in software companies whenever major AI labs release new capabilities. Ryan references the recent example where Figma's stock tanked after Anthropic released a new design feature in its latest Claude models. Visser confirms this is not an isolated incident but a structural pattern.

"Every single thing that people own as a store of value gets disrupted by AI," Visser states flatly. "There's not a single thing that you guys own in your lifetime that won't be disrupted by artificial intelligence." He then walks through the implications: if you own artwork, how do you know it's real? If you can create perfect digital copies—and Visser notes he is already dubbing his videos into other languages with his exact lip movements, at minimal cost—then the differentiation between authentic and fake collapses entirely.

The critical mechanism is terminal value destruction. Stocks are valued based on the present value of their future cash flows. When AI capabilities advance to the point where a software company can be replicated with a simple prompt, the uncertainty around future cash flows skyrockets, and prices fall toward zero. "The way that people value assets in the fiat system is based on what they'll have as value in the future. No one would buy stocks if they thought they were gonna go down over the course of the next decade." When AI advances rapidly—Visser references the timeline between his November 5th conversation and the release of Opus 4, which happened in between—the belief that a company will generate value five or ten years from now becomes increasingly speculative.

Visser then connects this to the broader financial system: "Then when you think about software names, you realize you're dealing with the largest sector in most of the private credit world where it's anywhere from 25% of the levered portion of the portfolio or it's closer to 50% in the unlevered, and you get a situation where you start to realize that private credit is being impacted by the disruption that's happening in SaaS." The implication is staggering—if SaaS companies are being disrupted and private credit is heavily exposed to them, the fiat financial system itself faces structural risk.

## Quantum as a Distraction, AI as the Real Force

Ryan raises a thoughtful challenge: given the unprecedented nature of the AI revolution, how can anyone have such conviction about a fundamentally uncertain future? Visser's response is revealing. He acknowledges that outcomes are probabilistic—there are twenty horses in the Kentucky Derby, and any can win. But his conviction is grounded in data, not speculation. "There is no question that artificial intelligence has improved significantly in just the three and a half years since ChatGPT. It is impossible to look back and to go through [the evidence]."

He distinguishes between quantum computing—a theoretical future risk that functions as the number one objection to Bitcoin investing—and AI disruption, which is happening now. "Quantum is something in the future. It is mythical at this point. It's not real, but it is something that progress will get to." This creates a parallel: just as quantum concerns suppress Bitcoin valuations by creating uncertainty about future value, AI concerns are suppressing SaaS valuations. But here is the crucial difference: AI disruption is demonstrably real, observable in quarterly earnings and stock price reactions. Quantum is not.

"Most people don't need to get involved in Bitcoin because they've got semiconductor stocks and they've had the hyperscalers and they've had all these different things to put their money in, which have provided similar returns to Bitcoin despite everyone's belief. The hyperscalers have done just as well, or the Mag Seven has done just as well, NVIDIA has done just as well."

The implication is that AI has been a powerful force benefiting many assets—semiconductor stocks, hyperscalers, AI infrastructure companies. But Visser believes we are now entering a new phase where AI "starts to consume everything," and at that point, the assets that provided similar returns will face their own disruption. Bitcoin, by contrast, becomes more valuable precisely because it cannot be disrupted by AI in the same way.

## Why This Cycle Will Be Different

Visser distinguishes the current moment from previous Bitcoin cycles in several important ways. First, there has been what he calls a "massive distribution"—long-term holders have been selling, which makes sense given the price action and the macro environment. But this distribution creates the conditions for a powerful move higher. "Retail and ETFs are buy and hold, and they're gonna be raising their weightings." When the next breakout occurs, Visser does not think "this one is gonna stop as quickly" as previous cycles.

This is consistent with his view that Bitcoin has already bottomed. Unlike other cycle investors who have appeared on Bankless and remained bearish in the short term while bullish long-term, Visser is explicitly bullish now. He sees the forces aligning—scarcity thesis strengthening, distribution creating supply squeeze potential, institutional and retail demand structures changing—and believes the next move higher will be more sustained than previous cycles.

---

## The Scarcity Portfolio Thesis

Throughout the conversation, Visser hints at a broader portfolio framework he calls the scarcity portfolio. The core idea is that as AI destroys abundance across asset classes—software companies, creative works, knowledge-based professions, potentially even physical labor through robotics—the only assets that retain value will be those with genuine scarcity. This includes Bitcoin, gold, and potentially other assets with hard constraints on supply.

The timing matters because AI is accelerating. "I was just on a call before this with a very sophisticated investor who asked me a question, and I said, my number one theme right now is compute shortage." Visser's themes on his website—a hundred of them created in the last four months—are all related to AI infrastructure buildout and the agentic world. They are up on average 30-40%. But the scarcity thesis is the other side of the trade: if AI destroys value in most assets, it simultaneously creates demand for the assets it cannot destroy.

## Detailed Takeaways

**1. Bitcoin's scarcity is a feature, not a bug, in an AI-dominated world.** While AI creates infinite copies of software, content, and intelligence, Bitcoin's physical mining constraints and fixed supply of 21 million coins make it genuinely rare. This is why Visser calls Bitcoin the "purest form of the AI trade"—it benefits from the same forces that destroy everything else. Investors should think about which assets have supply constraints AI cannot bypass.

**2. Terminal value destruction is the mechanism of AI's impact on stocks.** Every time a major AI lab releases a new capability, software companies see their stock prices collapse because their future cash flows become uncertain. This is not speculation—it is observable in the 20-40% drops that follow major model releases. This mechanism will eventually affect even the companies currently benefiting from AI buildout.

**3. Private credit faces hidden exposure to SaaS disruption.** Visser points out that private credit portfolios are heavily weighted toward software companies—25-50% depending on leverage structure. As these companies face AI-driven terminal value destruction, the private credit market may face structural stress. This creates potential opportunities in credit markets but also systemic risks to monitor.

**4. The fiat system itself is at risk because it lends against assets being disrupted.** Traditional finance does lending based on the value of assets. When those assets—SaaS companies, software businesses, knowledge-work enterprises—face disruption from AI, the collateral underlying the financial system weakens. This is why Visser views AI as "a very disruptive force to the entire framework of what is the fiat system."

**5. The next Bitcoin breakout will be different from previous cycles.** Distribution has occurred, ETF and retail buyers are accumulating with long time horizons, and the underlying thesis (scarcity in an AI-abundant world) is stronger than ever. Visser expects the next sustained move higher to be more powerful and less reversible than cycles of the past.
