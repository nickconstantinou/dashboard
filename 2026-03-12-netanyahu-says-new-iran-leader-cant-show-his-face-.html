Netanyahu says new Iran leader 'can't show his face in public' • FRANCE 24 English

Introduction

In a striking display of wartime leadership that defied conventional press protocols, Israeli Prime Minister Benjamin Netanyahu delivered his first public news briefing since the conflict with Hezbollah escalated into open warfare. The address, broadcast from what appeared to be an empty room with no journalists physically present, contained a layered message targeting multiple audiences simultaneously—each component carrying significant implications for the ongoing conflict and the broader Middle Eastern geopolitical landscape.

The briefing, which FRANCE 24's Jerusalem correspondent Katrina Posski described as "almost not really a press availability," revealed far more about the Israeli government's strategic communications approach than about the military situation on the ground. Selected journalists were permitted to submit questions via telephone, with their identities obscured from view. The Central Thesis: Eliminating Tyrants and the New Iranian "Puppet"

The core of Netanyahu's public message centered on what he characterized as a transformative moment in the conflict with Iran and its Lebanese proxy Hezbollah. Speaking directly to cameras in what correspondents described as his first public appearance since the war began, the Prime Minister declared that Israel had "eliminated the tyrant" alongside "many senior figures in the terrorist regime"—a reference to the recent targeted killings that have reshaped Hezbollah's leadership structure.

What appeared to particularly animate Netanyahu was Iran's replacement for Nasrallah, whom he repeatedly referred to as "Moaba," describing this individual as "the puppet of the revolutionary guards, cannot show his face in public." This characterization served multiple purposes: it sought to undermine the legitimacy of Iran's new representative in Lebanon, suggested internal weakness and fear within the Iranian power structure, and simultaneously appealed to both Israeli domestic audiences by projecting strength and to Iranian audiences by implying that their government was unstable and vulnerable.

The Prime Minister explicitly addressed the Iranian people during this briefing—a strategic communication choice that represented what Posski noted had been "commented very significantly this evening here in Israel." Netanyahu stated that Israel was doing "everything possible" to "lay the groundwork to remove the regime," yet he tempered this aggressive rhetoric with a more pragmatic acknowledgment: "you can lead a horse to water," he said, implying that while Israel could create conditions favorable to regime change, the ultimate success of such an effort would require the Iranian people themselves to "take to the streets and somehow topple their own government."

This framing revealed an important nuance in Israel's strategy—it signaled awareness that military pressure alone could not accomplish regime change in Tehran, while simultaneously maintaining rhetorical commitment to that objective and keeping maximum pressure on both Hezbollah and its Iranian sponsors.

The Lebanese Theater: Preparing for a Long War

Perhaps the most operationally significant element of Netanyahu's briefing involved his characterization of the conflict with Hezbollah as an extended campaign rather than a short-term military operation. Posski observed that the Prime Minister was "about a long war in Lebanon, assuring Israelis who are, as they enter the third week of this war, getting quite frightened by the possibility of just an unending life in bomb shelters."

This framing of an extended conflict was reinforced by statements from Israel's military leadership. The Israeli army chief of staff, addressing the nation separately on the same day, "urged Israelis to prepare for a long war"—unprecedented language that signaled to both domestic and international audiences that the current military campaign would not conclude quickly. Combined with the evacuation orders issued for "all of South Lebanon," which Posski described as "almost impossible to fulfill and also without precedent," these statements painted a picture of a conflict that was likely to intensify rather than diminish in the near term.

The threat implicit in these statements was amplified by earlier comments from Netanyahu's Defense Minister, who issued an ultimatum to the Lebanese government: either "disarm Hezbollah and remove it from the Israeli south"—as stipulated in the November 2024 ceasefire agreement—"or Israel would take the territory and get the job done." This explicit territorial threat represented a significant escalation in Israel's stated position and suggested that the government was prepared, if necessary, to expand the conflict beyond current boundaries.

The convergence of these messages—from the Prime Minister, the Defense Minister, and the military chief of staff—indicated a coordinated governmental strategy designed to prepare Israeli public opinion for what may become a prolonged and potentially expanding military campaign in Lebanon.

The Political Theater: Netanyahu's Trial and the Presidential Pardon

Perhaps the most unexpected element of the briefing involved Netanyahu's extended and passionate demand that Israeli President Isaac Herzog cancel his ongoing corruption trial. Posski reported that "something like half of this event was dedicated to an enraged Netanyahu demanding that the Israeli president cancel his trial," describing the Prime Minister as "demanding that the president suspend the legal proceedings" against him.

Netanyahu's language on this subject was notably emphatic, repeatedly stating that the president "has to do it" and characterizing it as "the right thing to do." Notably, however, he did not use the word "pardon"—a deliberate linguistic choice that reflected the complex legal situation surrounding his request. Earlier in the day, the pardons division of the Israeli justice ministry had determined that what Netanyahu had submitted "did not qualify him for a president," effectively blocking the legal pathway he had attempted to pursue.

This legal determination tied "the hands of the president of Israel who would be taking an illegal step now were he to suspend the legal proceedings the corruption trial against Netanyahu." The Prime Minister's decision to dedicate substantial portion of a wartime briefing to this subject, as Posski astutely observed, represented an effort at "painting himself as the victim"—a rhetorical strategy designed to reinforce claims of innocence while simultaneously suggesting that his ongoing trial somehow compromised Israel's wartime leadership.

This intersection of personal legal jeopardy with wartime governance illustrated a remarkable moment in Israeli political history: a Prime Minister facing multiple corruption charges using a national emergency to publicly pressure for the suspension of his trial, while simultaneously projecting an image of wartime strength and determination against external enemies.

Detailed Takeaways

Theatrical Control of Message: The unusual format of this briefing—conducted in an empty room with journalists participating only by phone—represented a deliberate departure from normal press protocols. By eliminating the possibility of spontaneous follow-up questions and controlling exactly which queries were permitted, Netanyahu's team ensured that the Prime Minister could deliver his full message without journalistic interruption while creating the appearance of accountability. This format itself communicated a message about the relationship between the government and the press during wartime.

Dual-Audience Communication Strategy: Every major element of the briefing appeared designed to appeal to different audiences simultaneously. The threats against Iran's leadership served domestic Israeli interests while also addressing the Iranian people directly. The discussion of a long war acknowledged civilian hardships while reinforcing resolve. The attacks on the judicial system connected to his political base while attempting to resolve personal legal challenges.

Legal Strategy Meets Military Strategy: The extensive portion of the briefing devoted to his trial revealed that Netanyahu views the war through both military and personal political lenses. His demand that the president cancel proceedings—despite the justice ministry's ruling that this was legally impermissible—demonstrated a willingness to use wartime authority to challenge institutional constraints on his power.

Escalation Signals: The coordinated messaging from the Prime Minister, Defense Minister, and military chief of staff regarding Lebanon represented a clear signal that Israel was preparing public opinion for potential significant escalation. The evacuation orders for all of South Lebanon, described as "without precedent," suggested planning for military operations that could affect the entire border region.

Regime Change Limitations: The candid acknowledgment that Israel could "lead a horse to water" regarding Iran, but could not force the Iranian people to overthrow their government, revealed an important strategic realism beneath the aggressive rhetoric. This acknowledged the limits of military power while maintaining political commitment to the objective of regime change.

Who This Is For

This briefing holds particular significance for several distinct audiences. Policy analysts and Middle East specialists will find the carefully calibrated messaging toward Iran and Lebanon particularly instructive, as it reveals how Israel communicates both its capabilities and its limitations to various audiences simultaneously. The explicit acknowledgment that regime change requires domestic Iranian action, despite continued military pressure, represents an important nuance in understanding Israel's long-term strategic objectives.

Journalists and communications professionals will find the format and execution of this briefing noteworthy as a case study in managed wartime communications. The elimination of press availability elements while maintaining the appearance of accessibility represents a significant evolution in how governments communicate during conflicts.

Political observers will find the intersection of personal legal interests with national wartime strategy particularly revealing. The way Netanyahu integrated his own trial into a broader national security narrative demonstrates how wartime contexts can be leveraged for political purposes beyond their immediate military objectives.

General audiences seeking to understand the current conflict will benefit from understanding the multiple layered messages contained within this single briefing, recognizing that statements directed at different audiences may serve different purposes and contain different implications.

The significance of this briefing ultimately lies not in any single announcement but in the comprehensive picture it painted of an Israeli government using wartime communications to address multiple strategic objectives: demonstrating resolve against adversaries, preparing domestic opinion for extended conflict, and attempting to leverage national emergency for personal political benefit. Each element connected to the others in a carefully orchestrated presentation that revealed much about how the current Israeli leadership perceives both the threats it faces and the opportunities that crisis presents.
