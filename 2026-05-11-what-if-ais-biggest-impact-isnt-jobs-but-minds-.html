The Quiet Revolution Inside Your Skull

How Artificial Intelligence Is Reshaping Human Cognition—and Why It Matters More Than Job Displacement

For the past several years, the dominant conversation around artificial intelligence has centered on one anxiety: will machines take our jobs? From factory floors to law firms, headlines have warned of automation erasing careers and destabilizing economies. But investment manager Tom Slater argues we've been asking the wrong question entirely.

In a recent conversation on the *Maren Talks Money* podcast, Slater—who manages the Scottish Mortgage Investment Trust and leads the US equity team at Bailey Gifford—lays out a provocative thesis: AI's most profound impact won't be on employment figures or company valuations. It will be on the physical structure of your brain and the nature of human thought itself.

"The question isn't whether your job will be automated," Slater explains. "It's whether your mind will be automated instead."

This distinction matters enormously. While job displacement follows predictable patterns tied to specific tasks and industries, cognitive transformation touches every person who interacts with these systems—including those who never feared for their careers.

---

When Technology Rewires the Brain

Slater's argument draws on a striking historical parallel: the last time a cognitive technology achieved near-universal adoption, it didn't just change behavior—it changed biology.

Two centuries ago, only about twelve percent of the world's adults could read. Today, that figure stands at approximately eighty-seven percent. We typically celebrate this as an educational triumph, a precondition for democracy and economic development. But Slater, drawing on research from the book *The Weirdest People in the World*, points to something more fundamental: the acquisition of literacy physically rewired human brains.

When humans learned to read, the neural connection between brain hemispheres actually thickened. We repurposed regions previously dedicated to recognizing faces—a high evolutionary priority for social creatures—and redirected them toward decoding abstract symbols. The trade-off was real: reading proficiency came at some cost to facial recognition ability. But the return was an entirely new mode of human cognition.

"We shouldn't assume that cultural technology operating on your brain is impossible," Slater observes. "It's absolutely possible for a cultural technology to reach inside your skull and change the organ that makes you human."

The critical insight here is speed. Reading spread gradually over generations, giving societies time to adapt. AI has no such patience. Everyone with internet access now has immediate access to systems that can generate text, analyze data, and make decisions that previously required human judgment. The transformation isn't approaching—it's already embedded in daily life.

---

Who Controls How Humans Think?

The historical comparison reveals something else: the mechanisms of cultural evolution are being quietly captured.

Slater identifies three factors that drive cultural change: variation (the introduction of new ideas), transmission (how ideas spread between people), and selection (which ideas gain prominence). Historically, institutions controlled these levers. The Catholic Church, for instance, shaped European thought for centuries by limiting what ideas could vary, how they transmitted, and which perspectives survived.

Today, according to Slater, technology has assumed that role—and we're not paying attention.

"AI becomes supercharged variation because the number of ideas you can test, that you can generate, grows exponentially," he explains. "And ideas that humans just wouldn't have reached."

Consider transmission. A century ago, if a million children asked their parents why the sky is blue, they would have received nearly a million different answers—each shaped by local knowledge, personal experience, and individual reasoning style. Now, those same children ask AI assistants and receive identical explanations. The diversity of thought that once emerged from distributed knowledge-gathering has collapsed into centralized responses.

Selection has followed the same pattern. Social media algorithms now determine which ideas spread and which quietly disappear. Slater offers an observation that reframes some tech industry mysteries: "I think you now understand why Elon Musk bought Twitter." Not for its advertising revenue or user base, but for its control over the mechanism by which human culture propagates.

The question this raises is uncomfortable: are we having any meaningful debate about whether we want technology to govern these fundamental processes? Or are we passively accepting a transformation in how human civilization thinks?

---

The Experiment That Shows What's Already Happening

The stakes become concrete in research Slater cites from MIT, where neuroscientists studied brain activity while participants wrote essays under three conditions: completely unaided, with access to search engines, or with AI assistance.

The results were stark for the AI-assisted group. Approximately eighty percent of participants who used AI to generate their essays couldn't provide a single correct quote from what they'd just written minutes earlier. The cognitive effort that traditionally produces durable learning—formulating arguments, selecting evidence, constructing sentences—had been entirely outsourced.

But here's the crucial nuance: the same study showed that *engaging* with AI produced entirely different outcomes. When participants used AI as a research tool—asking questions, pushing back on responses, seeking to understand relationships between concepts—their brains showed the same activation patterns as those who'd written essays by hand.

"There's something about that effortful interaction, that seeking to gain knowledge, that is the crucial part in actually developing cognitive skills," Slater explains. The issue isn't AI itself; it's the passive delegation of thinking to machines.

This finding has profound implications for education, professional development, and any context where skill-building matters. The technology doesn't inherently harm cognition—but using it as a replacement for effortful learning does.

---

The Trade You Don't Know You're Making

The passive delegation problem compounds over time in ways users don't recognize.

When someone uses AI to draft an email, generate a report, or solve a problem, they gain efficiency. They save time. They accomplish more. But they're also making a trade: cognitive capability for convenience. And here's what Slater identifies as the most dangerous aspect of this exchange: the user doesn't realize they've made it.

"People mistake their ability to use AI tools with their mastery of the subject they're working on," he explains. "They get better at using the AI, but they confuse that with thinking they're good at the actual topic."

This confusion is systematic. Normally, the Dunning-Kruger effect creates a corrective mechanism: as people invest effort in learning something, they become *more* aware of how much they don't know. The struggle to understand complexity reveals its depth. But using AI short-circuits this feedback loop. Users achieve outputs without effort, never confront the difficulty that would reveal their knowledge gaps, and therefore never develop the metacognitive awareness that guides further learning.

The result is what Slater calls "active weakening"—cognitive capability that doesn't just fail to develop but actually deteriorates. Each time someone delegates a task to AI that they could have done themselves, the corresponding neural pathways receive less stimulation. Over time, the capability genuinely atrophies, just as navigational memory fades when everyone relies on GPS.

---

Why Experience Becomes More Valuable—and Why That's a Problem

Slater frames AI as a "force multiplier" rather than a "tide that lifts all boats." The distinction matters enormously for understanding its economic and social effects.

An experienced scientist who has manually worked through statistical analyses can use AI to generate results faster and explore more hypotheses. But that scientist can also evaluate whether the AI's output is conceptually sound—whether the methodology makes sense, whether the conclusions follow from the data. The prior mastery allows genuine evaluation.

Someone without that foundation faces a fundamentally different situation. They cannot meaningfully assess AI output; they can only check whether it looks reasonable on the surface. They lack the expertise to identify when a system produces technically plausible but conceptually meaningless results—exactly the kind of error that occurs when large language models confidently generate incorrect information.

This creates a troubling cascade in professional development. For years, economists and technologists have predicted that AI would eliminate entry-level jobs—the routine tasks that new graduates learn on. But Slater identifies what happens next: if those foundational roles disappear, where do people develop the expertise required to supervise and evaluate AI systems?

"You have to judge whether what your agent has told you is correct," Slater observes, "but you no longer have the foundational skills to be able to do that."

The next generation of knowledge workers may find themselves promoted into roles requiring judgment they never had the opportunity to develop. The middle rungs of career ladders disappear precisely when workers need them most.

---

What This Means for Individuals and Institutions

Slater's argument suggests that passive acceptance of AI's cognitive effects is not a neutral choice—it actively shapes who humans become.

For individuals, the implication is clear: engagement matters more than efficiency. Using AI to think *with*—to explore, question, and understand—preserves and even enhances cognitive capability. Using AI to think *instead of* produces the opposite result. The technology is the same; the approach determines whether it strengthens or weakens the mind.

For organizations, Slater's analysis suggests that uncritical adoption may carry hidden costs. A company that automates junior roles and trains remaining staff to rely on AI systems may find, years later, that its employees cannot perform the judgment work that automation cannot handle. The efficiency gains may purchase a temporary advantage while undermining a longer-term capability.

For society, the stakes extend further. If AI systems control how ideas vary, transmit, and are selected, human culture itself may evolve in directions no one chose. Slater doesn't argue that this transformation is inherently negative—but he insists that it deserves conscious deliberation rather than passive acceptance.

"We need to ask: are we going to unthinkingly let this change happen," he asks, "or do we have a debate about how these tools are used and where they're used?"

---

The Audience This Argument Reaches

This conversation speaks most directly to knowledge workers in their twenties through fifties—professionals who use AI systems regularly and who have enough career ahead of them for the cognitive changes to matter significantly. It should particularly resonate with educators, managers responsible for professional development, and anyone who makes decisions about how AI should integrate into workplace workflows.

Investors will also find value in Slater's reframing. If AI functions as a force multiplier rather than a democratizing force, the competitive implications shift. Organizations with deep expertise may compound their advantages faster than those without. Traditional measures of AI adoption may miss the more important question: whether that adoption strengthens or weakens the human capital it relies upon.

The core insight—that how we use AI matters as much as whether we use it—applies regardless of industry or role. Anyone who has noticed themselves reaching for AI tools without thinking, or who suspects their memory and reasoning feel different than they used to, will find Slater's framework both validating and challenging.

---

The Question Nobody's Asking

Throughout the conversation, Slater keeps returning to a single uncomfortable observation: the most important transformation AI will bring isn't the one dominating headlines, investor presentations, or policy debates. It's the quiet, continuous, largely invisible reshaping of human cognition that happens every time someone chooses convenience over effort.

"This is an awful lot more important than the valuations of AI companies," he tells Webb. "What's happening in your head?"

The answer matters more than any quarterly earnings report. And unlike financial metrics, cognitive change leaves no audit trail—only the gradual realization, perhaps years from now, that something once possible has become difficult or impossible.

The technology is indifferent to this outcome. The choice of how to engage with it is entirely human.
