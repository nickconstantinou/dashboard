# Bluey Phone Review: MKBHD's Honest Take on the Most Honest Phone Money Can Buy

## A Deep Dive into the $10.99 Device That's Redefining What a "Minimal Phone" Can Be

When you think of tech reviewer Marques Brownlee—better known as MKBHD—you probably imagine him unboxing the latest iPhone flagship or benchmarking gaming phones that cost more than a used car. So it came as something of a surprise when the renowned YouTuber found himself scrolling through Amazon's top-rated phones and stumbling upon a device so radically stripped down that it challenges the very definition of a smartphone. The Bluey Phone, manufactured by VTEC and priced at an almost absurd $10.99, caught his attention not because of its specs or innovative features, but because of what it *doesn't* have.

In a landscape where even the most dedicated "minimal phone" advocates still ship devices with touchscreens, cameras, and persistent internet connections, the Bluey Phone takes a concept that most manufacturers only pay lip service to and commits to it with almost startling thoroughness. MKBHD ordered one sight unseen, drawn by the incredible ratings it had accumulated, and what arrived at his door was something that defies easy categorization—is it a phone? A toy? A middle finger to the entire smartphone industry? The answer, as it turns out, is all of the above.

---

## First Impressions: The Unboxing Experience

The moment MKBHD received his package, he noticed something unusual about the presentation. The Bluey Phone ships in an open-faced cardboard box, meaning the device itself is visible and exposed right through the packaging—a shipping method he admits he'd never encountered before. This unconventional approach immediately signals that VTEC is operating in a different space than traditional smartphone manufacturers, prioritizing efficiency and cost-cutting over the premium unboxing experience that has become standard in the industry.

However, getting the rest of the way into the box proved to be what MKBHD describes as "kind of unintuitive and difficult." The phone was secured with cardboard fasteners behind the device that required both physical ripping and the unscrewing of small fasteners to release. He acknowledges that this doesn't feel particularly premium, but reasonably argues that such compromises are entirely forgivable at this price point. When you're spending less than eleven dollars on a communications device, certain manufacturing shortcuts become expected rather than disappointing.

The cost-cutting measures extend beyond just the packaging. Notably absent from the box are any charging cables, wall adapters, or documentation—standard inclusions for virtually every other phone on the market regardless of price. What's perhaps most striking, however, is the omission of a tool to open the back panel. The Bluey Phone features removable batteries, a rarity in 2026 smartphones, but accessing them requires a specialized opening tool that VTEC chose not to include. MKBHD notes this oversight with a degree of resignation, though he does point out that the phone arrives from the factory with enough initial charge to power on immediately out of the box.

---

## Hardware and Design: A Blast from the Past

Physically, the Bluey Phone sits in a size category that will feel immediately familiar to anyone who owned a smartphone from the mid-2010s. MKBHD describes it as roughly the same dimensions as a Nexus 6, the flagship Google phone released in 2014, though he notes it's actually a bit smaller. This makes it surprisingly compact by modern standards, fitting comfortably in a pocket while still being large enough to handle without fumbling.

The display immediately reveals this phone's true nature. The Bluey Phone rocks a 1.7-inch black and white screen—a stark departure from the vibrant OLED panels that dominate today's market. This isn't a compromised display trying to mimic full-color rendering; it's a genuinely minimal screen that serves only the most basic informational purposes. With such a small screen, the total pixel count across the entire display measures under one thousand pixels, a number that would make any modern smartphone user wince but perfectly serves the device's intentionally limited purpose.

The physical design also includes a visible antenna at the top of the device, an architectural element that has disappeared from contemporary smartphones entirely. This antenna serves no cellular function, given that the phone contains zero cell radios, but it adds to the retro aesthetic and suggests a device that operates more like a walkie-talkie than anything we'd traditionally call a phone.

---

## The Core Philosophy: What It Means to Be a "Minimal Phone"

Here's where the Bluey Phone becomes genuinely interesting—not as a piece of technology, but as a philosophical statement about our relationship with connectivity. MKBHD explains that he knows several people in real life who have switched to minimal phones in an effort to reduce their screen time and reclaim their attention from the endless scroll of social media. This is a real and growing movement, with reviewers and wellness advocates increasingly championing simpler devices as a solution to smartphone addiction.

But as MKBHD points out, most minimal phones on the market today still retain the core features that make smartphones addictive in the first place. They still have touchscreens. They still have cameras. They still connect to the internet and can run applications. The Light Phone, for instance, while marketed as a minimalist alternative, can theoretically still access certain web functions. These devices represent incremental steps toward simplicity rather than a true commitment to the concept.

The Bluey Phone, by contrast, commits fully. It contains no cell radios whatsoever, meaning it cannot make or receive phone calls in the traditional sense. There is no internet connection of any kind—no Wi-Fi, no cellular data, nothing. There are no cameras, a feature so fundamental to modern smartphones that its absence feels almost radical. The device relies exclusively on physical buttons for all interaction, and notably, even lacks Bluetooth connectivity.

MKBHD demonstrates this philosophy with a memorable example: when you type numbers into the Bluey Phone's keypad, it doesn't attempt to dial anyone or store contacts. Instead, it simply speaks the numbers out loud, narrating each digit as you press it. The device has no one to call and no network to reach, so it transforms what would normally be a functional action into a playful interaction.

## Unexpected Features: Games and Companionship

Given its radical simplification, the Bluey Phone still manages to include a few surprises. For one, the device does contain games—something that might seem counterintuitive for a phone designed to minimize distraction. MKBHD addresses this apparent contradiction directly, arguing that these built-in diversions are "much healthier than doom scrolling TikTok or Instagram." The games are simple, often nostalgic in nature, and deliberately uncompelling in the way that makes modern social media so habit-forming.

His personal favorite is a game that uses the phone's microphone in an unexpectedly creative way: you blow into the microphone, and the device responds by generating virtual bubbles on screen. It's a whimsical feature that transforms a passive action into an interactive experience, and MKBHD clearly delights in demonstrating it. The low-powered processor handles this simple animation without issue, and the entire game fits comfortably within the phone's extremely limited technical scope.

Another unexpected feature is the chat function, which allows users to have conversations with the phone's namesake characters: Bluey and Bingo, the Australian Blue Heeler dogs from the popular animated children's series. MKBHD preemptively addresses any concerns that this might be "chat AI slop," clarifying immediately that there is no artificial intelligence involved whatsoever. The chat function is exactly what it sounds like—a scripted interaction with beloved cartoon characters that provides companionship without connectivity.

The boot-up time for the Bluey Phone also deserves mention, purely for the comedic contrast it provides. MKBHD demonstrates powering on the device, and the speed is genuinely impressive—instant, even, by the standards of phones that take thirty seconds or more to fully load their operating systems. He jokes that this represents "one of the fastest booting phones," a backhanded compliment that highlights both the device's simplicity and the bloated nature of modern smartphone software.

## The Speaker and Audio Design

Audio output on the Bluey Phone comes from a single rear-facing speaker, a configuration that would typically draw criticism on flagship devices but works perfectly well here given the phone's intended purpose. MKBHD notes that the speaker grill and opening are "more than big enough" to prevent users from accidentally covering them with their fingers while holding the device. This, he observes, represents thoughtful design—understanding how the phone will actually be used and engineering accordingly, rather than cramming components into a form factor designed for entirely different priorities.

The speaker offers three distinct volume levels, giving users some control over audio output without overwhelming them with options. This simplicity extends to every aspect of the device's audio design; there's no equalizer, no sound profiles, no integration with streaming services. The speaker exists simply to produce sound when the phone requires it, whether that's announcing number presses or playing the simple audio effects that accompany its built-in games.

## MKBHD's Verdict: The Most Honest Minimal Phone

Throughout the review, MKBHD makes clear that comparing the Bluey Phone to traditional smartphones—or even to other devices in the minimal phone category—would be a fundamental category error. When he notes that the phone doesn't have "the thin bezels or high benchmark scores" to compete with devices costing thirteen, fifteen, or twenty dollars, it's delivered with a knowing smile. The Bluey Phone isn't trying to compete on those terms; it's operating in an entirely different competitive landscape where traditional metrics of smartphone quality are irrelevant.

His comparison to the Light Phone—a device often cited as the gold standard for minimal phones—reveals the philosophical gap between incremental and radical approaches to device simplification. The Light Phone 2, while simpler than most smartphones, still contains a touchscreen, can still theoretically browse certain web content, and maintains the fundamental architecture of a modern computing device. The Bluey Phone, by contrast, has embraced limitation as a feature rather than a compromise.

This commitment to radical simplicity produces tangible benefits that MKBHD highlights with genuine enthusiasm. With fewer than a thousand total pixels on the display, a processor that likely can't even run a benchmark test, and a screen refresh rate that never exceeds three to four frames per second, the phone's power requirements are negligible compared to any modern smartphone. Battery life extends from days to weeks—a transformation that anyone who finds themselves constantly hunting for an outlet will immediately appreciate.

Screen time, too, is fundamentally restructured by the device's limitations. Where even well-intentioned minimal phone users might accidentally find themselves browsing social media or watching videos, the Bluey Phone makes such behavior physically impossible. There's simply nothing to scroll through, no content to consume, no notifications to check. The doom scrolling that has become synonymous with smartphone use is "effectively reduced to zero," as MKBHD puts it. The phone becomes a tool for specific, intentional interactions rather than an endless source of passive consumption.

## Key Takeaways

**The Bluey Phone represents a thought experiment made physical.** Rather than merely simplifying existing smartphone architecture, VTEC has created a device that forces users to reconsider what they actually need from a phone. The absence of cameras, internet, and cellular connectivity isn't a bug—it's the entire point. For users frustrated with their own smartphone habits, this represents a category of device that truly addresses the problem rather than merely acknowledging it.

**Cost and complexity are inherently linked.** At $10.99, the Bluey Phone demonstrates that many features we consider essential in modern smartphones are actually optional luxuries that add both expense and cognitive overhead. By removing everything except the most basic functionality, the device achieves a price point and simplicity that no mainstream manufacturer has been willing to pursue.

**The packaging and presentation of a product communicate its values.** The exposed shipping method and difficult-to-open box aren't accidents—they're signals that VTEC is prioritizing function over form in every aspect of the device. MKBHD's acceptance of these compromises at this price point suggests that consumers may be willing to forgive stripped-down experiences when the price reflects genuine simplification.

**Intentional limitations can be healthier than unlimited options.** The built-in games and chat features work precisely because they're contained within a system that can't expand beyond its boundaries. The bubble-blowing game is charming exactly because it can't be monetized, monetized, or turned into a social platform. Simplicity creates space that more complex systems inevitably fill with engagement metrics and notification triggers.

## Who Is This For?

The Bluey Phone is not for everyone—in fact, it's probably not for most people. Its appeal is narrowly focused on individuals who have already recognized their smartphone usage as problematic and have taken active steps to address it, perhaps by purchasing devices like the Light Phone or establishing personal rules about screen time and app usage.

For this audience, the Bluey Phone represents the logical endpoint of the minimal phone movement. Where other devices offer compromise solutions that maintain some connectivity while reducing others, the Bluey Phone eliminates the possibility of compromise entirely. You cannot doom scroll on a device with no internet connection, no matter how strong your willpower. You cannot take photos for Instagram when your phone has no camera. You cannot text someone back at 2 AM when your phone doesn't know who you are or who anyone else is.

Parents of young children might also find value in the Bluey Phone as a first device for kids who aren't ready for the complexity and responsibility of a true smartphone. The cartoon branding makes it appealing to children familiar with the show, while the device's limitations provide natural guardrails against overuse and early exposure to social media.

MKBHD concludes his review with praise for VTEC's execution of a genuinely novel concept, congratulating "Blue Buie" on undercutting the minimal phone competition by a significant margin. It's clear that despite—or perhaps because of—the Bluey Phone's radical limitations, he finds it genuinely impressive as a product that knows exactly what it wants to be and commits fully to that vision without apology or compromise.
