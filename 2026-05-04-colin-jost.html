    The Smartless Podcast: Colin Jost Opens Up About SNL Life, Personal History, and Surviving as a Weekend Update Anchor

    In a wide-ranging conversation that jumps from absurdist comedy to surprisingly poignant personal revelations, Colin Jost joins the Smartless trio — Jason Bateman, Sean Hayes, and Will Arnett — for an episode that traces everything from the practical realities of working at *Saturday Night Live* to the strange details of his own upbringing. The conversation moves with the fluid energy characteristic of the podcast, touching on Jost's relationship with Lorne Michaels, his ongoing health management, and several revelations that offer rare glimpses into the private life of one of television's longest-serving comedy writers and performers.

    The episode arrives with Jost recently returned from London after his acclaimed run in the Broadway production *The Outsiders*, a show that has already earned him recognition in the awards circuit — though he notes he's still in the early stages of navigating that landscape, particularly given that *The Outsiders* is an Off-Broadway production with its own limited nomination processes. The travel and the transition back to civilian life — the "adrenaline dump" performers experience when a show ends — provide an early frame for the conversation, with Jost confessing that his return flight involved ordering "everything" on the menu: chicken, beef, a hot fudge sundae, and "like three pretzel rolls," all to compensate for three months of careful eating required by the production's vocal demands.

    ---

    The hosts quickly pivot to what they can infer about Jost's workspace, noting the distinctive asbestos ceiling tiles visible behind him. Jost confirms this with the dark humor of someone who has simply accepted the absurdity of his working conditions: the tiles are the kind you could lift and hide things behind, and there exists a mysterious large stain that predates his fifteen-year tenure in the office. When officials came to remove asbestos from the *SNL* offices five years ago, they informed Jost that his space was already compromised — he had been living and working over the carcinogenic material since his move-in. His resigned response to the revelation: "It's too late. It's way too late."

    The conversation about the physical space leads into a broader discussion of the show's culture, particularly around Lorne Michaels and the recent documentary about him. Jost attended the Lincoln Center premiere and describes the film as both "funny" and "kind of moving," though he notes he didn't necessarily learn anything new about Michaels. The documentary shows Lorne's homes in Amagansett and Maine — places where he retreats to escape and be in nature — and reveals a man who is, according to Jost, "very funny" in person. The hosts are skeptical, remembering Lorne as somewhat intimidating and reserved during their *SNL* appearances, but Jost insists he has always found Lorne to be "one of the most outgoing guys" with a constant stream of jokes. "You gotta slow him down," Jost explains. "He's so hilarious." The film features Lorne allowing crews into his private spaces in ways he historically resisted, and Jost credits this as evidence of a new willingness to let people "dig in" after decades of guarding his privacy.

    A particularly memorable detail involves a face mold that Jost facilitated for the *Smartless* hosts — a prop-making process at *SNL* where performers' faces are cast to create latex masks and prosthetics. Jost found the old form with his Polaroid attached and his head measurements documented, and he mailed it to the hosts after encountering it in the archives. The hosts joke about using the molds to disguise themselves as Jost for Halloween, prompting Jost to share the infamous Jeff Daniels horror story: during his hosting gig, the mold was applied incorrectly and fully adhered to his face. The removal process required hours of chiseling, leaving what Jost describes as "almost burns." Daniels nonetheless hosted the show that night, drinking Coca-Cola through straws for the entire broadcast.

    The Mystery of Colin Jost's Silent Childhood

    Perhaps the most striking personal revelation comes when the hosts probe a detail from the introduction — that Colin Jost did not speak until he was nearly four years old. Jost explains that he has no memory of this himself, but the story is apparently documented in family home videos showing him as a child "trying to sell a bike" with dialogue that sounded like a Soprano family member. When the hosts guess Tony Soprano, Jost corrects them: it was Carmela Soprano. He was speaking in her distinctive, concerned tone at age six or seven.

    His first word was "Will," and his second word — five years later — was "Chase," his own middle name. His parents consulted speech specialists at Staten Island University Hospital for guidance, but there was no diagnosis. The specialists simply concluded he was "not ready." His mother, Jost says, was never particularly worried because he appeared to comprehend everything being said to him. He was just choosing not to speak, a delay so extreme it crossed into the territory of near-total silence for several years. The hosts joke about his childhood self nodding along, winking, and forming the curved hourglass hand gesture of a wooden figure — his way of communicating without words.

    ---

    The conversation takes a more serious turn when Jost discusses the physical toll of *SNL*'s schedule, particularly the late-night writing sessions where the writers' room would be covered in candy every Tuesday. He had heart palpitations serious enough to call a doctor in Lorne's office after a particularly intense combination of stress, sleep deprivation, and sugar consumption. The doctor advised him to sleep more and reduce the candy intake. Jost's own theory at the time was that sugar made him so tired it acted as a sleep aid — he would reach the peak and then immediately crash, which felt like a feature rather than a bug. Eventually, the toll caught up with him.

    He now experiences atrial fibrillation regularly enough that Jimmy Kimmel has nicknamed him "Paddles," though he notes the original cardiac incident was probably just a panic attack rather than an AFib episode. The managing approach involves careful attention to what he eats and when, with the specific constraint that performers in productions requiring heavy vocal work must avoid eating before shows due to acid reflux and throat clearing. This is why his return from London involved such aggressive eating — he had been maintaining strict discipline for months and was finally liberated.

    Family Legacy and Unique Life Details

    Jost's mother's role as chief medical officer for the NYC Fire Department places her at the center of one of American history's most traumatic events. She survived the South Tower collapse, a fact that contextualizes the family's relationship to public service and crisis. This sits alongside other unusual life details in Jost's biography: he once went through twelve dumpsters searching for his wife's lost engagement ring, he and his wife named their son Cosmo, and he co-owns a decommissioned Staten Island ferry — something Jost has called "the single dumbest person in his life," suggesting both pride and bemusement at the acquisition.

    The conversation weaves through the hosts' various activities — Will is in Liverpool working with Tommy Hilfiger, which prompts speculation about whether the designer has ever been asked about the name's contraction, and Shani has returned from an Olivier Award-winning run in *Goodnight Oscar* that positioned him as an early Tony contender before it became clear that Off-Broadway productions have limited awards pathways. Jason pitches his idea for a mold removal company called "Adjacent Abatement," insisting the name alone would generate business through the comedy of the signage.

    Silence in childhood doesn't predict future eloquence — Jost's story challenges the assumption that developmental delays indicate lasting impairment. He spoke almost not at all until age four and now anchors one of television's most demanding live comedy programs, writing and delivering material that requires split-second timing and verbal precision. Parents facing similar situations might take comfort that comprehension often exists beneath the surface even when expression hasn't emerged.

    The physical environment of work matters more than we admit — Jost's revelation about asbestos in his office underscores how institutional spaces can carry hidden costs. Fifteen years of exposure before discovery represents a systemic failure, yet his matter-of-fact acceptance of it reflects a broader reality: people often adapt to working conditions rather than demand change, particularly when the alternative is leaving a job they need or love.

    Performance demands require extreme dietary discipline that creates rebound effects — The pattern Jost describes — strict food restriction during a show run, followed by aggressive consumption during recovery — illustrates a common cycle in performance professions. The body holds its state during active performance and then "releases" afterward, sometimes through illness or digestive distress. Understanding this pattern can help performers anticipate and manage the transition periods between show intensity and rest.

    Archive preservation has unexpected second lives — The face mold form represents a small piece of institutional history that Jost proactively preserved and shared, enabling the *Smartless* hosts to replicate the experience for themselves. Such artifacts carry both sentimental and practical value, and the instinct to keep rather than discard allowed something with entertainment value to extend beyond its original context.

    Medical expertise and personal history create profound context — The detail about Jost's mother's survival of the South Tower collapse reframes his own family narrative within the broader story of 9/11's aftermath and its effects on first responders and their families. Understanding someone's background includes recognizing these structural elements, not just biographical facts.
