The greatest first-person shooter of all time | Jeff Kaplan and Lex Fridman

Introduction

In this wide-ranging conversation, game designer Jeff Kaplan—formerly the visionary director behind Overwatch and now working at Blizzard Entertainment—joins Lex Fridman for an in-depth exploration of what makes a first-person shooter truly great. The discussion ventures beyond simple nostalgia to examine the fundamental design philosophies, mechanical innovations, and player experiences that define the FPS genre at its finest. Rather than simply listing favorite games, Kaplan offers genuine insight into what he believes constitutes excellence in interactive entertainment, drawing on his decades of experience creating some of the most beloved multiplayer experiences in gaming history.

The conversation also touches on the broader landscape of competitive gaming, examining how different titles have approached the challenge of creating satisfying combat systems, memorable multiplayer maps, and compelling reasons for players to return again and again. Kaplan's perspective is particularly valuable because he has sat on both sides of the design equation—having both studied what makes existing games work and then applied those lessons to create new ones. This dual experience gives him a uniquely nuanced understanding of what separates good games from great ones.

The Case for Quake: Purity of Movement and Combat

When asked to name the greatest first-person shooter of all time, Kaplan does not hesitate: Quake is the greatest. This answer is significant because it comes from someone who has spent his career creating highly polished, team-based shooters like Overwatch, yet he returns to Quake's raw, competitive foundation as the pinnacle of the genre. The reasoning behind this choice reveals much about what Kaplan values in FPS design—specifically, the relationship between player skill and game mechanics, and how movement itself can become a form of mastery.

Quake, developed by id Software and released in 1996, represented a quantum leap in 3D gaming technology and design philosophy. Unlike earlier shooters that emphasized narrative or environmental puzzles, Quake was built entirely around direct player-versus-player competition, with movement speed and aiming precision forming the core skills that separated excellent players from merely good ones. Kaplan's reverence for Quake suggests that he views the purest expression of the FPS genre as something closer to a competitive sport than to traditional game design—a framework where mechanical mastery provides the foundation for all other gameplay elements.

Rust and the Philosophy of Consequence

The conversation takes an intriguing turn when Fridman asks about Rust, a game that has developed a devoted (some might say cult-like) following despite—or perhaps because of—its punishing difficulty and player-versus-player focus. Kaplan provides a detailed explanation of what makes Rust unique: it is an open-world survival game set on a procedurally generated map that resets monthly, meaning every time players enter the game, the landscape is different. The island operates on a cycle where players must gather resources, build bases, and accumulate weapons and explosives, all while knowing that at any moment, any other player could kill them and take everything they own.

What makes Rust particularly fascinating from a design perspective is how it embraces what Kaplan describes as the "high highs" and "low lows" of player experience. In Rust, players can spend an entire week constructing what they believe is the perfect base, filling it with valuable loot, only to log in and discover they've been raided while offline. This concept of "offline raiding" is so prevalent in the Rust community that players simultaneously condemn it as the ultimate breach of gamer etiquette while also engaging in it regularly—a contradiction that speaks to the game's unique psychological intensity. Kaplan frames this as an essential truth about what makes Rust compelling: you cannot have the most triumphant moments without the possibility of the most devastating losses. The emotional stakes in Rust are real because the consequences are permanent, mirroring aspects of real life that most games carefully shield players from.

Call of Duty's Technical Mastery

When discussing more mainstream shooters, Kaplan turns his attention to the Call of Duty series, particularly Call of Duty 4: Modern Warfare and Modern Warfare 2, which he identifies as the pinnacle of that franchise's achievements, with Black Ops serving as a respectable third option. What makes these titles stand out in Kaplan's analysis is not merely their popularity or entertainment value, but the sheer technical excellence of their gunplay systems. He describes the shooting mechanics in Call of Duty as something every game developer should study, pointing to the visual effects, animation, modeling, and sound design that combine to create what he calls the best "gun feel" in any game ever made.

Beyond the weapons themselves, Kaplan discusses how the multiplayer map design in these titles represents a masterclass in flow and player interaction. He specifically mentions two maps—Crash from Call of Duty 4 and Terminal from Modern Warfare 2—as examples of design that is "off the hook," language that reveals his genuine admiration for how these virtual spaces guide player movement, create meaningful encounters, and establish memorable battle rhythms. Interestingly, Kaplan notes that he and Aaron Keller—who would later become the game director on Overwatch—used to study these maps extensively, analyzing how successful multiplayer environments function at a fundamental level. This practice of reverse-engineering successful designs demonstrates the intellectual rigor that underlies professional game development, transforming what casual players experience as fun into something that can be systematically analyzed and learned from.

The Overwatch Connection and Design Philosophy

Throughout the conversation, Kaplan's role as the former director of Overwatch provides context for understanding his perspective on game design. Overwatch represented a significant departure from the twitch-based, individual skill emphasis of Quake toward a more team-based, role-driven approach to multiplayer combat. Yet the underlying principles that Kaplan identifies as important—mechanical satisfaction, map flow, meaningful player choice—remain consistent across these different approaches to the FPS genre.

The mention of Aaron Keller succeeding Kaplan as Overwatch's game director suggests a continuity of design philosophy at Blizzard, where the lessons learned from studying classic shooters like Call of Duty maps have been applied to creating more modern, team-based experiences. This connection between past and present FPS design helps illustrate how the genre has evolved while retaining core principles of what makes combat feel good to play.

Key Takeaways

Mechanical excellence forms the foundation of great shooters. Whether discussing Quake's movement systems or Call of Duty's gun feel, Kaplan emphasizes that the most fundamental element of any first-person shooter is how it feels to play. Graphics, narrative, and content all matter, but if the core mechanical experience fails to satisfy on a visceral level, nothing else matters. This insight applies broadly to game design: the moment-to-moment interaction between player and game must be compelling before any other considerations become relevant.

Consequence and stakes create emotional engagement. Rust's punishing approach to player-versus-player interaction demonstrates that players will embrace significant difficulty and loss when the stakes feel meaningful. The emotional highs in gaming require the possibility of emotional lows—this principle extends beyond Rust to any game where player choices carry weight. Designers seeking to create memorable experiences should consider how to make success feel earned through the possibility of meaningful failure.

Studying successful games is essential to creating them. Kaplan's admission that he and Keller would study Call of Duty maps like Crash and Terminal reveals a professional approach to game design that treats existing successes as educational materials. This practice of analytical play—experiencing not just what works but asking why it works—represents a mindset that separates professional developers from casual players. Anyone interested in creating interactive experiences can benefit from this approach.

Map design is an overlooked art form. The detailed attention Kaplan pays to multiplayer map design highlights how virtual spaces shape player behavior and experience. Good maps create natural flow, generate meaningful encounters, and remain memorable long after players have moved on. This aspect of game development receives less attention than character design or story, yet it may be equally important to multiplayer success.

Different games serve different needs. The conversation reveals that no single FPS represents the "perfect" game—Quake, Rust, and Call of Duty each excel at different things. Some players seek the pure mechanical competition of Quake, others want the high-stakes survival tension of Rust, and still others appreciate the polished production values and accessible excellence of Call of Duty. Understanding what a game is trying to achieve helps both designers and players evaluate it on its own terms rather than against impossible universal standards.

Who This Is For

This conversation holds particular value for several distinct audiences. Aspiring game designers will find Kaplan's analytical approach to studying successful games particularly instructive, as it demonstrates how professional developers transform play experience into transferable knowledge. Competitive gamers interested in understanding why certain titles have endured will appreciate the deeper look at what makes Quake and Call of Duty successful at a mechanical level. Fans of Overwatch or Blizzard games more broadly will gain insight into the design philosophy that shaped one of gaming's most successful multiplayer franchises.

More broadly, anyone curious about what makes interactive entertainment succeed as a medium—whether as players seeking to understand why they enjoy certain games or as creators looking to understand the principles underlying player satisfaction—will find this conversation illuminating. Kaplan's combination of practical experience and analytical thinking provides a window into game design as both art and craft, revealing the deliberate choices that lie beneath experiences that often feel effortless to the player. The conversation ultimately suggests that great games are not accidents but the product of careful study, clear design intentions, and deep understanding of what makes interactive entertainment uniquely compelling.
