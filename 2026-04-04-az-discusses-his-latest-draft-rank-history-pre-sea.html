Az Discusses His Latest Draft & Rank History | Pre Season | Fantasy Premier League Tips 2025/26

Introduction

As the 2025/26 Fantasy Premier League season approaches—now just over a week away—content creators across the FPL community are unveiling their latest drafts and sharing their thought processes with audiences hungry for strategic insight. Among them is Az, one of the founding voices behind FDL Blackbox, who has released a candid two-part video series examining both his current team selections and, more unusually, his entire performance history in the game. This level of transparency is rare in the FPL content space, where creators often highlight their successes while glossing over struggles. Az takes the opposite approach, laying bare three years of disappointing finishes while acknowledging the fair criticism he has received from the community.

The video arrives at a significant moment for Az personally. Having been labeled an "expert" by the FPL Show and Scout—marketing terms he himself would never use—Az has faced increasing scrutiny over recent seasons as his ranks have tumbled from consistent top performers to finishes that left him embarrassed. With his wife Yelena having beaten him three years running, and facing relentless negativity on platforms like Twitter, Az uses this video not as a defensive retort but as an honest accounting of where he stands, what has gone wrong, and what changes he intends to make. The discussion serves as both a team reveal for his 2025/26 draft and a broader meditation on how to approach FPL after years of declining results.

The Honest Assessment: Az's FPL Performance History

Az begins by addressing what he calls "the elephant in the room"—the substantial criticism he has received for his recent performances. Rather than deflect or become defensive, he walks viewers through his complete FPL history from 2007 to the present, presenting the data without spin. His first three seasons, from 2007 to 2009, represented a learning period where he achieved an average rank of approximately 186,000, a respectable starting point for someone just beginning their FPL journey. He describes himself during this period as a "veteran" at best and more accurately an "enthusiast"—terms he prefers to "expert."

The years 2010 through 2012 marked a significant turning point. Az achieved his first top 5,000 finish with a rank of 3,800 in 2010/11, followed by a 175,000 finish the next season and then a 10,600 finish in 2011/12. He recalls that latter season with particular vividness because he finished just one point outside the top 10,000—a agonizing near-miss that left him determined to return and crack that threshold. The period from 2013 to 2015 proved to be his most consistent stretch, with an average rank of approximately 20,000 across those three seasons. He earned another top 5,000 finish during this run, including a remarkable 2,000th place finish in 2014, though he admits he doesn't remember that season particularly well.

From 2016 to 2018, Az believes he hit his peak. This coincided with his joining the Scout Cast, coming on toward the end of the 2016/17 season and finishing around 59,000. The following season brought his best-ever finish of 800th place, an achievement he describes with obvious pride. He managed to follow that with a 62,000 finish the year after, giving him an average rank of approximately 40,000 across those three seasons. By his own assessment, he was "quite high up" in the hall of fame tables during this period, though he never broke into the top 10 overall.

The 2019/20 to 2021/22 period saw more mixed results. Az started doing Blackbox in 2020, and that season was "pretty lousy" with a 135,000 finish, though he remembers being much lower in the ranks at certain points before managing a recovery. The 2021/22 season was notably strong, contributing to a three-year average rank of 52,000 across those seasons. He notes that across that four-season block, his three-year average was strong, with his lowest individual year being around 63,000.

Then came what Az describes without hesitation as "a complete disaster." The last three years have seen ranks of 143,000, 445,000, and 265,000. He is particularly harsh in his assessment of the 2023/24 season, calling the 445,000 finish "embarrassing" and "really bad." He explains the context: he had been sitting around 200,000 to 250,000 in rank and faced a choice between settling for a mediocre finish or pushing aggressively for top 100,000. He chose to push, ignoring players like Erling Haaland at home against Wolves and captained Cole Palmer, while also selecting Cristian Romero over Pedro Porro at Tottenham. He made multiple "tilt decisions" hoping some would pay off, but "absolutely none of them paid off," resulting in a catastrophic slide down the rankings. He acknowledges that his final position would look significantly better if he had simply coasted to a 150,000-200,000 finish instead of chasing a ambitious target that never materialized.

The Expert Question and Online Criticism

Az addresses directly the controversy of being called an "expert" by the FPL Show and Scout despite his recent poor results. He explains that "expert" is a marketing term used by these organizations, not a label he has ever applied to himself. "Never something I would ever call myself," he states plainly. When viewers have asked whether he can request they stop using the term, he acknowledges he has no control over how the Premier League chooses to market him. He frames the situation pragmatically: either he accepts being called an expert reluctantly while continuing to do work he loves for the Premier League and Scout, or he walks away from those opportunities entirely. He chooses to continue.

The online criticism has been substantial. Az describes being blocked by people on Twitter and receiving constant negativity, including being called "two cents"—suggesting his advice is worth very little. Rather than engaging extensively with this, he expresses a philosophical detachment. He has a full-time job outside of FPL content creation and does this work purely for enjoyment. His passion for the game dates back to 2007/08, and he loves creating content and working with the Blackbox team alongside Mark and newer collaborators Andy and Luke. As long as he continues to enjoy it, he has no plans to stop, though he acknowledges he would step away if the negativity became overwhelming.

Diagnosis: Why the Decline?

Az offers a thoughtful analysis of why his results have deteriorated so dramatically over the past three years. The primary issue, in his view, has been a stubbornness toward data-driven decision-making. He describes himself as someone who has historically relied heavily on watching football and using his own observations to inform picks. In the early years of his FPL career, this approach was more viable because fewer people were using advanced models and analytical tools, and there were fewer resources available to the average player. The playing field was more level.

However, the FPL landscape has transformed dramatically. The models and data tools now available are "so good at synthesizing that data," and Az acknowledges that he has resisted incorporating these resources fully into his decision-making process. He and Mark on Blackbox have "pushed too much against" the analytical approach in recent years, trying to fly the flag for traditional, gut-instinct-based FPL management. The results speak for themselves: neither has performed well. Az specifically cites his slow reactions to elite picks like Mohamed Salah, whom he ignored for too long last season, as evidence of this problem. He has been "too slow to react" to the best assets and has "completely ignored things like" data-driven review processes.

He recognizes the irony in his position. He has tried to maintain the approach that served him well in earlier years—watching matches, making personal assessments—but the game has evolved to a point where that alone is insufficient. The models catch things the human eye misses, and ignoring them puts a manager at a structural disadvantage. His takeaway is not that intuition has no value, but that it must work in conjunction with analytical tools rather than in opposition to them.

Looking Forward: The New Approach

While the video focuses primarily on reflection rather than detailed 2025/26 strategy, Az hints at a shift in his approach for the coming season. His acknowledgment that models and data need to be "incorporated a little bit" suggests a more balanced methodology going forward. The exact details of his current draft—including the specific fixtures he is targeting and the reasoning behind his player selections—will be explored further in the video, but the philosophical groundwork has been laid.

Az's stated goal for the new season is characteristically personal: he cannot lose to Yelena for a fourth consecutive year. The competitive element with his wife adds a human dimension to what is ultimately a hobby he deeply enjoys. Whether he can return to the form that produced top 5,000 and even top 1,000 finishes remains to be seen, but his willingness to examine his failures honestly suggests at minimum a renewed commitment to improvement.

Key Takeaways

1. Historical Performance Provides Context but Not Prediction

Az's journey illustrates that FPL success is not linear and that even experienced managers can experience prolonged slumps. His three-year blocks of performance—from consistent top finishes to recent struggles—demonstrate that the game evolves, strategies that worked in one era may fail in another, and ego can blind a manager to necessary adjustments. Understanding your own historical patterns helps identify when you are deviating from what made you successful.

2. The Relationship Between Data and Intuition Must Evolve

Rather than viewing analytical models and personal football knowledge as opposing forces, top FPL managers increasingly integrate both. Az's explicit acknowledgment that he has "pushed too much against" data-driven approaches—and his admission that neither he nor Mark performed well while doing so—provides a cautionary tale about ideological stubbornness. The most effective approach combines the pattern recognition of models with the contextual understanding that comes from watching matches.

3. Transparency and Community Engagement Matter

Az's decision to address criticism head-on, present his full performance history, and explain his thought processes represents a model for content creator engagement. Rather than hiding poor results or becoming defensive, he uses them as teaching moments. This approach builds trust with an audience that can smell defensiveness from a distance, and it models the kind of honest self-assessment that FPL managers should apply to their own teams.

4. Pressure and Tilt Decisions Compound Problems

Az's 2023/24 season serves as a masterclass in how making decisions under pressure can multiply losses. When he was sitting at 200,000-250,000 in rank, he had a reasonable finish within reach. By choosing to "push" for top 100,000 with aggressive, non-standard selections, he turned a mediocre season into a disastrous one. This pattern—forcing things hoping they would work rather than making sound decisions—replicates itself across many struggling FPL managers' seasons and represents one of the hardest behavioral patterns to break.

5. Content Creation Is a Long Game

Az's perspective on his online presence—he does this for fun, has a full-time job, and plans to continue as long as he enjoys it—offers a healthy counterweight to the intense pressure many FPL content creators face. The negativity on platforms like Twitter can feel overwhelming, but Az's refusal to be deterred suggests that sustainable engagement requires thick skin and genuine passion. His willingness to be vulnerable about failures, rather than curating only successes, ultimately strengthens his credibility.

Who This Content Is For

This video and its insights will be most valuable for FPL managers who are themselves struggling to understand why their results have not matched their expectations, as well as those who rely on content creator advice and want to understand the limitations and perspectives of the people providing it. The honest performance history is particularly useful for newer FPL players who may not understand the historical context of creators like Az, giving them a baseline against which to evaluate the advice they receive.

More broadly, the discussion about balancing data with intuition speaks to anyone who plays fantasy sports or engages in prediction markets where behavioral biases often override sound analysis. Az's specific example of how pressure led to tilt decisions in 2023/24 is a lesson applicable far beyond FPL—any competitive activity where emotional decision-making under pressure leads to suboptimal outcomes can benefit from this kind of reflection.

Finally, for fans of the FDL Blackbox channel or the FPL content ecosystem more generally, this video provides essential context for understanding Az's current positioning and philosophical approach. Knowing that he has explicitly acknowledged his recent failures and identified specific areas for improvement—particularly around data integration—gives viewers a framework for understanding his future recommendations and assessing them appropriately.
