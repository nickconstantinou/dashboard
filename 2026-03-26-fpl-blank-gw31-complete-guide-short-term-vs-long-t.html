Chip Strategy: Two Paths Forward

The video's strategic core revolves around two primary chip deployment options, each with distinct advantages depending on how the FA Cup quarterfinal between Liverpool and Manchester City concludes. Understanding these pathways requires managers to think several weeks ahead while maintaining enough flexibility to pivot if the expected scenarios don't materialize.

Option One: Wildcard 32, Bench Boost 33, Free Hit 34 becomes the preferred strategy if Liverpool defeat Manchester City in the FA Cup. This pathway assumes that Manchester City will have additional fixtures crammed into later gameweeks due to their European exit, creating enhanced double gameweek opportunities that justify the bench boost deployment in gameweek 33. The free hit in gameweek 34 then allows managers to construct a specialized squad for what appears likely to be a blank gameweek while preserving their wildcard for maximum flexibility.

Option Two: Free Hit 33, Wildcard 35, Bench Boost 36 emerges as the alternative if Manchester City defeat Liverpool in the FA Cup. Under this scenario, the double gameweeks shift later in the season, pushing the optimal bench boost opportunity to gameweek 36. This option requires more patience but potentially offers greater rewards if the fixture congestion materializes as predicted.

Harry is careful to note that these aren't rigid prescriptions—managers who believe Option One is superior might still employ it even if Manchester City wins the FA Cup, and vice versa. The FA Cup result serves as a major data point rather than a deterministic factor. However, the underlying logic remains compelling: Manchester City's continued European involvement (if they beat Liverpool) or their sudden fixture congestion (if they lose and other teams advance) fundamentally reshapes when double gameweeks will concentrate.

The Critical Timing Rule: Wait for FA Cup Results

One of the video's most emphatic directives concerns transfer timing. Harry pleads with managers—multiple times and with visible intensity—to avoid making early wildcard transfers until after the FA Cup concludes. This guidance isn't casual advice; it represents strategic necessity born from watching countless managers squander advantages through impatience.

The timeline explains why patience matters so acutely. Gameweek 31's deadline passes, then an international break intervenes, followed by FA Cup fixtures, and only then does gameweek 32 arrive approximately three weeks later. In that interim period between the FA Cup and gameweek 32's deadline, fixture announcements regarding doubles and blanks will finally be confirmed. Managers who transfer early based on predictions rather than confirmed fixtures risk discovering that their carefully constructed squad doesn't align with the actual fixture list.

Consider the practical implications. Teams like Manchester City and West Ham might have postponed fixtures rescheduled into unexpected gameweeks depending on their European results. Leeds versus West Ham represents one of the genuinely uncertain fixtures that could dramatically reshape the blank and double landscape. Without knowing exactly where these games land, committing to specific players or formations based on predicted fixtures represents unnecessary risk.

Harry acknowledges that waiting might feel uncomfortable—it means potentially missing out on value rises and accepting that other managers might surge ahead temporarily. However, the relative gains from early transfers typically amount to fractions of a point in value, while strategic missteps from incorrect fixture predictions could cost several transfers and significant rank positions.

The most successful managers maintain psychological flexibility, updating their beliefs as new evidence arrives rather than doubling down on initial predictions that might prove incorrect.
