# The Season So Far: A Comprehensive FPL International Break Review

## Introduction

The latest episode of *Who Got the Assist?*, recorded on Tuesday, March 24th, represents something of a departure from the show's typical format. With the Fantasy Premier League world momentarily paused due to the international break—granting managers approximately three weeks away from the pressure of weekly deadlines—hosts Sam and Nick have opted for what they describe as a "zoomed out" conversation rather than the usual structured episode. This stylistic shift proves timely, allowing for a broader reflection on the season's narrative arc rather than the granular, gameweek-by-gameweek analysis that typically defines FPL content.

Making his debut appearance on the show (remarkably, despite having appeared as both a guest and guest host on competing FPL podcasts including *Planet FPL* and *The Always Cheating*), longtime community figure Tom Campbell joins the conversation. His presence brings both credibility and a unique perspective, as Campbell has navigated this season with a deliberately unconventional approach. The episode covers several interconnected themes: Campbell's own tumultuous FPL journey this campaign, the emerging candidates for the prestigious "200 club," Mohamed Salah's impending departure from the Premier League and its implications for FPL managers, some striking revelations about the season's points distribution, and a preliminary discussion of wildcard strategies for the return to competitive play.

What emerges is less a tactical guide and more a philosophical meditation on how to approach FPL when the traditional methods fail to deliver satisfaction. The hosts acknowledge the absurdity of discussing yet another international break—they've done several of these conversations before—yet this one carries particular weight given the transformative events that have shaped the 2024-25 season thus far.

## Tom Campbell's Deliberate Departure from Convention

The conversation opens with Campbell offering a candid assessment of his own season, describing it as "relatively catastrophic" before immediately pivoting to acknowledge that appearances can be deceiving. He explains that his fortunes on the official FPL ladder actually coincided with his hosting duties on James and Sutch's *Planet FPL* podcast this season—his lowest rank of the campaign arrived precisely when he was appearing on their show, though his subsequent appearances have corresponded with an upturn in results. Campbell finds himself currently residing in what he self-deprecatingly describes as "the top 500k," a position he concedes may be generous in describing as "respectable" given his standing, though he maintains he's found some form of stability in recent weeks.

The root of Campbell's erratic season lies in a conscious decision made before the campaign's commencement. He reveals that he "hamstrung himself pretty severely quite deliberately," explaining that he had almost chosen not to play at all this season due to what he terms "FPL fatigue"—a pervasive sense of ambivalence that had characterized his experience throughout the previous campaign. When friends eventually convinced him to participate, Campbell resolved to approach the game differently, adopting what he describes as a strategy of taking "huge swings and taking huge swings for the fences."

Central to this philosophy was a self-imposed restriction: from gameweek one, Campbell decided he would not select Erling Haaland under any circumstances. This decision proved particularly painful during the Norwegian's blistering start to the season, when Haaland looked every bit the unstoppable force that had defined the previous campaign. Campbell admits it was "pretty miserable" to actively resist the obvious choice, though Haaland's subsequent decline has coincided with an improvement in Campbell's overall standing. Salah represented another pillar of Campbell's contrarian approach, though he notes that the Egyptian's underwhelming season by his extraordinary standards likely hasn't proved as significant an impediment as the Haaland decision.

Campbell describes his gameweek 31 approach—where he deployed his Free Hit chip—as emblematic of this philosophy. Rather than chasing the consensus, he constructed a team featuring a treble Fulham defense, including a captaincy on a Fulham defender. He also deliberately omitted two Tottenham players. When Nick questions whether "anyone in their right mind would do" this, Campbell's laughter suggests he's well aware of the unconventional nature of his approach, though he maintains it reflects "a sense of fun" and the possibility of achieving something spectacular. The hosts draw an analogy to FPL fatigue driving managers toward "side quests," and Campbell agrees, suggesting that after years of following the optimal path, there's appeal in taking "the scenic route."

When pressed on chip usage, Campbell reveals he's likely "burnt them all at this point"—an admission that illustrates just how far he's departed from conventional wisdom. However, he insists his decisions haven't been without research or thought, even if the execution appears reckless. He believes his choices remain "within the realms of some sensibility," even if the evidence on screen might suggest otherwise.

## The 200 Club: Chasing Elite Territory

The conversation then shifts to what the hosts call "the 200 club"—players who appear capable of accumulating 200 or more points by season's end. This distinction carries significant weight in FPL circles, as reaching such a threshold requires sustained excellence across the majority of a campaign. The hosts note that no player has yet achieved this milestone, but several candidates are emerging as genuine possibilities as the season enters its decisive phase.

The discussion implicitly centers on Salah, who has been a perennial member of this exclusive club in recent seasons. His extraordinary consistency—delivering attacking returns week after week while accumulating both goals and assists at remarkable rates—has made him an essential component of virtually every successful FPL strategy over the past few seasons.

## The Salah Goodbye: A Defining Era Approaches Its End

Perhaps the episode's most emotionally resonant segment arrives when the hosts address the news that broke literally moments before recording began: Mohamed Salah has announced his departure from the Premier League at season's end. While Salah hasn't explicitly named his destination, the expectation is that he'll pursue a lucrative opportunity in Saudi Arabia, following in the footsteps of other high-profile departures like Jordan Henderson and Steven Gerrard.

The hosts grapple with the magnitude of this news, recognizing that Salah has represented the bedrock of FPL strategy for multiple seasons. His combination of penalty-taking duties, set-piece responsibility, and consistent goal-scoring threat made him not merely a good option but effectively a mandatory selection. Managers who avoided Salah invariably found themselves at a significant disadvantage, while those who invested their precious budget in the Egyptian immediately gained a foundation for competitive squads.

The conversation touches on what Salah's departure means for the game's meta going forward. With the league's most reliable premium asset leaving, the strategic landscape shifts dramatically. The hosts speculate about who might emerge to fill that void—whether another established star will ascend to similar importance or whether the premium asset landscape will become more democratized.

## Data Revelations: The Season's Distributional Truth

Tom Campbell reveals he's compiled extensive data on the season's points distribution, describing it as containing "some quite shocking realizations" that will prompt listeners to reconsider their assumptions about the campaign. While the full data dump occurs later in the episode, the hosts preview that Campbell's findings challenge conventional narratives about how points are distributed across the FPL manager population.

The implications of such data extend beyond mere curiosity. Understanding the distribution of points—whether most managers cluster around certain totals or whether significant stratification exists—can inform strategic decisions about risk tolerance and template-following versus differentiation.

## Wildcard Considerations: Preparing for Return

The episode concludes with preliminary discussion of wildcard strategies for the imminent return to competitive FPL. With the international break providing breathing room, managers can contemplate their next moves with greater clarity. The hosts acknowledge that many managers will be working with limited information—injury statuses, fixture changes, and form fluctuations remain uncertain during the break—yet the opportunity to plan cannot be squandered.

The conversation hints at the complexity awaiting managers upon their return. With several chips potentially still available and the season entering its decisive phase, wildcard decisions carry enormous weight. The hosts suggest that the data Campbell has compiled, combined with the evolving narrative around key players, should inform these decisions—but they stop short of offering definitive tactical recommendations, preferring instead to let listeners draw their own conclusions.

## Key Takeaways

**Strategic Burnout Requires Deliberate Response**: Campbell's experience demonstrates that FPL fatigue is a genuine phenomenon that can undermine otherwise capable managers. His response—not merely to continue playing but to fundamentally reimagine his approach—represents one viable path forward. When traditional optimization fails to generate satisfaction, deliberately embracing suboptimal strategies can restore the sense of discovery and risk that made the game enjoyable initially.

**Contrarianism Carries Opportunity Costs**: While Campbell's philosophy of "taking huge swings" provides entertainment value and occasionally delivers spectacular results, his honest admission of a "relatively catastrophic" season illustrates the mathematical reality of FPL. Each decision to deviate from expected value represents a potential points sacrifice. The key insight isn't that contrarianism is wrong, but that managers should be conscious of their motivations—whether they're making unusual choices for strategic advantage or simply for entertainment.

**The Post-Salah Landscape Remains Undefined**: With Salah's departure confirmed, FPL managers face an unprecedented situation. The premium asset hierarchy will need reconstruction, and managers who have relied on Salah as their cornerstone must fundamentally reassess their squad construction. This uncertainty represents both risk and opportunity—those who correctly identify Salah's successor as the season's essential asset will gain significant advantage.

**Data-Driven Self-Awareness Matters**: Campbell's compilation of points distribution data, while not detailed in this episode, underscores the value of understanding broader FPL trends. Rather than operating based on intuition or consensus assumption, successful managers increasingly rely on empirical evidence about how points actually distribute across the manager population.

**The International Break Serves Strategic Purpose**: Despite the hosts' playful acknowledgment that they should be "totally logged out of the game," the break actually provides valuable planning time. Decisions made during the break—about chip deployment, transfer strategy, and portfolio construction—can provide crucial advantages when competitive play resumes.
