    Setting the Stage: Two Podcasters Geeking Out

    In what feels like a conversation between old friends who stumble upon a shared obsession, Dan Carlin welcomes Daniele Bellelli—university lecturer, author of four books, and host of the History on Fire podcast—for a freewheeling discussion about archaeology organized through an Ebenezer Scrooge framework: the ghost of archaeology past, the ghost of archaeology present, and the ghost of archaeology future. The metaphor works better as a loose organizing principle than a rigid structure, but it gives the conversation room to wander through time, geography, and culture while maintaining direction.

    The episode opens with Carlin's characteristic self-deprecating humor about their mutual qualifications—or lack thereof. Neither host is a trained archaeologist, though Bellelli's anthropology background gives him somewhat more credibility than Carlin's generalist's curiosity. What's immediately apparent is that both share an genuine fascination with how humans throughout history have engaged with their own ancient past, and that fascination becomes contagious as the episode unfolds.

    What makes this conversation particularly valuable is its scope. Rather than focusing narrowly on modern archaeological methodology, the discussion traces humanity's relationship with its own history from ancient civilizations up through the Renaissance and into the 19th century's "Indiana Jones era" of treasure hunting masquerading as scholarship. The journey reveals uncomfortable truths about why we preserve the past, who benefits from its interpretation, and how often the impulse to recover history has been inseparable from the impulse to possess it.

    The Ghost of Archaeology Past: Ancient Peoples and Their Own Antiquity

    Religious Preservation and Its Limits

    One of the episode's most fascinating threads examines how and why ancient peoples preserved— or failed to preserve—sites from their own past. The pattern that emerges is striking: preservation typically required ongoing religious or political viability. When ancient Egyptian temples and tomb sites remained connected to living worship practices, they were maintained, refurbished, and sometimes even restored after antiquity robberies. The religion kept the site relevant.

    But this preservation had a shelf life. Once belief systems changed, those same sites often became relics to be ignored or actively shunned. Carlin cites a particularly illuminating example: after Rome converted to Christianity, some ancient polytheistic temples weren't merely abandoned. They were avoided altogether—not because Christians doubted the previous gods' existence, but because they believed those deities were actually demons in disguise. Engaging with such sites risked demonic contamination.

    This pattern repeats across cultures and centuries. The Coliseum in Rome survived demolition for building materials during the medieval period specifically because early Christians believed Christian martyrs had been killed there. The arena became holy ground consecrated by blood, and therefore untouchable—preserved not for its classical architecture or engineering significance, but for its perceived spiritual importance. Only later, when Renaissance thinkers rediscovered Roman traditions, did the Coliseum acquire its modern identity as a historical monument rather than a religious one.

    Invented Lineages and Political Legitimacy

    The conversation turns to what might generously be called "creative historiography" and less generously be called outright fabrication. The pattern Carlin and Bellelli identify is nearly universal: rulers throughout history have sought connections to glorious pasts, often inventing those connections when actual lineage was unavailable.

    Consider Nabonidus, the Neo-Babylonian king from around 550 BCE. This monarch reportedly showed genuine interest in the Akkadian Empire—specifically the great King Naram-Sin, who ruled more than a millennium before Nabonidus. The question of whether this interest was purely scholarly or partly political illustrates a fundamental tension: if you can claim descent or connection to one of history's first great empire builders, your own credentials to rule receive an enormous boost. "Sargon," Carlin notes, means "true king"—so connecting yourself to that lineage carries profound implications for your own legitimacy.

    Bellelli offers the Mesoamerican parallel. Long after Teotihuacan declined into memory, nearly every subsequent Mesoamerican state attempted to establish connections to it—whether those connections existed historically or not. The psychological and political utility of claiming descent from something universally respected outweighed any concern for accuracy. In many cases, these invented genealogies eventually trace backward through dozens of generations until they reach some deity, establishing the ruler not merely as a political successor but as something approaching divine.

    What makes this pattern historically valuable despite its inaccuracy? Bellelli suggests these "lies" reveal significant truths about the cultures that told them. What does it say about a society that its rulers felt compelled to fabricate such connections? What does it reveal about human psychology and the political necessity of appearing legitimate? Even deliberately false claims to the past illuminate the values, fears, and aspirations of the people making them.

    The Question of Genuine Curiosity

    Against this backdrop of political utility, the hosts investigate whether anyone in the ancient world genuinely studied the past for its own sake. Ramesses II, often called the first Egyptologist for his apparent restoration of ancient monuments, serves as a complicated case study. Did this powerful pharaoh restore the pyramids of his predecessors out of philosophical appreciation for ancient wisdom, or because associating himself with those great builders enhanced his own prestige? The answer is probably "both," but the political benefits are hard to ignore.

    Carlin notes a famous medieval Indian scholar named Kalhana who may have written something approaching a genuine history book—focused on reconstructing what actually happened rather than what would be politically useful. Kalhana's work represents a rare example of something approaching modern historiographical instincts in the pre-modern world.

    The broader pattern, however, suggests that pure curiosity was the exception rather than the rule. When ancient Chinese scholars studied their past, the implicit question was almost always "what can we learn that we can use now?" Artifacts represented hidden knowledge with practical applications, not windows into vanished ways of life. The concept of studying the past purely to understand it—without ulterior political, religious, or practical motivation—wouldn't truly emerge until much later.

    The Renaissance and the European Rediscovery

    The Classical Revival and Its Implications

    The episode's discussion of the Renaissance represents a crucial transition point in how Europeans engaged with their own ancient history. The rediscovery of classical Greek and Roman texts, philosophy, and art didn't just enrich European culture—it fundamentally transformed how people understood the relationship between past and present.

    When Aristotle, Plato, and other classical thinkers were rediscovered and translated, their ideas infused Renaissance science and philosophy with ancient classical values. But this intellectual movement had a material dimension too: people became intensely interested in the physical artifacts of Greece and Rome. Where could genuine antiquities be found? What had survived? What could be acquired?

    Carlin draws a direct line between this renewed interest and the development of what we might call the "treasure hunting" mentality toward the past. The question wasn't primarily "what did this civilization look like?" or "how did these people live?" The immediate question was "where is the shiny object?" The drive toward understanding the past was inseparable from the drive to possess its remnants.

    Napoleon and the Egyptian Campaign

    The Napoleonic invasion of Egypt in 1798 provides a perfect case study in how political competition shaped early archaeology. When Napoleon addressed his troops before the Battle of the Pyramids, his famous declaration that "40 centuries of history are looking down on you" captured the moment's significance. Here was a European power encountering one of humanity's most ancient civilizations firsthand.

    What made the Egyptian campaign archaeologically significant was that Napoleon brought scientists with his army. While soldiers fought and conquered, scholars examined ruins, documented inscriptions, and began the systematic study of ancient Egypt. The campaign sparked a European obsession with Egyptian artifacts that persists to this day.

    The irony, Carlin notes, is that European competition may have actually protected Egyptian antiquities from complete destruction. France and Britain were both vying for Egyptian artifacts, and neither wanted the other to succeed. When the British under Horatio Nelson destroyed the French fleet at the Battle of the Nile, the scramble continued on land. Both powers found themselves seeking permits and official approval from the Turkish Ottoman authorities who technically controlled Egypt—not because either power respected Ottoman sovereignty, but because valid permits could be used to legally block the rival European power from excavating or removing artifacts.

    Without that rivalry, the episode suggests, the destruction might have been even more thorough. Two European powers seeking to outmaneuver each other created bureaucratic constraints that, however accidentally, preserved more of Egypt's heritage than either power would have permitted alone.

    The Personal Collections and Public Museums

    The era's attitudes toward antiquities reflected assumptions that would be scandalous today. Wealthy Europeans commissioned agents to travel the world collecting artifacts—not just for museums, but for private estates. Statues, obelisks, and architectural elements ended up as "backyard pieces" in English gardens. The concept of cultural heritage as belonging to its place of origin simply didn't exist in the same way.

    This arrangement benefited the collectors and the emerging European museum system while depleting the source countries. It also created the collections upon which many early archaeological institutions were built—collections whose provenance was often deeply problematic and whose acquisition was frequently enabled by imperial power differentials rather than ethical excavation.

    Giovanni Belzoni: The First Archaeologist as Tomb Robber

    The Circus Strongman Who Rediscovered Egypt

    The episode's most colorful character study centers on Giovanni Battista Belzoni, a former circus strongman who became one of the most famous early explorers of Egyptian antiquities. Standing six feet seven inches tall and possessed of enormous physical strength, Belzoni literally didn't fit into many of the passages he attempted to explore—a limitation he documented with some frustration in his writings.

    Belzoni was responsible for rediscovering numerous tombs in the Valley of the Kings that had been inaccessible or unknown for millennia. The word "rediscovering" is intentional here: these sites had been found and used in antiquity, then lost to time. Belzoni's contemporaries often compared him favorably to ancient tomb robbers, a comparison he seems to have accepted with some pride.

    One of his more endearing quirks was his insistence on commemorating his discoveries by carving his name graffiti-style into tomb walls. These inscriptions survive to this day, functioning as a kind of 19th-century "Kilroy was here" across Egyptian archaeological sites. Carlin notes this with characteristic dark humor: "That's your father of archaeology in a way."

    The Business of Robbing the Dead

    The title of this episode—"Dig This!"—takes on darker meaning in Belzoni's context. Bellelli and Carlin read directly from Belzoni's own accounts of his excavation philosophy. The historian William H. Stiebing Jr., in his book *Uncovering the History of Archaeology*, provides crucial context: "Throughout his explorations, Belzoni continually looked for portable antiquities and papyri that he could sell to the consul general or other European collectors. He openly admitted that his purpose in entering ancient tombs was to 'rob the Egyptians of their papyri.'"

    The quotation marks around that phrase indicate this is Belzoni's own characterization of his work. He wasn't embarrassed by what he was doing. He wasn't disguising commercial motives behind scholarly language. He was in Egypt to extract valuable artifacts and sell them to wealthy European collectors.

    The destruction this approach caused was immense. Ancient tombs were opened, often for the first time in millennia, and the contents were assessed purely in terms of what could be transported and sold. Papyrus documents—the most fragile and valuable records of ancient Egyptian civilization—were particularly vulnerable. Rather than careful excavation with documentation, Belzoni and his contemporaries often simply removed everything portable and moved on.

    Carlin reads from Belzoni's own descriptions of exploring dark, unventilated tomb passages—accounts that convey the physical danger and discomfort of these expeditions while simultaneously revealing how different this work was from modern archaeology. Where contemporary archaeologists might spend years documenting a single site, Belzoni was breaking in, extracting what had value, and moving on to the next discovery.

    Why It Matters: Separating Discovery from Understanding

    The contrast between Belzoni-era archaeology and modern practice illuminates a fundamental tension in how humans have approached the past. Discovery and understanding are not the same thing. Belzoni "discovered" sites that had been lost for millennia, but his methods prevented the kind of careful documentation that would allow future scholars to reconstruct the context of those discoveries.

    What was the relationship between the artifacts in a tomb? What could the positioning of objects tell us about Egyptian beliefs, practices, or social organization? These questions were largely unasked in the early era of collecting, because the immediate economic value of portable artifacts overshadowed any scholarly interest in what those artifacts might reveal.

    This doesn't mean Belzoni and his contemporaries deserve only condemnation. They operated within frameworks of their time, and many genuinely believed they were preserving and protecting artifacts that might otherwise be lost to local populations who had little interest in ancient monuments. But acknowledging the limitations of their approach helps us appreciate how much archaeological methodology has evolved—and how much more there is to learn from sites that either weren't completely excavated or were excavated more carefully.

    The Value of Understanding How We Understand the Past

    Why This History of Archaeology Matters

    The episode's ultimate value lies not in any specific archaeological fact but in its illumination of human psychology and motivation. Why do we preserve the past? Why do we destroy it? Why do we study what came before us? The answers, it turns out, are rarely purely intellectual.

    Political legitimacy has driven countless acts of preservation and restoration. Religious conviction has both protected and endangered ancient sites. Economic interest has motivated both careful scholarship and barbaric looting. National pride and ethnic identity have shaped which pasts get emphasized and which get ignored.

    Understanding this history doesn't diminish archaeology as a discipline— it humanizes it. Every ancient site we study today exists in a palimpsest of previous interpretations, previous interventions, previous acts of preservation and destruction. Recognizing that pattern helps us interpret the evidence we find more thoughtfully.

    The Continuing Evolution

    The episode's Scrooge framework—past, present, future—invites us to consider where archaeology is going. Contemporary archaeology increasingly grapples with questions of who owns the past, who should control access to sites, and how indigenous and local communities should be involved in studying their own heritage. These debates reflect continuing evolution in how humans understand their relationship to what came before.

    The ghost of archaeology present might involve questions about repatriation of artifacts, community archaeology, and digital preservation technologies. The ghost of archaeology future might consider how climate change threatens coastal sites, how artificial intelligence transforms how we analyze finds, and how changing demographics in the discipline itself reshape what questions get asked.
