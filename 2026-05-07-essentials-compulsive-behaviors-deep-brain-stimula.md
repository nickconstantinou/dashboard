## Introduction

Neurosurgery represents one of medicine's most profound intersections of technology, anatomy, and human suffering. Within this field, deep brain stimulation (DBS) has emerged as a revolutionary approach for treating neurological and psychiatric conditions by implanting electrodes that deliver targeted electrical pulses to specific brain regions. In this episode of Huberman Lab Essentials, Stanford professor Dr. Andrew Huberman hosts Dr. Casey Halpern, Chief of Stereotactic Functional Neurosurgery at Penn Medicine, to explore how neurosurgeons conceptualize and interact with the human brain—and how electrical modulation of specific neural circuits might address conditions ranging from Parkinson's disease to obsessive compulsive disorder, addiction, and eating disorders.

Dr. Halpern's work sits at the boundary between established surgical practice and cutting-edge research. He performs DBS and focused ultrasound procedures, delivering what he describes as "a medication in the form of electricity" into very small regions of the brain. Yet his deeper motivation extends beyond treating movement disorders: he is actively investigating how reward circuits, particularly the nucleus accumbens, contribute to compulsive behaviors that devastate millions of lives. The conversation reveals why compulsive behavior—whether manifesting as OCD, addiction, or eating disorders—shares a fundamental neurological architecture, and why understanding this shared circuitry might unlock more effective treatments for the estimated 30% of OCD patients who fail conventional therapies.

## Key Concepts & Arguments

### The Scope of Neurosurgery and DBS Fundamentals

Dr. Halpern begins by clarifying the breadth of neurosurgery, which encompasses brain tumors, aneurysms, traumatic brain injury, spine surgeries, and peripheral nerve disorders—roughly 90% of what neurosurgeons do nationally involves spinal procedures like herniated disc repairs and lumbar fusions. As a functional neurosurgeon, he specializes specifically in deep brain stimulation and focused ultrasound ablation. The latter, recently FDA-approved for tremor, represents a non-invasive alternative to surgical ablation.

The DBS procedure itself involves implanting a very thin, insulated wire deep into a brain region involved in a particular condition—most commonly Parkinson's disease for motor symptoms. The wire's tip contains multiple small contacts that deliver electrical stimulation to surrounding tissue. Notably, the electrode placement is not itself the therapy; rather, the therapy is the electrical current flowing through those contacts into precisely targeted neural tissue. Dr. Halpern emphasizes that while the electrodes sit in a small region, areas within a few millimeters can be affected, potentially causing temporary side effects like brief laughter or panic if limbic circuits are stimulated. These side effects, however, can sometimes be therapeutic—and it was precisely these unexpected psychiatric improvements in Parkinson's patients that first hinted at broader applications for DBS beyond movement disorders.

### The Accidental Discovery Leading to DBS for OCD

Dr. Halpern recounts that many patients with Parkinson's disease also suffer from psychiatric comorbidities including depression and OCD. When neurosurgeons implanted DBS electrodes to address motor symptoms, patients sometimes reported that their gambling problems improved or their mood lifted. This phenomenon, initially puzzling, revealed something fundamental: brain circuits do not operate in isolation. Motor pathways interweave with limbic circuits involved in emotion, meaning electrical modulation of one system can affect another.

This convergence of circuits explains why stimulating certain brain regions can evoke laughter or alter mood—effects observed in clinical settings when neurosurgeons test electrode placements. Importantly, this understanding opened doors for developing DBS therapies for primary psychiatric conditions, not just motor disorders. The same technology originally designed to quiet tremor could potentially modulate obsessive thoughts and compulsive behaviors by targeting the neural circuits underlying these conditions.

### Understanding OCD as a Spectrum and the Urge Despite Risk

Dr. Halpern offers a nuanced perspective on obsessive compulsive disorder, distinguishing between clinically diagnosable OCD and the obsessive personality traits that can actually serve as assets. He notes that "famous CEOs" and professionals in high-stakes fields like surgery and science often possess some level of OCD traits that, when controlled, enhance performance. The disorder itself exists on a spectrum, with patients whose obsessions and compulsions may not fully meet diagnostic criteria occupying middle ground.

From his perspective as a neurosurgeon, Dr. Halpern conceptualizes OCD as involving dysfunction in both cortical and subcortical structures. The prefrontal and orbital frontal cortex show hyperfunctioning in OCD patients—these areas are overactive in ways that need normalization. These cortical regions send projections to subcortical structures called the basal ganglia, including the caudate, putamen (together forming the dorsal striatum), and the ventral striatum. This interconnected system is where Dr. Halpern focuses much of his research energy, particularly the nucleus accumbens within the ventral striatum.

The nucleus accumbens serves as a central hub for gating reward-seeking behavior. When this system is perturbed, compulsive behavior emerges: a rat will continue pursuing a reward despite receiving foot shocks, and an OCD patient will check their door thirty times until 3 AM without sleep because of an overwhelming urge. Dr. Halpern emphasizes that contamination behaviors (hand-washing for hours, obsessive toothbrush cleaning) and risk-taking despite consequences represent different expressions of the same underlying dysfunction. This "urge despite risk" also characterizes addiction—drug users pay dealers despite knowing the lethal dangers—and eating disorders where patients purge despite severe health risks.

### The Neural Mechanisms of Compulsion

The common denominator across OCD, addiction, and eating disorders, according to Dr. Halpern, is the pursuit of rewards despite risks. This shared feature suggests a common neural architecture involves the nucleus accumbens and the cortical areas projecting to it. He explains that repeated exposure to powerful rewards—whether drugs of abuse or other intense stimuli—can "hijack" normal nucleus accumbens functioning, creating habitual patterns that persist despite harmful consequences. The goal of targeted interventions is therefore to disrupt these habitual, recurring patterns through precise modulation of the affected circuits.

### Current Treatment Landscape for OCD

Dr. Halpern outlines the current therapeutic options for OCD, beginning with medications that target neurotransmitter systems. SSRIs (selective serotonin reuptake inhibitors) represent the first-line pharmacological treatment, though tricyclics can also be helpful. Both work primarily on the serotonin system, but this system interacts extensively with both norepinephrine and dopamine systems, making medication responses difficult to predict and often requiring trial-and-error approaches.

Psychotherapy, specifically exposure response prevention, represents the most effective non-pharmacological option and functions as a specialized form of cognitive behavioral therapy offered by trained psychologists. Dr. Halpern notes that Penn Medicine houses a clinic founded by Edna Foa dedicated to providing these cognitive therapies, which help patients gradually habituate to anxiety-triggering stimuli and reduce compulsive behaviors. Both pharmacological and psychotherapeutic approaches help many patients manage their symptoms effectively.

However, approximately 30% of OCD patients still suffer from the disorder despite these treatments, and among those, many experience severe symptoms. These treatment-resistant patients represent Dr. Halpern's primary motivation for pursuing surgical interventions. He emphasizes that while the available surgical options—DBS and ablation—are "worth pursuing," they are "not optimal," and he feels obligated as both a researcher and clinician to develop more effective therapies.

### Surgical Interventions: DBS and Ablation

When conventional treatments fail, two surgical options exist. Deep brain stimulation involves implanting electrodes that deliver electrical modulation to specific brain regions, with approximately 50% of patients achieving meaningful responses. However, Dr. Halpern stresses that even responders still have symptomatic OCD—the intervention reduces symptoms substantially but does not eliminate the disorder.

Capsulotomy represents the ablation approach, where neurosurgeons can heat and destroy small brain regions (typically 3-4 millimeters in diameter) rather than delivering ongoing electrical stimulation. Some in the field view the target region as "very safe to destroy" and essentially vestigial, like an appendix, while others argue that modulation without destruction represents a safer approach. Dr. Halpern has observed patients do very well following these ablations, suggesting the brain can compensate for such small, targeted destruction without obvious functional loss.

Despite their potential benefits, patients remain hesitant to undergo these procedures. The hesitation stems partly from the surgical risks inherent in any brain operation, but more significantly from knowing that the chance of a "transformative effect" is quite low. When patients understand that even DBS responders typically still have significant OCD symptoms, many choose not to proceed. This reality drives Dr. Halpern's research emphasis on developing more targeted, disease-specific, and symptom-specific therapeutic approaches.

### The Remarkable Plasticity and Precision of Brain Intervention

Dr. Halpern reflects that one of the most "amazing" aspects of brain intervention is sometimes the lack of effect. Neurosurgeons can traverse certain brain regions without causing observable adverse effects on patient function—at least within what testing can reveal. Conversely, destroying tiny regions (3-4mm) can produce significant therapeutic benefits with no obvious side effects after recovery. This duality demonstrates both the brain's remarkable capacity to compensate for small damages and the precision required to achieve therapeutic effects without harm.

### Research Approaches: From Parkinson's to Compulsion

Dr. Halpern describes his research strategy for understanding and treating compulsive behaviors. He leverages tools developed for Parkinson's surgery—in that procedure, when electrodes are placed in motor structures, neurosurgeons can "hear" tremor cells by converting electrical signals into audible tones. The frequency of these signals corresponds to the frequency of hand tremor, allowing precise identification of target regions. One by one, neurosurgeons advance a very fine wire, listening to electrical activity until they encounter cells firing at tremor frequencies, then stimulate or quiet them to test whether tremor resolves.

Recognizing that similar electrophysiological approaches could help understand reward and compulsion circuits, Dr. Halpern applies this methodology to investigate the nucleus accumbens and associated structures. His collaborative research involves both invasive recordings (using electrode-based surgery similar to epilepsy monitoring) and non-invasive imaging approaches, combined with genetic studies, to understand OCD at multiple levels. The goal is to identify where obsessions originate in the brain and develop better ways to interrupt them—improving upon current interventions that, while helpful, leave room for substantial therapeutic advancement.

## Detailed Takeaways

### 1. Compulsive behaviors across diagnoses share a neural pathway

OCD, addiction, and eating disorders all converge on the same fundamental problem: the nucleus accumbens fails to appropriately gate reward-seeking, leading to pursuit of rewards despite severe risks. This explains why a drug addict will obtain heroin despite knowing the lethal consequences, why an OCD patient will check their door thirty times at the expense of sleep, and why someone with binge eating disorder will continue eating despite medical danger. Recognizing this shared architecture suggests that treatments targeting this circuit might address multiple disorders, potentially offering new hope for patients whose conditions have historically been treated in isolation from one another.

### 2. Surgical interventions for OCD, while imperfect, represent a valuable option for treatment-resistant patients

With 50% responder rates for DBS and observable benefits from targeted ablations, these surgical options provide meaningful relief for patients who have exhausted pharmacological and psychotherapeutic approaches. Understanding that responders still have symptomatic OCD sets realistic expectations, but for severe cases where daily functioning is devastated, even partial improvement can be transformative. Patients considering these interventions should weigh the risks of surgery against the substantial ongoing suffering from treatment-resistant OCD.

### 3. The brain exhibits remarkable resilience and specificity

That neurosurgeons can traverse certain brain regions without causing detectable functional impairment, while simultaneously producing dramatic therapeutic effects through stimulation or ablation of tiny targets (3-4mm), speaks to the brain's extraordinary complexity and adaptability. This dual observation—that we can both safely pass through and strategically destroy small brain regions—provides philosophical insight into neural organization while also guiding surgical approaches that minimize harm while maximizing therapeutic benefit.

### 4. Medication and therapy are first-line but insufficient for a significant minority

SSRIs, tricyclics, and exposure response prevention therapy represent effective treatments for many OCD patients, but the approximately 30% who do not respond adequately represent a substantial population in need of alternative interventions. This underscores the importance of comprehensive treatment approaches that systematically try multiple options before considering surgical interventions, while also highlighting the ongoing need for research into more effective pharmacological and therapeutic agents.

### 5. Personal traits and clinical disorders exist on spectrums

Dr. Halpern's observation that obsessive-compulsive traits serve many successful professionals, including himself ("as a neurosurgeon, I'm really obsessive about safety and compulsive about my surgical procedures"), illustrates that the same cognitive features underlying OCD can enhance performance when contained within adaptive bounds. This perspective destigmatizes OCD while recognizing that when these traits become uncontrollable and interfere with daily functioning, they constitute a disorder requiring treatment. Understanding this spectrum may help individuals recognize when traits have crossed from adaptive to pathological.
