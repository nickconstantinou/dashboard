Why the Vikings Refused to Turn Back: The Psychology of Great Explorers

A Summary of Lars Brownworth's Lecture on the Unyielding Spirit of Norse Exploration

---

Key Insights

  • Vikings reached North America 500 years before Columbus, having sailed throughout the known world to England, Spain, Italy, Russia, North Africa, the Middle East, and Paris—all without navigational instruments like a compass
  • Naddod was the first Norseman to reach Iceland, though his arrival was entirely accidental, having been blown off course while attempting to sail west from Norway
  • Irish monks preceded Vikings to Iceland in simple skin canoes, motivated by a desire to escape civilization and draw nearer to God; when Vikings arrived, the monks "ran away" immediately
  • Brownworth identifies two distinct psychological drivers for exploration: the hardened raider driven by resilience and will, versus the spiritual seeker pursuing escape and solitude from the world
  • Tennyson's poem *Ulysses* captures the Viking ethos in its closing lines—"to strive, to seek, to find, and not to yield"—which Brownworth describes as "the flame that burns in all of us"

    • ---

    Introduction

    Lars Brownworth, a historian and author known for his accessible explorations of medieval history, delivers a compelling meditation on what drove the Vikings to become some of history's most remarkable explorers. In this lecture, Brownworth grapples with a question that seems almost incomprehensible by modern standards: what psychological engine powered a people to shove off from the fjords of Norway and sail into waters they could not navigate, toward lands they could not confirm existed, with death as their only certain destination?

    The timing of this discussion feels particularly resonant. In an age of GPS navigation, satellite imaging, and exhaustive geographic surveys, we have largely lost touch with what it meant to push into genuine uncertainty—to depart from shore knowing that you might never return, that the ocean might swallow you whole, that the very shape of the earth remained a mystery. Brownworth suggests that studying the Viking spirit offers us something essential: a window into that irreducible human drive to explore, to discover, to refuse the comfortable boundaries of the known world. The Vikings, he argues, "remain so popular today" precisely because they embody qualities we admire most about human beings—the unyielding will to push forward when every rational calculation suggests turning back.

    What makes Brownworth's approach particularly compelling is his refusal to reduce Viking exploration to a single motivation. Instead, he presents a nuanced picture of at least two distinct psychological profiles driving these journeys: the relentless raider who sails simply because he cannot stop sailing, and the solitary monk who paddles into the unknown seeking something divine. Both, he suggests, share a fundamental refusal to yield.

    ---

    The Accidental Discovery That Started Everything

    Brownworth opens his exploration with a story that immediately humanizes the Viking achievement: the tale of Naddod, who holds the distinction of being the first Norseman to reach Iceland—though he achieved this distinction entirely by accident. Sailing westward from Norway, Naddod was blown off course by storms and found himself on an unexpected shore. The "mind-blowing part," as Brownworth puts it, is what happened when he decided to land and explore this accidental discovery.

    Upon disembarking, Naddod encountered two humans: Irish monks who had somehow arrived before him. Brownworth pauses to let the enormity of this moment register—imagine the scene. A Norwegian sailor, expecting perhaps nothing but empty ocean for hundreds of miles, steps onto an unknown shore and finds that someone else has already been there. But here is the detail that truly staggers the imagination: these monks had reached Iceland "in a canoe. A skin boat."

    The geographic reality is stark when you examine a map. Ireland sits far to the southwest of Iceland. Yet these monks, in a vessel that could barely hold a single person, had somehow paddled northeast across open ocean to reach an island they believed would allow them to escape the corruption of the secular world. Brownworth notes that they "just went north because they were trying to get away from the world," and in doing so, they had pushed further from civilization than almost any human being of their era.

    When the Vikings finally arrived in force, these monks demonstrated what Brownworth calls "pretty smart" judgment: they fled immediately. The monks had come seeking solitude and spiritual closeness with God; they had no interest in confronting armed Norse warriors. Their retreat, Brownworth suggests, represents not cowardice but clarity—they knew precisely what they wanted and what they did not want.

    The Penguin Parallel: Werner Herzog and the Nature of Exploration

    To illustrate the psychological profile of the explorer, Brownworth shifts to an unlikely source: a Werner Herzog documentary about penguins. The film features a penguin who, unlike its fellows, leaves the colony and begins walking directly toward the mountains. As Brownworth recalls the narration, Herzog explains the penguin's "thinking" in his distinctive German accent—the creature is moving "towards certain death." The penguin, Herzog observes without sentiment, is simply deranged.

    Brownworth finds in this penguin an unexpected mirror of both the Viking and the monk spirit. "There's something one human or a small group of humans just decide to go," he explains. "Just go. And not look back." The penguin did glance back briefly—just once—but ultimately continued toward the mountains and almost certain death.

    This comparison, while playful, carries genuine analytical weight. Brownworth is identifying what he calls the "monk spirit" alongside the "Viking spirit"—two faces of the same irreducible impulse. The monk does not seek danger for its own sake, nor glory, nor plunder. He seeks distance from everything he knows. The Viking does not calculate risk-benefit ratios; he simply cannot remain stationary. Both exhibit what might clinically be called self-destructive behavior, yet Brownworth insists that this same impulse lies at the heart of "one of my favorite aspects of human beings."

    The key distinction Brownworth draws is between resilience and withdrawal, will and escape. The raider sails because he cannot stop sailing; the monk paddles because he cannot tolerate remaining where he is. One pushes forward into danger; the other retreats from the familiar. Both, however, reject the status quo absolutely and act upon that rejection regardless of consequence.

    ---

    The Two Psychological Profiles of Exploration

    Brownworth's framework for understanding exploration centers on these two distinct motivations, and he develops each with considerable nuance.

    The Warrior-Raider Profile represents the Vikings most commonly imagined in popular culture: hard, relentless individuals who possessed what Brownworth calls "the resilience and the will to keep going." These were people who could sail into hostile waters, land on foreign shores, fight, plunder, and immediately set out again—not because they had calculated that the rewards outweighed the risks, but because stopping felt impossible. The Viking raider embodied an almost compulsive forward momentum, a refusal to yield that bordered on the pathological.

    Brownworth connects this profile to what he describes as the Viking's defining characteristic: an unshakeable commitment to their purpose "holds to sail beyond the baths of all the western stars until I die." This is not rational behavior. It is not even particularly strategic behavior, at least by modern standards. It is the behavior of people who had decided that retreat was simply not an option, that the unknown was preferable to the known simply because it was unknown.

    The Spiritual Escapist Profile, represented by the Irish monks who preceded the Vikings to Iceland, presents a contrasting but equally powerful motivation. These men did not seek glory or wealth. They sought "to get away from the world" so they "could be closer to God, they could be closer to themselves." Theirs was an exploration born not of aggression but of ascetic rejection—a desire to strip away the complications and corruptions of civilization and find some essential truth in solitude.

    Brownworth is careful to note that both profiles coexisted within the Viking world, and that the line between them was not always distinct. Some explorers may have begun as raiders and become seekers; others may have sought solitude and found themselves compelled into violence by circumstance. The crucial commonality, he emphasizes, is the refusal to accept the world as it is presented—the insistence that the map is incomplete, that there must be something beyond the horizon.

    ---

    Tennyson's Ulysses and the Flame Within

    To crystallize the Viking spirit, Brownworth turns to what he calls "my favorite poem": Alfred, Lord Tennyson's "Ulysses." He quotes the work's famous concluding lines: "to strive to seek, to find, and not to yield." These words, Brownworth suggests, capture something essential about the Viking soul—and, by extension, something essential about the human capacity for greatness.

    The full passage Brownworth invokes speaks to an explorer who has already achieved everything conventional success might offer, yet feels compelled to sail again:

    > "My purpose holds to sail beyond the baths of all the western stars until I die."

    The sentiment is deeply anti-utilitarian. Ulysses—unlike the penguin—fully understands that he is sailing toward death. He does not expect to find anything specific. He cannot calculate a meaningful return on investment for this final voyage. He sails because the sailing is what matters, because the alternative (staying home, aging gracefully, accepting death in comfort) is intolerable to him.

    Brownworth connects this directly to the Vikings' enduring cultural legacy. "We name our satellites, our football teams, our cruise ships" after them, he observes. "There's this romantic hook... of a people who did not yield." The Vikings have become symbols precisely because they embody something we recognize in ourselves but rarely manifest: the willingness to push into the unknown not despite the danger, but because of it.

    The phrase Brownworth returns to repeatedly is that the Vikings "embody the part the flame that burns in all of us that we admire most about human beings." This flame—the drive to strive, seek, find, and never yield—is something he identifies as universally human yet rarely exhibited in such pure form. The Vikings, he suggests, serve as mythological representatives of that impulse, which is why they continue to capture our imagination centuries after their civilization faded.

    Detailed Takeaways

    1. Courage and Recklessness Often Look Identical from the Outside

    One of Brownworth's most nuanced observations concerns the difficulty of distinguishing genuine courage from mere recklessness. The Vikings did not possess superior technology or knowledge; they simply proceeded as if the risks did not exist. The monks who paddled to Iceland in skin boats possessed no special capabilities—they simply prioritized their spiritual goals above any rational self-preservation instinct. Brownworth's point is that history remembers those who acted, not those who calculated risks carefully and chose safety. "We may die," the Viking spirit seems to say, "but I'm going to do this."

    2. The Best Explorers Often Stumbled Into Discovery

    Naddod's accidental arrival in Iceland represents a broader truth about exploration: the most significant discoveries often came not from deliberate expeditions but from people who found themselves in the wrong place at the right time. Naddod was trying to sail west from Norway; the storms delivered him somewhere else entirely. Brownworth uses this to suggest that the willingness to embark—in any direction, under any circumstances—matters more than the precision of one's plans. The monks, similarly, were not conducting geographic surveys; they were fleeing the world and happened to find Iceland.

    3. Opposite Motivations Can Produce Identical Actions

    The framework Brownworth develops—that of the raider and the monk—reveals that profoundly different psychological states can generate remarkably similar behavior. Both types leave. Both refuse to look back. Both accept the possibility of death as the price of departure. The raider flees forward; the monk flees outward. One seeks; the other escapes. Yet both exhibit what Brownworth calls "unyielding focus on going out there, of taking the leap into the unknown, into the scary, and never stopping." This insight suggests that exploration is less about specific motivations and more about the rejection of stasis.

    4. We Are Drawn to Those Who Refuse to Yield

    Brownworth's observation about Viking cultural ubiquity—satellites, football teams, cruise ships—speaks to something deep in human psychology. We name our most ambitious technological achievements after the Vikings because they represent an ideal we wish to embody: the absolute commitment to forward motion regardless of consequence. "They embody the flame that burns in all of us," Brownworth says, "that we admire most about human beings." This suggests that the Viking spirit resonates not because it is foreign, but because it is familiar—that we recognize in them a version of our own suppressed impulses toward risk and discovery.

    5. Solitude and Adventure Are Not Contradictions

    The Irish monks complicate any simple narrative of Viking exploration as aggression. These men sought the most remote corners of the known world not for conquest but for contemplation. Their journey to Iceland in a skin canoe was, in its own way, as audacious as any Viking raid. Brownworth's inclusion of this story serves as a reminder that exploration can be spiritual as well as material, that the drive to find what lies beyond can be a religious impulse rather than a warlike one. Both the monk and the raider, in the end, were trying to reach something they couldn't find at home.

    ---

    Who This Is For

    This lecture appeals most directly to anyone wrestling with questions about human motivation, risk tolerance, and the psychology of achievement. Brownworth's synthesis of historical narrative and philosophical reflection makes the material accessible without sacrificing depth; readers need not have any prior knowledge of Viking history to follow and appreciate his arguments.

    The content holds particular value for those who feel themselves pulled toward unconventional paths—entrepreneurs considering major pivots, artists weighing the costs of commitment, anyone facing a choice between safety and the unknown. Brownworth's framing suggests that the impulse to explore, to push beyond comfortable boundaries, is not eccentricity but a fundamental human trait that some individuals manifest more fully than others. The Vikings, in this reading, become not superhuman heroes but recognizable human beings who simply refused to yield.

    Additionally, readers interested in the intersection of spirituality and adventure will find much to contemplate in Brownworth's treatment of the monastic explorers, whose journeys were driven not by ambition but by faith. The tension between the monk and the raider—between spiritual withdrawal and aggressive expansion—offers a framework for understanding different expressions of the same essential impulse.

    Finally, those who simply enjoy exceptional storytelling delivered with passion and intellectual rigor will appreciate Brownworth's characteristic blend of humor, insight, and genuine enthusiasm for his subject. The Werner Herzog penguin comparison, the dramatic pause around Naddod's accidental discovery, the invocation of Tennyson—all demonstrate how lively and engaging serious historical reflection can be when practiced by someone who clearly loves the material.

    The enduring question Brownworth leaves his audience with is one worth sitting with: What would you sail toward, if you knew you might die? The Viking spirit answers: anything. The flame burns. The only question is whether we will feed it or let it extinguish.
