## Introduction

Selling a business is often portrayed as the culmination of an entrepreneur's journey — the golden finish line where years of sacrifice finally pay off. Yet according to Rohan Oza, a veteran consumer packaged goods (CPG) entrepreneur who has built and sold multiple brands, most founders are woefully unprepared for this moment. "You work six years, seven years, eight years, and then 50% of the value is going to be how good you are at this M&A thing at the end that you've never done before, but it's the biggest deal of your life," Oza explains in a candid conversation about exit strategy.

Oza speaks from experience. Over a 25-year career in the CPG industry, he has established relationships with virtually every major beverage and food company — Coca-Cola, PepsiCo, Hershey, Mars, and KDP — and has been involved in landmark acquisitions including the $4.1 billion sale of Vitaminwater to Coca-Cola and the sale of Poppi to PepsiCo. These aren't just success stories; they are case studies in the strategic decisions, negotiation tactics, and psychological dynamics that separate transformative exits from life-changing disappointments.

The stakes couldn't be higher. For the entrepreneur, the sale represents the ultimate validation and financial reward for years of risk and sacrifice. For the acquirer, it's often a routine transaction that moves the needle on quarterly earnings only if the integration succeeds. This fundamental asymmetry — what Oza calls an "unfair advantage" held by institutional buyers — is the central challenge facing every founder who reaches the exit door.

---

## The Asymmetric Battlefield of M&A Negotiations

### Why Founders Start at a Disadvantage

The core problem, as Oza sees it, is that the parties entering an M&A negotiation are operating from radically different positions of experience and risk exposure. "A reputable buyer has done this many times, and it doesn't matter to them necessarily if it fails," Oza notes. "They'll just get fired, worst case scenario." For the buyer, a failed acquisition is a career setback. For the seller, it's a potential life-altering outcome — not just in terms of finances, but in legacy, team welfare, and personal identity tied up in the business.

This asymmetry creates what Oza describes as a "very unfair advantage" that favors the buyer. The buyer's team has negotiated dozens of acquisitions, understands the full spectrum of deal structures, knows how to extract value through earnout provisions, and has emotional distance from the outcome. The founder, by contrast, has likely never sold a company before, is emotionally invested in the business they've spent years building, and faces cognitive biases that can cloud judgment precisely when clarity matters most.

The solution, Oza argues, is threefold: build relationships long before you need them, hire expert representation, and develop the psychological resilience to play a long game when necessary.

---

## Relationships: The Currency of M&A

### Building Networks Before You Need Them

Oza's success in exits stems significantly from decades of relationship-building within the CPG industry. "I've established and I don't have relationships everywhere but you know I have a relationship with a lot of big CPGs — Coke, Pepsi, Hershey, Manderlays, KDP," he explains. These relationships didn't materialize overnight; they accumulated through years of working in the industry, collaborating with executives, and maintaining connections across multiple ventures.

The concrete value of these relationships became evident during Oza's involvement with Poppi. When it came time to sell, "we brought a banker" — in this case, Goldman Sachs — and Oza leveraged his existing connections to initiate conversations. "They actually brought Pepsi to the table initially," he recalls. Without those relationships, the process would have started from scratch, missing crucial windows and potentially missing key decision-makers entirely.

For entrepreneurs without Oza's decades of industry experience, this presents an obvious challenge. The relationship advantage isn't something you can manufacture in the weeks leading up to a potential sale. It requires years of deliberate networking, genuine relationship-building, and demonstrating value to potential acquirers before you're asking them for anything. Industry conferences, advisory board positions, and strategic partnerships can all serve as relationship-building vehicles.

## Lessons from Vitaminwater: The Art of Aggressive Valuation

### Learning from Darius and Coca-Cola

Perhaps no deal illustrates Oza's M&A philosophy more clearly than the acquisition of Vitaminwater by Coca-Cola. While Oza didn't negotiate that deal directly — "the founder of Vitaminwater, Darius, did brilliantly, and I learned from him" — he was present as an observer when Darius walked into a meeting with Coca-Cola's leadership.

The moment that stuck with Oza was Darius's opening ask. "When he said the number starts with a four, I almost fell off my chair," Oza recalls. "I'm like, did he just say four?" The eventual sale price of $4.1 billion validated Darius's aggressive positioning. "I thought 4B and Coke came out back at 4.1. I'm like, all right, guy."

What made this negotiating tactic effective? Oza identifies the critical variable: brand strength. "If you have a great brand, it's okay to be slightly unhinged on your expectation." The key phrase is "if you have a great brand" — the tactic only works when you have genuine leverage. Founders must honestly assess whether their brand commands premium valuation before adopting aggressive positioning.

The psychological dynamics here are crucial. Opening with an extreme demand sets an anchoring point that influences all subsequent negotiations. Even if the final number lands below the opening ask, it lands higher than it would have with a conservative opening. This is classic negotiation theory, but it requires the confidence and brand equity to pull it off.

## Timing: The Variable That Defies Forecasting

### Why Poppi Sold for Half of Vitaminwater Despite Being Bigger

The story of Poppi demonstrates that even experienced entrepreneurs face variables beyond their control. "Poppy sold for half of what Vitamin Water did," Oza explains. "Except Poppy grew faster than Vitamin Water and is a bigger scale brand than Vitamin was. But timing was different, right? At that time there was a willingness and the money was more liquidity was freer and greater to pay more."

This observation extends far beyond the CPG industry. "The same with tech valuations, right? People were investing in 2021 at bonkers valuations, then slowed down, and now AI is also — there's a timing mechanism." Market conditions, interest rates, available capital, and broader economic sentiment all influence what acquirers are willing to pay at any given moment.

The implication for founders is not to chase perfect timing — which is impossible to predict — but to maintain "belief and faith in who you are" while remaining adaptable. Oza cautions against benchmarking your deal against the biggest numbers you've ever seen, calling it "a danger entrepreneurs have." The trap is saying, "This company sold for seven times revenue, I'm going for seven, and if I don't get it, I'm out." As Oza puts it, "Take it easy. Beauty is in the eye of the beholder."

## The Art of Walking Away

### How Oza Turned Down Pepsi Twice

The Poppi sale to PepsiCo wasn't a straightforward transaction. After Goldman Sachs introduced Pepsi as an initial suitor, Oza received an offer that he rejected. "Without getting into it, the number and the offer was inadequate," he explains. "So I did what I said is a risky move, which is I walked."

Notably, Oza walked a second time as well, after a different potential acquirer approached. "I also walked because I didn't like the construct of the deal." The deal structure apparently included earnouts — contingent payments based on future performance — rather than a full upfront buyout.

This is where Oza's prior exit experience became crucial. "I was blessed to have had exits before. They haven't had any," he notes. The pressure on the other side — Poppi's other executives and investors — was intense. "And even Stevie was losing it at one point because they're like, 'This is life-changing money that Rose is saying no to.'"

The gamble paid off. "Third time's a charm. When Pepsi came back, I ended up negotiating that directly with them." Rather than relying solely on bankers and intermediaries, Oza engaged directly in the final negotiation, likely securing more favorable terms than would have emerged through back-channel communications.

The lesson is not to refuse reasonable offers out of stubbornness, but to have clear criteria for what constitutes an acceptable deal structure. For Oza, a full buyout without complex earnout provisions was apparently a red line. Founders should identify their own non-negotiables before entering negotiations.

## Representation and Process

### Why Goldman Sachs Matters

Oza's recommendation for any founder contemplating a sale is straightforward: "Get a good banker, you know, get good rep." For Poppi, that meant Goldman Sachs, one of the world's most prestigious investment banks. While Oza had the relationships and experience to drive key elements of the deal, the Goldman team provided process expertise, valuation analysis, and negotiating support that would have been difficult to replicate independently.

Investment banks add value in several ways beyond raw negotiating skill. They run competitive processes that can create bidding wars between multiple potential acquirers. They provide valuation frameworks that justify pricing to internal stakeholders on both sides. They manage the extensive documentation and legal processes required for large transactions. And they can serve as buffers during emotionally charged negotiations, allowing founders to maintain strategic control while delegating tactical discussions.

## Generosity and Legacy

### Including Others in the Win

Beyond the financial mechanics, Oza reflects on the personal dimensions of a successful exit. "When you have a massive win like this, you always have to step back and realize how blessed you are, not take it for granted. See what you could do for others and those around you."

What distinguished Oza's approach was his decision to include others in the deal. "A good thing for me was I let a lot of people into the deal. So it wasn't just me. I had family, friends. One of my closest childhood friends was in the deal." This approach reflected both generosity and practical wisdom — shared success creates a wider network of people with a stake in the entrepreneur's future endeavors.

"When you affect other people beyond yourself, that's huge," Oza notes. The exit becomes not just a financial transaction but a statement of values and a foundation for future relationships and ventures.

## Detailed Takeaways

### 1. Treat the Exit as a Distinct Skill Set Requiring Dedicated Preparation

Most entrepreneurs spend years honing product development, team building, fundraising, and operations — skills that receive extensive coverage in business media and education. Yet Oza's observation that "50% of the value is going to be determined at the end" should prompt a fundamental reorientation. Founders need to treat M&A preparation as a primary skill development priority, not an afterthought to be handled when a buyer appears. This means reading about deals in your industry, understanding valuation methodologies, and building relationships with investment bankers and M&A advisors well before you need them.

### 2. Build Relationship Capital Long Before You Need to Deploy It

Oza's success wasn't accidental — it emerged from decades of consistent relationship cultivation with the exact executives who would later become decision-makers in acquisition processes. For entrepreneurs earlier in their journey, this means being deliberate about industry engagement. Join advisory boards, speak at conferences, take meetings with potential strategic partners even when you don't need anything from them. These connections create optionality when exit time arrives.

### 3. Use Anchoring Aggressively — But Only When You Have Real Leverage

The Darius play — opening with an extreme demand that sets a high psychological anchor — works, but only if your brand genuinely justifies premium valuation. Before adopting aggressive positioning, honestly assess your competitive advantages, growth trajectory, and strategic value to potential acquirers. If you lack strong leverage, an aggressive opening may simply signal unreasonable expectations and kill the deal.

### 4. Know Your Non-Negotiables and Walk When They're Violated

Oza walked from two potentially lucrative offers because he disliked the deal structure. This willingness to reject apparently good offers in pursuit of the right offer reflects a critical M&A competency. Before entering negotiations, identify what you will and won't accept: Is it a full buyout versus earnout? Is it a particular valuation floor? Is it retention of key team members? These criteria prevent emotional decision-making under the pressure of a live negotiation.

### 5. Timing Is Partly Out of Your Control — Maintain Relative Flexibility

The comparison between Vitaminwater and Poppi reveals how much market timing affects exit multiples. Founders cannot control interest rates, available capital, or broader market enthusiasm at the moment of sale. What they can control is their own flexibility about timing. Being cash-flow positive and not desperately needing an exit provides leverage to wait for favorable market conditions. Build businesses that don't require immediate liquidity events.

Particularly valuable content exists for entrepreneurs at the growth stage — companies that have achieved product-market fit, generated meaningful revenue, and are beginning to consider exit pathways.
