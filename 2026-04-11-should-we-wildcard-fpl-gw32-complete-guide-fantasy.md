## The Four Main Chips: Timing and Strategy for Each

### The Wildcard

FPL Harry identifies four main weeks for playing the wildcard: Gameweeks 32, 33, 34, and 35, with 32 and 35 being the most popular choices. The rationale for wild carding in Gameweek 32 centers on maximizing the bench boost, as it allows managers to completely restructure their squad to target the double in Gameweek 33 while potentially freeing themselves to use the free hit in the subsequent blank in Gameweek 34.

However, Harry acknowledges that wildcarding in 32 isn't universal. "There are some people who will be wild carding 32 in order not to do that," he explains, referring to managers who simply want to reset their squad rather than specifically targeting the bench boost. For those with strong teams in Gameweek 32 who don't want to wildcard this week, waiting until Gameweek 35 is a viable strategy—particularly if you've already used your free hit or don't have bench boost available.

Gameweek 34 is "only really viable if you've already used your free hit," Harry notes, as it helps you manage the transition from the double in 33 to the blank in 34. This is a more advanced strategy essentially requiring you to wildcard in 34 specifically to navigate that blank gameweek without a free hit.

### The Free Hit

The free hit chip, in Harry's analysis, has essentially two viable deployment windows: either in the double (Gameweek 33) or in the blank (Gameweek 34). The reason is straightforward—targeting both weeks simultaneously without playing either a free hit or wildcard is "almost impossible," in Harry's words. The strategy breaks down into two main approaches.

First, you can free hit in Gameweek 33 to load up on as many double gameweek players as possible, accepting that you'll likely need to use another chip or take hits in Gameweek 34 when those players blank. Alternatively, you can perform well in Gameweek 33 with your regular team and then deploy the free hit in Gameweek 34, when you'll "likely not have that many players" from the doubling teams due to the blank. The choice depends on your squad composition and risk tolerance.

### The Bench Boost

For bench boost, Gameweek 33 represents the optimal timing because "it's got the most fixtures" and "the most doubles," giving managers the highest chance of fielding a strong bench with six, seven, or even eight fixtures. The more doublers you can include on your bench, the more points you accumulate from this chip.

However, Harry identifies several other viable weeks. Gameweek 32 works if your team looks strong, as there's no restriction on when you can play it. Gameweek 35 and 37 are single gameweeks but offer "quite a lot of good fixtures," particularly with Leeds hosting Burnley at home in Gameweek 35. Gameweek 36, despite having only two teams doubling (six players), could work if you pair double Crystal Palace defense with double Manchester City, as this would "push the likes of [Gabriel] and [Saliba] to be part of your bench boost." The key insight is that bench boost viability depends not just on total fixtures but on which teams are doubling and whether you have coverage of those teams in your squad.

### The Triple Captain

Harry reveals Gameweek 36 as his "favorite" week for the triple captain, despite the league potentially being decided by then. He argues that "even if Manchester City lose to Arsenal in a couple of weeks, there is still not something to fight for mathematically in gameweek 36," but notes that there's still likely to be something to play for in Gameweek 33.

For single gameweek options outside the doubles, Harry suggests Liverpool at home to Brentford in Gameweek 38 is "still a decent opportunity" despite Salah's form and minutes risk, describing it as "probably my favorite single gameweek left outside of the doubles." He acknowledges this is "a romantic thing" and that most managers will play their triple captain in another week, but the Brentford fixture represents a solid fallback option.

## Chip Strategy Combinations: Tailoring to Your Situation

This is where FPL Harry's analysis becomes particularly valuable—he recognizes that not all managers are in the same position, and the optimal strategy depends entirely on which chips you have remaining.

### Scenario A: All Chips Available

For managers with their wild card, free hit, bench boost, and triple captain all still to play, Harry recommends the following sequence: wild card in Gameweek 32, bench boost in Gameweek 33, free hit in Gameweek 34, and triple captain in Gameweek 36.

This strategy allows you to wildcard this week to set up for the doubles, play your bench boost in the biggest double gameweek, free hit in the subsequent blank when you'll likely have few players from those doubling teams, and save the triple captain for the smaller but still valuable double in Gameweek 36. Harry acknowledges that "the issue with that for some people is they don't want to wild card in 32 because they think their team is too good to wild card," but he "still would recommend this strategy to pretty much everyone who's got a lot of chips to play and a lot of transfers."

### Alternative Strategies When You Have All Chips

For managers who don't want to wildcard in 32 but have a strong team this week, Harry offers two alternatives.

The first is the "free hit 33, wildcard 35" strategy. Under this approach, you play your team normally in Gameweeks 32 and 34, use the free hit in Gameweek 33 to load up on double gameweek players, and then wildcard in Gameweek 35 for the final four weeks of the season. For the remaining chips, you'd use triple captain in Gameweek 36 (which Harry believes "is better for the triple captain than the bench boost") and bench boost in Gameweek 37, which "has some quite good single gameweek fixtures." This strategy is "particularly good if your team is not only strong in 32 but also strong in 34."

The second alternative is wild carding in Gameweek 33, free hitting in Gameweek 34, bench boosting in Gameweek 35, and triple captain in Gameweek 36. This "fits slightly better if your team looks very strong this week, but actually not that strong in gameweek 34"—perhaps because you own Newcastle and Brentford players who have "very difficult gameweek 34 fixtures, but quite good fixtures this week."

### Scenario B: Triple Captain Already Used

For managers who have already used their triple captain—Harry mentions using his on Gabriel earlier in the season—the strategies change slightly. The wildcard 32, bench boost 33, free hit 34 sequence "still applies" and remains effective. However, it "loses a little bit of appeal because you don't need to leave gameweek 36 open because you don't have the triple captain." In this scenario, you might consider using bench boost in Gameweek 36 instead, as there's less value in preserving a gameweek for a chip you no longer possess.

Harry notes this scenario is "particularly strong if you have lots of transfers available," as the ability to navigate between gameweeks without using chips becomes more viable when you have transfer flexibility.

## Detailed Takeaways: The Core Strategic Insights

**The double-then-blank sequence in Gameweeks 33 and 34 fundamentally dictates chip deployment.** With six teams doubling in 33 and all six blanking in 34, managers must choose which week to target aggressively. The free hit becomes essential for at least one of these weeks because loading up on doublers for 33 leaves you with a barren squad for 34, while avoiding doublers for 33 means you'll likely underperform that week. This is not optional—it's mathematical necessity for serious contenders.

**Wild card timing depends on your current squad strength, not absolute strategy.** Harry emphasizes that "if you're slightly lighter on transfers and you've got a great team this week, that's when you might start to conclude" that wildcarding now isn't optimal. The textbook strategy of wildcard 32, bench boost 33, free hit 34 assumes you need to rebuild—but if your team already looks strong for the doubles, waiting until Gameweek 35 to wildcard for the run-in makes more sense. The decision should be driven by honest assessment of your current squad against the fixture schedule.

**Bench boost timing is about more than just total fixtures.** While Gameweek 33 has the most doubles and therefore the most bench boost potential, Harry reveals that Gameweek 35 could be strong because "Leeds having Burnley at home" represents a particularly favorable fixture. Similarly, Gameweek 36 could work if you specifically load up on Crystal Palace and Manchester City doubles, as having multiple doublers on your bench increases the value of the chip. The takeaway is that bench boost is more flexible than many managers assume—it doesn't have to be in the biggest double.

**Gameweek 36 is undervalued for triple captain.** Despite the league potentially being decided, Harry makes a compelling case for Gameweek 36 because there's "still not something to fight for mathematically" in most scenarios by Gameweek 33, and the double fixtures for Manchester City and Crystal Palace represent good value. The smaller double is more predictable than it appears, and saving triple captain for Gameweek 38 when Salah might have minutes concerns is riskier than using it on the guaranteed doubles in 36.

**Transfer quantity matters for chip strategy.** Harry explicitly notes that his recommendations assume managers have "a lot of transfers" available. Those with fewer transfers might be better served by the wildcard 32 approach because they need the chip to restructure their squad adequately. Understanding your transfer stock is prerequisite to choosing the right strategy.
