# China Blocks Meta's $2 Billion Manus Deal: A Turning Point in US-China AI Competition

## Introduction

The intersection of artificial intelligence, geopolitics, and corporate strategy reached a dramatic inflection point recently when China summarily blocked Meta's $2 billion acquisition of Manus, an AI startup that had been celebrated as one of the most promising agentic AI companies in the world. The blocking of this deal, combined with the detention of Manus's two co-founders in China, represents what commentators describe as an unprecedented development — the first time in two decades that China has moved to unwind a completed acquisition involving American capital.

For context, Meta CEO Mark Zuckerberg had been on a spending spree totaling approximately $25 billion in pursuit of building a competitive agentic AI system. After failed attempts to acquire Safe Super Intelligence from Ilya Sutskever, reaching out to Mustafa Miramani's Thinking Machines, and being rejected by Perplexity, Meta eventually landed on Manus as its ticket into the agentic AI race. The deal appeared to be sealed and done, with Manus employees working under Meta's corporate structure, the CEO already installed in Meta's C-suite, and the technology being deeply integrated into Meta's advertising infrastructure.

What makes this situation particularly striking is the timing and the precedent it sets. The co-founders were detained in March 2026, their passports confiscated during a meeting with China's National Development and Reform Commission — essentially the agency that runs China's entire economy. Today, China announced the complete blocking of the deal in a single sentence, demanding Meta reverse the entire transaction. The timing is especially notable occurring four weeks before President Trump's scheduled meeting with Xi Jinping in Beijing.

## The Manus Acquisition: Context and Significance

Manus emerged in March 2025 with an AI agent described as something closer to an AI operating system than a simple chatbot or assistant. The platform was capable of performing complex, long-form agentic tasks with memory and contextual awareness — a capability that at the time had not yet been fully developed by OpenAI, Anthropic, or Google. Within seven days of launch, Manus had amassed a 2 million person waiting list, with invite codes selling on secondary markets for as much as $1,500 each.

The founders, recognizing that their Chinese origin would limit their appeal to American investors and users, relocated the company from Beijing to Singapore — ostensibly to distance themselves from Chinese jurisdiction. They raised a significant round from Benchmark Capital, one of America's most prestigious venture capital firms, and within six months achieved a $90 million revenue run rate. The founders believed they had successfully subverted Chinese authority by moving their headquarters to a neutral country.

The deal with Meta came together as part of Zuckerberg's broader strategy to secure a competitive agentic platform. The $2 billion acquisition was completed, with the ink dried on the deal. Manus's technology began being integrated into Meta's existing infrastructure, particularly the advertising platform where it was being used by advertisers to spin up their projects and automate repetitive manual tasks. The results were substantial — a 30% quarterly revenue jump attributed directly to Manus's capabilities within the Meta ecosystem.

However, the assumption that relocating to Singapore would place Manus beyond Chinese reach proved fundamentally flawed. China, the hosts argue, operates more as a dictatorship in this context, and the government maintained that Manus was still effectively a Chinese company — founded in China, staffed originally by Chinese employees, and built on foundations that China believes it still has claim to.

## China's Unprecedented Intervention

The blocking of the Manus deal represents something China has never done before: unwinding a completed, legal transaction involving foreign capital. For the last 20 years, China has happily accepted American investment and allowed foreign acquisitions of Chinese companies. This deal marks what the hosts describe as a definitive turning point — a signal that China now views AI assets as strategic national resources that cannot leave Chinese jurisdiction regardless of corporate restructuring or legal technicalities.

The mechanism of intervention was straightforward but severe. The two Manus co-founders — CEO and Chief Scientist — received a summons from China's National Development and Reform Commission in March. They attended what they believed would be a routine questioning about the Meta deal. Instead, they were informed they would not be permitted to leave the country. Their passports were confiscated, and they were blacklisted from international travel. Today, China has extended this intervention to officially ban the entire acquisition and request Meta reverse the deal entirely.

The question now facing Meta is whether they want to escalate this into a direct confrontation with China. The company has already spent $2 billion, has hundreds of former Manus employees working under its employment, has an entire Singaporean office with over 100 workers from Manus, and has the CEO already integrated into Meta's C-suite. The technology has been deeply implemented and is actively generating revenue. Unwinding this would be exceptionally complicated and costly. Yet China is demanding exactly that.

The hosts suggest this situation reveals something critical: China is now treating AI researchers, workers, and AI products themselves as national assets with geopolitical implications. They believe if they don't retain control over these assets, the United States will leverage them to "wipe out" Chinese competitive advantage in what has become the defining technological race of the 21st century.

## China's AI Independence Strategy

The Manus blocking is not an isolated incident but rather the latest manifestation of a deliberate, coordinated Chinese strategy to achieve technological independence from the United States. Over the past several months, China has taken concrete steps to reduce its dependence on American technology that would have seemed unthinkable just a few years ago.

Most significantly, China has refused to use NVIDIA GPUs for training its own AI models at top Chinese AI labs. This is remarkable given that NVIDIA dominates global AI chip production and American sanctions had previously restricted China's access to advanced chips. Rather than accepting limited access to NVIDIA technology, China essentially told the American chip giant "Nah, we're good" and committed to developing its own semiconductor capabilities.

The Chinese government has mandated that all major AI labs — including DeepSeek, Moonshot (Kimi), and Alibaba — train their AI models using Chinese-made chips, specifically Huawei processors. This mandate explains how these companies have managed to release frontier-level models at a pace matching or exceeding American competitors despite not having access to the world's most advanced training hardware.

The strategy is working. Within a single week, China released three major models: DeepSeek V4, Kimi K2.6, and Qwen 3.6. Independent benchmarks show these models performing at 90-95% of the capability of GPT 5.4 and Claude Opus 4.7 — the current frontier models from OpenAI and Anthropic respectively. These Chinese models are not only competitive on quality but offer something the American models cannot: full open-source availability. Users can download these models, run them on their own hardware, and fine-tune them without restriction. For developers who cannot afford to train their own models from scratch — a process requiring hundreds of millions of dollars — Chinese models offer an accessible alternative with nearly equivalent capability.

## The Hardware Architecture Gap

One of the most significant revelations in this discussion concerns the fundamental architectural differences between American and Chinese AI chips — differences that many observers are not adequately addressing.

American chip design, particularly NVIDIA's architecture, is optimized around pre-training — the initial large-scale training runs that establish base model capabilities. This makes sense given American constraints: the US has limited energy availability and pre-training runs are extraordinarily energy-intensive. The hardware is built with the understanding that extensive pre-training will be the primary computational burden.

China took a different approach. Recognizing they have approximately three times more energy available than the United States, Chinese chip designers concluded that the path to the best AI model occurs not during pre-training but during post-training and inference. This represents a fundamentally different optimization target. Chinese chips are specifically designed for inference — the process of running and refining models after initial training — rather than the massive pre-training runs that characterize American AI development.

This architectural divergence has significant implications. The hosts argue that China has essentially built a technology stack that wouldn't necessarily operate or work efficiently within the American system. The hardware differences are so substantial that attempting to run American-developed models on Chinese infrastructure or vice versa would encounter serious efficiency problems.

The emphasis on inference rather than pre-training also explains the velocity of Chinese AI advancement. Because Chinese labs are spending less time on the expensive, energy-intensive pre-training phase and more time on post-training optimization and inference, they can iterate faster and publish models more frequently. This is analogous to what Google has observed with its own models: smaller, distilled models often outperform larger models precisely because they're optimized for inference and efficiency rather than raw scale.

The acceleration in Chinese model releases — DeepSeek V4, Kimi K2.6, Qwen 3.6, all within one week — reflects this fundamentally different approach to AI development. The gains are happening at the inference layer, where China's hardware is specifically optimized to operate.

## The Token Usage Data

The narrative that Chinese AI models are inferior or unused is directly contradicted by data from OpenRouter, a platform that provides access to multiple AI models and tracks actual token consumption — a metric more reliable than benchmarks because it reflects real-world usage patterns.

Analysis of OpenRouter data reveals that Chinese models consistently take the number one spot for token consumption. This metric indicates that developers, researchers, and companies are actively choosing Chinese models for their projects, particularly those who cannot afford to train or fine-tune their own models from scratch. The token usage data suggests Chinese models have surpassed American model consumption according to publicly available metrics.

For developers without hundreds of millions of dollars to spend on pre-training their own foundation models, Chinese open-source models offer an attractive proposition: nearly equivalent capability at a fraction of the cost, with the ability to run locally and customize freely. This accessibility is driving adoption in ways that closed-source American models cannot match.

## NVIDIA's Strategic Position

The situation creates an interesting dynamic for NVIDIA that the hosts explore in some depth. The conventional narrative frames the US-China AI competition as a straightforward rivalry where one side's success necessarily comes at the other's expense. The reality, as NVIDIA CEO Jensen Huang's recent interview on the Dwarkesh Podcast suggests, is more complex.

If open-source models dominate, everyone who wants to use them needs to train and run them on compatible hardware — which means NVIDIA GPUs. This is fundamentally different from closed-source models, which companies like Anthropic and OpenAI are increasingly developing to run on custom hardware stacks like TPUs that don't require NVIDIA processors. When closed-source wins, NVIDIA gets sidelined from the inference stack. When open-source wins, NVIDIA remains essential.

This explains why NVIDIA has been leaning heavily into supporting open-source AI development. The company has even launched its own enterprise version of OpenClaw called NemoClaw. By driving adoption of AI more broadly, whatever is built on top of NVIDIA hardware will demand more NVIDIA hardware in the future. The strategy is to sell picks and shovels to everyone participating in the gold rush, regardless of which specific applications succeed.

The evidence for this strategy's success is in NVIDIA's market capitalization. The company recently surpassed a $5 trillion market cap, having gone above and then below this threshold before settling comfortably above it. While Jensen Huang couldn't explicitly state during interviews that open-source AI benefits NVIDIA's business, the logic is clear: open-source requires open hardware, and NVIDIA owns the open hardware market.

The interview where Jensen became somewhat defensive — responding "I'm not a loser" when pressed on NVIDIA's position — may reflect at least partially his awareness that open-source models benefit his company more than closed-source alternatives. If everything becomes closed-source, custom silicon becomes dominant, and NVIDIA's position in the value chain becomes much less certain.

## Implications for Meta

The Manus situation leaves Meta in an uncomfortable position. The company has struggled to develop competitive AI products despite spending tens of billions of dollars. Their Llama models have gained some traction in the open-source community, but Meta's consumer-facing AI implementations have not achieved the success their advertising business has enjoyed with Manus's integration.

The Manus technology was delivering tangible results — a 30% quarterly revenue jump from advertising automation alone. Without a clear path to unwinding the Chinese government's intervention, Meta faces the prospect of losing technology that was already generating substantial value while simultaneously lacking a ready alternative.

Zuckerberg's strategic challenge is significant. After failing to acquire Safe Super Intelligence, being rejected by Thinking Machines, missing on Perplexity, and spending $15 billion for half of Scale AI just to gain access to Alexander Wang, Meta finally achieved its agentic AI breakthrough through Manus. Now that breakthrough is under threat from geopolitical forces entirely outside the company's control.

The hosts note this is particularly brutal given Meta's broader product challenges. The company has been unable to launch a successful AI-native product despite enormous investment and resources. Manus represented not just a technology acquisition but potentially a path to relevance in the AI era. Having that path suddenly blocked by foreign government intervention creates a strategic void that will be difficult to fill.

## Detailed Takeaways

### 1. National Sovereignty Over Corporate Structure

The Manus case demonstrates that China will not respect corporate restructuring designed to evade its jurisdiction. The founders believed relocating from Beijing to Hong Kong to Singapore would place them outside Chinese authority. China has made clear that jurisdiction follows the people and the origin, not the corporate address. Any company or startup considering similar moves should understand that Chinese law follows Chinese nationals regardless of where they nominally reside or where their legal entity is registered.

### 2. AI Assets Are Now Geopolitical Assets

The $2 billion Manus deal is not being treated by China as a commercial transaction between private parties. It's being treated as a matter of national strategic importance. This means traditional deal structures, legal agreements, and corporate formalities may offer less protection than companies assume. For startups with valuable AI technology, the question of who controls access to that technology has become inseparable from broader questions of national security and strategic competition.

### 3. The Pre-Training vs. Inference Architecture Divide

American and Chinese AI development are diverging not just in terms of specific models or applications but in fundamental hardware architecture. The US optimizes for pre-training; China optimizes for inference. This means the two countries are building technology stacks that don't easily interoperate and are optimized for different phases of the AI development pipeline. Companies operating internationally will need to navigate increasingly distinct technology ecosystems rather than assuming a global convergence toward common standards.

### 4. Open-Source Models Have Crossed the Capability Threshold

The assumption that Chinese open-source models are significantly inferior to American closed-source alternatives is no longer supportable. Three models released within one week — DeepSeek V4, Kimi K2.6, and Qwen 3.6 — perform at 90-95% of the latest GPT and Claude frontier models. Token usage data from OpenRouter confirms these models are actively being used by developers worldwide. For companies that can accept open-source deployment, Chinese models now represent a viable competitive alternative at substantially lower cost.

### 5. Hardware Independence Enables Model Independence

China's decision to mandate Huawei chips for AI training at major Chinese labs was a deliberate choice to avoid dependence on American technology. This decision, initially seeming like a constraint, has proven to be a catalyst for developing inference-optimized hardware that produces competitive models. The implication is that American export controls, rather than limiting Chinese AI development, may have accelerated Chinese semiconductor independence in ways that ultimately strengthen China's competitive position.
