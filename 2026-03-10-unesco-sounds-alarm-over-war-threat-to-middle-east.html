UNESCO sounds alarm over war threat to Middle East's heritage sites • FRANCE 24 English

Introduction

The escalating conflict in the Middle East has brought with it a devastating consequence that extends far beyond immediate human casualties and destroyed infrastructure. UNESCO, the United Nations Educational, Scientific, and Cultural Organization, has issued a stark warning about the growing threat to some of humanity's most priceless cultural treasures. The recent bombing campaigns across the region have already inflicted visible damage on several World Heritage sites, prompting urgent calls for protecting these irreplaceable monuments of human civilization.

The situation represents what cultural heritage experts describe as a pattern all too familiar from previous conflicts in the region. As military operations expand across multiple nations, the geographical scope of endangered cultural sites has grown correspondingly, with ancient palaces, historic cities, and millennia-old architectural treasures now sitting directly in the path of ongoing hostilities. UNESCO's intervention marks an attempt to prevent the kind of wholesale destruction that characterized conflicts in Syria and elsewhere, where ancient heritage was reduced to rubble alongside civilian infrastructure.

This report examines the specific sites already damaged, the broader risks facing the region's cultural heritage, and the measures UNESCO is attempting to implement to safeguard these irreplaceable treasures.

Key Concepts and Arguments

The Destruction of Shahel Sutun in Isfahan

The report begins with documentation of damage to Shahel Sutun, also known as the Palace of Forty Columns, in Isfahan, Iran. This architectural gem is considered one of the finest examples of Persian architecture, representing the pinnacle of Safavid-era design and craftsmanship. The palace, which served as part of the royal complex of Shah Abbas I, features exquisite tilework, elaborate painted ceilings, and architectural innovations that influenced building design across the region for centuries.

According to the report, the force of recent explosions has shattered windows and wooden beams within the structure. Perhaps most significantly, millennia-old wall decorations—delicate tile work and painted surfaces that represent centuries of artistic tradition—have been damaged by the blasts. These decorative elements are not merely aesthetic features but represent irreplaceable records of Persian artistic achievement, religious iconography, and cultural identity that cannot be recreated once destroyed.

The targeting of this particular site illustrates what UNESCO officials describe as a troubling pattern in modern warfare: the inability or unwillingness to distinguish between military objectives and the civilian infrastructure that houses humanity's shared cultural inheritance. As one UNESCO representative noted in the report, what is happening is clear to all—civilians pay the price in modern conflicts, and civilian infrastructure pays the price, including the destruction of priceless historical heritage.

Parallels to Syrian Heritage Destruction

The UNESCO official drew explicit parallels to the destruction of heritage sites in Syria, where years of civil war resulted in the near-complete demolition of ancient monuments in Palmyra, the destruction of countless medieval souks and mosques, and the looting of museums housing artifacts spanning millennia of human history. The reference serves as both a warning and a call to action, suggesting that without urgent intervention, the current conflict could produce similarly catastrophic losses for Middle Eastern heritage.

The Syrian conflict demonstrated that once heritage sites are damaged or destroyed in wartime, the losses are essentially permanent. Reconstruction can sometimes recreate the external appearance of buildings, but the authentic historical fabric—the original materials, the subtle wear patterns that speak to centuries of use, the specific artistic techniques of master craftsmen—cannot be recovered. This is why international law recognizes certain cultural sites as protected entities that should never become military targets, and why UNESCO's warning carries such urgency.

Additional Sites Affected

Beyond Isfahan, the report documents damage to several other significant heritage locations. Last week, the 16th century Golestan Palace in Tehran, another Safavid-era royal complex and one of the oldest historical monuments in the Iranian capital, was damaged by nearby military activity. This palace, which served as the residence of the Qajar dynasty and features some of the finest examples of Persian garden architecture and tilework, now joins the growing list of sites requiring assessment and potentially emergency conservation work.

The city of Tire in Lebanon and the White City of Tel Aviv have also been affected according to UNESCO. The White City, a UNESCO World Heritage site, represents a unique architectural achievement as one of the largest concentration of International Style architecture in the world, built under the British Mandate in the early 20th century. Its inclusion on the list of affected sites demonstrates that the conflict's reach extends beyond ancient monuments to include more recent but still significant architectural heritage.

Expanding Risk to World Heritage Sites

Perhaps most concerning is UNESCO's assessment that with the expansion of the conflict, all regions containing listed sites are now at risk. The organization has identified specific sites in immediate danger, including the Citadel of Eril in Iraqi Kurdistan, a historic fortress representing centuries of Mesopotamian civilization and more recent medieval construction. Beyond Iraq, World Heritage sites in Cyprus, Jordan, Turkey, and Palestine now face varying degrees of risk depending on how the conflict evolves.

This geographical spread illustrates the cascading nature of regional conflicts in an interconnected world. What began as a specific military confrontation has expanded to affect multiple nations, each containing heritage sites that represent not only national treasures but components of humanity's shared cultural inheritance. The Roman ruins of Jordan's Jerash, the Crusader castles of Cyprus, the Ottoman-era architecture of Turkey's historic cities—all now sit within a conflict zone whose boundaries continue to shift.

UNESCO's Response and Mitigation Efforts

In response to these threats, UNESCO has taken the step of sharing the geographical coordinates of World Heritage sites with all parties involved in the conflict. This represents a concrete attempt to implement the principles of international humanitarian law that protect cultural property during armed conflict. By providing precise locations of protected sites, the organization hopes that warring parties will be able to avoid these locations, designate them as protected zones, or at minimum exercise extreme caution when military operations approach them.

This approach builds on mechanisms established by the 1954 Hague Convention for the Protection of Cultural Property in the Armed Conflict and its two protocols, which create legal obligations to protect cultural heritage during warfare. However, the effectiveness of such measures depends entirely on the willingness of warring parties to respect these protections—a willingness that has proven inconsistent in many recent conflicts.

Beyond Heritage: Education for Children

The report concludes by noting that UNESCO insists access to education for children caught up in the conflict remains a priority alongside heritage protection. This reflects the organization's broader mandate that extends beyond stone monuments to encompass human development. In conflict zones, schools often become targets, displaced children lose access to education, and an entire generation's future can be compromised alongside the destruction of physical infrastructure.

UNESCO's dual emphasis—protecting both tangible heritage and intangible human potential—represents a holistic understanding of what is at stake in regional conflicts. The destruction of ancient palaces and archaeological sites makes headlines and generates international concern, but the disruption of education for millions of children may prove even more damaging to societies that will need educated populations to rebuild when the bombs eventually stop.

Detailed Takeaways

The Irreplaceability of Cultural Heritage Must Drive Urgent Action: When UNESCO officials warn that "priceless heritage is being destroyed," they emphasize a fundamental truth about cultural property: unlike other forms of infrastructure that can be rebuilt with sufficient resources, ancient monuments represent unique historical records that exist in only one location with only one set of physical characteristics. The shattered windows of Shahel Sutun cannot be replaced with identical components, and the wall decorations damaged by blast force represent techniques that may no longer be practiced. This irreplaceability is precisely why international humanitarian law singles out cultural property for special protection—the loss is permanent in a way that distinguishes it from other wartime damage.

Modern Conflicts Consistently Target or Endanger Civilian Infrastructure: The UNESCO representative's observation that "civilians pay the price" and "civilian infrastructure pays the price" reflects a documented pattern across contemporary warfare. While international law theoretically distinguishes between military and civilian targets, the practical reality of modern conflicts often blurs this distinction, whether through deliberate targeting of civilian infrastructure, use of imprecise weapons in populated areas, or simple proximity of heritage sites to military objectives. The destruction of Syrian heritage sites provided extensive documentation of this pattern, and the current conflict is following a disturbingly similar trajectory.

Regional Conflicts Create Cascading Risks for Heritage Sites: The expansion of the Middle East conflict to affect sites in Iran, Lebanon, Israel, Iraq, and potentially beyond demonstrates how regional instability creates cascading risks for cultural heritage. What begins as a bilateral dispute or localized conflict quickly becomes a multi-national crisis when allies become involved, borders become contested, and military operations cross international boundaries. Heritage site managers and international organizations must therefore think regionally rather than nationally when assessing risks, anticipating that instability in one country will quickly affect neighboring nations.

International Coordination Can Provide Meaningful Protection When Implemented: UNESCO's decision to share coordinates of World Heritage sites with all warring parties represents a concrete mechanism for protection that has proven effective in some previous conflicts. When parties to a conflict agree to respect protected zones, cultural heritage can survive even intense military operations. The effectiveness of this approach depends entirely on political will and military practicality, but it provides a framework that organizations like UNESCO can activate even in the midst of escalating violence.

Heritage Protection and Human Development Are Inseparable: UNESCO's insistence that education for conflict-affected children remains a priority alongside physical heritage protection reflects an understanding that cultural preservation cannot be separated from human welfare. Societies exist not only through their monuments and artifacts but through their people, their knowledge systems, and their capacity for future development. A comprehensive response to conflict must therefore address both the preservation of the past and the protection of future potential.

Who This Is For

This report holds particular value for several distinct audiences. Cultural heritage professionals and archaeologists will find specific information about sites already damaged and those at highest risk, along with documentation of the types of damage being inflicted on Persian and Levantine architecture. The report provides actionable intelligence for organizations working to assess damage, plan conservation interventions, and advocate for stronger protections.

International relations and security analysts will benefit from the documentation of how regional conflicts expand geographically and what second-order effects such conflicts produce beyond immediate military concerns. The targeting of heritage sites represents both a humanitarian concern and a potential indicator of conflict escalation patterns that may prove useful for early warning and policy analysis.

General audiences with interest in current events will gain understanding of why cultural heritage protection matters even in the midst of humanitarian crises focused on civilian casualties. The destruction of ancient monuments may seem like a secondary concern when people are dying, but as UNESCO's response demonstrates, international organizations recognize that these losses compound over time and represent damage to shared human inheritance that extends far beyond the immediate conflict zone.

Educators and students will find useful material for discussing the intersection of warfare, international law, and cultural preservation. The specific examples provided—Shahel Sutun, Golestan Palace, the White City—provide concrete case studies for understanding how theoretical protections translate into practical challenges during actual conflicts.

Ultimately, this report serves as both a warning and a document of record. It captures a moment in an ongoing conflict when international institutions are attempting to prevent the worst outcomes based on lessons learned from previous catastrophes. Whether these efforts succeed remains to be seen, but the information it contains will prove valuable for historians, policymakers, and citizens seeking to understand how the current conflict is reshaping not only political boundaries but the physical landscape of human cultural achievement.
