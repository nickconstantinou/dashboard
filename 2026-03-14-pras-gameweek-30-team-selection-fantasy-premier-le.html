Pras' Gameweek 30 Team Selection | Fantasy Premier League Tips 2025/26

Introduction

The Fantasy Premier League landscape has shifted dramatically following midweek European competitions, and The FPL Wire's Pras is here to help you navigate the uncertainty. In this video, recorded for Gameweek 30, Pras addresses the cascading implications of Manchester City, Chelsea, and potentially Crystal Palace being eliminated from European competition—developments that have fundamentally altered the fixture planning terrain FPL managers thought they understood just days earlier.

After a welcome 10-day reprieve from FPL agony during the FA Cup, managers returned to find their carefully constructed chip strategies potentially rendered obsolete. Pras acknowledges that while the Tuesday podcast covered various chip strategies—his own being Free Hit in Gameweek 33, Wildcard in Gameweek 35, and Bench Boost in Gameweek 36—the European results have introduced a level of unpredictability that demands a fundamentally different approach. The core message is straightforward: stop trying to predict the unpredictable, and for heaven's sake, do not hit that Wildcard button next Friday.

Pras opens by reviewing his own Gameweek 29 performance, where he experienced his first red arrow after six consecutive green weeks, dropping from just under the top 500,000 to approximately 733,000 in overall rank. Despite this setback, he remains philosophical about the nature of FPL and optimistic about the season's remaining gameweeks.

The European Earthquake: What Changed

The midweek European results represented a significant turning point for FPL planning, and Pras walks through each outcome in detail. Manchester City suffered a convincing defeat in their Champions League fixture, significantly reducing their odds of progression though they still retain a mathematical chance. More dramatically, Chelsea squandered a 2-2 draw to lose 5-2, effectively ending their European campaign. Meanwhile, Crystal Palace played out a goalless home draw in their Conference League tie, meaning they could be eliminated next week if they lose the return leg.

These results carry profound implications for fixture planning. Pras explains that teams eliminated from European competition now have every midweek free for the remainder of the season—Manchester City, Chelsea, Newcastle, and potentially Crystal Palace all fall into this category. This fundamentally changes where their Premier League fixtures can be scheduled, as there are no longer European matches creating midweek congestion.

The previously assumed landscape—where certain teams' fixtures were locked into specific gameweeks due to European commitments—no longer applies for these clubs. Pras emphasizes that the Crystal Palace situation is particularly fluid because they could still progress or be eliminated, making any concrete planning around their fixtures essentially guesswork at this stage.

Why Your Chip Strategy Needs to Wait

This is where Pras delivers his most emphatic recommendation: stop trying to plan your chips right now. The reason is straightforward but crucial to understand. Throughout this season, managers have relied on a reasonably predictable framework—certain teams would be busy in Europe, certain fixtures would move to specific blank or double gameweeks based on those commitments. That framework has now collapsed into uncertainty.

Pras explains that we're entering territory where even the most knowledgeable FPL analysts—people like Tala James from Planet FPL and Ben Krillin—are essentially making educated guesses. Sky Sports and TNT Sports have scheduling preferences that even they don't know for certain yet, and one small decision at the Premier League or broadcast level could cascade into entirely different fixture arrangements. The confidence managers had about where specific fixtures would land has decreased significantly.

His specific advice is brutal but necessary: do not plan to click the Wildcard button next Friday after the Gameweek 31 deadline, even though the temptation will be enormous with a seemingly attractive three-week Wildcard window. Pras recommends taking the international break completely away from FPL—don't check price movements, don't stress about your team, just return after the FA Cup quarterfinals and reassess from there.

Understanding the Fixture Implications

Pras walks through the practical consequences of the European results using a visual aid showing which teams remain in the FA Cup (indicated by blue bubbles). These are the only teams whose fixtures can potentially move, and the logic works as follows: if a team is out of Europe, their midweeks are completely free, meaning their Premier League fixtures could theoretically be scheduled in any of the available midweek slots across Gameweeks 32 through 37.

However, Pras is careful to note that this doesn't mean fixtures will scatter randomly. The Premier League still wants to avoid clashing with other major football events—there's no desire to schedule a Premier League match opposite a Champions League tie like Bayern Munich versus Real Madrid. Additionally, TV companies have preferences for certain slots, and there are still factors that will concentrate fixtures in certain gameweeks rather than others.

The base case scenario—which hasn't changed materially despite the European results—still involves several double gameweeks. The most likely arrangement features Arsenal doubling in Gameweek 33, Brighton doubling because they play Chelsea, Newcastle doubling, and Manchester City doubling with Burnley. This remains the scenario that FPL analysts consider most probable, though Pras stresses this is perhaps only 15-20% likely to actually occur given all the moving parts.

There's also an interesting precedent from last season: when Nottingham Forest had a free midweek, their game simply stayed in Gameweek 34 itself rather than moving. This means it's entirely possible that we get only one double gameweek (Gameweek 33) for the rest of the season, with Manchester City's fixture against Burnley staying exactly where it is and their Crystal Palace fixture joining it in Gameweek 33.

Reviewing Gameweek 29 Performance

Pras takes a brief moment to review his own team's performance, providing some transparency about how his decisions played out. After six consecutive green arrows—his best run of the season—Gameweek 29 brought him back down to earth with a 20-30 point score that dropped him from just inside the top 500,000 to approximately 733,000 overall.

The frustrating element was that his team was actually well-constructed; the players simply didn't deliver. His defense performed admirably, with Ryan Gabrielle and James Hill (who came on for the injured Anderson) both doing well. However, his attacking options—Van Dijk, Ekit, Imbumo, and the entire Brentford attack—failed to return meaningful points, while the players he didn't select, particularly Jao Pedro and Semeno, excelled for their respective teams.

This, Pras notes, is simply how FPL works sometimes. One bad gameweek can swing your rank by hundreds of thousands, but with many gameweeks remaining, there's no reason to panic. His optimism about the season's end remains intact, and he encourages his viewers to maintain similar perspective.

Key Takeaways for FPL Managers

First, adopt a flexibility-over-commitment approach. The European results have introduced too much uncertainty for rigid planning. While it's tempting to lock in a chip strategy based on what you think will happen, the reality is that the fixture landscape could shift significantly in either direction. Keep your options open and avoid making moves that box you into a single outcome.

Second, resist the Wildcard temptation next Friday. The three-week window looks attractive, but hitting your Wildcard now means you're making decisions based on incomplete information. Wait until after the international break when more will be known about European qualification outcomes and fixture scheduling.

Third, understand which teams have free midweeks. Manchester City, Chelsea, Newcastle, and possibly Crystal Palace now have every midweek free for the rest of the season. This means their fixtures could go anywhere, and planning around assumed double gameweeks for these teams is now genuinely speculative.

Fourth, focus on the immediate next two weeks only. Rather than trying to map out your entire season from here, concentrate on Gameweeks 31 and 32. Make decisions that serve your team well in the short term while preserving flexibility for later.

Fifth, use FPL.team as a reference but not gospel. Pras mentions Pete's website provides James from Planet FPL's assumptions about fixture locations, but emphasizes this is just one scenario among many with perhaps 15-20% probability. It's useful information but should be treated as one possible future rather than certainty.
