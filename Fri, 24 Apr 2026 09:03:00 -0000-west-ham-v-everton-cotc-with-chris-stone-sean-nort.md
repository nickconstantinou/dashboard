<p># West Ham v Everton Clash: Premier League Survival Battles and FPL Strategies for the Run-In

**Planet FPL's Correspondents Break Down the Relegation Scramble and Fantasy Football Decisions That Could Define Your Season**

---

As the Premier League season hurtles toward its dramatic conclusion, the battle for survival has reached fever pitch—and for Fantasy Premier League managers, the pressure is equally intense. In this latest edition of Planet FPL's Clash of the Correspondents, recorded on deadline day with the two teams' XI largely confirmed, host James sat down with West Ham United correspondent Chris Stone and Everton correspondent Sean Norton to dissect both the real-world football implications and the FPL strategies flowing from them.

The timing could hardly be more significant. Both West Ham and Everton enter the run-in separated by just a handful of points from the relegation zone, while Nottingham Forest's involvement in the European picture adds another layer of complexity to the survival picture. For FPL managers, understanding how these clubs approach their remaining fixtures—and which players are most likely to deliver returns—could be the difference between a season-winning haul and a frustrating blank.

---

## The Battle at the Bottom: West Ham's Cautious Optimism

Chris Stone opened the discussion with a sense of measured hope that would define much of West Ham's outlook. "Cautiously optimistic," he called it. "It's in our hands, technically. So that's a different situation than we had two or three months ago." This represents a remarkable turnaround for a club that was deeply mired in trouble not so long ago.

The context for this newfound optimism came from Monday night's result—a point gained against Crystal Palace that, while frustrating in the moment, proved valuable in the broader context as Palace fought back into the game. "It would have been good to get the three points," Chris acknowledged. "That would have put a lot more pressure on you. That would have made a big difference." The admission revealed the interconnected nature of the survival battle—every point dropped or gained ripples across the entire table.

When pressed on how many points West Ham need for safety, Chris was frank about the uncertainty. "I think 40 would be just about enough. But I think with our goal difference, three wins would make us comfortable, but three wins out of five is a tough ask." The discussion then veered into darker territory as Chris recalled the infamous 0-2-0-3 season—a campaign where both West Ham and Bolton produced Champions League-quality form in the final weeks yet still finished on 42 points and went down. "It was the record points that anyone's gone down with," Chris noted grimly. "So it was terrible, terrible bad luck, bad timing."

This historical perspective serves as a sobering reminder that mathematics alone cannot guarantee survival. The vagaries of form, goal difference, and the results of direct rivals all play their part.

---

## Nottingham Forest: The Wildcard in the Survival Equation

Perhaps the most surprising element of the survival discussion was the continued involvement of Nottingham Forest, a club simultaneously fighting relegation and progressing to the Europa League semi-finals. Chris Stone refused to rule them out of the relegation conversation despite Forest sitting three points ahead of West Ham.

"I would [still count Forest as involved], yeah. I mean, they're three points ahead of us. Obviously you need to pick up some points desperately because they're that much further ahead of you. But I still don't think they're out of it." This assessment rested on a crucial observation: both West Ham and Everton would need to catch Forest, making it a two-team chase rather than a simple head-to-head scenario.

The fixture list makes Forest's situation particularly intriguing. Their remaining games are Sunderland, Chelsea away, Newcastle away, Manchester United away, and Bournemouth at home. "Bournemouth's probably the one," Chris suggested, noting that Bournemouth have been the best of those five teams over the past few weeks. The complication: Bournemouth at home represents Forest's final-day fixture, and if the Cherries are still hunting for European qualification themselves, that game becomes fraught with additional pressure.

Sean Norton offered his own assessment, predicting Tottenham as his pick to go down—a comment that briefly derailed the discussion into friendly territory before they returned to the survival picture.

---

## Everton Under Pressure: The Merseyside Derby Fallout

For Everton, the conversation shifted to Sunday's Merseyside Derby, a match that represented a rare opportunity for the club to enter as favorites against Liverpool—and one they ultimately failed to capitalize on.

Sean Norton described the peculiar atmosphere that pervaded Goodison Park. "We started well, we did, yeah. The atmosphere at the beginning was fantastic. We started well. I don't know, there was something, there was a weird feeling in the stadium of tension between both fans." The tension stemmed from an unusual dynamic: Evertonians nervously aware that their team was expected to win, a position they rarely occupy in this fixture.

"The atmosphere at the beginning was brilliant, but then after about the first 10 minutes, it just felt like both sets of fans were just gripped with... just on edge because obviously if we did win, that would have been huge for us and they would have started getting a bit, hang on, is this season going to go even worse?" The psychological burden of being favorites clearly affected Everton's performance—a phenomenon Sean recognized from personal experience. "Every time I've gone into a North London derby, feeling confident, it normally shoots me in the face."

The result effectively ends Everton's hopes of finishing in the top five, though the discussion touched on an intriguing scenario where sixth place might still secure Champions League qualification. More immediately, Sean was candid about Everton's ambitions: "If you offered me Conference League now, I'd rip your hand off." This wasn't merely about European football—Sean wanted silverware. "It's been far too fucking long. I've absolutely fed up. I need to see Everton win a competition and it doesn't necessarily have to be a European one, but I think the Conference League is our best chance of winning something soon."

The irony that West Ham—the team Sean was discussing survival with—won the very competition he coveted wasn't lost on the participants. "The problem is when you go in the Conference League, Chris probably knows this, but his team obviously went on and won it. You almost feel obliged, like, you've got to win it."

---

## Everton's Peculiar Home-Away Split

One of the more fascinating statistical revelations concerned Everton's home and away records. Despite playing at the same stadium all season, the Toffees have developed dramatically different identities depending on whether they're playing at Goodison or on the road.

"We have the 14th best home record, the third best away record," Sean explained. "Same amount of points, both. Yeah, it's mad, isn't it?" When James suggested this might indicate they're simply better away from home, Sean pushed back against that interpretation. "I still think I've kept it pretty consistent all season. I do think it's a bit of a... a little bit of a quirk of numbers and stuff. We just seem to, if anything, we give more soft goals away at home. We are a little bit more resilient away."

This psychological dimension—more "brain fart moments" at home—provides a fascinating FPL data point. Managers considering Everton's defenders might want to weight away fixtures more heavily than the raw underlying numbers suggest.

---

## FPL Strategy: The Jarrod Bowen Question

The discussion then pivoted to the fantasy implications of the weekend's fixtures, with Jarrod Bowen emerging as perhaps the most universally-owned West Ham asset heading into a crucial period.

Chris Stone was unequivocal: "I think Gerard Bowen is probably going to be in the majority of three-hit teams... It's a bit of a no-brainer not to have him because he's had the most goalie involvements of our players." This assessment rested on both the volume of involvement and Bowen's defensive contributions. "He's had about two or three in the last four or five games. He's hit two or one, which for forward isn't a bad little bonus."

However, Sean offered a contrarian perspective that challenged the conventional wisdom. While acknowledging Bowen's consistency—"One of the things I've always loved about him as an asset is his consistency and you don't worry about fixtures so much"—he questioned whether three-hit managers should prioritize a player who "never felt like much of a hauler."

"He's due a goal, he's hit the post three times out of the last four games and he's going to get chances. If we score, he's going to be involved in some way." But Sean's counter-argument was compelling: on a three-hit, managers might want to target players more likely to explode for large hauls rather than steady contributors who return modest but consistent scores.

For those wildcarding this week, Sean confirmed Bowen would be part of his plans despite facing his own team. "He normally does well against us. He regularly returns against us." West Ham's fixture run—Brentford away, Arsenal, Newcastle away, Leeds home—provided additional comfort, with Sean viewing all of them as manageable for Bowen.

---

## The Summerville Dilemma: Premium Price, Uncertain Returns

The discussion then turned to Leeds midfielder Summerville, a player priced at 5.5 who represents a different kind of FPL challenge: a player who showed explosive potential in January but whose end product has been questioned.

Chris Stone, speaking as someone who watches Summerville every week, was candid. "I think he's a terrific little player, Chris. When I watch him I'm like getting nervous about him but I don't quite know if the end product is going to be there." This assessment tracked Summerville's trajectory: four goals in a row in January, an injury interruption, and a subsequent dry spell with "only one more goal which was scored after a cock up at Craven Cottage" in the games between.

The critical question was whether January represented Summerville's true level or merely a hot streak. "We look at people like Sean's got James Gardner and you're like I know he's just going to keep ticking over with them Defcons and these on-set plays and stuff. So the game's changed now. This time last year it would go Summerville. Now it's like I've got to push for it. Even though he's only 5.5 he has got to be notching returns because there are other players that will definitely return regularly."

For FPL managers, this represents a fundamental shift in how budget options must be evaluated. The 5.5 price point no longer guarantees a player will coast along with minimal returns; the market has become more competitive, and managers demand more from their enablers.

---

## Jobe Bellingham vs. James Garner: The Midfield Debate

The conversation then addressed a specific FPL debate: which Sunderland midfielder offers better value for three-hit teams—Jobe Bellingham or James Garner?

Sean was clear in his preference. "I think I'll probably go with Jobe Bellingham. Just because of the fact I'm almost definitely going with Jared Bowen. So I think I'd rather split it that way than bubble up." The reasoning prioritized haul potential over floor security. "I just prefer the fact that he's a more offensive player. And more likely to get a goal or get an assist. I'm not overly keen on... I know Defcon points are important but I'd just rather go for the player that's likely to get attacking returns more so than Defcon."

The numbers supported this preference. Since returning from injury, Bellingham has made 10 starts, contributing 3 goals and 3 assists—evidence of a player who "came back and hit the ground running." Sean went further, suggesting Bellingham was Everton's best player against Liverpool. The points-per-match comparison also favored Bellingham at 5.1 compared to Garner's 4.4.

For three-hit managers seeking explosive options rather than consistent floor contributors, this analysis pointed clearly toward Bellingham.

## Fixture Analysis: The Run-In Implications

Throughout the discussion, both correspondents provided crucial context for their teams' remaining fixtures—and the implications for FPL assets.

For West Ham, the run-in presents a mixed picture. Arsenal's remaining fixtures were deemed so favorable that Chris agreed with the assessment that West Ham away represents the Gunners' toughest remaining game. "I think playing the bottom teams is always hard in the first season. I don't know. Most teams, top number side, but even they start showing a bit of fight, haven't they?" This psychological factor—bottom teams playing with greater freedom as survival becomes mathematically impossible—complicates fixture-based planning.

For Everton's final three fixtures—Palace away, Sunderland at home, and Tottenham away—the picture looked more favorable. "These fixtures still okay this weekend," Sean noted, though he acknowledged rotation risks against City. The middle fixture against Sunderland represented an opportunity for European-chasing Sunderland to demonstrate their quality, while the Tottenham game could occur "in the middle of anarchy" depending on Tottenham's league position.

Nottingham Forest's final-day fixture against Bournemouth emerged as potentially decisive. With all three relegation-battling teams playing at home on the final day, someone would inevitably "wreck their stadium" with either celebration or despair.

## Detailed Takeaways

**1. The relegation battle involves three teams, not two.** While West Ham and Everton prepare to face each other, Nottingham Forest's three-point advantage means they remain firmly in the conversation despite their European success. For FPL managers, this creates unusual dynamics where a team in European competition still has motivation to fight for league points—potentially making Forest assets more valuable than their apparent safety suggests.

**2. Jarrod Bowen remains the primary West Ham asset despite fixture concerns.** His consistent involvement in chances—both offensive and defensive—makes him the default choice for three-hit teams. The fact that he regularly returns against Everton should provide additional confidence despite facing his own team. However, managers should recognize that Bowen's value comes from steady returns rather than explosive hauls.

**3. Everton's home/away split is psychological rather than tactical.** The 14th-best home record versus 3rd-best away record represents a genuine phenomenon but stems from "brain fart moments" at Goodison Park rather than systematic tactical issues. FPL managers should mentally adjust Everton's attacking potential upward for away fixtures and downward for home ones.

**4. The Summerville profile has changed in the current market.** Where 5.5 mids could previously be trusted to tick over with minimal returns, the competitive landscape now demands more from budget enablers. Managers who invested in Summerville hoping for consistent returns are likely disappointed; those considering him now must weigh his ceiling against his unreliable floor.

**5. Jobe Bellingham over James Garner for three-hit teams.** The offensive profile and superior points-per-match average (5.1 vs 4.4) make Bellingham the better choice for managers seeking haul potential rather than floor security. Combined with the likely inclusion of Jarrod Bowen, splitting the two options rather than doubling up on Sunderland seems the optimal approach.
