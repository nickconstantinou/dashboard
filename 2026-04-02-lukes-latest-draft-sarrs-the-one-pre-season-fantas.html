Introduction

With the 2025/26 Fantasy Premier League season approaching, FPL managers across the world are refining their strategies, weighing up fixture schedules against player form, and trying to predict which signings will settle quickly and which will take time to adapt. Luke from FPL Blackbox (known on social media as this_fpl) has emerged as one of the community's more thoughtful voices—someone who balances data-driven insights with practical wisdom gained from years of navigating the chaos of FPL's first weeks.

In this video, Luke walks viewers through his current draft team, presenting what he openly acknowledges as his "most sensible" approach rather than a high-risk, high-reward roster. The reason for this caution, as he explains, is that the pre-season landscape remains fluid: multiple clubs are still finalizing their transfer business, the striker situation across several teams remains unresolved, and the usual early-season chaos of new signings, injury adjustments, and fixture changes looms large.

Luke is refreshingly honest that this particular draft might ultimately be the one he goes with on deadline day. He's not chasing clicks with outlandish predictions or controversial picks. Instead, he's building what he calls his "safety net"—a team constructed around players with clear roles, established systems, and minimal uncertainty heading into Gameweek 1. This philosophy shapes every decision in his squad, from goalkeeper to forwards.

The Philosophy: Playing Safe When Uncertainty Abounds

One of the central themes Luke returns to throughout the video is his deliberate choice to avoid newly signed players in his opening squad. This isn't a universal rule, he clarifies, but rather a personal preference born from painful experience. "I told myself from the last few years that I was one of the people who had Barco," he admits, referencing his mistake of jumping on recent signings who hadn't yet proven themselves in their new environments.

The reasoning behind this caution is multifaceted. When a player transfers to a new club, especially from overseas, managers frequently indicate they'll need time to integrate. Luke recalls the common refrain: "We need to work him into our system. We need to get him into training for a few days, introduce him slowly, get him up to speed." This isn't just manager-speak—it has practical implications for FPL managers. A player who hasn't trained fully with his new teammates will likely be eased into the lineup, meaning fewer minutes and potentially fewer returns in those crucial early Gameweeks.

There's also a price dynamic to consider. When thousands of managers pile into a newly signed player expecting immediate returns, the price often rises rapidly. But if that player doesn't start straight away, those managers begin selling, driving the price down and forcing sellers to navigate the transfer market while simultaneously holding onto a player who isn't delivering. "You get that value back," Luke acknowledges, "but I don't want to be carrying any worries at the beginning of the season when the player base is so active."

This philosophy doesn't mean Luke will never select new signings—he explicitly mentions one player at the end of the video who could feature depending on whether a move goes through. But for his core squad, he's prioritizing certainty over potential.

Goalkeeper Strategy: The 4.5 Million Sweet Spot

Luke's goalkeeper selection tells you everything you need to know about his risk tolerance. He opens with his pick: Keller of Brentford, priced at 4.5 million. The thinking behind this choice, while not groundbreaking, reflects a deliberate philosophy about resource allocation.

"I just don't really want to spend more than 4.5 on my goalkeepers," Luke states plainly. He's aware that the market offers cheaper options—Dravka at 4.0 million appears to be heading toward a starting role—but he considers Keller the best of the 4.5 million bracket. This isn't an exciting pick, and Luke knows it. "We're going to be a hell of a long video because that's how long it took for me to talk about Keller," he jokes.

The Brentford context is important. The club recently changed managers, which always introduces some uncertainty. Additionally, Brentford have historically conceded plenty of shots, meaning clean sheets may not come frequently. Luke isn't expecting Flecken-like consistency from the previous season, but he believes Keller is a capable goalkeeper who can make saves when called upon.

What about the premium goalkeeper options? Luke acknowledges that premium keepers like Raya have served him well—he's essentially owned Raya for the past three seasons. "When you buy someone like Raya or an Allison outside of injury, you're pretty much guaranteeing yourself they're going to be in the top three scoring goalkeepers," he says. "That security is quite good. That's what you're paying for."

However, he notices a pattern in recent seasons: a 5.0 or 4.5 million goalkeeper typically either beats the premium options or comes very close. At the season's start, when funds are tightest and every million matters, he prefers to take his chances with a budget option and address the position later if needed. "I just feel like have a 4.5, maybe address it later."

One potential complication involves West Ham. The Hammers appear to be in the market for a goalkeeper, possibly signing one imminently. If they bring in Lord (Nick Pope) and he's ready to play, that could disrupt the current arrangement. Luke admits he'd find it hard not to pivot to the West Ham keeper if that situation materializes, but for now, Keller remains his choice.

The Chelsea option—Sanchez at 4.5 million—comes up briefly. The underlying models are reportedly quite high on Sanchez both before and after Cole Palmer's continued presence. But Luke has never been tempted. He wants the security of a proven 4.5 option, not an uncertainty.

Defense Strategy: Chasing Attacking Returns from Unlikely Sources

Moving to defense, Luke reveals his philosophy about defenders has evolved significantly. As a former Sky Sports employee who participated in their FPL game (now defunct), he recalls when "collecting bonus points for your defenders was like the name of the game." That world no longer exists.

"Clean sheets are at a premium these days in the Premier League," Luke observes. "It's very hard to collect them." Rather than chasing the rare clean sheet or hoping for attacking contributions from premium defenders, he's selected two players specifically for their ability to rack up points through goals and assists: Tocigoski and Marillo, both priced at 4.0 million and both delivering four goals and four assists last season.

The math here is straightforward. "If I was to come back and Tocigoski and Marillo have got four and four each, eight points—lovely, I'll take that all day," Luke says. Compare this to his typical defender returns: "I often get twos and ones from my defenders." Even the most attacking premium defenders offer no guarantee of goals and assists. "You can have the most attacking defender in the world—there's no guarantee they actually end up getting as many goals and assists, even if they look really sexy and they're right up top."

This approach reflects a pragmatic acceptance of variance. Premium defenders like Alexander-Arnold or Robertson can deliver massive hauls, but they also blank frequently. Budget defenders with genuine attacking output—albeit fewer chances to convert them—can provide steady returns that add up over the season.

Luke admits he's not completely set on Tocigoski repeating last season's output. "Players don't really change their stripes too much," he explains. "They've pretty much got the same setups, the same managers, they're the same players. These are giant blockheads, and they just love to get involved. It'd be a surprise if they weren't able to achieve that regularly again." He's treating their output as repeatable behavior, not a statistical anomaly.

Porro: The Exception to the Budget Rule

Tottenham's Porro stands out as Luke's most expensive defensive asset, and he acknowledges the tension this creates. "I have got my reservations with Porro just because I mean, Spurs from last season—who wouldn't be slightly worried?"

Tottenham did undergo a manager change and signed Palinha as a defensive midfielder, which could improve their defensive structure. But last season's collapse was significant, and even with these changes, the underlying concerns linger. So why include him?

"It's the fixtures, primarily, and then also the fact that he can be very attacking," Luke explains. When he runs the numbers through expected points models, Porro comes out near the top over the first five or six weeks. "Who am I to argue with it if everyone's saying that's all going to be the case?" It's a candid admission that the data is guiding him despite his gut feeling.

There's also the matter of set pieces. Porro takes Tottenham's set pieces—Luke describes him as "definitely on all them set pieces." With Maddison's unfortunate injury likely to keep him out, Porro's role in dead-ball situations may even increase. In pre-season friendlies, including a 4-0 loss to Bayern Munich, Luke noticed that Spurs' only big chance came from Porro. "Like he is just a very creative player."

Luke sees some contradiction in his thinking—he's prioritized defensive contributors, yet Porro is arguably the most attack-minded fullback in his squad. But the fixtures and set-piece role make him too valuable to ignore. "I think I'm going to have him to begin with, and we'll see where we go essentially."

Midfield: Where the Money Flows

Without revealing his specific picks, Luke hints at the structure of his midfield, joking that whenever he shares his team in various Discord communities, "they give me like a pregnant woman emoji with a big around the middle." The midfield is where he's concentrated his resources.

"Midfield is just stacked with all the money," he admits. "And maybe I'm spending too much there, but I feel like midfield is like the place where you get your points, where you earn your coin."

This reflects a common FPL philosophy: midfielders score more consistently than forwards, and premium midfielders ( Salah, Son, Palmer) offer multiple avenues to points through goals, assists, and bonus. Luke acknowledges that cheaper midfielders do occasionally emerge as differential picks, but "knowing who they are at the beginning of the season is quite tough."

The natural consequence of this approach becomes clear: if midfield is stacked, the forward line must be budget-heavy. Luke hints that this explains the structure viewers will see when he eventually reveals his attackers.

Key Takeaways for FPL Managers

1. Newly signed players carry hidden risks that can compound early-season problems. Managers frequently want to integrate new arrivals slowly, which means fewer minutes and potentially declining prices. Unless you have strong conviction that a signing will start immediately, consider avoiding them for your initial squad. The chaos of Gameweek 1 is manageable; the chaos of holding an overpriced, non-starting player while scrambling to fix your team is not.

2. Budget goalkeepers can match premium options, especially early in the season. Luke's strategy of not exceeding 4.5 million reflects a pattern recognition: at least one budget option typically performs as well as or better than premium keepers in any given season. With funds tight at the start, this approach preserves money for positions where premium options create more substantial advantages.

3. Budget defenders with proven attacking outputs offer reliable floors. The days of chasing clean sheets from budget defenders are largely over. Players like Tocigoski and Marillo provide value through consistent underlying attacking metrics—goals and assists—that compound over a season even without clean sheet bonuses. Luke's eight-point return expectation (4+4) from a 4.0 defender beats most alternatives.

4. Fixture models matter, especially in the first six weeks. Porro represents Luke's willingness to follow data over gut instinct. When multiple models consistently project a player as a top performer over the opening fixture run, that evidence should outweigh concerns about form or recent history. The first six weeks set the tone for your season and your confidence.

5. Structure your squad around positions where premiums create the largest advantages. Luke's heavy midfield investment reflects a strategic choice: spend where the points-per-game differential between premium and budget is largest. This necessarily means accepting budget options elsewhere, but the math often works out better than spreading funds evenly.

Who This Content Is For

If you're someone who tends to chase differentials, stack new signings, or panic early in the season, Luke's disciplined approach offers a counterbalance. His philosophy emphasizes stability, proven outputs, and accepting that some factors are simply beyond your control.

Experienced managers who have lived through seasons of holding overpriced, non-starting players will find Luke's warnings about new signings particularly resonant. Younger or less experienced players will benefit from understanding why structure matters more than excitement at the season's beginning. And anyone who has ever debated whether to spend big on a goalkeeper will appreciate his clear-eyed assessment of where that money is better deployed.

The FPL landscape is unpredictable by design. Luke's draft won't win any popularity contests, and he doesn't claim it will. But it might just be the kind of sensible, boring team that keeps you in the game through August and September while others burn their transfers on early-season chaos. Sometimes, the smartest play is the one that keeps your options open—and Luke's draft does exactly that.
