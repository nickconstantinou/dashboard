# Play Those Chips: FPL Gameweek 32 Strategy and Chip Season Analysis

## The Context of a Shifting Landscape

The international break may have provided welcome respite for Fantasy Premier League managers, but it has also fundamentally reshaped the strategic landscape for the season's crucial final phase. In the latest episode of the beloved FPL podcast *Who Got the Assist?*, hosts Tom and Sam reconvened on Tuesday, April 7th, to dissect how the Easter weekend's FA Cup quarterfinal results have dramatically altered the chip strategy calculus for managers across the globe. Tom opened the show acknowledging his current state of recovery from a chest infection, humorously noting his voice was "croakier than usual" and promising to mute liberally to spare listeners—though he quipped that Sam may need to "take the lead a little bit" during the episode.

The podcast derives its name from the FPL community's shared obsession with assists, and the chemistry between the two hosts remains evident as they navigate the serious business of FPL strategy through banter and genuine enthusiasm for the game. Sam wasted no time in expressing his unbridled joy at one particular result from the break, declaring that watching Southampton knock Arsenal out of the FA Cup was "probably the most happy I've been watching football in a long, long time"—a moment he cheekily promised to "milk" throughout the podcast. The implications of that result, however, extend far beyond mere satisfaction for rival fans; it has removed what was expected to be a highly valuable double game week for Arsenal, reshaping the entire chip strategy framework that managers have been building toward all season.

## Gameweek 31 Performance Review

Before diving into forward-looking strategy, Tom and Sam took stock of how they individually navigated the blank game week 31, a fixture congestion period that already feels like "a proper lifetime ago" according to Sam. For Tom, the weekend represented both triumph and heartbreak in equal measure. He revealed he made three transfers during the blank—a significant commitment that included parting with Erling Haaland, bringing in Joao Pedro for coverage purposes, swapping in Callum Wilson for his captaincy potential, and taking a gamble on Morgan Gibbs-White as a "vibes-based punt price" play. This marked Tom's first hit of the entire season, a notable admission given the careful bankroll management most successful FPL managers employ.

The results nearly justified the aggressive approach. Tom found himself just four points from a green gameweek, though he acknowledged that his transfers did put him six points better off than he would have been without them—a testament to the value of pre-planned moves even when taking hits. The ultimate culprit for missing the green arrow? James Hill scoring an own goal, which would have been avoided had the defender received his usual "death con" (a term the community uses for set-piece delivery duties). Tom also suffered the frustration of watching Malikets Tchoumeni get benched by Eddie Howe, denying him points from a player who had run an astonishing 44-game streak without being rested. Tom's ire toward Howe was palpable, declaring he "just hate[s] Eddie Howe's guts right now" and describing him memorably as a "big stupid pie face." Despite the near-miss, Tom finished the gameweek inside the top 100,000, describing this as "a fine place to be" as chip season approaches.

Sam's birthday weekend delivered a considerably greener experience, with his 63 points translating to a climb of approximately 60,000 places, reaching around 332,000th overall. However, he confessed to still being haunted by missing his first top 1 million rank of the entire season by a single point—made more painful by the realization that a late set-piece update for Wolves defender Jhon Duran would have provided the extra points needed. His weekend was bolstered by Conor Coady's continued strong performances, Ryan Kelleher delivering a nine-point haul, and Morgan Rogers finally breaking his blanking streak with an assist. Sam had also captained Bruno Fernandes for 13 points but admitted the transfer activity—which included bringing in Harry Wilson for Declan Rice—nearly matched his eventual score even without making any moves, suggesting the market movements weren't as critical as they initially appeared.

## The FA Cup Fallout: Restructured Double Game Weeks

The most consequential development discussed was the dramatic reshuffling of the double game week calendar following Easter weekend results. Sam explained that the FA Cup quarterfinal weekend delivered two major pieces of news that have "pretty significant knock-on effects to everyone's strategy." First, the anticipated Arsenal double game week has been effectively halved, now representing only a single fixture following their cup exit to Southampton. Second, Newcastle's double game week situation has similarly been affected by their own cup elimination, though the specifics depended on their result against Leicester City.

However, the news wasn't universally negative. Sam highlighted a "nice surprise" that emerged from the West Ham versus Leeds United fixture, where whichever team progressed would have their rearranged fixture moved into game week 33. Most predictors hadn't expected this outcome, making it a pleasant bonus for managers who had positioned assets from either side. The hosts confirmed that six teams will be doubling in the upcoming schedule, though exactly which teams and when those doubles fall will create varying strategic windows for managers with different chip availability and team compositions. The one remaining fixture that still needs relocation will "very likely" become a double game week in game week 36, providing a clear target date for managers planning their final wildcard or bench boost deployments.

## Chip Strategy Deep Dive

With the fixture list now settled, Tom and Sam transitioned to a comprehensive discussion of optimal chip strategies for the season's conclusion. They acknowledged that managers will have vastly different situations depending on which chips they have remaining and their current squad composition. The conversation explored how the specific combination of chips a manager holds—whether that's a wildcard plus free hit, triple captain alongside bench boost, or other permutations—should fundamentally alter their approach to the double and blank game weeks ahead.

Tom revealed he had already activated his wildcard, hitting the button late the previous day after "pondering it for a bit." He admitted there was "another strategy" he found attractive but ultimately pulled the trigger for reasons that would become clear during their team reveal discussion. Sam, meanwhile, had made his wildcard decision considerably earlier in the process, demonstrating perhaps more decisive action or alternatively more certainty in his planned approach. The critical point both hosts emphasized is that there is no single "correct" chip strategy for everyone—the optimal play depends heavily on factors like current rank, bank value, team structure, and individual tolerance for risk.

The hosts specifically addressed managers who might be operating with different chip combinations than their own, noting that a "different chip team setup" might mean a "different chip strategy" could be more suitable. For example, managers holding both a wildcard and free hit might prioritize using one in game week 37 while saving the other for the final double, whereas managers with only a bench boost remaining might simply need to time it correctly to their strongest double game week. The discussion acknowledged that those sitting in the top 100,000 like Tom or the 300,000 range like Sam will be operating with different constraints and opportunities than those fighting at the very top of the leaderboard or those in mid-table security.

## Wildcard Team Reveals and Differentiating Strategies

In what became one of the episode's most anticipated segments, Tom and Sam conducted live reveals of their respective wildcard teams—though they confessed they had not yet seen each other's squads, making it genuinely exploratory. Tom's wildcard had been activated the previous evening, and his team was "completely gone now, pretty much" according to his own assessment, with only "two or three players" remaining from his previous squad. This complete rebuild reflects the aggressive repositioning many managers are undertaking as they pivot toward assets that will maximize returns during the upcoming double game weeks.

The discussion around single game week players who could provide differentiation proved particularly valuable. With so many managers expected to concentrate on doubling teams, finding singular assets from single-game teams that nonetheless have favorable fixture runs can create meaningful rank advantages. The hosts explored whether certain players from teams with single fixtures might still warrant inclusion despite their lack of double game week coverage, particularly if they offer exceptional form, set-piece responsibilities, or positional advantages that outweigh the fixture-counting deficit.

## Form Versus Fixture: The Eternal FPL Debate

The traditional "form versus fixture" analysis that typically dominates FPL discussion took a slightly different tone in this episode, with Tom and Sam acknowledging that the unique circumstances of the season's business end require nuanced consideration beyond simple fixture difficulty ratings. With so much on the line and so many variables in flux, the hosts suggested that form might actually matter more than usual in some positions while fixtures become paramount in others, particularly for defenders and goalkeepers where clean sheet potential compounds across double game weeks.

The conversation touched on specific players whose recent form warrants attention regardless of their immediate fixture situation, while also highlighting assets where the opposite is true—players who have been blanking but whose upcoming schedule suggests imminent returns. Morgan Gibbs-White emerged as a specific example from Tom's recent transfers, representing what he called a "vibes-based punt"—an approach that acknowledges sometimes the underlying numbers and underlying opportunity matter more than recent points returns, particularly for budget-enabling assets whose price movement and ownership percentage might make them valuable even without immediate returns.

## Key Strategic Takeaways for FPL Managers

**The FA Cup results have permanently altered the strategic landscape.** Managers who had built their chip strategies around Arsenal and Newcastle doubles now need to recalculate, potentially freeing up chips they had earmarked for those game weeks or requiring repositioning of funds toward other doubling assets. The West Ham-Leeds bonus double in game week 33 creates a new target, while the anticipated final double in game week 36 remains the likely culmination point for managers with remaining chips.

**Chip timing requires individualized planning based on your specific situation.** The hosts emphasized throughout that there is no universal "right" answer for when to play chips like wildcard, free hit, or bench boost. Managers must consider their current rank, chip inventory, team structure, and bank value when making these decisions. Someone in the top 10,000 has vastly different imperatives than someone in the top 100,000 or top 500,000.

**Single-game week differentiators exist and can be valuable.** While the instinct during double game weeks is to flood your team with doubling assets, finding one or two players from single-game teams who offer exceptional form, set-piece value, or positional advantage can create meaningful rank separation from competitors making more conventional choices.

**The final game weeks will be concentrated into a tight window.** With one fixture still needing placement and most doubles clustering toward game weeks 35-37, managers should expect an intense finishing sprint where multiple chips may need to be deployed within a short timeframe. This makes having a clear plan now rather than improvising later absolutely critical.

**Performance in game week 31 nearly didn't require the transfers made.** Sam's admission that his score would have been nearly identical without any activity serves as a reminder that sometimes the transfers managers stress over barely move the needle, while other times—like Tom's situation—transfer decisions become the difference between colors. Context matters enormously in every decision.
