# How AI Will Transform Your Business: Insights from a Former Tesla Executive

The artificial intelligence revolution isn't just a technological shift—it's a fundamental restructuring of how businesses will create value, solve problems, and generate opportunity in the coming decades. That message comes through with unmistakable conviction from a former Tesla executive who has spent his career at the intersection of cutting-edge technology and entrepreneurial creation. In a wide-ranging conversation about AI's trajectory, he draws on decades of experience—from early experiments with neural networks in college to building supply chain systems at one of the world's most innovative companies—to make a compelling case that we're living through one of the most significant periods of economic transformation in human history.

The speaker's perspective is particularly valuable because he brings both technical credibility and business-building experience to the discussion. His time at Tesla gave him firsthand knowledge of how AI systems can be deployed at scale in complex operational environments, while his background in algorithmic trading and multiple startup ventures provides context for understanding how technological shifts create new markets and opportunities. The conversation moves from philosophical questions about technological displacement to concrete examples of AI businesses being built today, offering listeners both inspiration and practical insight into where this technology is heading.

## The Personal Journey: From Neural Networks to the AI Revolution

The speaker begins by tracing his own relationship with artificial intelligence, and the story reveals how long he's been thinking about these technologies. During college, he worked afternoons coding trading algorithms at what he describes as the world's largest options and futures trading platform, located in the Chicago area where he attended school. Even back then, in what he calls the "caveman AI" era, his team was experimenting with the earliest neural networks to gain an edge in markets through automated trading.

What strikes him now, looking back, is the gap between what they were attempting with rudimentary computing power and what has become possible with modern technology. He describes his current mindset with the phrase, "This is what we were trying to do three decades ago," conveying both vindication and excitement about the technological leap that's finally arrived. The analogy to caveman AI isn't self-deprecating—it's an acknowledgment that while their early attempts were primitive by today's standards, they were grappling with the same fundamental questions about how machine learning could be applied to complex decision-making problems.

This personal history matters because it grounds the conversation in practical experience rather than abstract speculation. The speaker isn't just someone who's read about AI in articles; he's someone who was doing the difficult early work of trying to make these systems work, long before they were mainstream concerns. That background gives weight to his optimism about what's now possible.

## The Pattern of Technological Progress: Why Every Revolution Creates More Than It Destroys

The speaker's central thesis about AI centers on a pattern he sees repeating throughout economic history: every major technological revolution creates more jobs and more economic value than it destroys, even when the initial reaction is dominated by fear about displacement. His argument isn't naive optimism—it's grounded in an analysis of how technological change actually works in practice.

He acknowledges that there's "a lot of hand-wringing at the beginning of every technological revolution because humans are really good at seeing the first-order effect, which is job destruction, but they're not good at seeing the second-order effect, which is job creation that happens by entrepreneurs on the back end." This observation gets to the heart of why technological transitions feel threatening even when they ultimately prove beneficial. The jobs that disappear are concrete and visible; the new jobs that will emerge are speculative and invisible until they materialize.

The speaker makes a bold claim: "I cannot name a technical revolution that's happened that's resulted in less GDP and less jobs. It just doesn't work that way." While this statement is presented without explicit qualification, the rest of the conversation suggests he's making a general historical argument about pattern recognition rather than a mathematical guarantee. His confidence in this pattern shapes his entire approach to AI—both as an investor building AI-powered companies and as someone who sees opportunity where others see threat.

## The Telephone Operators: A Case Study in Creative Destruction

To illustrate his point about first-order versus second-order effects, the speaker walks through a detailed historical example involving telephone operators—a story that demonstrates the complexity of how technological displacement actually unfolds. He begins by describing what long-distance phone calls looked like in his youth: you had to dial zero and talk to a human operator, typically a woman in your local town who would literally plug cords into different parts of the phone network to establish your connection. These operators were the physical switches that made the telephone system work.

In the late 1960s and early 1970s, Bell Labs invented electronic switches that could perform this function automatically. By the end of the 1970s, the speaker explains, there were 800,000 people employed as telephone operators in the United States, and they were almost universally recognized as being on the verge of losing their jobs to this new technology. By the late 1970s or early 1980s, 100% of those jobs had indeed disappeared, replaced by electronic switching systems.

But here's where the story gets interesting, and where the speaker's emphasis on second-order effects becomes crucial. The reason electronic switching mattered so much more than just eliminating operator jobs was that it fundamentally changed the economics of making a phone call. Once long-distance calls no longer required human labor and could be handled by electronic signals, the cost of such calls dropped dramatically. This created the conditions for toll-free numbers—those now-familiar 1-800 numbers—that allowed businesses to offer customer service and sales lines that customers could call without charge.

Entrepreneurs seized on this opportunity in ways that nobody could have predicted when the electronic switch was first invented. Companies like 1-800-Flowers, 1-800-Junk, and countless insurance companies built new business models around toll-free dialing. These businesses required centralized call centers to handle the volume of incoming calls, which created an entirely new industry. By the early 1980s, millions of people were employed in call centers—a job category that hadn't existed a decade earlier. Hundreds of software firms were created to serve this emerging industry, including the speaker's own startup in the 1980s.

The speaker uses this example to make a broader point: while people were lamenting the loss of operator jobs, they couldn't see the entrepreneurs who were about to create toll-free dialing businesses, call centers, and the supporting software ecosystem that would eventually employ millions. He tells his own teams that they cannot see through to the other side of the current AI transformation, but they can get a hint of where things might go if they think creatively. This is both practical advice for his organizations and a philosophical stance about how to approach technological uncertainty.

## AI-Powered Supply Chain: A Concrete Example of Transformation

Moving from historical examples to present-day applications, the speaker describes one of his current companies that he finds most exciting: an AI-driven supply chain automation business built by a team that previously created Tesla's supply chain systems. This example demonstrates how AI is being applied to complex business problems and offers insight into what the next generation of AI businesses might look like.

The team at Tesla had built a sophisticated supply chain automation platform well beyond what existed anywhere else in the industry, using machine learning starting around 2017. When the opportunity arose to apply those same skills with modern AI capabilities, the results were dramatically different. The speaker explains that AI allows this team to solve super complex problems much faster than would have been possible with earlier technologies, while also using AI agents to understand the specific workflows and operational requirements of client organizations in a fraction of the time previously needed.

He offers a specific example: the team recently engaged with one of the largest grocery delivery platforms in a particular country (which he declines to name publicly at this stage). The AI system was able to understand the complex work rules and operational structure of this large organization within hours and then design and implement a complete system and workflow within the same timeframe. This speed of implementation stands in stark contrast to traditional enterprise resource planning systems, which typically take nine to twelve months to deploy.

The speaker identifies two key capabilities required to make this work. First, you need genuine expertise in supply chain optimization to know which rules are appropriate, which are problematic, what to tell the AI agents to do, and how to evaluate whether their outputs are correct. Second, you need the ability to supervise and guide the AI agents effectively—judging whether their work is correct or needs to be refined. This hybrid model of human expertise combined with AI capabilities as an "exoskeleton" is what allows the business to grow rapidly while maintaining quality.

The competitive advantage is substantial: the company can implement solutions in days rather than months, and can deliver value back to customers far faster than traditional approaches. The speaker characterizes this as the kind of business that will become increasingly common as AI capabilities continue to expand.

## The Software-as-Labor Question: Will AI Eliminate the Jobs It Creates?

One of the most provocative threads in the conversation addresses a concern that many people are beginning to articulate about the AI transition: if AI systems can essentially perform the work that humans do, then even the new jobs created by AI might eventually be automated away. The speaker frames this directly: the software is essentially working like labor. The system he describes essentially functions as a consultant—coming in, learning workflows, documenting processes, and then improving or automating them.

His interlocutor raises the troubling implication: if even the businesses created by AI will primarily use AI labor, then perhaps all humans become is "mechanics for the robots and for AI." This question represents a genuine philosophical challenge to the optimistic narrative about technological progress, and the speaker's response is notably honest. He acknowledges that he hasn't been able to fully wrap his head around this concern either, and he hasn't heard a compelling case that resolves it completely.

However, he does offer a historical response based on the spreadsheet revolution. When spreadsheets first emerged, they eliminated the need for the human calculators who had worked in skyscrapers, performing the tedious calculations that spreadsheets could perform instantly. And the speaker's point is that people who tell this story often end it there, treating spreadsheet technology as purely a story of job destruction. But the skyscrapers weren't empty. The spreadsheet enabled sophisticated pricing engines that transformed financial markets.

Once digital spreadsheets became available, entire new markets became possible. Traders could price options, futures, and derivatives in ways that were impossible when such calculations required manual computation. The Black-Scholes model and other sophisticated financial tools became available to anyone with a personal computer, enabling markets for financial instruments that couldn't have existed before. These new markets employed hundreds of thousands of people in the financial industry—the speaker specifically mentions options exchanges, the Merc (Chicago Mercantile Exchange), and the ability to syndicate loans as industries that grew from this transformation.

His argument is that the creation of these markets was "unknowable on the other side when the spreadsheet was just being invented." Nobody building the first spreadsheet could have predicted that it would eventually enable the creation of multi-trillion dollar derivatives markets and employment for hundreds of thousands in the financial sector. The same logic, he suggests, applies to AI: the jobs that AI creates may be similarly unpredictable in their specifics, but the historical pattern suggests they will emerge and multiply.

The speaker's ultimate position is that he cannot fully buy into the pessimistic ending point that AI somehow ends job creation permanently. He hasn't seen that pattern in history, and he doesn't think he can fully accept it as inevitable based on current evidence.

The phrase "the highest ELO game" suggests that the speaker sees this as a competition where the stakes are existential for the companies involved—win, and you reshape global economic power; lose, and you become irrelevant.

## Key Takeaways for Business Leaders and Entrepreneurs

**Every technological revolution creates more opportunity than it destroys.** The speaker's historical analysis of telephone operators and spreadsheets demonstrates that job creation from technological change is not just a silver lining—it's the defining characteristic of how these transitions actually unfold. Businesses that understand this pattern can position themselves to capture second-order effects rather than being paralyzed by first-order disruptions.

**AI enables new business models that weren't previously possible.** The supply chain automation example shows that AI isn't just making existing processes faster—it's enabling entirely new approaches to problem-solving. Organizations that implement solutions in days rather than months have fundamental competitive advantages that create new market opportunities.

**Human expertise remains essential, but must be combined with AI capabilities.** The hybrid model of skilled humans using AI as an "exoskeleton" emerges as the speaker's preferred framework. Expertise in domains like supply chain optimization is still necessary to evaluate AI outputs and guide implementation, but AI amplifies what those experts can accomplish by orders of magnitude.

**Creativity and imagination are the primary constraints on finding opportunity in transformation.** While the speaker acknowledges we cannot see through to the other side of the AI revolution, he emphasizes that thinking creatively about second-order effects can reveal hints about where value will be created. Entrepreneurs who can imagine new applications and new markets, rather than simply optimizing existing ones, will capture the most value.

**The scale of investment and competition in AI is unprecedented.** With major technology leaders and enormous capital committed to AI development, the pace of capability advancement is likely to accelerate. Businesses that engage with these technologies early will develop the understanding and experience needed to adapt as the landscape evolves.
