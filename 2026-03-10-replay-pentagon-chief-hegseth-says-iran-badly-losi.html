Pentagon Chief Hegseth Declares Iran "Badly Losing" as Operation Epic Fury Enters Day 11 With Intensifying Strikes

Introduction

In a stark and emotionally charged briefing from the Pentagon on the tenth day of what is now entering its eleventh day, Secretary of Defense Pete Hegseth delivered a forceful update on Operation Epic Fury, the ongoing military campaign against Iran. Speaking alongside the Chairman of the Joint Chiefs of Staff, Hegseth left no doubt about the Trump administration's determination to pursue what he characterized as a "laser focused maximum authority mission" with "ruthless precision" until Iran is "totally and decisively defeated."

The briefing came amid escalating tensions in the Middle East and represented the latest in a series of high-profile Pentagon communications about the campaign. Hegseth, who acknowledged carrying "the weight of brothers lost to Iranian terrorist proxies" from his own military service, framed the operation as a generational fight—one that he suggested his generation of soldiers understands intimately after decades of conflict with Iranian-backed militant groups. The Secretary's remarks, which included direct引用 from Psalm 144 and personal acknowledgments of fallen service members, underscored the gravity with which the administration views the Iranian threat.

The Strategic Framework: Three Core Objectives

Hegseth laid out the administration's three explicit military objectives for Operation Epic Fury, presenting them as a continuation of the mission he had articulated from the first briefing. These objectives, he emphasized, remain unchanged and are being executed with what he described as overwhelming force.

The first objective involves the systematic destruction of Iran's ballistic missile stockpiles, missile launchers, and the entire defense industrial base that produces them. This encompasses not only the weapons themselves but the manufacturing capacity to create new missiles and drones. The Secretary characterized this as essential to preventing Iran from being able to "race toward a nuclear bomb to hold the world hostage," a scenario he declared President Trump would "never allow."

The second objective focuses on the complete destruction of the Iranian Navy. According to the briefing, in the first ten days of the conflict alone, more than fifty Iranian naval ships have been engaged in the campaign using a combination of artillery, fighter aircraft, bombers, and sea-launched missiles. Admiral Cooper, cited in the briefing, noted that an Iranian drone carrier ship had been struck and sunk, and U.S. Central Command continues to hunt and strike mine-laying vessels and storage facilities.

The third objective represents the long-term strategic aim: permanently denying Iran the ability to develop nuclear weapons. This objective is described as the ultimate goal that underpins the entire operation, with the administration making clear that no outcome short of total elimination of Iran's nuclear pathway would constitute success.

Progress Assessment: Quantifying Military Success

The Pentagon officials provided specific metrics to support their assertion that the operation is achieving its objectives. According to the briefing, since the operation began, U.S. Central Command has struck more than 5,000 targets across Iran's southern flank. U.S. Strategic Command bombers have deployed dozens of 2,000-pound GPS-guided penetrating weapons against deeply buried missile launchers, and multiple one-way drone factories have been struck in an effort to neutralize Iran's autonomous weapons capability.

The statistics presented showed dramatic reductions in Iranian offensive capabilities. Ballistic missile attacks from Iran have decreased by 90% from their initial levels, while one-way attack drone launches have declined by 83% since the beginning of the operation. These figures, the officials suggested, demonstrate the effectiveness of the campaign's focus on degrading Iran's military and industrial infrastructure before it can be deployed against American personnel, facilities, and regional partners.

The Chairman specifically highlighted that Iran had fired the lowest number of missiles they had been capable of firing in the last 24 hours—a stark contrast to the intensity of U.S. strikes, which Hegseth announced would be "yet again our most intense day of strikes inside Iran" with the most fighters, bombers, and most refined intelligence operations ever deployed.

Regional Context and International Dynamics

Hegseth addressed the broader geopolitical landscape, asserting that Iran's regional position has significantly weakened during the operation. He noted that Iran's neighbors, including what he described as "former allies in the Gulf," have abandoned both the Iranian regime and its proxy forces. Hezbollah, the Houthis, and Hamas, he claimed, are either "broken, ineffective, or on the sidelines" as a result of the campaign and previous military pressures.

The Secretary specifically accused Iranian forces of deliberately positioning military assets in civilian infrastructure, stating that "they fire missiles from schools and hospitals, deliberately, deliberately targeting innocents because they know their military is being systematically degraded and annihilated." This characterization was presented as justification for the intensive nature of the strikes and the administration's insistence that the operation would continue until total victory is achieved.

The briefing also acknowledged the ongoing role of regional partners, with U.S. forces and allied nations along the southern flank continuing to execute intercepts against one-way attack drones using fighter aircraft and attack helicopters.

Honoring the Fallen: The Human Cost of Conflict

The briefing took a solemn turn as both officials dedicated significant time to honoring American service members who had lost their lives in the operation. The Chairman, who had just returned from Dover Air Force Base the previous night where fallen troops were being returned to American soil, read aloud the names and honored the memories of four service members.

Major Jeffrey O'Brien and Chief Warrant Officer 3 Robert Marzen, both from the U.S. Army Reserves 103rd Sustainment Command out of Des Moines, Iowa, were the final two fallen to return home. Staff Sergeant Benjamin Pennington of the U.S. Army's First Space Brigade from Fort Carson, Colorado, was honored in a ceremony the previous night. The Chairman shared that he had met Pennington's family—his mother, father, and sister—who showed him a drawing Ben had made in kindergarten that clearly demonstrated his lifelong ambition to serve his country as a soldier. The Chairman described Pennington as "absolutely a great one."

Additionally, Major Sorfleet Davius was welcomed home after passing away from a medical condition while on duty in Kuwait. The Chairman expressed the nation's gratitude to all the families, stating that their sacrifice and the example set by their service members would never be forgotten and that they would forever be part of the joint force family.

The Carrier Fleet: Behind the Scenes of Naval Operations

In an unusual departure from typical military briefings, the Chairman took time to highlight the work of sailors aboard the Ford Carrier Strike Group, noting that their deployment had been extended after "months at sea" and that they continue to "take the fight to the enemy over and over again, night after night."

He specifically recognized the sailors aboard the USS Ford and USS Abraham Lincoln—particularly those known for wearing yellow shirts, who are responsible for operating the catapults, taxiing jets around the flight deck, and managing the complex ballet of launching and recovering aircraft. These young sailors, with an average age in their early twenties, represent what the Chairman called "the unsung heroes of naval aviation."

He painted a vivid picture of their working conditions: "30 knots of wind in your face, the deck is slippery, covered in grease, it's noisy, there are propellers spinning, there's jet blast everywhere, helicopters are running, your head is on a swivel." Yet despite these conditions, these sailors direct multi-million dollar fighter jets into precise landing positions, enabling naval aviators to conduct America's operations. The Chairman described this as "a beautiful symphony of jets" executed by a world-class team combining naval aviation professionals with the aviators in those aircraft.

Political and Strategic Philosophy: Defining the Mission

Hegseth took explicit care to distinguish Operation Epic Fury from previous American military engagements in the Middle East. "This is not 2003," he declared. "This is not endless nation building under those types of quagmires we saw under Bush or Obama. It's not even close."

The Secretary characterized the current approach as one focused on "decisive victory with brutal efficiency, total air dominance, and an unbreakable will to accomplish the president's objectives on our timeline." He emphasized that his "generation of soldier will not let that happen again" and that President Trump had "very clearly ran against those kinds of neverending, nebulously scoped missions."

The messaging made clear that the administration views the operation as a bounded campaign with clear objectives and a defined endpoint—rather than an open-ended commitment. Hegseth stated explicitly: "We do so on our timeline and at our choosing," suggesting that while the intensity would increase, the duration of the conflict remained within American control.

Religious Dimension and Closing Remarks

In an unusual inclusion for a Pentagon briefing, Secretary Hegseth closed with a direct quotation from Psalm 144, describing it as drawing strength from scripture. He read: "Blessed be the Lord, my rock, who trains my hands for war and my fingers for battle. He is my loving God and my fortress, my stronghold and my deliverer, my shield in whom I take refuge."

He prayed for the Lord to "grant unyielding strength" to American warriors, "unbreakable protection" to service members and the homeland, and "total victory over those who seek to harm them" before concluding with "God bless our troops and this mission."

Key Takeaways

The administration has established measurable benchmarks for success. The 90% reduction in ballistic missile attacks and 83% decrease in drone attacks provide concrete metrics that officials are using to demonstrate progress, even as they promise intensified strikes ahead.

The campaign is explicitly designed to eliminate Iran's military production capacity, not merely respond to attacks. By targeting drone factories, missile production facilities, and the defense industrial base, the operation aims to create a long-term reduction in Iranian offensive capabilities rather than simply punishing previous aggression.

American military leadership is acutely aware of the political and generational stakes. Hegseth's explicit comparisons to the Iraq and Afghanistan wars, combined with his references to personal losses and his generation's understanding of the fight, signals that the Pentagon is sensitive to domestic political concerns about endless conflict.

The operational tempo will continue to increase. With the announcement that each day would be "our most intense day of strikes inside Iran," the briefing made clear that the administration intends to apply overwhelming pressure rather than negotiate a settlement.

The human cost is being acknowledged publicly and prominently. By dedicating substantial briefing time to honoring fallen service members and highlighting the grueling work of carrier deck sailors, the Pentagon appears to be simultaneously preparing the American public for potential casualties while building support for the mission.

Who This Briefing Is For

This briefing is primarily directed at several audiences simultaneously. American citizens and policymakers receive reassurance that the military campaign is achieving measurable progress and is bounded by clear objectives rather than endless commitment. Regional allies and partners in the Gulf and elsewhere receive signals that American resolve remains strong and that Iranian military capacity is being systematically degraded. Potential adversaries receive explicit warning that strikes will intensify and that the administration will not be deterred by international pressure or negotiation demands.

The briefing also serves an internal military audience, acknowledging the sacrifices of service members and their families while building morale by framing the mission as historically significant and winnable. For journalists covering Middle Eastern affairs, congressional members overseeing defense policy, and defense analysts studying American military strategy, this briefing provides essential insight into how the Trump administration is presenting its approach to what it characterizes as an existential threat from Iran.
