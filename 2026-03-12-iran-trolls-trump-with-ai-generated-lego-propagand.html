Iran trolls Trump with AI-generated Lego propaganda video • FRANCE 24 English

Introduction

The war between Iran and the United States has entered its thirteenth day, and the battle is no longer confined to traditional battlefields. Beyond the physical conflict involving missiles, strikes, and casualties, a parallel struggle is unfolding across social media platforms—one where AI-generated videos, memes, and internet culture have become weapons of choice. This new frontier of digital warfare represents a significant shift in how nations communicate their narratives and troll their adversaries during armed conflicts.

In a segment featured on FRANCE 24 English, reporter Vedica Bahal examined how both Iran and the Trump administration have embraced an unconventional social media playbook that blends artificial intelligence, memes, and pop culture references to advance their respective agendas. The analysis reveals a fascinating dynamic where the United States' own tactics are being mirrored and weaponized back against it, demonstrating how the boundaries between traditional state propaganda and internet culture have become increasingly blurred.

The Emergence of AI Propaganda in the Iran-US Conflict

The conflict has witnessed Iran adopting a strategy that closely mimics the approach long employed by the Trump administration's social media strategy. According to Bahal, Iran through its state news agency released an AI-generated video depicting former President Donald Trump in a Lego animation format. This video, titled "Narrative of Victory," was explicitly designed to troll the American leader and undermine his administration's messaging surrounding the conflict.

The content of this video is notable for its sophisticated political messaging. It accuses Trump of initiating the conflict specifically to distract from the Jeffrey Epstein files—a conspiracy theory that has circulated in certain political circles. The video depicts Trump and Israeli Prime Minister Netanyahu looking at pictures of Jeffrey Epstein, after which Trump becomes frustrated and presses a large red button to deploy a missile. The video also references the tragic attack on a girls' school in Minab, Iran, which killed numerous civilians, implying American responsibility for the strike.

The sophistication of these AI-generated materials extends beyond simple ridicule. A second video released by Iran shows Ayatollah Ali Khamenei's son being named as a successor, with footage depicting Trump sweating as crude oil prices skyrocket and an American attempt at regime change in Iran failing. Through these productions, Iran is not merely responding to American rhetoric but actively constructing an alternative narrative that presents itself as the victim of American aggression while depicting the United States and Israel as aggressors.

The American Social Media Offensive

The Trump administration has been quick to employ its own social media strategy throughout the conflict. As Bahal explains, the official White House account has been publishing a series of war propaganda videos filtered through the lens of internet culture. One notable example splices unclassified missile detonation videos with footage from SpongeBob SquarePants, creating a jarring juxtaposition between military violence and children's entertainment.

Another video, titled "Strike" on social media, depicts an American bowler striking pins while cutting to a bizarre representation of Iranian regime officials as bowling pins—a metaphor celebrating the strikes against Iran. The administration also shared a video titled "Justice the American Way," which montage clips from films like Tropic Thunder, Dragon Ball Z, and Top Gun with military footage. Significantly, this video was shared less than a week after an air strike in Minab killed numerous girls at a school, and just twenty-four hours after the Pentagon confirmed two Americans were killed by a drone.

Perhaps most controversially, the White House released a video comparing the war in Iran to a video game, splicing missile detonation footage with Nintendo Wii footage and light-hearted music. Critics have characterized this approach as "gifying" the war—transforming military violence into entertainment content that feels detached from its real-world consequences.

Public Response and Criticism

The social media reaction to these materials has been predominantly negative. Users have widely criticized the White House administration for what they perceive as an unserious and embarrassing approach to war communication. Multiple posts from social media users, including a US Army veteran of the Iraq war, have emphasized that war is not a game and cannot be treated as entertainment content.

One particularly pointed comment observed: "Hundreds of people are dead. Little girls are dead. Six Americans are dead. Millions across the Middle East are terrified. It's not a game. It's not a meme. It's not another chance to troll the libs. It is war." This sentiment encapsulates the widespread concern that such content minimizes the genuine human cost of military conflict.

The virality of these videos, critics argue, is deliberately designed to distract from criticism of the war itself. By wrapping military action in familiar and entertaining formats, the administration potentially numbs audiences to the gravity of what's actually occurring in the Middle East.

Analysis: The New Era of Meme Warfare

The conflict between Iran and the United States represents what Bahal describes as "a new stage of trolling in this war"—a meme war occurring simultaneously with actual physical conflict. The back-and-forth between the two nations demonstrates how internet culture and digital propaganda have become central to modern warfare.

What's particularly noteworthy is Iran's apparent strategy of responding in kind to American styles of communication. Rather than relying solely on traditional state media channels, Iran has embraced the exact same tactics the US employs—AI generation, meme culture, pop culture references—to construct and disseminate its own version of events. This reciprocal dynamic suggests that the landscape of international communication during conflicts has fundamentally changed.

The Archdiocese of Chicago's response to this phenomenon offers a sobering perspective: "This horrifying portrayal demonstrates that we now live in an era where the distance between the battlefield and the living room has been drastically reduced." The gamification and entertainment-ification of warfare through social media content creates a dangerous psychological distance between audiences and the real consequences of military action.

Key Takeaways

First, the boundaries between entertainment and warfare have collapsed. The use of video games, children's cartoons, and pop culture references in official government communications about active military conflicts represents a fundamental shift in how wars are communicated to the public. This approach fundamentally alters how audiences process information about casualties, strikes, and geopolitical developments.

Second, AI-generated propaganda is becoming accessible to nation-states. Iran's ability to produce sophisticated AI videos depicting American political figures demonstrates that these technologies are no longer limited to well-resourced tech companies or Western governments. The democratization of such tools has significant implications for information integrity during conflicts.

Third, the "meme war" is a genuine strategic dimension of modern conflict. What might seem frivolous or amusing on the surface represents a calculated communication strategy. Both sides are using these tools to shape narratives, undermine opponents, and communicate with domestic and international audiences in ways traditional propaganda could not achieve.

Fourth, public trust in official war communications faces new challenges. When military action is presented alongside SpongeBob and Nintendo Wii content, the credibility and seriousness of government messaging becomes questionable. This erosion of traditional authority in communication carries implications for public support and understanding of military operations.

Fifth, the human cost of war risks being minimized. Critics are correct that the entertainment format of these communications makes violence feel abstract and distant, potentially desensitizing audiences to genuine suffering and casualties that represent real human tragedy.

Who Should Pay Attention to This Development

This segment is essential viewing for several distinct audiences. International relations scholars and policy analysts will find the strategic dynamics of meme warfare particularly relevant as they examine how digital communication tools reshape traditional statecraft. Journalists and media professionals should note the implications for covering conflicts where official sources distribute entertainment-style content alongside traditional press materials. Anyone interested in the intersection of technology, politics, and communication will find this a compelling case study in how AI and social media are transforming information warfare.

The broader public should also be aware that the content consumed during conflicts is increasingly manufactured for specific psychological effects. Understanding that official government communications about military operations may be deliberately designed to entertain rather than inform represents a critical media literacy challenge for contemporary audiences.

The Iran-US conflict serves as a preview of how future wars will likely be communicated—not through traditional press conferences and carefully worded statements, but through AI-generated content, memes, and social media posts designed to go viral. Whether this represents progress in transparency or a dangerous erosion of seriousness in matters of life and death remains to be seen, but the phenomenon is unmistakably now underway.
