I Need A Cuddle | Planet FPL S. 9 Ep. 46 | GW31 Review

Introduction: When Your Only Joy Comes From Others' Misery

The latest episode of Planet FPL finds hosts Serge and James in a peculiar position—commiserating together over a brutal Gameweek 31 that saw both their Premier League teams suffer defeats, leaving them languishing at 17th and 18th in the actual table. But in true FPL fashion, their shared misery became a source of dark humor, with the hosts noting that the only bright spot in their weekend was Arsenal's defeat in the Carabao Cup final. "It's a bad weekend when your joy comes from other people's defeats," James acknowledged, encapsulating the bleak reality of a week where eight games yielded limited returns and both hosts found themselves on red arrows.

The episode arrives at a pivotal moment in the FPL calendar. With only eight fixtures in Gameweek 31 and a two-week break incoming due to FA Cup quarterfinals, managers are facing critical decisions about whether to wildcard now or wait. Serge, who is departing the country in three days, revealed he plans to hold his wildcard until after the FA Cup weekend to see how the fixture landscape develops. The hosts emphasized that this break is a chance to "switch off, log out, and take a break" because "there's a lot that's going to happen in the next couple of weeks" when the season reaches its climax.

This review covers eight matches from Gameweek 31 while exploring the strategic implications of the upcoming double gameweek situation, the form of key players like Harry Wilson and Bruno Fernandes, and the psychological toll of a season that has seen both hosts suffer five consecutive red arrows.

The Personal Nightmare: Both Teams in Freefall

Serge's Catastrophic Week

Serge opened up about his harrowing Gameweek 31 experience, detailing a sequence of events that left him nursing another red arrow—his fifth in succession. The horror began when his captain, Harry Wilson, delivered a remarkable 26 points in a blank gameweek with just eight fixtures. "You're thinking, 'All right, this could be all right,'" Serge recalled, only to watch his remaining picks implode. From Harry Wilson, he received points, along with defcons from Egot Thiago and Van Dijk—the latter a player who "never seems to get actually get a defcon because I feel like every time I've had a defcon from him, he's either conceded more than two or himself booked."

The suffering didn't end there. Serge revealed he had sold Freddie Antonio for Harry Wilson, a transfer that appeared doomed from the start. "I did think as soon as [Palmer] got injured in the warm-up and Freddy Pots was shot into the lineup, he's guaranteed to score just to make my transfer from Pots to Wilson worthless," Serge explained. "Didn't happen." His final tally of 51 points represented another red arrow, dropping him from 1.21 million to 1.225 million in rank. "It's not the biggest red arrow, but it's not green," he acknowledged.

What made the week particularly painful was the absence of clean sheet points. "A lot of people had Brentford like a Keller or something like that," Serge noted, identifying where he lost ground. His squad featured Bruno Fernandes, Harry Wilson, and Van Dijk—no clean sheets meant no bonus points from his defenders. The defensive choices particularly hurt: Alderete, Van Dijk (three points), Brobbey, and Fernandez all returned nothing. "That's what's ended up hurting me," Serge concluded.

James's Rollercoaster Ride

James fared slightly better with 58 points but still dropped approximately 750 places to around 14,000 in rank. His week was defined by the volatility of the Bournemouth versus Manchester United match, which he described as "so swingy in terms of individual players."

Like Serge, James captained Harry Wilson and expressed relief when the Fulham player scored nine points. "It's still a good return. If you'd have told me to start the week Wilson gets nine points, I'd have been very happy with that," he admitted. However, James's weekend started catastrophically when Bruno Fernandes returned well on Friday night with a 13-pointer, immediately putting him "14 points down against the field straight away on Friday night." This was followed by James Hill's own goal adding "insult to injury" with no defcon to accompany it.

James's team featured Brighton against Liverpool, whereEcate went off early, though Brighton scored early. Van Dijk delivered only a three-pointer. Jockey Manderson provided a late defcon hit in the Fulham game against Burnley for a four-pointer. Notably, James had no coverage of the Everton versus Chelsea match but did have the "treble Brentford going into the Leeds game"—a decision that paid off when Dango wasn't in the starting lineup, leaving James wondering "what's going on here?" Most impressively, Eigor Thiago delivered a defcon that James found "the most entertaining aspect of it" while watching, noting that "he must be in defcons here" given how he was "getting his head on everything that leads had from a set piece perspective." Keller's clean sheet turned a seven-pointer on Saturday night into a nine-pointer after the update—a welcome surprise.

The Strategic Dilemma: To Wildcard or Not to Wildcard

Serge's Wildcard Plans

Serge laid out his thinking for the upcoming gameweeks, confirming he is "logging out" and will "hit the wildcard button" after the FA Cup weekend. His frustration with his current squad was evident as he listed players he no longer understands: "Robert Sanchez being one, Enzo Fernandez being one, Brian Brobbey being one." He added, "I don't need Alderete. I can probably lose Jarrod Bowen maybe depending on how the fixtures fall."

The appeal of wildcarding is clear—Serge has "a triple Arsenal there in reserve" and wants to rebuild around better fixtures. However, he acknowledged the complexity of the decision: "It depends what happens with the fixtures in terms of the doubles blanks afterwards."

The Warning About Premature Wildcards

The hosts issued a crucial warning to managers considering an immediate wildcard: some may find themselves "wild card into a team that looks worse than what they have in 32." James elaborated that this depends entirely on "what happens with the fixtures in terms of the doubles blanks afterwards."

They discussed how Gameweek 31 might become a "dead end 32" for some managers, particularly if Southampton win and Port Vale win in the FA Cup quarterfinals. "We spoke about this weeks ago about the idea that dead end 31 might for some people become dead end 32," Serge explained. The advice is clear: "Wait" before making wildcard decisions.

The Form vs. Fixtures Paradox

A significant discussion centered on the tension between picking players based on fixture doubles versus their current form. James noted that "Chelsea obviously if Chelsea win and double and you want three of them but they don't—they don't look in great form right." Even a bad Chelsea team, he acknowledged, can still deliver, but the form is worrying.

The example of Brighton was particularly instructive: "Brighton against Burnley and then actually Brighton's double at the moment or top away Chelsea at home—you wouldn't at the start of the season go that's a great double, but actually right now right now yeah." Similarly, "Liverpool as well like they could beat City in a single game but they're not in like great form, are they at the moment?" The implication is clear—managers are buying these teams because they double based on paper, but when they "trust what you see with your eyes," the selections become more questionable.

Player Analysis: Winners, Losers, and Curious Cases

Harry Wilson: The Controversial Captain

Both hosts had captained Harry Wilson, with divergent outcomes. Serge was pleased with 26 points, while James secured a more modest nine points but was "relieved" given Wilson's reputation as a "game kind of player, isn't he?" Serge acknowledged that "Harry Wilson doesn't look great. Liverpool away, but we've seen again at the weekend like he is any game kind of player." James's bench featured Van Dijk (Fulham home), Timber (played but didn't start), Gabriel, Saliba, and White—indicating a strong Arsenal contingency awaiting activation.

Van Dijk: The Defcon Curse

Van Dijk's persistent failure to deliver defcon points was discussed at length. Serge explained: "I feel like every time I've had a defcon from him, he's either conceded more than two or himself booked. And so, you never feel like you're actually getting the points off him." Despite this, Van Dijk remains a popular pick due to Liverpool's fixtures and potential for clean sheets.

James Hill: From Big Tasty to Little Tasty

The hosts couldn't resist mocking James Hill's transformation, referencing a screenshot shared by Martin showing the Chelsea defender "from sofa school where he looked absolutely tiny." Serge quipped: "So he's no longer big tasty. He's just little tasty." James added: "I think we call that just the cheeseburger." This lighthearted moment underscored the camaraderie between the hosts despite their collective struggles.

Eigor Thiago: The Entertaining Defcon

Eigor Thiago's late defcon in the Brentford match was highlighted as a rare bright spot. James noted: "Watching the game that was the most entertaining aspect of it and I thought he's—he must be in defcons here. It felt like he was on the head getting his head on everything that leads had from a set piece perspective." The defcon delivered four points in a game where Brentford kept a clean sheet.

Keller: The Sneaky Nine-Pointer

Brentford's goalkeeper Keller provided unexpected value when his clean sheet was upgraded from seven to nine points in the post-match update. "That was pretty nice," James admitted, having owned the Brentford defense as part of his "treble."

Malik Chow: The Popular Pick That Failed

Malik Chow's absence from Newcastle's starting lineup caught many managers off guard. James noted this was "a top reference for many people this week in terms of like a one-week defender signing or even a two-week defender signing." His exclusion represented a significant trap for managers who had invested in Newcastle's defense based on the promise of playing time.

The Upcoming Break: Strategy and Mindset

The Three-Week Gap Explained

The hosts clarified the upcoming schedule: "We've got a two-week break followed by FA Cup. So we've actually got a three-week break from FPL and Premier League." This means Gameweek 32 will be a blank gameweek for most teams, with only those involved in FA Cup quarterfinals potentially playing. The break includes international football, adding another layer of uncertainty around player fitness and potential injuries.

The Advice: Log Out and Wait

Both hosts strongly recommended taking a break from FPL content and decision-making. "Everybody should be in the mindset of switch off, log out, and take a break," Serge emphasized. The intensity of the season, combined with the upcoming fixture congestion, makes this an ideal time for managers to reset and avoid rash decisions. "Because there's a lot that's going to happen in the next couple of weeks," Serge added, hinting at the dramatic swings that characterize the business end of the season.

James confirmed he hadn't even looked at his "bus team" (his main squad), indicating how little energy he had for FPL after his difficult week. The hosts' shared exhaustion spoke to the emotional toll that FPL can take, particularly when both one's actual team and fantasy team are performing poorly.

Key Takeaways for FPL Managers

1. Wait Before Wildcarding

The hosts emphasized that Gameweek 32's fixture picture remains unclear, and wildcarding now could result in a worse team than you currently have. If your squad looks okay for Gameweek 32, consider waiting until after the FA Cup weekend to see which teams secure doubles and which blank.

2. Form Matters More Than Fixtures Sometimes

While double gameweeks are attractive, picking teams in poor form (Chelsea, Liverpool) may prove counterproductive. "When you trust what you see with your eyes, does it?" James asked, highlighting the importance of watching actual performances rather than relying solely on fixture lists.

3. The Break Is a Strategic Opportunity

With three weeks between meaningful Premier League action, managers have time to assess their squads, research potential transfers, and most importantly, rest mentally. "Switch off, log out, and take a break" isn't just relaxing advice—it's strategic wisdom to avoid making emotional decisions during a stressful period.

4. Avoid Popular Picks That Might Flop

Malik Chow's omission demonstrated that popular one-week defenders can fail to even start. Researching team news and understanding rotation risks is crucial, especially with the fixture congestion approaching.

5. Clean Sheets Win Gameweeks

Both hosts suffered from lacking clean sheet coverage in Gameweek 31. With limited matches, the teams that kept clean sheets (Brentford, Arsenal, Liverpool in various matches) delivered significant value. Prioritizing defensive coverage in future gameweeks, particularly for teams with good clean sheet potential, remains essential.

This episode serves FPL managers who are navigating the tricky period between Gameweek 31 and the season's conclusion.
