# The Shadow Economy Behind AI's Most Valuable Companies

In the world of high-profile AI companies, there's a parallel marketplace operating just out of sight—a multi-billion dollar shadow economy where shares of Anthropic, SpaceX, and OpenAI change hands through a tangled web of brokers, syndicates, and platform middlemen. Most investors will never access this market directly, yet it shapes valuations, influences who gets to participate in the AI boom, and has become a surprisingly lucrative profession for those positioned within it.

Dio Casares, a principal at Patagon, recently appeared on the Bankless podcast to pull back the curtain on this secretive ecosystem. With Patagon running both a proprietary investing business and a client-facing service that helps people find secondary market access, Casares occupies a unique vantage point from which to observe the mechanics, participants, and tensions that define this hotly contested space.

The conversation illuminates how a combination of gargantuan private company valuations, extended timeframes before any public offering, and fierce demand for exposure to leading AI firms has created conditions for an emergent market that operates with minimal transparency—and where the rules remain largely unwritten.

---

# Understanding the Secondary Market Structure

## Two Flavors of Secondaries

Casares distinguishes between what the industry calls "primary secondaries" and true secondary transactions, though he acknowledges the terminology can feel contradictory. In primary secondaries, net new money actually reaches the company. Special Purpose Vehicles (SPVs)—legal entities created to hold shares on behalf of multiple investors—are formed and layered, sometimes as "SPVs on top of SPVs," to inject capital into funding rounds. This benefits companies because they're selling shares and receiving proceeds, while employees who sell approved shares through these mechanisms also convert equity into cash with company blessing.

True secondary transactions involve cashing out shares that were already purchased from the company—someone who acquired equity at an earlier round selling to a new buyer. This has traditionally been the domain of venture capitalists and early investors waiting for an IPO or acquisition to exit. But as Casares explains, the dynamics have shifted dramatically: "You have funding rounds that are multiples of that. And so, obviously, the liquidity profile and the time frame has changed."

## Why This Market Emerged

Two secular trends have created the conditions for this secondary market explosion. First, company valuations have grown astronomical. When a single funding round can reach billions of dollars, the absolute dollar amounts flowing through private markets dwarf anything seen in previous eras. Second, companies are staying private far longer than historical norms. This extended private phase gives the market time to mature, attract more participants, and develop increasingly sophisticated infrastructure—participants who have every incentive to keep these transactions informal and opaque.

# The Approved and Unapproved Markets

## Company-Sanctioned Transactions

Anthropic and OpenAI have not been naive to the existence of secondary markets. Both companies have implemented approved mechanisms to channel demand into controlled transactions. The most significant is the employee tender offer, where companies allow workers to sell shares directly at the current round price—Anthropic has permitted tenders of up to $30 million per employee. This serves a dual purpose: it provides liquidity to insiders who might otherwise seek informal buyers, while simultaneously removing sell pressure from the chaotic secondary market before it materializes elsewhere.

Companies also work directly with large private equity firms through "direct deals"—transactions that appear on official CapTables and receive company approval. These firms then syndicate the investment opportunity to their networks, charging fees for their facilitation services. Crucially, the company receives the capital, making these transactions competitive rather than extractive.

## The Dark Market: Platforms and Problems

Against this backdrop of sanctioned transactions stands an entirely different ecosystem—one that Anthropic and other companies actively disdain and frequently target with cease-and-desist letters. Platforms like Hiive and Forge operate by identifying large blocks of shares, then mass-emailing their entire user bases—Casares notes these platforms "don't take KYC, doesn't do any kind of checks, anyone can set up"—advertising available inventory at prices often pitched as discounts to prevailing secondary market rates.

This behavior creates multiple problems for the companies being traded. When informal platforms advertise 20% discounts on shares, it undermines the company's ability to raise capital in new rounds at favorable terms. As Casares explains, "They're in the bargain business. They're trying to find blocks below the prevailing secondary price or the round price." If prospective investors can simply wait for discounted shares through these channels, why would they participate in primary funding rounds at higher prices?

There's also a psychological dimension. Price spreads in secondary markets—where sellers demand low prices and buyers ask high prices—can signal market inactivity or lack of confidence. Companies want unified, strong price discovery, not fragmented trading at unpredictable discounts.

# The Legal Minefield

## Securities Law Compliance

Beyond competitive concerns, these informal secondary markets carry genuine legal risk. In the United States, securities regulations require a six-month holding period for shares of private companies before they can be freely resold. When platforms facilitate rapid buying and selling—where shares trade "back and forth" repeatedly—each transaction potentially violates this rule.

Casares acknowledges that some platforms may have engineered workarounds: "Maybe they've done something on the back end where they have kind of an obfuscation of that." But he notes that "US regulators tend to think if you're dealing with assets that have a US nexus that they have purview." This creates a compliance obligation for the companies themselves. Anthropic cannot simply look away when it becomes aware of unauthorized secondary trading. If the company knows these markets exist, it faces pressure to take action—lest regulators accuse it of complicity in securities violations.

# The Scale of Opportunity

## $200 Billion and Growing

Measuring this market precisely proves difficult given its informal nature, but Casares offers some striking figures. Private market fundraising now exceeds the total value of IPOs, a shift that has persisted for several years. Recorded secondary market transactions exceed $200 billion—a figure that likely understates true volume given how much happens off-record.

The economics for intermediaries are staggering. On many Anthropic deals, brokers charge 10% one-time fees plus carry—meaning if $10 billion flows through secondary channels to clients, roughly $1 billion accrues to brokers and facilitators. This has predictable effects on where talent flows. "Even people at funds," Casares observes, "they make more money doing these kinds of transactions now than doing primary capital into deals. And so a lot of people are shifting into this market because of that."

## The Hinge Profile Economy

The cultural absurdity of this market has begun entering mainstream consciousness. Casares references a San Francisco dating app profile—a Hinge user who listed "friends with people at Anthropic" as his most salient attribute, promising "zero fee commission for dates." The implication is stark: access to Anthropic shares has become a form of social currency, a status signal in tech circles as valuable as any professional credential.

Another viral tweet captured the phenomenon more directly: a broker who noted that facilitating a single Anthropic secondary deal generated more income than their entire net worth accumulated over two decades of working. Casares confirms he spoke with this individual directly, describing the case as "relatively interesting." The message is clear: for those positioned within the right networks, this secondary market represents not just investment opportunity but generational wealth generation through intermediation alone.

# How the Access Economy Functions

## The Three Types of Players

Understanding how this market actually operates requires grasping its three distinct participant categories. First, there are those who possess access—relationships with companies, early investors, or employees who hold shares and might be willing to sell. They monetize this access by connecting sellers with counterparties.

Second, there are those who possess buyers—family offices, high-net-worth individuals, or funds seeking exposure to the hottest private companies but lacking direct relationships. These intermediaries find demand and match it with supply.

Third, there are rare individuals and firms who do both. Patagon represents this category, simultaneously maintaining relationships with companies raising capital and networks of clients seeking allocation. This dual positioning allows them to capture value at multiple points in the transaction chain.

The result, as Casares describes it, is a market characterized by "wild west reputation" and "insider setup." Regular investors cannot simply approach Anthropic and request a million-dollar allocation in the current funding round. The barriers are intentional—companies want control over who owns their equity, and the existing shareholder base has every incentive to preserve their preferential positioning.

---

# Who Benefits and Who Loses

## Winners in the Current Structure

Established venture funds, family offices with existing relationships, and sophisticated intermediaries with network access have all positioned themselves to extract significant value from this structure. Employees at companies like Anthropic benefit from tender offers that let them diversify wealth accumulated in illiquid equity. Large PE firms raising direct deals can charge substantial fees for their facilitation services.

## Challenges on the Horizon

Several pressures threaten to disrupt this equilibrium. Regulators may eventually move against platforms that facilitate rapid secondary trading in violation of securities law, particularly as these transactions involve US-based companies and investors. Companies themselves have shown willingness to crack down on unauthorized secondary sellers, and the existence of sanctioned tender programs reduces the pool of desperate sellers seeking informal buyers.

The greatest irony may be that success could eventually undermine the market. If companies like Anthropic eventually IPO, the massive secondary market for their shares would compress dramatically—public markets would provide the liquidity that currently only sophisticated insiders can access. The question is not whether this market will eventually normalize, but how much value will be captured by intermediaries before that normalization arrives.

# Conclusion

The secondary market for Anthropic stock represents something genuinely novel in capital markets: a multi-billion dollar, largely unregulated ecosystem where shares of the world's most valuable private companies trade through informal networks, broker relationships, and platform intermediaries. Dio Casares's analysis reveals a market driven by structural forces—gigantic valuations, extended private holding periods, and insatiable demand for AI exposure—that show no signs of abating.

For investors permanently excluded from these transactions, the story is sobering. The AI boom is generating extraordinary wealth, but the distribution mechanisms favor those with pre-existing relationships and network access. For those inside the system, the economics remain extraordinarily attractive—10% fees on billion-dollar deals have a way of concentrating talent and capital in whatever channels those fees flow through.

The market will continue evolving. Companies will refine their approaches to employee liquidity, regulators will eventually establish clearer boundaries, and public offerings may one day bring these shares to wider markets. Until then, the shadow economy will persist—Wild West reputation intact, insider setup firmly in place, and hundreds of billions of dollars flowing through channels most people will never see.
