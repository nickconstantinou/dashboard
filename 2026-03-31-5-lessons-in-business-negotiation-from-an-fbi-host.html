5 Lessons in Business Negotiation from an FBI Hostage Negotiator: Christopher Voss on Transforming Your Approach to Deals

Introduction

Christopher Voss spent twenty-four years as an FBI hostage negotiator, handling some of the most high-stakes situations imaginable—negotiations where lives hung in the balance and where a single misstep could prove catastrophic. After retiring from the Bureau, Voss discovered something surprising: the psychological principles that kept people alive in hostage situations were remarkably applicable to the conference rooms where business deals are made. In this compelling presentation, Voss challenges conventional business negotiation wisdom and offers a radically different framework for achieving better outcomes—one built on empathy, information gathering, and trust rather than leverage, positional bargaining, and compromise.

The core insight Voss presents is deceptively simple: most business negotiators approach deals with fundamentally flawed assumptions about what actually drives successful outcomes. They enter negotiations focused on making their case, calculating leverage, and determining which items are "must-haves" versus "giveaways." Voss argues this approach is backwards—it prioritizes the negotiator's own position over understanding the other party's actual needs, pressures, and hidden information. The result is suboptimal deals that leave value on the table and damage the relationships that enable long-term business success.

For executives who have spent years in negotiations and believe they already understand the landscape, Voss offers a sobering challenge: the very experience that makes them confident may also be what's holding them back from achieving truly excellent results. His message isn't about learning new tricks or tactics—it's about fundamentally reorienting how one thinks about the negotiation process itself.

The First Lesson: You Always Have Hidden Information

Voss opens with a provocative challenge to a piece of conventional negotiation wisdom. He recalls a book—though he can't recall its exact title—that contained what it called the "ten commandments of negotiation." One of these commandments declared "it is what it is," essentially advising negotiators to trust their experience and go with their gut. Voss disagrees with this completely, and his reasoning reveals something profound about the nature of business negotiations.

He asks executives a simple question: when is there ever a negotiation where you don't have closely held information you don't want to reveal to the other side? This might include budgets, pressures from other deals, quarterly profit demands, board expectations, or other proprietary concerns. Every executive, Voss observes, has information that "would change everything" if the other side knew about it. And here's the crucial realization—the other side has the same situation. They too have information that would change everything if you knew it.

This creates what Voss calls a fundamental problem: if both sides are operating with imperfect information based on their own positions, then the "must-haves" and "giveaways" they've identified may be completely wrong. The executive who thinks they know exactly what they need and what they're willing to give up is actually working from an incomplete picture. The solution isn't to make a better case or apply more leverage—it's to sit down, gather information, build trust, and then rethink what the best deal actually looks like.

The challenge, Voss acknowledges, is that this is genuinely difficult to get experienced executives to do. They're confident in their read of situations. They've "seen this before." But that very confidence may be preventing them from seeing what's actually there. Getting out of this perspective—seeing the negotiation fresh rather than through the lens of experience—is what Voss identifies as "a really hard thing."

The Second Lesson: The Counterintuitive Way to Build Trust

Once executives buy into the idea that they need better information, they naturally want to know: how do I actually build trust with someone who's initially not willing to trust me? Most people assume the answer involves finding common ground—asking about hobbies, families, or shared experiences. Voss identifies this as one of the worst ways to build trust.

The problem with common-ground hunting is that it makes the other side feel "squashed," even when the conversation feels warm and friendly. The other person senses that you're trying to get something from them, and this diminishes rather than builds trust. Similarly, the obvious approach of asking good questions to gather information backfires because "questioning somebody makes them feel cornered," and nobody trusts someone who has cornered them.

Voss introduces what he calls the "labeling" technique—a tool from hostage negotiation that most business people have never learned. The approach is simple but requires real presence: instead of launching into questions or common-ground hunting, you simply observe the other person and reflect back what you see. Voss demonstrates with a recent encounter with a colleague he hadn't seen in months. Rather than asking "How are you? How can I help you?"—the typical nonsensical how questions—he looked at the man for about three seconds, soaking in his state, and said, "You seem centered."

The colleague paused and then revealed significant information: he had just come off "the mountain," a place where he goes to meditate, work out, and engage with Asian philosophies and martial arts. He shared that the last time Voss had seen him, he hadn't been on the mountain in months and had been "wound tight." Now, after about a month on the mountain, he felt phenomenal. In seconds, Voss had learned more about this person's inner state and priorities than dozens of questions would have yielded.

The key to effective labeling, Voss explains, is that the observation must be accurate. If the person seems stressed and you say "you seem centered," you'll look like an idiot. But when done correctly, the label almost functions as a subtle compliment—the person feels seen and understood without feeling interrogated. It's not about whether the label is positive or negative; it's about accurately reflecting what you observe.

The Third Lesson: The Power of "No-Oriented" Questions

After establishing trust through labeling, how do you then guide the negotiation toward substantive discussion without creating friction? Voss demonstrates with his own approach: after the colleague shared about the mountain, Voss didn't push toward the business agenda. Instead, he sat back, waited to see if the other person would pick a next step, and then attempted to trigger movement using what he calls "no-oriented" questions.

He illustrates with a specific example: instead of asking "Do you want to talk about what we're here for?"—which pushes for a "yes" answer and creates friction—Voss asked "Is it ridiculous to talk about what we're here for?" The colleague responded "No, no. It wouldn't be ridiculous for us to do that." By going for "no" rather than "yes," Voss created a sense of safety and permission. Yes-oriented questions create pressure and demand something from the other person; no-oriented questions offer an escape route and let them feel in control.

The psychology here is crucial: "going for yes creates friction, but going for no creates protection and safety." In high-stakes negotiations, people need to feel safe before they can be open and creative. The counterintuitive insight is that by asking questions that give the other person permission to say no, you actually increase the likelihood they'll engage positively.

The Fourth Lesson: Compromise Correlates to Mediocrity

Voss then addresses a concept that most business people hold sacred: compromise. He asks the audience to consider the United States Congress—they compromise constantly, yet virtually no one would give them an A+ rating. The implication is stark: if Congress represents compromise in action, and Congress is widely seen as dysfunctional, then maybe compromise isn't the path to excellence that conventional wisdom suggests.

Voss frames the issue in terms of values: how do you feel about compromising your principles? Most people instinctively feel uneasy about this—because compromise, by definition, means everyone loses something. And that loss is fundamentally incompatible with "a long-term relationship of trust and prosperity." When you compromise, you're training the other side that they can't fully trust your position, because you'll move away from it under pressure.

The problem, Voss explains, is that people "sort of feel like it's a necessary evil" and "settle for that"—accepting mediocrity because they believe they can't get to a better outcome. But this acceptance becomes a self-fulfilling prophecy. The replacement for compromise isn't harder bargaining or more aggressive leverage—it's a different approach to understanding what the other party actually needs.

The Fifth Lesson: Making Others Feel Heard

So if not compromise, what actually works? Voss's answer is striking in its simplicity: make the other person feel completely heard. He estimates that more than 30% of deals will be made "in your favor on the spot" if you can make the other party feel completely heard on their position, and "about half of the deals are made on the spot once the other side feels completely heard."

This is a remarkable claim—that just by making the other party feel truly understood, you might get 25-30% of deals resolving immediately in your favor without any further negotiation. The mechanism isn't about trickery or manipulation; it's about removing the friction that comes from people feeling misunderstood. When someone feels truly heard, they become more flexible, more creative, and more willing to find solutions that work for both parties.

The implication is that the traditional approach of making your case, stating your position, and pushing for concessions is actually creating the friction that prevents optimal deals. The alternative isn't passive—it's actively working to understand the other side so thoroughly that they want to work with you toward a solution that benefits everyone.

Detailed Takeaways

Takeaway One: Assume Both Sides Have Game-Changing Information You're Not Aware Of

The foundational shift Voss advocates is moving from a position-focused approach to an information-focused approach. Rather than entering a negotiation knowing what you want and what you'll give up, assume that both you and the other party have information that would dramatically change the landscape if shared. This creates intellectual humility and motivates the genuine curiosity needed to uncover hidden value. The practical application: before every significant negotiation, remind yourself that your current position may be based on incomplete information, and approach the conversation seeking to fill those gaps rather than defend your position.

Takeaway Two: Replace Questions with Observations

The technique of labeling—making accurate observations about what you see and reflecting them back—creates a fundamentally different dynamic than asking questions. Questions create pressure and the feeling of being interrogated; observations create connection and the feeling of being seen. The practical application: in your next negotiation, resist the urge to ask questions and instead focus on observing what the other person is communicating through their behavior, language, and energy. Reflect back what you notice without judgment or agenda.

Takeaway Three: Use "No" to Create Safety

Yes-oriented questions ("Do you want to discuss the project?") create pressure and demand a commitment. No-oriented questions ("Would it be ridiculous to discuss the project?") offer permission to decline and create a sense of safety. When people feel safe, they're more open and creative. The practical application: reframe your negotiation openings to allow the other party to say no easily. "Is this a bad time to talk about..." is far more effective than "Can we talk about..."

Takeaway Four: Stop Celebrating Compromise

Compromise is often presented as the hallmark of sophisticated negotiation, but Voss argues it actually correlates to mediocrity. When you compromise, both parties lose something, and the resulting deal is less than optimal for either side. The alternative isn't winner-take-all bargaining but rather finding solutions that create new value neither party had initially considered. The practical application: resist the gravitational pull toward compromise as a settlement mechanism. Instead, dig deeper into the underlying interests to find creative solutions that meet both parties' needs without either making concessions.

Takeaway Five: Prioritize Making the Other Side Feel Heard

The single most powerful intervention in negotiation may be simply ensuring the other party feels completely understood. This doesn't mean agreeing with them—it means demonstrating that you genuinely grasp their position, their constraints, and their perspective. The practical application: before pushing for your own outcome, deliberately work to summarize the other party's position accurately and ask if you've understood them correctly. Only when they confirm they feel heard should you move toward solving the problem together.

Who This Is For

This content is specifically designed for business executives, entrepreneurs, and professionals who regularly engage in high-stakes negotiations. If you're someone who has negotiated for years and believes you understand the process, Voss's message is particularly valuable because it challenges the very confidence that experience builds. The audience that will benefit most includes C-suite executives closing major deals, sales professionals navigating complex enterprise sales, entrepreneurs raising capital or partnering with other companies, and anyone who finds themselves repeatedly negotiating and wants to improve their outcomes.

The value here isn't in learning new tactics—it's in fundamentally reorienting how you think about negotiation. Voss's framework suggests that the reason many experienced negotiators don't achieve better results isn't lack of skill or intelligence but rather a flawed mental model about what negotiation actually is. By shifting from position-focused to empathy-focused, from case-making to understanding, and from compromise to creative problem-solving, even veteran negotiators can dramatically improve their results. The techniques require practice and real presence, but the underlying mindset shift can happen immediately—for those willing to challenge their assumptions.
