<p># Build Mental Toughness & Perform Under Pressure: A Deep Dive with Dr. Lenny Wiersma

## Introduction

Performance under pressure is one of the most sought-after and least understood skills in human development. Whether you're a professional athlete facing a championship moment, a coach guiding a team through a critical game, or a professional navigating a high-stakes presentation, the ability to regulate your mind and emotions when the stakes are highest separates good performers from elite ones. Yet most of what people believe about mental toughness is based on mythology, misconceptions, or surface-level advice that falls apart the moment real pressure arrives.

In this episode, host Andrew Huberman sits down with Dr. Lenny Wiersma, a professor of sport psychology for over 25 years and one of the most respected researchers and practitioners in the field. Unlike many figures in the mental performance space who trade in motivational platitudes, Wiersma brings the rigor of a scientist and the pragmatism of someone who has worked extensively with Major League Baseball players, UFC fighters, big wave surfers, Olympic champions, and extreme athletes of all kinds. His work has been published widely in academic journals, and his practical experience working at the highest levels of sport gives him a credibility that few in this space can match.

The conversation opens with an examination of Alex Honnold—the world-renowned free solo climber who scaled Yosemite's El Capitan without ropes—and dismantles the popular narrative that he simply "doesn't feel fear." From there, the discussion spirals outward into visualization science, the psychology of confidence versus belief, the right framework for understanding self-talk, emotional regulation versus emotional control, biofeedback tools, the underrated art of corner coaching, and much more. Throughout, Wiersma demonstrates why he has become a trusted figure for athletes and coaches who need someone who can translate complex psychological principles into concrete, usable tools.

## The Alex Honnold Misconception and the Psychology of Fear

The episode begins by examining one of the most compelling case studies in modern extreme sports: Alex Honnold's free solo ascent of Freerider on El Capitan. The popular narrative surrounding Honnold suggests he is simply wired differently—that he lacks the fear response that would prevent most humans from climbing 3,000 feet without safety equipment. Wiersma dismantles this idea immediately, calling it both "academically wrong" and "deeply unfair" to what Honnold has actually built.

The reality, according to Wiersma, is that Honnold experiences fear like anyone else. What distinguishes him is not the absence of fear but rather his extensive psychological preparation and his sophisticated relationship with that fear. Honnold has spent years training his nervous system, developing coping mechanisms, and building a psychological toolkit that allows him to manage fear rather than be controlled by it. The implication is significant: if Honnold's performance can be understood as a skill set rather than an innate trait, then the principles underlying his success become trainable for others.

This distinction matters enormously for how we conceptualize extreme performance. When we attribute extraordinary feats to innate gifts or genetic differences, we remove agency from the performer and render their achievements inaccessible to others. When we understand these performances as the product of deliberate psychological training, we open the door to broader application. Wiersma's perspective suggests that anyone operating in high-pressure environments can learn to reshape their relationship with fear, anxiety, and arousal—provided they approach it with the same systematic dedication that elite athletes bring to physical training.

## Risk Assessment and the Confidence-Risk Relationship

From the Honnold discussion, the conversation turns to the intricate relationship between risk assessment and confidence. Wiersma explains that elite performers in high-risk domains don't simply ignore danger or suppress their awareness of it. Instead, they develop highly refined abilities to assess risk accurately and calibrate their confidence appropriately.

For an extreme climber, this means understanding precisely which holds are safe, which movements carry risk, and how environmental conditions affect the danger level. For a UFC fighter, it means knowing your opponent's patterns, your own capabilities, and the specific risks of various tactical choices. For a big wave surfer, it means reading ocean conditions, understanding your own limits, and making split-second decisions about which waves are rideable and which should be let go.

What emerges from this discussion is a model where confidence is not the opposite of awareness but rather the product of it. When performers have genuinely developed their skills and have accurate information about the demands of their environment, their confidence becomes grounded in reality rather than delusion. This kind of grounded confidence is robust because it can withstand challenge—it doesn't collapse when things get difficult because it's based on accumulated evidence rather than wishful thinking.

Wiersma notes that one of the dangers in mental performance work is inadvertently building false confidence that lacks this foundation. A coach who constantly tells an athlete to "believe in yourself" without also ensuring that the athlete has genuinely developed the skills and self-awareness to back up that belief is creating a fragile psychological structure that may collapse under real pressure.

## Trainability of Extreme Athletes

The conversation then addresses a question that has fascinated researchers and the public alike: are extreme athletes born or made? Wiersma's answer reflects both scientific honesty and practical wisdom. He acknowledges that genetic factors play a role in determining who is drawn to extreme sports and who has the physiological profile that makes certain extreme performances possible. However, he is equally clear that the psychological skills required to perform at the highest levels are fundamentally trainable.

This view aligns with the broader field of sport psychology, which has moved away from trait-based models that emphasized innate psychological characteristics toward skill-based models that emphasize teachable competencies. The traits that matter most for extreme performance—fear regulation, attention control, emotional regulation, confidence management—are all responsive to deliberate training. Wiersma has seen this repeatedly across his decades of work with elite performers.

What varies is how much training different individuals need. Someone with a naturally calmer nervous system may require less work on arousal regulation, while someone with a more reactive system may need to develop more sophisticated coping strategies. But the work is always possible, and the outcomes, while variable, are consistently positive for athletes who commit to the psychological training process.

## Wipeout Planning: Preparing for Failure Without Dwelling on It

One of the more practically valuable segments addresses what Wiersma calls "wipeout planning"—the deliberate process of imagining failure, setbacks, or worst-case scenarios. This might seem counterintuitive. Isn't positive thinking supposed to be the goal? Wiersma explains that positive thinking without realistic contingency planning is incomplete and potentially dangerous.

Consider a big wave surfer. If they paddle out only imagining perfect rides and spectacular barrels, they are poorly prepared for the reality that they will occasionally wipeout, get held under water, and need to make split-second decisions about their safety. Wiersma describes how he works with surfers to have them mentally rehearse not just their successful performances but also their wipeouts—the physical sensations of being held under, the decision-making process for when to swim up versus wait for the next wave, the emotional experience of fear and disorientation.

This kind of mental rehearsal accomplishes several things. First, it reduces the novelty of the experience. When a wipeout actually occurs, the surfer has already metabolically and emotionally processed the scenario in their mind, which reduces the stress response. Second, it builds specific decision-making skills that can be accessed under pressure. Third, it communicates to the nervous system that failure is survivable, which paradoxically reduces anxiety and frees up attentional resources for performance.

The key, Wiersma emphasizes, is balance. Wipeout planning should not become rumination or catastrophizing. The goal is to develop comprehensive mental preparation that includes failure scenarios without being dominated by them. The best performers maintain a positive performance orientation while simultaneously being realistic about the possibility of setbacks.

## Visualization Science: What Actually Works

The episode provides one of the most thorough treatments of visualization science available in a popular format. Wiersma begins by clarifying that visualization is not simply "thinking positively" or "picturing success." At its core, visualization is the mental rehearsal of specific experiences in a way that engages the same neural and physiological systems involved in actual performance.

The brain does not fully distinguish between vividly imagined experiences and real ones. When you mentally rehearse a movement pattern with high specificity and intensity, your motor cortex activates, your muscles show subtle electrical activity, and your stress hormones respond as if you were actually performing. This is why visualization can build skills, but it is also why poorly constructed visualizations can undermine performance.

Wiersma walks through a powerful example: Michael Phelps, the most decorated Olympic swimmer in history. Between races at the Beijing Olympics, Phelps would visualize not just successful swims but specific mishaps—a broken goggle filling with water, a poor start, a cramp in his calf. He would then visualize himself coping with these scenarios successfully, adjusting his stroke, maintaining his rhythm, finishing the race despite the disruption. When one of these exact scenarios occurred—his goggle filled with water during a race—he was prepared. He had already processed the emotional response and developed the technical plan for dealing with it. He swam the race and won.

This example illustrates a crucial principle: effective visualization includes adversity. When athletes visualize only perfect performances, they are unprepared for the inevitable deviations from the ideal that occur in actual competition. By incorporating failure imagery into visualization practice, athletes build the psychological flexibility needed to maintain performance when things go wrong.

## The Right Way to Think About Self-Talk

One of the most conceptually valuable sections addresses self-talk, and it begins with a significant reframing. Most advice about self-talk focuses on whether it should be positive or negative—the implication being that if you just say the right things to yourself, performance will improve. Wiersma argues this is the wrong frame entirely.

The question is not whether self-talk is positive or negative. The question is whether self-talk is functional or dysfunctional. Functional self-talk serves performance; dysfunctional self-talk undermines it. A critical piece of functional self-talk might involve telling yourself "this is hard" or "I'm struggling"—statements that acknowledge difficulty but are directed toward adaptive response. Dysfunctional self-talk might involve the same words but in a tone and context that triggers anxiety or disengagement.

From this foundation, Wiersma introduces several practical frameworks. The first involves the distinction between first-person and second-person self-talk. First-person self-talk means talking to yourself as "I"—"I can do this." Second-person self-talk means talking to yourself as "you"—"You can do this." Research suggests that second-person self-talk can create psychological distance that makes the message more impactful, similar to how hearing encouragement from a coach lands differently than encouragement from yourself.

Wiersma also discusses the use of nicknames or personal mantras. Many elite athletes use specific phrases that have accumulated meaning over time—references to previous successes, inside jokes with coaches, or condensed reminders of core values. These phrases work partly because of their emotional associations and partly because they bypass the analytical mind that can sometimes interfere with automatic performance.

## Confidence Versus Belief: A Crucial Distinction

Perhaps the most conceptually rich section of the episode addresses the distinction between confidence and belief. In common usage, these terms are often used interchangeably, but Wiersma argues they refer to meaningfully different psychological phenomena that have different implications for performance.

Confidence, in Wiersma's framework, refers to a general sense of self-efficacy in a particular domain. You might be confident in your ability to perform a specific skill or succeed in a particular type of challenge. Confidence is domain-specific and develops through accumulated experience and mastery.

Belief, by contrast, refers to something deeper and more global. Beliefs are the fundamental convictions you hold about yourself, about the world, and about how things work. Beliefs are harder to change than confidence because they are more integrated into your identity and your understanding of reality.

This distinction has practical implications. If an athlete lacks confidence, targeted skill work and experience accumulation can address the problem. But if an athlete lacks belief—if they hold a fundamental conviction that they are not capable of certain achievements, that success is not for people like them, that the world is rigged against them—then confidence-building alone will not be sufficient. The deeper belief must be addressed, which typically requires longer-term psychological work and often involves identity-level shifts.

Wiersma notes that this framework helps explain why some athletes with impressive skills underperform under pressure while some athletes with more modest skills seem to rise to every occasion. The difference often lies not in confidence but in belief. An athlete who lacks the belief that they deserve success or can handle success may undermine their own performance through conscious or unconscious mechanisms.

## Emotional Regulation Versus Emotional Control

Another important reframing comes in the discussion of emotional management. Popular advice often suggests that performers should control their emotions—stay calm, suppress anxiety, eliminate negative feelings. Wiersma argues this is not only ineffective but potentially counterproductive.

The distinction between emotional control and emotional regulation is critical. Emotional control implies suppression—the attempt to stop unwanted emotions from existing. Research consistently shows that suppressed emotions tend to intensify, persist, and interfere with performance. When you try not to feel anxious, you often become more anxious. When you try not to feel angry, that anger often surfaces in distorted ways.

Emotional regulation, by contrast, involves changing the trajectory of emotions—downregulating intense arousal, upregulating engagement when flat, shifting emotional states in helpful directions. This is not suppression but modulation. The emotion is acknowledged, allowed to exist, and then intentionally shaped.

Wiersma introduces a practical tool: the 3-2-1 reflection framework. This involves three levels of reflection on an experience. First, what happened—the objective facts of what occurred. Second, what it meant—the interpretations, attributions, and emotional responses triggered by what happened. Third, what it revealed—deeper patterns, values, or beliefs that the experience illuminated. This framework helps athletes process experiences fully without getting stuck in rumination or avoiding necessary emotional work.

## Labeling Emotions and the Science of Naming

The conversation delves into the neuroscience of emotional labeling. Wiersma explains that putting words to emotions—naming what you are feeling—has a measurable calming effect on the nervous system. This is not merely psychological; it involves actual brain activation patterns. When the prefrontal cortex engages in labeling, it has an inhibitory effect on the amygdala, the brain region most associated with fear and emotional reactivity.

The practical implication is that athletes benefit from developing emotional vocabulary and the habit of quickly identifying what they are feeling in the moment. "I'm anxious" is more regulatory than "I feel terrible." "I'm frustrated" is more regulatory than "everything is wrong." The act of labeling creates a small but meaningful distance between the emotion and the identity, which opens space for intentional response rather than automatic reaction.

Wiersma also discusses what he calls the "next play mindset"—the ability to quickly release one moment and redirect attention to what comes next. This is particularly relevant in sports like football, baseball, and basketball, where performers must be able to let go of previous plays, whether positive or negative, and fully engage with the present moment. The skill involves both emotional regulation (releasing the residue of previous experiences) and attentional control (directing focus to what is actually happening now rather than what just happened or what might happen).

## Co-Regulation: How Performers Influence Each Other's Nervous Systems

One of the more fascinating topics covered is co-regulation—the phenomenon where one person's emotional and physiological state affects another's. Wiersma explains that humans are wired for social regulation of their nervous systems. When you are around someone who is calm, your own nervous system tends to calm. When you are around someone who is anxious or agitated, your own arousal tends to rise.

This has profound implications for team sports and coaching. A coach who can maintain composure in high-pressure moments provides a co-regulation benefit to their athletes, helping them stay calmer than they would on their own</p>
