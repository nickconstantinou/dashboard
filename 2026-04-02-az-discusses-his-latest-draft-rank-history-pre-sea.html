Az Discusses His Latest Draft & Rank History | Pre Season | Fantasy Premier League Tips 2025/26

A Comprehensive Summary

---

Introduction

Fantasy Premier League content creator Az has returned with his latest draft reveal video for the 2025/26 season, marking the second team reveal on the FDL Blackbox channel following Andy Mullen's earlier contribution. With Luke expected to follow, this series represents the channel's collective approach to helping viewers prepare for the new campaign, which is now just over a week away from kickoff. The video format follows a familiar pattern—Az walks viewers through his current draft, discusses fixture reasoning, and explores potential player additions—but this particular upload takes a notably different tone than previous seasons.

Before diving into his team selections, Az spends a substantial portion of the video addressing what he calls "the elephant in the room": the significant criticism he has faced on social media regarding his recent FPL performance. This transparency serves as both an accountability statement and a context-setting exercise for newer viewers who may be unfamiliar with his broader track record. The self-described "enthusiast" rather than "expert" candidly acknowledges several consecutive seasons of disappointing finishes, including a catastrophic 445,000th-place result in 2023/24, while simultaneously defending his continued involvement in the FPL content space. His reasoning centers on passion for the game dating back to the 2007/08 season, the enjoyment he derives from creating content, and the professional relationships built through work with organizations like Scout and the Premier League's FPL Show, where marketing terms like "expert" are applied to contributors regardless of their personal preference.

---

Historical Performance: The Complete Record

Az opens his performance review by acknowledging that while the last three years have been genuinely poor, his broader track record tells a more nuanced story. His FPL journey began in the 2007/08 season, which he identifies as a learning period alongside the following two campaigns, during which he achieved an average rank of approximately 186,000. He describes this phase as formative, recognizing that the game was substantially different before the proliferation of data analytics tools, community resources, and advanced metrics that now dominate the FPL landscape.

The turning point came between 2010 and 2012, when Az began demonstrating genuine competence. He secured his first top-5,000 finish in 2010 with a rank of 3,800, followed that with a 175,000 finish the next year, and then achieved a 10,600 finish in 2012—a campaign he recalls vividly because he finished a single point outside the top-10,000 threshold, leaving him determined to return. That determination paid dividends over the following several seasons, with Az averaging a rank of approximately 20,000 between 2013 and 2015, including a particularly strong 2,000th-place finish in 2014 that he admits he doesn't remember in great detail.

The period between 2016 and 2018 marked what Az considers his prime, coinciding with his introduction to the Scout Cast (approximately halfway through the 2016/17 season, finishing 59,000 that year) and culminating in his best-ever finish of 800th place the following season. He backed this remarkable achievement with a 62,000 finish the year after, resulting in a three-year average rank of approximately 40,000. Az reflects that during this period, he appeared on the hallowed leaderboard tables within the community's recognition systems, though he never managed to break into the top-10.

The subsequent period from 2019 through 2021 saw more mixed results. Az launched FDL Blackbox in 2020, the same year he recorded his worst finish to that point at 135,000, though he recalls being significantly lower in the rankings before rallying. The 2021/22 season proved exceptional, with his average rank across those three years settling at 52,000—a figure that, while not elite, represented solid performance considering the competitive landscape had intensified considerably.

---

The Decline: Why the Last Three Years Have Been Catastrophic

Az is unflinching in his assessment of his recent performances, describing the last three seasons as "a complete disaster" with "no two ways of looking at it." The 2022/23 season saw him finish 143,000th, though he notes he had fallen to approximately one millionth in the world with roughly six weeks remaining before staging a remarkable recovery that ultimately saw him pip his colleague Mark by a single point on the final day of the season. This narrow victory provides what Az describes as his only real justification for an otherwise disappointing campaign, as he managed to avoid what could have been an even more damaging result.

The 2023/24 season represents the nadir of his FPL career, with a catastrophic 445,000th-place finish that Az describes without hesitation as "embarrassing" and "really bad." He offers a partial explanation: having fallen to around 200,000-250,000, he faced a choice between settling for a poor-but-respectable finish or pushing aggressively for a top-100,000 finish. Az chose the aggressive path, making a series of high-variance decisions—captaining Haaland against Wolves at home over safer options, selecting Romero over Porro, and making numerous other tilt-driven choices in hopes they would pay off. None of them did, resulting in a spectacular tank down the rankings. Az acknowledges that the season would look considerably more respectable if he had simply coasted to a 150,000-200,000 finish instead, calling the 445,000 result "a real blemish on the record."

Last season (2024/25) saw a 265,000th-place finish, representing continued struggle without the dramatic crash of the previous year. Az identifies his primary failing as being "too slow" to capitalize on emerging premium assets, most notably Mohamed Salah, whom he ignored for far too long. He recognizes this pattern as consistent across his three poor seasons: a tendency to move against the template rather than with it, to over-rely on personal intuition and past experience, and to dismiss data-driven approaches that the community had embraced successfully.

---

The Expert Question and Content Creator Dynamics

A significant portion of Az's address concerns his complicated relationship with the title "expert," a designation applied to him by the FPL Show and Scout, which produce content for the Premier League. He is notably uncomfortable with this label, describing it as a marketing term rather than a self-assessment. When asked whether he could request they stop using it, Az responds that he has no control over how the Premier League chooses to market him, and that the alternative—declining the work entirely—was never really viable given his genuine passion for the projects involved. He expresses that he accepts the designation "reluctantly" but carries on regardless because he loves working with these organizations.

This tension between his self-perception as an enthusiast and the community's expectation of expert-level performance has contributed to the criticism he receives. Az acknowledges that some of the negative feedback is "rightly so" given his recent results, while simultaneously expressing frustration with what he perceives as excessive hostility. He mentions that many people have been blocked on Twitter and that he has been called various names, though he emphasizes that he simply cannot be bothered to engage with such behavior given his full-time job and the voluntary nature of his content creation. He reaffirms his commitment to continuing the work for as long as he enjoys it, with no current plans to step away despite the pressure.

---

Analytical Reflection: Why the Data Approach Matters

Az offers a particularly candid piece of self-analysis when discussing the fundamental reason behind his recent struggles. He suggests that in earlier seasons, when fewer analytical tools existed and less of the community had access to sophisticated data, his approach of watching substantial amounts of live football and making decisions based on direct observation provided a meaningful edge. However, as models, expected goal matrices, and comprehensive data synthesis tools have become increasingly sophisticated, the gap between data-informed and intuition-only decision-making has widened considerably.

He specifically mentions that both he and Mark on Blackbox have "pushed too much against that over the last few years," essentially flying the flag for traditional approaches in a game that has become increasingly statistical. Their mutual struggles during this period—Mark finished in the top-100,000 last season but has had similarly poor years otherwise—suggest that the instinctual approach no longer provides sufficient edge against the field. Az indicates that incorporating data-driven insights more thoroughly will be essential going forward, recognizing that the balance between experience-based judgment and quantitative analysis must shift toward the latter.

---

Detailed Takeaways

1. Template Alignment vs. Anti-Template Positioning

Az's repeated mistake over the past three seasons has been positioning himself too aggressively against community consensus. While contrarian thinking can yield significant advantages in FPL, doing so consistently without proper analytical backing has proven costly. The key insight is that anti-template decisions should be deliberate and data-supported rather than emotional reactions to popular choices. For viewers, this suggests a middle ground: following template builds for core structure while using contrarian thinking for the final few slots where differential potential exists.

2. The Importance of Adapting to Game Evolution

The FPL landscape has transformed dramatically since Az's early success, with tools like expected goals, expected assists, shot maps, and team xG figures now widely accessible. What worked in 2012 simply cannot work in 2025 without modernization. This takeaway extends beyond FPL to any competitive pursuit: stale methodology inevitably yields to those who embrace available tools. The lesson is not to abandon intuition entirely, but to use data as the foundation upon which experience-based adjustments are layered.

3. Risk Management During Mid-Season Rallies

The 2023/24 catastrophe demonstrates the danger of abandoning disciplined risk management in pursuit of dramatic recovery. Az's decision to make multiple high-variance choices simultaneously—captaining Haaland away from safer options, making defensive backswap decisions—all but guaranteed either a spectacular finish or a spectacular failure. The takeaway is clear: when recovering from a poor position, incremental improvement through steady, low-variance decisions is preferable to gambling everything on a series of unlikely outcomes.

4. Transparency as Community Currency

Az's willingness to address criticism openly, acknowledge poor performance without excuse, and contextualize his track record represents a refreshing approach to content creator accountability. Rather than deflecting or ignoring negative feedback, he engaged with it directly while maintaining boundaries around what he will and will not tolerate. For content creators, this suggests that authenticity and transparency can serve as effective counters to criticism, provided the underlying passion and enjoyment remain evident.

5. The Personal Motivation Factor

Beneath the strategic discussion lies a simple, relatable competitive motivation: Az desperately wants to avoid losing to his wife Yelena for the fourth consecutive year. She has beaten him three years running, and he describes the situation as "just getting a bit embarrassing." This human element adds stakes beyond mere rank chasing and provides a clear benchmark for evaluating his 2025/26 season: avoiding domestic defeat takes priority over elite finishes. While tongue-in-cheek, this framing captures the personal investment that makes FPL compelling for millions of players worldwide.

Who This Content Is For

This video serves multiple audiences with distinct value propositions. For viewers unfamiliar with Az's broader work and track record, the video provides essential context for understanding who they're watching and why his perspectives might (or might not) carry weight. The transparent rank history allows new subscribers to calibrate their expectations appropriately rather than discovering three seasons of poor finishes after-the-fact.

For established followers who have witnessed his recent struggles firsthand, the video offers both validation of their frustrations and a potential turning point narrative. Az explicitly signals his intention to adapt his approach, suggesting that his 2025/26 content may reflect greater incorporation of data-driven thinking alongside his traditional eye-test methodology.

For FPL players more broadly, the content provides a case study in what NOT to do: the dangers of tilt-driven decision-making, the risks of over-relying on experience at the expense of information, and the importance of managing variance rather than chasing dramatic comebacks through high-risk plays. Finally, for those who enjoy the personal drama underlying competitive gaming, the underlying narrative of Az attempting to reclaim former glories while avoiding further embarrassment against his wife offers compelling entertainment value independent of specific team selection advice.

The journey Az now faces—reconnecting with the form that produced consistent top-50,000 finishes while adapting to an evolved analytical landscape—represents a challenge familiar to many competitive FPL managers. Whether he succeeds in this endeavor will define his content narrative for the season ahead.
