    Introduction

    The most important question facing crypto investors right now has nothing to do with protocol upgrades, on-chain metrics, or mining dynamics. It's about Artificial Intelligence.

    This is the central argument that unfolds throughout a wide-ranging conversation between Ryan Hoegner of Bankless and Michael Nadeau of TDR Podcast and TDR Research. The discussion centers on what they describe as an extraordinary correlation between crypto markets and the Nasdaq — one that has never been stronger than it is in 2026. When Bitcoin rallies, tech stocks rally in near-lockstep. When the Nasdaq stumbles, crypto follows. This dynamic means that if you want to understand where crypto is headed in the near-to-medium term, you first need to understand whether the AI boom that's driving equities is sustainable, overextended, or somewhere in between.

    Nadeau approaches this question not as a permabull or a permabear, but as a researcher trying to rigorously map the landscape. He draws heavily on the framework developed by economist Carlota Perez in her seminal book *Technological Revolutions and Financial Capital*, which describes how breakthrough technologies consistently follow predictable patterns of adoption, over-investment, and eventual correction before reaching sustained maturity. This framework, he argues, is the right lens for understanding where AI stands today — and by extension, where crypto may be headed.

    The conversation walks through valuation data, growth metrics, historical comparisons to the 1999 .com era, and the contrarian signals being sent by legendary investors — ultimately arriving at a nuanced, data-grounded perspective that acknowledges we are clearly in historically expensive territory without claiming to know exactly when or how a reversal might occur.

    Key Concepts & Arguments

    The Crypto-Nasdaq Correlation and Its Implications

    Nadeau opens by presenting a chart showing Bitcoin's correlation to the Nasdaq by year, noting that 2026 represents the highest correlation ever recorded. This is not a marginal effect — it's a structural shift in how crypto assets are pricing. The implication is significant: crypto investors who believed they were operating in an uncorrelated asset class are now, at least in this current market regime, functionally long tech equities with extra volatility.

    The Nasdaq's approximately 25% rally since late March 2026 has corresponded almost exactly with Bitcoin's own surge since early February. This tight temporal alignment suggests that capital flows are treating crypto as a high-beta expression of the AI trade rather than as an independent asset class. Nadeau frames this as a bear market rally within crypto itself — he characterizes the broader crypto market as being in a bear phase — but one being temporarily sustained by the extraordinary momentum in traditional tech markets.

    The danger, as he frames it, is that if AI is in the late stages of a speculative frenzy — and the data he presents suggests this is a legitimate possibility — then crypto's current lift could reverse when the music stops in tech.

    The Perez Framework: Understanding How Technology Revolutions Unfold

    To ground the "bubble" discussion in something more rigorous than rhetoric, Nadeau introduces the framework from Carlota Perez's *Technological Revolutions and Financial Capital*. Perez describes a recurring pattern across every major technological transition — from railroads to automobiles to the internet — in which a breakthrough innovation passes through a series of phases: eruption, frenzy, turning point, synergy, and maturity.

    This framework closely mirrors the Gartner Hype Cycle, though Perez's version includes a crucial financial dimension: at each stage, capital markets behave in predictable ways. The eruption phase is characterized by genuine innovation capture — a technology demonstrates real capability and captures public imagination. The frenzy phase is where things get interesting: easy capital, high leverage, widespread FOMO, and a narrative that gets stretched far beyond what the current reality can support.

    Nadeau explicitly maps the current AI moment onto this framework. He identifies ChatGPT's release in late 2022 as the eruption phase — a genuine breakthrough that suddenly made AI's potential tangible and legible to the general public. The period from 2024 through 2025 represents, in his view, the frenzy phase, characterized by enormous capital influx, widespread building activity, and rapidly appreciating asset prices.

    But here's the critical nuance: the Perez framework doesn't tell you when the frenzy ends. It tells you that it ends. The slope of the curve — how steep it is, how long the frenzy lasts — depends on the genuine order-of-magnitude of the technology itself. Nadeau references Nate Silver's concept from his own work, which suggests that each technology has a different "Richter scale" of impact. The mobile phone might have been a magnitude 6; the internet possibly a 7 or 8; AI might be a 9. We simply don't know yet. And that uncertainty is precisely what makes timing the top impossible.

    The crucial point Nadeau emphasizes is that the crypto market has experienced this same pattern multiple times. Every major crypto innovation has generated its own mini-cycle through the Perez phases. Understanding this pattern doesn't make prediction possible — but it does make the terrain navigable.

    Reading the Valuation Data: The Shiller CAPE Ratio

    To move from theoretical framework to concrete data, Nadeau pulls up the Shiller cyclically adjusted price-to-earnings (CAPE) ratio, one of the most historically reliable measures of broad equity market valuation. Unlike trailing PE ratios, the CAPE ratio averages ten years of earnings and adjusts for inflation, providing a smoothed view that reduces the distortion caused by recent volatility.

    The current reading is approximately 42. This number demands context. In 1999, at the peak of the .com bubble, the ratio reached just over 44. In 1929, before the Great Depression, it reached approximately 33-34. We are currently above 1929 levels by a meaningful margin and within striking distance of the all-time peak set in 2000. The only period in recorded history when the market was more expensive than it is right now was precisely at the top of the dot-com bubble — a period that preceded one of the most dramatic drawdowns in market history.

    Nadeau is explicit about what this means: stocks are not cheap. When you purchase equities at a Shiller PE above 40, the historical evidence suggests that ten-year forward returns are poor. The ratio is not a timing tool — it doesn't tell you when a correction comes — but it does tell you that you are buying future return capacity at a steep discount relative to current price.

    He also notes that this chart was pulled out in 2021 as well, when the ratio approached 40 and many observers called a bubble. Instead, the market continued to rally. This illustrates the core frustration with valuation analysis: the signal can be clear and yet the timing can be wildly uncertain.

    The Bull Case and Why It's Being Challenged

    Nadeau acknowledges that there is a serious bull case for current valuations, and he wants to address it directly rather than dismiss it. The primary bull argument centers on the quality of the companies driving the AI buildout. Unlike the .com era, where many companies were unprofitable or even pre-revenue, the major hyperscalers — the companies financing the most significant AI infrastructure — are extraordinarily profitable. They generated free cash flows that funded their capital expenditure programs without meaningful debt issuance until recently. The argument is that these are not speculative bets on future earnings; they are earnings-generating machines that are deploying capital into an expanding opportunity set.

    This is a legitimate point. However, Nadeau turns to a specific data point to complicate this narrative: Q1 blended earnings growth for the S&P 500 is tracking at 27.7%. This is an extraordinary number — and one that actually mirrors what was happening in 1999. The suggestion that "this time is different because the fundamentals are better" is undermined by the fact that 1999 also saw exceptional earnings growth. The market was not buying stocks with no earnings; it was buying stocks with very high earnings that were priced at even higher multiples.

    This is not to say the AI infrastructure buildout is undeserved or that the companies are poorly run. It's to say that the bull case about fundamentals does not differentiate the current moment from 1999 as cleanly as some bulls suggest. The PE expansion — the extent to which prices have risen faster than earnings — is still occurring, even if earnings are strong.

    The Contrarian Signal: Warren Buffett and Berkshire Hathaway

    One of the more striking data points in the discussion is Warren Buffett's current positioning. Buffett, who has been investing for sixty years, has stated publicly that there have been only five moments in that six-decade span where he found valuations low enough to be attractive buyers. He has described the current market as functioning "more like a casino."

    Berkshire Hathaway has been net sellers of equities over the past several years. They are holding significant cash reserves and have not been deploying capital aggressively. This is not a fringe or reckless position — it's the most celebrated long-term investor in history signaling that he does not find current prices attractive relative to intrinsic value.

    Nadeau presents this not as a reason to panic or to short the market, but as a signal worth taking seriously. When the investor who famously advises to "be fearful when others are greedy" is holding record cash in a market that has rallied substantially, that is a data point, not a conclusion.

    What's Different and What Isn't: 1999 vs. 2026

    One of the subtler arguments in the conversation is about the comparison between the current AI boom and the 1999 .com bubble. Some bulls argue that the differences are so significant that historical comparisons are misleading. Nadeau engages with this directly, acknowledging the valid points — the profitability of hyperscalers, the genuine utility of modern AI products, the institutional nature of much of the capital flowing in — while simultaneously noting that these differences do not eliminate the fundamental dynamic of speculative excess.

    He specifically challenges the narrative that "this is different from 1999 because growth is real." His data shows growth was also real in 1999. The question is not whether the technology is real — it clearly is — but whether the current price fully reflects a realistic rather than an optimistic trajectory of that growth's future value.

    Detailed Takeaways

    1. The AI question is the crypto question — for now.

    The unprecedented correlation between crypto and the Nasdaq means that crypto investors cannot afford to ignore equity market dynamics. If AI stocks correct significantly — whether due to valuation reversion, a liquidity event, or political/regulatory disruption — crypto is unlikely to decouple and sail higher on its own narrative. This is especially true given Nadeau's view that crypto is already in a bear market, with the recent rally being sustained by external tailwinds rather than internal fundamental improvement. Investors building positions in crypto should be monitoring AI sentiment, Nasdaq flows, and tech sector risk appetite as leading indicators of crypto direction.

    2. Valuation extremes don't predict timing — they predict outcome.

    The Shiller CAPE ratio at 42 does not mean a crash is imminent. It means that future returns from this starting point will be lower than returns from cheaper starting points. Historical data is consistent on this point. Investors who buy at these valuation levels are not paying for future return capacity — they are paying for past return already captured. The market can remain expensive far longer than skeptics expect, and the Perez framework explains why: the genuine productivity gains from a technology like AI can sustain elevated valuations while the underlying reality catches up. What the data cannot tell you is whether that catch-up happens in six months or six years. Position sizing and risk management need to account for this extended uncertainty rather than betting on a specific timeline.

    3. The Perez framework provides a map, not a GPS.

    Understanding that we are in the frenzy phase of an AI technology revolution tells you important things: that capital will eventually be over-allocated, that the narrative will stretch beyond current reality, and that a correction — a "trough of disillusionment" — is historically inevitable before the plateau of productivity is reached. But it does not tell you where exactly on that curve you are standing today. Nadeau's honest conclusion is that "we're in later stages of that frenzy period" but that "who knows how long the later stage could actually last." Investors should use this framework to prepare for the eventual correction rather than to predict it, building portfolios that can survive and potentially benefit from a significant drawdown in AI-adjacent assets.

    4. The bull case on fundamentals is real but insufficient as an investment argument.

    The profitability of AI companies is not in question. The growth rates are extraordinary. The technology is transformative. None of this changes the arithmetic of buying assets at historically elevated valuations. Investors should not be seduced by the quality of the companies into ignoring the quantity of the price they are paying. This is the central lesson of the .com era: some of the most important, most transformative companies in history were still terrible investments at the prices investors paid for them at the peak. Nvidia, Amazon, and countless other companies that would go on to reshape the economy still lost 80% of their value in 2000-2002. Quality does not immunize against valuation correction.

    5. Warren Buffett's positioning is a signal, not a trade.

    Berkshire Hathaway's net selling and record cash position represents the judgment of the most successful long-term investor in history that current prices do not offer adequate margin of safety for long-term capital allocation. This should be weighted heavily, not as a trigger to sell everything immediately, but as a reason to examine your own position sizing, concentration risk, and liquidity readiness. It is not a prediction that a crash is imminent; it is a reminder that the best investors in history have always seen value in preserving capital during periods of maximum speculative excess. For crypto investors specifically, this should reinforce the importance of maintaining dry powder and avoiding the assumption that current momentum is permanent.

    Who This Is For

    This discussion is most valuable for crypto-native investors and DeFi participants who want to understand the macro regime within which their portfolios are currently operating. If you hold meaningful crypto exposure, particularly Bitcoin or Ethereum, and have been wondering why your assets seem to move in lockstep with the Nasdaq, this conversation provides the analytical framework and data to understand that relationship and its implications.

    It is also valuable for generalist investors who want to understand the AI boom's investment implications beyond the surface-level bull narrative. Nadeau doesn't argue that AI is over — he explicitly believes it's a genuine, transformative technology — but he provides the data and historical framework to assess whether the current pricing of that belief is rational or has outrun reality.

    Lastly, it is essential for anyone building conviction about when to deploy capital in either crypto or tech equities. Nadeau's conclusion is not that the market is definitely topping — he explicitly disclaims that — but that the terrain is now significantly more dangerous than it was a few years ago, that historical precedent strongly favors eventual correction, and that positioning accordingly — preserving capital, avoiding concentration in assets most exposed to AI sentiment — is the rational response to the data even without knowing the exact timing of the next phase transition.
