Macbook Neo Review: Better than you Think!

Introduction: A Budget Laptop That Changes Everything

In the landscape of consumer technology, Apple's most consequential releases often come wrapped in premium price tags and headlines about revolutionary features. The iPhone X, the Vision Pro, and countless other flagship products have dominated tech discourse for years. Yet according to tech reviewer MKBHD, Apple's potentially most disruptive product of the last decade isn't a flagship at all—it's the MacBook Neo, a $599 entry-level laptop that represents a fundamental shift in how Apple approaches the budget computing market.

After spending a week with the device, MKBHD argues that this unassuming laptop poses an existential threat to the entire Windows and Chromebook laptop industry. The reasoning isn't that the Neo introduces groundbreaking technology never before seen—quite the opposite. Instead, it's the combination of familiar components executed so well and priced so aggressively that it could fundamentally reshape consumer expectations. With a student discount bringing the price down to just $4.99, Apple has essentially created a computing appliance that competes not just on specifications but on pure value proposition.

The MacBook Neo runs on the A18 Pro chip, the same processor found in the iPhone 16 Pro, making it the first Mac to ship with an iPhone chip rather than Apple's dedicated M-series silicon. This decision, which might initially seem like a cost-cutting measure, actually represents a stroke of strategic genius that leverages Apple's massive iPhone manufacturing scale.

The Performance Revolution: iPhone Power Meets Mac OS

The most surprising aspect of the MacBook Neo is just how capable its A18 Pro chip proves to be in practice. When MKBHD ran benchmark tests, the results were striking: the Neo achieved over 8,500 multi-core points on Geekbench, which places it essentially on par with the original M1 chip from the MacBook Air. The GPU performance in OpenCL and Metal benchmarks similarly matched M1 numbers, while single-core performance actually exceeded M1 levels, coming closer to M3 territory.

This benchmark performance translates directly to real-world usability in ways that might surprise skeptics. In testing, MKBHD found that web browsing, spreadsheet work, music streaming, email management, and word processing all performed flawlessly—even when multitasking across these applications simultaneously. The bursty, lightweight tasks that define most everyday computing behave identically to how they would on a $3,000 MacBook Pro.

The key insight here is that this isn't some compromised, barely-functional budget device. The A18 Pro was originally designed to handle 4K video editing on the iPhone 16 Pro, so running Mac OS for basic productivity tasks represents relatively easy work for this silicon. MKBHD notes that there's no special performance mode as found on higher-end M-series laptops, just a low-power mode that users can toggle if they want to extend battery life further.

The RAM Reality: Why 8GB Still Works

One of the most contested aspects of modern laptops involves memory allocation, and the MacBook Neo arrives with just 8GB of RAM—a figure that might give potential buyers pause. However, MKBHD explains how Apple's memory architecture makes this amount workable for typical users.

When RAM fills up, the system defaults to swap memory, utilizing the SSD for additional virtual memory access. The Neo's storage drives read at approximately 1,500 megabytes per second, which, while not as fast as higher-end machines, remains more than adequate for swap operations. This is the same approach Apple used successfully with the M1 MacBook Air, which also shipped with 8GB of RAM and proved capable for regular use despite initial skepticism.

The caveat MKBHD raises involves long-term durability: as these laptops age and their SSDs fill up with data, performance can degrade during swap operations. However, for a typical user upgrading from older Intel-based machines, the Neo will still feel significantly faster than what they're leaving behind. The conclusion is clear: 8GB isn't ideal, but it's genuinely sufficient for the target audience.

Apple's Manufacturing Advantage: The Chip Economics

What makes the MacBook Neo particularly fascinating from a business perspective is how Apple leverages its iPhone manufacturing scale to achieve impossible price points. The company already produces millions of A-series chips annually for the iPhone, bringing unit costs down to remarkably low levels through sheer volume.

MKBHD points to a telling example: when Apple needed a chip to power the speakers and webcam in their new Studio Display, they didn't develop new silicon—they simply dropped in an A19 Pro. This demonstrates just how abundant and inexpensive these chips have become. The Studio Display thus contains 12GB of RAM, more than the Neo itself, simply because Apple had these chips lying around at minimal additional cost.

This manufacturing advantage creates a concerning outlook for competitors. Windows laptop manufacturers and Chromebook makers simply cannot match Apple's ability to subsidize chip costs across such massive product lines. Apple can essentially print powerful enough chips at near-zero marginal cost and build entire laptops around them, undercutting competitors while maintaining healthy profit margins.

Build Quality and Design: Premium Feel at Budget Prices

Despite its low price, the MacBook Neo doesn't feel like a budget device. The aluminum chassis provides the signature Apple build quality that's become synonymous with the brand, eliminating the keyboard deck flex commonly found in plastic competitors. The hinge is perfectly balanced, allowing users to open the lid with a single finger—a small detail that somehow matters for daily usability.

The display is a 13-inch LCD panel at approximately 1440p resolution with 60Hz refresh rate and 500 nits of brightness. While not the vibrant mini-LED panels found in the MacBook Pro line, it's more than sufficient for indoor use and represents a perfectly reasonable screen for the price point.

The trackpad deserves special mention—MKBHD describes it as "excellent" with real clicking feedback, matching what users would find in Apple's most expensive machines. The keyboard itself is identical to the MacBook Pro keyboard in terms of switches and feel, though it lacks backlighting and features an off-white color matched to the aluminum chassis. Users who upgrade to the 512GB storage option also gain Touch ID functionality in the corner of the keyboard.

Port selection includes two USB-C ports (one supporting USB 3 speeds, one USB 2), a headphone jack positioned in a slightly unusual location, and a 1080p webcam that's perfectly adequate for video calls.

Color and Personality: Breaking From Apple's Neutral Past

Apple has embraced color in ways previously reserved for iPhones, applying the same playful approach to the MacBook Neo. The review unit MKBHD received was the "citrus" colorway, which he describes as essentially matching the exact shade of lemon-lime Gatorade.

The color coordination extends throughout the device: the keyboard keys are barely yellow, the little rubber feet at the bottom match the chassis color, and even the software wallpaper and accent colors sync with the chosen finish. On the blue Neo, software accents appear in blue; the pink version features blush-pink accents. This level of detail suggests Apple isn't simply repurposing older designs but genuinely investing in making these budget machines feel special.

Battery Life: Efficiency That Defies Expectations

The MacBook Neo ships with a physically smaller battery than the MacBook Air—Apple opted for a cheaper lithium-ion cell rather than lithium-polymer—yet achieves comparable runtime. The A18 Pro chip's remarkable efficiency allows this, sipping power during light tasks much like the iPhone does.

In testing, MKBHD found he could get through almost an entire day of writing, web browsing, video watching, emailing, and social media use before needing to charge. The included 20-watt charger is modest but sufficient; a zero to full charge takes just over an hour. Users can also plug into higher-wattage chargers for faster charging when needed.

The theoretical question of how good battery life could have been with a full MacBook Air-sized battery remains unanswered—Apple chose efficiency over capacity, and the results speak for themselves.

Target Audience Analysis: Who Should Buy This?

MKBHD developed a rating system to help potential buyers determine if the Neo fits their needs:

Students and General Users (A+): The Neo was literally designed for this audience. At $599 (or $4.99 with student pricing), it represents better value than virtually any Chromebook while offering the full Mac ecosystem. For anyone making TikToks, consuming content, or handling basic productivity, this is the obvious choice.

College Students (A): With the caveat that certain majors requiring specialized software may need more power, most college students would be well-served by the Neo. The combination of performance, build quality, and battery life makes it ideal for campus life.

Writers (A): MKBHD wrote this entire review on the MacBook Neo and found it perfectly suited for the task. Word processing, email, web research—it's everything a writer needs with no compromises. The keyboard is identical to the $3,000 MacBook Pro minus backlighting.

Photographers (B-): The Neo can handle photo editing in Pixelmator, Lightroom, and even Photoshop to a degree. However, the display doesn't cover the full P3 color space, meaning serious photographers will need to connect an external monitor. For those who already work with external displays, the Neo can handle serious photo work.

Programmers (B): For basic coding tasks, the Neo proves surprisingly capable. Users won't be running local large language models or performing heavy compilation work due to memory limitations, but for learning to code or handling lighter development tasks, it's a viable option.

The Broader Implications: Why This Matters

The MacBook Neo represents a pivotal moment in personal computing not because of what it contains, but because of what it signals about Apple's strategy and the competitive landscape. By essentially repurposing iPhone components that were already being manufactured at massive scale, Apple has created a laptop that undercuts the entire Windows and Chromebook ecosystem on price while still delivering a genuinely premium experience.

For competitors, this poses an existential question: how do you compete with a $599 laptop that offers Mac OS, Apple Silicon performance, and the build quality of a device costing five times as much? The answer isn't obvious, and that's precisely what makes the Neo so potentially disruptive.

What makes this development particularly significant is that Apple didn't need to engineer new technology or spend billions on R&D to achieve this. They simply applied existing, mature technology in a new context—a textbook example of how vertical integration and manufacturing scale can create competitive moats that traditional PC manufacturers struggle to cross.

Conclusion: The Budget Laptop Paradigm Shift

The MacBook Neo challenges our assumptions about what budget laptops can and should be. It's not a compromised device that users should feel guilty about buying—it's a genuinely capable machine that happens to be affordable. For the vast majority of users who never push their computers beyond web browsing, document editing, and media consumption, this laptop represents not a compromise but an excellent choice.

MKBHD's initial impressions video had suggested that viewers of tech content—the people who watch YouTube reviews and spec-check their purchases—might find the Neo underwhelming. But after deeper analysis, it's clear that this device was never meant for that audience. It's meant for the billions of people who simply need a computer that works well, lasts all day, feels good to use, and doesn't break the bank. On those criteria, the MacBook Neo doesn't just compete—it establishes a new standard that others will spend years trying to match.
