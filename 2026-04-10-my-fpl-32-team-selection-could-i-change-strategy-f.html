My FPL Gameweek 32 Team Selection: Chip Strategy, Player Analysis, and Transfer Decisions

Introduction

Fantasy Premier League managers up and down the country are facing what may well be the most consequential week of the entire season. Gameweek 32 represents the final gateway before the last seven gameweeks of the campaign begin, and with it comes the need to finalize chip strategy for the run-in. In this video, popular FPL content creator FPL Harry walks through his team selection for Gameweek 32 while grappling with one of the biggest strategic questions facing managers up and down the leaderboard: whether to play the wild card now or save it for later.

FPL Harry finds himself in a particularly interesting position heading into this week. He possesses all three of his chips—the wild card, free hit, and bench boost—still intact, giving him significant flexibility in how he approaches the closing stages of the season. However, he also enters Gameweek 32 with five free transfers available, a situation that has created what he describes as a "huge headache" as he weighs whether to use those transfers to improve his team immediately or hold them in reserve for a potential wild card play. The video explores not just his team selection but the broader strategic thinking that goes into maximizing rank over the final seven weeks of the season, making it essential viewing for any FPL manager looking to gain an edge in the closing stages.

Key Concepts and Strategic Analysis

The Chip Strategy Dilemma

The central theme of this video revolves around chip strategy for the final seven weeks of the season. FPL Harry begins by explaining that he was initially pretty set on a specific approach: using the free hit in Gameweek 33, the wild card in Gameweek 35, and the bench boost in Gameweek 36. This represented the most conservative path, preserving the powerful wild card for a week when more teams have favorable fixtures while using the free hit to navigate a potentially tricky blank or double gameweek.

However, the availability of five free transfers has complicated this plan significantly. Harry explains that the chance of him wild carding in Gameweek 32 has grown from approximately a one-in-four chance (75% probability he wouldn't play it) to now closer to a one-in-three chance. Despite this shift, he still maintains that the most likely outcome is that he doesn't play the wild card in Gameweek 32, estimating around a 66% probability that he sticks with the original plan of free hit at 33, wild card at 35, and bench boost after that.

The reasoning behind this hesitation comes down to his assessment of his current team. Harry believes his squad looks "too good" in both Gameweek 32 and Gameweek 34 to justify burning a wild card in either of those weeks. Playing a chip in either of those gameweeks would mean sacrificing a strong starting XI for what may be only marginal gains. However, he acknowledges that the power of five free transfers presents a genuinely attractive alternative, and he specifically considers the scenario of selling Gabriel Martinelli to maximize the bench boost in Gameweek 33, a move that could significantly improve his team for that particular week.

Gameweek 31 Performance Review

Before diving into his Gameweek 32 plans, Harry provides a quick recap of how Gameweek 31 went for his team. He describes it as "a long, long time ago" given everything that has happened since—the FA Cup drama, the chip strategy debates, and the international break—but the results were encouraging. He achieved a Green Arrow, moving up approximately 10,000 places in the overall rankings, which he was "pretty happy" with given that he didn't think his team looked particularly great heading into the week.

One interesting note from Gameweek 31 was that Harry once again rolled his transfer, giving him five free transfers to use in Gameweek 32 rather than the usual two. This decision to save transfers has become a recurring theme for him this season, and it has left him with the flexibility headache he now faces. His total of 65 points in Gameweek 31 was "five above my safety score," continuing his momentum in the right direction with seven weeks left in the season. He notes that his highest scorer was Bruno Fernandes, though he acknowledges that "everyone seemed to captain him on Monday night," meaning his captaincy choice didn't provide significant rank gains. Harry Wilson also returned, making it a pretty good week overall, though Harry admits "it definitely could have been worse."

Goalkeeper and Defensive Analysis

Moving into his team selection, Harry begins with his goalkeeper David Rajka, who faces Bournemouth at home. He's doubling up in goal with Gabriel, which provides solid coverage at the back. Gabriel, of course, played for Arsenal in the Champions League on Tuesday evening, so Harry is "fully ready for him to be back" for the Bournemouth fixture with no real concerns about his availability.

In defense, Harry is starting Crystal Palace defender Maxence Lacroix away at Brentford, a fixture that presents some interesting considerations. He has Marc Cucurella at home to Newcastle and Reece James away at Liverpool on the bench. There is "a slight bit of doubt" in his mind about whether Lacroix or Cucurella is the slightly better pick this week, particularly given that Crystal Palace play on Thursday evening in the Conference League.

This timing is crucial because Harry "just feels like their focus is absolutely going to be on that Conference League, not only this Thursday, but also next Thursday as well." He explains that depending on how Thursday's game goes, Palace could potentially shift their focus back to the Premier League if it looks like they're either definitely going through or definitely going out of the competition, but his instinct is that Lacroix remains the better option. He notes that Cucurella "hasn't really been hitting Defcon that much recently," making Lacroix the preferred choice despite the tough away fixture.

The Brentford defender is also his starting option, though Harry acknowledges he could potentially upgrade that position given that he has five transfers to use. He considers players like Jarrod Bowen of West Ham (home to Wolves on Friday night) or perhaps Diego Dalot of Manchester United (home to Leeds) as potential upgrades, though he isn't certain either represents enough of an improvement over Lacroix this week to justify the transfer. "I don't think the clean sheet probabilities combined with that much attacking threat are really that beneficial," he explains, suggesting he's happy to keep his current defensive setup rather than making a change there.

Midfield Selection and Options

In midfield, Harry has a Manchester United double-up with Bruno Fernandes and Alejandro Garnacho, who faces Leeds at home. He does have "a slight concern about Garnacho's minutes" and whether he will play more than 70 minutes, but he's keeping him in the team for now. He notes he could transfer Garnacho to someone like Kaoru Mitoma, potentially selling Jacob Murphy (currently on his bench) to someone like Summerville of West Ham to make room in his front seven, but again, these moves "don't feel like they really fall into that category" of having significant upside.

His three cheap midfielders are Harry Wilson, Elliot Anderson, and Jacob Murphy. Wilson has the hardest fixture but is "probably the best pick," while Anderson is on the bench at home to Aston Villa. Harry explains his thinking: "Wilson versus Murphy both with away fixtures does feel slightly more difficult. I just look at Liverpool playing in the Champions League on Wednesday night then having the next Champions League game next Tuesday. Wilson being in very good form. Liverpool not looking great defensively, meaning I have gone with Wilson as the starting player in my team."

He adds that he's "not fully locked in with that" and could still change his mind, as Murphy "could force his way into my starting lineup." He also mentions benching Chris Richards, meaning he's only having one Everton player in his team against Brentford, which he considers "probably the right way to go about it" given that he already has Lacroix starting and the Brentford-Everton game could be tricky.

Forward Analysis and Transfer Options

In attack, Harry has Dominic Solanke alongside Nicolas Jackson and Erling Haaland. Of these three, Jackson "still feels like the one that comes with the most amount of risk." His next Champions League game is on Tuesday after Wednesday night's match, with a Saturday game at home to Fulham in between before the Merseyside derby against Everton in Gameweek 33. Harry expects Jackson to start all of these games, but there's "a chance that Alexander Isak is back in contention for Liverpool" in the short term, though he doesn't think Isak will be starting any games over the short-term horizon.

The key question Harry faces is whether to transfer Jackson to someone else. He explains: "I've got to decide quite how likely I think he is to start that game against Fulham or not because if he is going to start, I don't think he's a bad pick. I think he's still probably reasonable enough to play and hold on to, but I do think there is a risk there."

The most likely transfer he identifies is upgrading Jackson to Bowen in midfield, a move he describes as "the most likely" option. The reasoning extends beyond just Gameweek 32: "In Gameweek 34, Liverpool host Crystal Palace whereas Bowen has Everton at home, and I think it's slightly better for Garnacho than it is for Jackson, who will continue to be a minutes risk once we get that and Alexander Each comes closer to being back to full fitness in a few weeks as well." This added long-term element makes the move even more attractive, as it addresses both immediate and future fixture concerns.

He notes that holding onto Haaland provides "a bit of a shield against the wild card 32 teams" because he still has Haaland away at Chelsea while "most people or quite a lot of people actually sold Haaland a couple of weeks ago before the blank gameweek to buy MoSalah in order to sell him then to Anthony Gordon." This gives him exposure to a highly owned player who could differentiate his team from those playing their wild cards now.

Detailed Takeaways

The value of saving transfers across gameweeks cannot be overstated. Harry's decision to roll his transfer in Gameweek 31, giving him five free transfers in Gameweek 32, has given him exactly the kind of flexibility that separates successful FPL managers from the rest. While most managers are locked into making just one or two changes, Harry has the freedom to completely restructure significant parts of his team if needed. This strategic patience, even when it means potentially missing out on points in the short term, provides enormous strategic optionality later in the season.

Chip strategy must be adapted based on your specific team situation. Harry's analysis shows that there's no one-size-fits-all approach to using chips. His original plan of free hit 33, wild card 35, and bench boost 36 made sense for his team, but the specific fixtures and his transfer situation have caused him to reconsider. The key insight is that chip decisions should be driven by your actual squad's strengths and weaknesses, not by generic advice that works for the "average" team. Your specific player ownership, fixture difficulties, and transfer situation all factor into what should be a highly personalized decision.

Fixture congestion and European competition significantly impact player selections. Harry's concern about Crystal Palace players, who play on Thursday evening in the Conference League, highlights how fixture scheduling impacts FPL decisions. Teams competing in multiple competitions face rotation risks, and these late-week games can affect player freshness and motivation. Similarly, his analysis of Liverpool's Champions League schedule and its impact on Wilson's fixture shows how European competitions directly influence FPL strategy.

Long-term planning should factor into immediate transfer decisions. The most telling example is his consideration of the Jackson to Bowen transfer. While the immediate upgrade makes sense for Gameweek 32, he also factors in that Jackson will become a "minutes risk" once Isak returns and that Bowen has better fixtures in Gameweek 34. This multi-game thinking is what separates reactive managers from proactive ones who consistently rank well.

Differentiating from the herd can provide significant rank advantages. Harry's decision to keep Haaland rather than selling him for Mo Salah (and subsequently Anthony Gordon) reflects a contrarian strategy. While many managers made that transfer chain to get more Manchester City exposure, Harry's retention of Haaland gives him a "shield" against those playing their wild cards in Gameweek 32. When a player is highly owned, having them can protect your rank; when they're slightly less owned, not having them can provide an edge. The key is understanding ownership trends and positioning accordingly.
