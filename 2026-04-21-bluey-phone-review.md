# Bluey Phone Review: MKBHD's Hands-On with the Most Extreme Minimal Phone Yet

## A $10.99 Device That Commits to Going Offline Harder Than Any "Dumb Phone" on the Market

---

When most people think of MKBHD—Marques Brownlee, the internet's most followed tech reviewer—they imagine discussions of the latest iPhones, Samsung flagships, or cutting-edge gadgets with price tags that would make your wallet weep. But in a surprising detour from his usual fare, Brownlee recently found himself unboxing something far more humble: the Bluey Phone, a $10.99 device made by VTEC and based on the wildly popular Australian animated series *Bluey*. The discovery came about when Brownlee was scrolling through Amazon's top-rated phones and noticed this peculiar device had accumulated impressive ratings—yet somehow hadn't landed on his review radar, possibly due to its niche appeal as a children's product rather than a mainstream consumer electronics item.

The Bluey Phone isn't trying to compete with flagship smartphones. It's not even attempting to rival the so-called "minimal phones" or "dumb phones" that have gained traction among digital wellness advocates. Instead, it represents something far more radical: an almost comically stripped-down mobile device that makes devices like the Light Phone look like feature-packed powerhouses by comparison. What makes this review particularly compelling isn't just the novelty of the product itself, but Brownlee's surprisingly enthusiastic assessment of its purpose and execution.

---

## The Unboxing Experience: Novelty Meets Functionality

Brownlee describes the unboxing of the Bluey Phone as "interesting," though his phrasing carries a hint of diplomatic understatement. The phone arrives in what he calls an "open-faced cardboard box"—essentially, the device ships exposed rather than sealed within traditional packaging. Brownlee admits he hasn't encountered this approach before, calling it a unique choice in his extensive experience with consumer electronics.

However, the novelty doesn't extend to user-friendliness. Getting fully into the packaging proved "kind of unintuitive and difficult," requiring Brownlee to rip some cardboard to access fasteners behind the phone holding it in place, followed by unscrewing these fasteners. He notes these components "didn't feel very premium," though he considers this forgivable given the extremely low price point of just $10.99.

The phone also ships with no charger or cable included, which Brownlee mentions matter-of-factly. More notably, despite being a rare 2026 device that features removable batteries—a genuine rarity in an era of sealed, non-user-serviceable smartphones—the back cover requires a specialized tool to open, and that tool isn't included in the box. On a positive note, the phone arrives from the factory with enough charge to power on immediately, which Brownlee demonstrates when the device boots up with the characteristic Bluey greeting: "Want to play with us? Let's do this."

---

## Hardware Design: Big Phone, Tiny Screen, Zero Connectivity

Physically, the Bluey Phone sits "definitely on the big side, but not too big." Brownlee compares it directly to the Nexus 6, noting it's actually "a little bit smaller" than Google's old phablet. The form factor suggests a design that children can comfortably hold, even if the overall footprint resembles a smartphone from a bygone era.

The most striking aspect of the hardware is the display: a mere 1.7-inch black and white screen. This isn't a simplified color display or a low-resolution color panel—it's genuinely monochrome, reminiscent of calculators or entry-level devices from decades past. Brownlee notes that across the entire display, you have "less than a thousand total pixels," and the device operates at "never go[ing] above 3 to 4 frames per second." These aren't limitations that result from processing constraints or design oversight—they're intentional features that serve the device's core philosophy.

The top of the phone features an antenna, which might suggest cellular capability. Brownlee is quick to clarify that this isn't the case: the Bluey Phone has no cell radios whatsoever, no internet connection of any kind, no cameras at all, and exclusively physical buttons. There's not even Bluetooth connectivity, which Brownlee notes seems like an obvious feature for a modern device, especially one marketed to children who might want to connect headphones.

---

## What It Actually Does: Speaking Numbers and Blowing Bubbles

When Brownlee demonstrates what happens when he types numbers on the Bluey Phone, the response is pure absurdity: instead of attempting to make a phone call or store contact information, the device simply speaks each number aloud. He enters "6, 7" and the phone responds by saying "Six... seven" in a cheerful voice. It's unclear whether this serves any practical purpose, but it certainly fits the whimsical spirit of the Bluey brand.

The speaker on the back offers three volume levels, and while a single rear-facing speaker might normally be cause for complaint, Brownlee praises the implementation. He notes that "the grill and the opening are more than big enough to ever accidentally block it with your fingers"—a small but thoughtful design choice that prevents the frustrating experience of muffling your own device.

Despite positioning itself as a minimal phone, the Bluey Phone does include several built-in games. Brownlee acknowledges this might seem "counterintuitive for a minimal phone," but he frames these games as "much healthier than doom scrolling TikTok or Instagram." His personal favorite is a bubble-blowing game that uses the device's microphone: you literally blow into the mic, and the phone generates bubbles on screen. It's a genuinely charming feature that encourages physical interaction rather than passive consumption.

Brownlee demonstrates the phone's boot time with visible delight, noting "look how fast this boots up" as the device powers on nearly instantaneously. The Bluey greeting plays again—"Want to play with us?"—and within moments, Brownlee is blowing virtual bubbles, remarking "Already on. So, one of the fastest booting phones."

---

## The Chat Feature: Bluey and Bingo as Your Only Contacts

One unexpected feature Brownlee explores is a chat function built into the phone. Given the prevalence of AI-powered assistants in modern devices, one might assume this involves some form of generative AI—"chat AI slop," as Brownlee puts it. However, the reality is much simpler and arguably more charming: the only conversational partner available on the Bluey Phone is Bluey and Bingo themselves, the two dog characters at the center of the animated series.

This isn't a chatbot powered by large language models or an attempt to create an AI companion. It's a feature specifically designed for fans of the show, allowing children (or adults, let's not judge) to have limited interactions with beloved cartoon characters. It suggests the device is squarely aimed at families who already watch Bluey and want to extend that engagement into a physical toy.

---

## Brownlee's Verdict: The Most Committed Minimal Phone Ever Made

Brownlee explicitly addresses how this device should be evaluated, cautioning that "if other reviewers are reviewing this phone up against other more expensive flagships, they're making a mistake here." The Bluey Phone doesn't have thin bezels, powerful processors, or impressive benchmark scores—in fact, Brownlee jokes he's "not even sure this thing's processor can run a benchmark to be honest."

Instead, he frames the device within the context of the "minimal phone" movement, where people seek to reduce their smartphone dependency by switching to stripped-down alternatives. While devices like the EIN or Light Phone have attempted this balance, Brownlee argues they still feature "huge touch screens and cameras and internet connections and apps"—still offering pathways to distraction, even if somewhat constrained.

The Bluey Phone takes a fundamentally different approach. With no cameras, no internet connection, and a display that can barely render content, the device achieves something its more sophisticated competitors cannot: a complete absence of doom-scrolling potential. Brownlee breaks down the practical implications: battery life stretches to "weeks instead of days," screen time becomes "minutes instead of hours," and "total doom scrolling is effectively reduced to zero."

He concludes his assessment with genuine enthusiasm: "This is good. This is a good minimal phone, especially for the price." More than that, he notes the device appears to be "undercutting the competition by, from what I can tell, quite a bit," making it not just an effective tool for digital wellness but an accessible one at that.

## Key Insights from the Bluey Phone Review

### The Spectrum of Minimalism Has a New Extreme End

Brownlee's review reveals that the minimal phone category has a hidden depth to its market segmentation. While devices like the Light Phone offer reduced functionality within a smartphone-like package, there's an audience—and possibly a larger one than manufacturers realize—for devices that commit fully to analog operation. The Bluey Phone represents the logical endpoint of this philosophy: not a simplified smartphone, but a device that shares more DNA with toys and basic communication devices from previous decades.

### Price Point Enables Radical Experimentation

At $10.99, the Bluey Phone can make design decisions that would be commercially unviable for devices in the $200-$300 minimal phone category. The use of cardboard packaging, the absence of included accessories, and the deliberately limited display all become features rather than flaws when the price matches the ambition. This suggests there's room in the market for extremely low-cost devices that serve very specific functions without attempting to be "good enough" at everything.

### Children's Products May Lead the Digital Wellness Movement

Rather than being primarily targeted at adults seeking to reduce screen time, the Bluey Phone's branding and features suggest it's designed for children—and this may be its most significant market positioning. Parents worried about their kids' device usage have traditionally faced a binary choice: a full smartphone or nothing. The Bluey Phone offers a third option: a device that maintains the psychological appeal of having a "real phone" while inherently preventing access to social media, inappropriate content, or excessive screen time. The built-in games and character interactions transform what could feel like deprivation into genuine fun.

### Build Quality Excuses Don't Apply at This Price

Brownlee's willingness to overlook the "not very premium" cardboard fasteners and the lack of included accessories demonstrates an important principle: expectations calibrate to price. At $10.99, consumers should expect exactly what they receive—a functional device with limited features, wrapped in minimal packaging. The real question isn't whether this approach feels premium, but whether it works. For the Bluey Phone's intended purpose, the answer appears to be yes.

## Who Should Pay Attention to This Review

This review will prove most valuable for **parents of young children** who are considering when and how to introduce their kids to mobile devices. The Bluey Phone offers a middle ground between complete device abstinence and full smartphone access, potentially delaying the age at which children need their own connected devices without leaving them entirely without a "phone" for communication or play.

**Digital wellness advocates and minimalism enthusiasts** will also find value in Brownlee's analysis. While many in this community have gravitated toward devices like the Light Phone or Punkt, Brownlee's observation that these devices still offer pathways to distraction highlights a potential gap in the market. The Bluey Phone proves that complete disconnection is technically feasible and commercially viable, even if the implementation is quirky and niche.

Finally, **tech reviewers and product designers** might extract lessons about intentional constraint. The Bluey Phone succeeds not despite its limitations, but because of them. Every removed feature—whether cameras, internet connectivity, or a color display—represents a decision that serves the device's core purpose. This approach to product design, where removal becomes a feature rather than a shortcoming, could inform future development across multiple categories.

---

## Conclusion: A Device That Commits Where Others Hesitate

The Bluey Phone, as reviewed by MKBHD, isn't trying to be your next daily driver or your backup device for travel. It's a focused tool designed for a specific purpose—keeping children (or anyone else) connected to the concept of having a phone without connecting them to the internet, social media, or endless scrolling. At $10.99, it's accessible in a way that more expensive minimal phones simply aren't, and its willingness to remove essentially all modern smartphone features represents a commitment to that mission that competitors have shied away from.

Brownlee's enthusiasm throughout the review—impressed by the boot speed, delighted by the bubble-blowing game, and genuinely complimentary of the device's digital wellness credentials—suggests he's found something genuinely interesting in a category he'd typically overlook. Whether the Bluey Phone becomes a cultural phenomenon or remains a niche product for Bluey fans, it demonstrates that the minimal phone movement has room for experimentation at every price point, and that sometimes, the most constrained device is the one that actually solves the problem it's trying to address.
