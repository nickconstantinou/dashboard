The psychology of video games: What motivates gamers | Jeff Kaplan and Lex Fridman

An In-Depth Exploration with Jeff Kaplan and Lex Fridman

---

Introduction

In the ever-evolving landscape of video game design, understanding what actually makes games fun—and why players keep coming back—remains one of the most elusive and crucial challenges facing developers today. In this thought-provoking conversation, game designer Jeff Kaplan, renowned for his work on *World of Warcraft* and *Overwatch*, sits down with Lex Fridman to explore the psychological underpinnings that drive player engagement and motivation.

The discussion arrives at a particularly timely moment. As the gaming industry generates hundreds of billions of dollars annually and gaming populations explode across demographics, the question of what genuinely motivates players has never been more commercially or artistically significant. Kaplan, speaking with the perspective of someone who has spent decades in the trenches of game development, offers a refreshingly honest and self-aware examination of how designers think about fun—and how often they get it wrong.

What makes this conversation particularly valuable is Kaplan's willingness to admit the limitations of his own understanding. Early in the discussion, he describes himself as "at best a quack psychologist," a tongue-in-cheek acknowledgment that game design, despite its massive economic footprint, remains more art than science. Yet within that admitted uncertainty lies profound insight into the complex interplay between extrinsic rewards and intrinsic satisfaction that keeps players engaged across thousands of hours of gameplay.

---

Key Concepts and Arguments

The Fundamental Challenge of Designing for "Fun"

The conversation opens with a deceptively simple question: how do designers actually fine-tune the core gameplay loop to maximize fun for the maximum number of people? Kaplan acknowledges this as one of the most difficult problems in game development, largely because "we all to some degree lack the self-awareness of how we tick." The challenge isn't merely technical—it's deeply psychological and personal.

Kaplan makes a crucial observation that reveals the complexity of this problem: when asked to describe the type of gamer they are, most people—including game designers themselves—tend to describe "the type of gamer I wish I was or the type of gamer I want you to think I am versus the type of gamer I actually am." This self-deception extends to the designers trying to create experiences for others. A designer might believe they understand what motivates players, only to discover their assumptions don't match reality.

The Essential Foundation: Playing Everything

For anyone aspiring to become an exceptional game designer, Kaplan offers straightforward but demanding advice: "You cannot be an exceptional game designer without playing the shit out of as much as you can and understanding on a deep level." This isn't about playing only the greatest hits or the most acclaimed titles. The "weirdest part," Kaplan notes, is that "you learn just as much from a shitty game that you do from an amazing game."

A poorly designed game can contain brilliant systems that were simply executed wrong—perhaps the tuning was off, the interface was confusing, or the visceral polish was missing. Understanding the difference between a flawed concept and a flawed execution teaches designers to diagnose problems in their own work. Kaplan describes his own playing experience as a constant analytical process: "When I'm playing, I'm not only like thinking about what makes this fun. I'm thinking about what makes this not fun. But I'm also watching everyone around me. My wife plays games, my kids play games, and understanding like, well, what do they do? And how are they different to me? Why are they finding enjoyment in this? Why are they not? What did they miss?"

The Two Fundamental Drivers: Extrinsic and Intrinsic Motivation

Kaplan identifies the "two driving motivators" as extrinsic and intrinsic motivation, and he emphasizes that players shift between these states at different times throughout their gaming lives. Extrinsic motivation involves external rewards—points, loot, achievements, leaderboard positions—while intrinsic motivation comes from the internal satisfaction of the activity itself: the joy of mastery, creativity, or simply exploring a beautiful world.

Loot serves as a primary example of extrinsic motivation, but Kaplan immediately complicates this simplification. Using *Overwatch*'s loot box system as an example, he explains that the system was "designed by a game designer not by a business person or whatever—not a commercial person." The team paid meticulous attention to what Kaplan calls "the visceral opening of the box, the sound it makes, the graphics, like the way things spill out and animate." This executional attention transformed a simple reward mechanism into something genuinely satisfying.

The psychology here engages what Kaplan describes as "the lizard brain part of it"—the instinctive, emotional response to seeing a chest or reward and knowing it's going to feel good. But simultaneously, there's "the spreadsheety part of it"—the analytical consideration of whether the loot represents an actual upgrade. Great game designers, according to Kaplan, understand how to tap into both of these dimensions simultaneously.

The Elements of Fun: A Taxonomy

Turning to his own framework for understanding player motivation, Kaplan draws an analogy to fiction writing: just as writers study plot, character development, setting, and theme, game designers must understand the "elements of fun." These aren't standardized anywhere in any textbook—Kaplan notes that no such textbook has been introduced to him—but he identifies several key categories:

Progression represents the sense that "I'm investing into this game. And then the game is recognizing my investment"—whether through leveling, accumulated gold, or other markers of time spent. This investment-based motivation creates a feedback loop where players feel their effort is being acknowledged and rewarded.

Mastery encompasses "pure raw skill"—the satisfaction of becoming genuinely good at something, of having your proficiency recognized either by other players or by the challenges you're now able to overcome.

Creativity and customization go hand-in-hand. Customization can be purely aesthetic, like "the black curly hair and I put an earring in my character," or it can be systemic, like creating "the whirlwind barbarian and I'm the first to do it." Both forms allow players to express their identity within the game world.

The Dynamic Nature of Player Motivation

Perhaps the most nuanced insight Kaplan offers is that players don't remain statically motivated by one factor. Using himself as an example, he describes being "not really loot motivated" but rather "more motivated by seeing the content the world has to offer." Yet he frequently finds himself "loot motivated for some period of time" because there might be "a dragon or a demon somewhere that I can't beat without this level of armor and sword."

Similarly, when facing a difficult boss, a player might shift into "creativity mode"—analyzing what abilities the enemy uses, what counters exist, and how to construct a new approach to overcome the challenge. These cycles of motivation aren't failures of design but rather the natural way players engage with complex systems. Understanding this dynamic flow allows designers to create experiences that meet players wherever they are in their current motivation cycle.

---

Detailed Takeaways

1. Self-Awareness Is the Designer's Greatest Challenge

The fundamental obstacle in game design isn't technical complexity—it's psychological. Designers must understand not just what motivates them personally, but how their own biases and preferences might differ from the players they're trying to serve. Kaplan's admission that we often describe the gamers we wish to be rather than the ones we actually are serves as a cautionary principle for anyone creating experiences for others. This requires ongoing observation of actual players, including watching friends and family play, and being "raw honest with exactly what you're seeing" rather than what you want to see.

2. Failure Teaches More Than Success

The counterintuitive principle that "you learn just as much from a shitty game that you do from an amazing game" carries profound implications for professional development in game design. Bad games often contain kernel ideas worth extracting—mechanics that would work with better tuning, interfaces, or polish. Train yourself to diagnose what specifically failed in a bad experience, and you'll develop diagnostic skills that pure consumption of excellent games won't provide.

3. The Mind Engages Through Dual Pathways

Great reward systems activate both the "lizard brain" (emotional, instinctive) and the "spreadsheet brain" (analytical, evaluative) simultaneously. The visceral satisfaction of opening a loot box means nothing if the contents aren't meaningfully evaluated against what the player already has. Designers must attend to both the emotional packaging and the logical substance of reward systems.

4. Motivation Is Contextual and Fluid

Players don't have single, fixed motivation profiles. A player primarily interested in exploring narrative content might temporarily become loot-motivated to access the next area. A player who generally ignores cosmetic rewards might suddenly engage with them when facing an unbeatable challenge. Design systems that support multiple motivation types simultaneously allow players to find their own path through the experience.

5. The Unwritten Rules of Fun

Kaplan's frustration that these "elements of fun" remain undefined in any textbook he knows of suggests an industry still maturing. Designers must develop their own frameworks through experience and observation rather than relying on established academic literature. This places additional burden on practitioners to document what they learn and share those insights across the industry.

---

Who This Is For

This conversation holds particular value for several distinct audiences. Aspiring game designers will find Kaplan's practical advice about consuming games analytically—studying both successes and failures—immediately applicable to their development practice. His framework for understanding motivation provides conceptual tools they can use when making design decisions.

Current game developers and designers may appreciate Kaplan's honest admission of uncertainty and his emphasis on observation and self-awareness. His breakdown of extrinsic versus intrinsic motivation offers a vocabulary for discussing and debating design decisions with colleagues.

Product designers and marketers outside gaming might find the psychology of motivation transferable to their own fields. The principle that external rewards must be paired with internal satisfaction, and that the "visceral" execution matters as much as the underlying system, applies broadly to any experience design.

Informed players curious about why games feel the way they do will gain perspective on the deliberate psychological engineering behind their favorite pastimes. Understanding that designers are genuinely trying to create satisfying experiences—even when they sometimes fail—can enrich one's appreciation of the medium.

For all these audiences, the conversation ultimately demonstrates that creating fun remains part science, part art, and significantly larger part honest self-examination. The games that succeed in engaging players for thousands of hours do so not through manipulation but through genuine understanding of what makes human beings tick—which, as Kaplan reminds us, we consistently overestimate our own comprehension of.
