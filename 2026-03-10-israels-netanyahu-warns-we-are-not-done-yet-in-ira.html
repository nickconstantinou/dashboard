Israel's Netanyahu warns 'we are not done yet' in Iran in break with Trump • FRANCE 24 English

Introduction

Israeli Prime Minister Benjamin Netanyahu has issued a stark warning to the international community: Israel's military offensive against Iran is far from over. In statements delivered to media outlets, the Israeli prime minister emphasized that the operation—which has targeted Iran's clerical leadership—continues to degrade Tehran's military capabilities, and he indicated that the campaign could extend well beyond what United States officials have suggested is acceptable. This defiant posture marks a notable divergence from the position taken by U.S. President Donald Trump, who has advocated for a quicker resolution to the conflict.

The situation unfolds against an increasingly complex regional backdrop, with Israel simultaneously engaged in military operations on two distinct fronts. While the campaign against Iran has drawn significant international attention, Israeli forces are also conducting extensive operations against Hezbollah in Lebanon—a conflict that analysts believe will prove far more protracted. France 24's Olivia Bizo, reporting from Jerusalem, provides crucial on-the-ground perspective about the strategic calculations driving Israel's approach, the domestic political dynamics fueling public support for the war, and the devastating human toll unfolding along the northern border with Lebanon.

Key Concepts and Arguments

The Two-Front War: Strategic Calculations and Operational Realities

Netanyahu's declaration that the war with Iran "could last longer than what Donald Trump is saying" reflects a fundamental tension between Israeli strategic objectives and American diplomatic preferences. According to analysis from security experts cited by Bizo, the distinction between these two theaters of operation could not be more pronounced. In Iran, Israeli military actions are being conducted in close coordination with American forces, and the United States has made clear its preference for bringing the conflict to a conclusion as rapidly as possible. This coordination imposes certain constraints on Israeli operational freedom, as the Biden administration—despite its support for Israel's right to defend itself—has sought to prevent the situation from escalating into a broader regional conflagration that could draw in American forces and destabilize global energy markets.

The dynamics in Lebanon present an entirely different picture. Israeli officials have indicated that the campaign against Hezbollah will persist for considerably longer than the operations in Iran, and Bizo noted that Israeli forces are essentially operating without the same level of international oversight or coordination that characterizes the Iranian campaign. The French government, for instance, warned Israel against bombing southern Lebanon, but these warnings have been "largely and completely ignored," according to Bizo's reporting. This unilateral freedom of action means that Israeli forces can pursue their objectives in Lebanon without the same degree of diplomatic pressure that constrains their operations across the border in Iran.

Domestic Support and Political Motivations

Perhaps the most striking element of the current conflict from a political perspective is the overwhelming public support it has generated within Israel. Polling data from last week reveals that approximately 80 percent of the Israeli population supports the war against Iran, with many citizens viewing Tehran's missile attacks as an existential threat to the nation. This level of public consensus stands in marked contrast to the deeply divided sentiment that characterized the earlier war in Gaza, which fractured Israeli society along political and ideological lines.

Bizo reported that she spoke with numerous Israelis who questioned why military action against Iran had not been undertaken sooner, suggesting a widespread belief that the government had delayed too long before confronting the Iranian threat directly. The almost universal acceptance of the current campaign stands in sharp contrast to the contentious debates that surrounded previous military operations, particularly the ongoing conflict in Gaza.

However, dissenting voices do exist, though they remain in the minority. Bizo attended a protest in Jerusalem last weekend where dozens of demonstrators gathered to oppose both the war and Netanyahu's government. Among those voices was a woman who suggested that the prime minister may be using the conflict as a political instrument—specifically as a distraction from his ongoing corruption trial and as a means of boosting poll numbers ahead of elections expected later this year. While these critics represent a small minority, they introduce an important note of political skepticism into what is otherwise a remarkably unified national posture.

The Human Cost Along the Northern Border

Bizo provided vivid firsthand testimony from her visit to northern Israel, just steps from the Lebanese border. Throughout the day she spent in the region, she witnessed continuous missile strikes, air defense interceptions, and drones passing overhead—experiences that underscore the immediacy of the threat facing communities in the north. Looking across the border into Lebanon, she observed hundreds of buildings that had been completely destroyed by Israeli bombardment, a testament to the intensity of the campaign against Hezbollah.

The Israeli military has issued evacuation orders for approximately 700,000 people in southern Lebanon, attempting to create a buffer zone that will protect Israeli communities in the north from Hezbollah rocket and missile fire. Yet notably, despite the ongoing attacks and the visible destruction in Lebanon, Israeli authorities have not issued evacuation orders for communities in northern Israel. This asymmetry in evacuation policies raises questions about the government's assessment of risk versus the strategic value of maintaining a visible Israeli presence in border communities.

Bizo interviewed one resident, a woman named Ron, who chose to remain in her community near the border despite the danger. Ron had evacuated following the October 7th attack in 2023 but returned with her family in March of the following year. She expressed confidence in Israel's defense systems and stated that she believes the current war will ultimately protect her children in the long term, enabling them to continue living in their community rather than being permanently displaced. This sentiment captures the complex calculus facing many Israelis in the north—between the genuine fear of attack and a determination to maintain normalcy in the face of persistent threat.

Detailed Takeaways

Netanyahu's defiance of American timeline preferences signals Israel's willingness to act independently when it perceives its core security interests at stake. The prime minister's explicit statement that the war could extend beyond what Trump has advocated reveals a strategic divergence that goes beyond mere rhetoric. Israel clearly believes that degrading Iran's military and nuclear capabilities requires sustained pressure that cannot be accommodated to American diplomatic timelines. This independent streak is particularly evident in the Lebanon theater, where Israel has effectively disregarded international appeals for restraint.

The dual-front conflict creates fundamentally different operational environments that will likely produce different outcomes. The coordination with American forces in Iran imposes constraints that do not exist in Lebanon, where Israeli forces enjoy something approaching free rein. This distinction suggests that while the Iranian campaign may reach some form of resolution in the nearer term—particularly if diplomatic pressure from Washington intensifies—the conflict with Hezbollah could extend for months or even years, reshaping the geopolitical landscape of the region in ways that extend well beyond the current crisis.

The 80 percent public approval rating represents an extraordinary level of national unity that provides Netanyahu with significant political cover. When a conflict generates this level of domestic support, it becomes extraordinarily difficult for political opponents to challenge the government's conduct of the war without appearing unpatriotic or out of step with mainstream opinion. This consensus also strengthens Israel's negotiating position, as the government can point to unified national will when resisting international pressure to curtail operations.

The potential exploitation of wartime politics for domestic purposes raises troubling questions about leadership motivations. The protester's accusation that Netanyahu may be using the conflict as a distraction from his corruption trial and as campaign fodder for upcoming elections deserves serious consideration, even if it represents a minority viewpoint. Leaders facing legal jeopardy sometimes find that external conflicts provide convenient opportunities to shift public attention and rally support—a dynamic that observers should monitor carefully as the war continues.

The absence of evacuation orders for northern Israel while Lebanon bears the brunt of displacement reveals a stark asymmetry in how the two sides are experiencing the conflict. The 700,000 Lebanese civilians who have been ordered to flee represent a massive humanitarian displacement, while Israeli communities in the north largely remain in place. Whether this reflects confidence in air defense capabilities, political calculations about demonstrating normalcy, or simply a different assessment of acceptable risk, it creates a profoundly unequal humanitarian situation that will likely shape international perceptions of the conflict.
