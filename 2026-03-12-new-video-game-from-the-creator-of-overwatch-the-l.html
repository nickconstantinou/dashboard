New Video Game from the creator of Overwatch: The Legend of California | Jeff Kaplan and Lex Fridman

In the world of video game development, few names carry as much weight as Jeff Kaplan. As the creative force behind both World of Warcraft and Overwatch, Kaplan has shaped the gaming experiences of millions of players worldwide. But after leaving Blizzard Entertainment, Kaplan has embarked on an entirely new journey—one that represents a dramatic departure from the bright, team-based hero shooters and fantasy MMOs that made him famous. In a revealing conversation with Lex Fridman, Kaplan unveiled his new studio, Kinugyama, and their inaugural project: an ambitious open-world game set in an alternate-history version of California during the gold rush era. This announcement marks not just a new chapter for Kaplan personally, but a fascinating case study in how veteran game developers approach creative reinvention when freed from the constraints of corporate game development.

The interview provides an intimate look at Kaplan's creative philosophy and the vision behind a game that defies easy categorization. Unlike the carefully polished, marketing-driven reveals typical of major studios, Kaplan's approach represents something increasingly rare in the industry: a developer simply wanting to make the game he himself would want to play, and trusting that authentic enthusiasm will find its audience.

The Birth of Kinugyama: A Studio Built on Creative Freedom

The conversation begins with Kaplan addressing perhaps the most unconventional aspect of his new venture: the studio name itself. "Our company name is Kinugyama which most people will struggle to pronounce," Kaplan admits with characteristic self-deprecation. This straightforward acknowledgment sets the tone for what follows—a refreshingly honest discussion about game development stripped of corporate polish. Kaplan mentions that the name carries deep personal meaning to him, though he playfully defers explaining the significance to a later point in the conversation.

The decision to found Kinugyama represents Kaplan's desire to define his own creative identity beyond the shadow of Blizzard. Having spent years at one of the world's largest game companies, he and longtime collaborator Tim Miller recognized an opportunity to create something fundamentally different. "We want to define what a Kinugyama game is," Kaplan explains, emphasizing that this isn't an attempt to replicate or crib from Blizzard's formula. Instead, it's about establishing a new creative framework built on personal inspiration rather than commercial calculation.

The studio currently operates with just 34 employees—a stark contrast to the hundreds of developers typically assigned to AAA projects at major publishers. This small, tight-knit team approach allows for more cohesive vision and faster decision-making, qualities that Kaplan suggests will translate into a more personal, intentional gaming experience. The interview reveals a developer who feels genuinely liberated by the constraints of operating outside the traditional publishing ecosystem.

The Legend of California: An Alternate History Survival Experience

The centerpiece of the conversation is Kaplan's new game, officially titled "The Legend of California." When pressed for details about the project, Kaplan provides a surprisingly comprehensive overview of both the game's setting and its mechanical philosophy. "It's an open world game," he explains. "People are going to call it a survival crafting game. People like to compartmentalize these. I think it's an action game. It's a game that takes place on a mythical island of California in the 1800s in the gold rush."

This setting represents a bold creative choice that immediately distinguishes the project from Kaplan's previous work. Rather than the futuristic optimism of Overwatch or the high fantasy realms of World of Warcraft, The Legend of California draws inspiration from American frontier history—though Kaplan is quick to clarify that historical accuracy is explicitly not the goal. "We're not trying to make a historical game. There's no historical accuracy to this," he states definitively. The island itself, for instance, is entirely fictional: "The island when first discovered is uninhabited. That's already not true. As we know, there were lots of people in California. It's an island, which we know is not true."

Instead, Kaplan describes the game as existing in a space between realism and surrealism. "It's this beautiful almost ultra-realistic version of California, but it's in an alternate history, alternate version of California where it's an island almost like an Atlantis type of ethereal island, but still very realistic to what the California terrain is." This conceptual framework allows the development team to capture the aesthetic and emotional resonance of the gold rush era—prospectors, cowboys, mining operations—while maintaining complete creative freedom to reshape geography and narrative to serve the gameplay experience.

The thematic ambition here is significant. Kaplan explicitly frames world-building as his primary artistic motivation: "I love creating worlds. everything that I've worked on before from World of Warcraft to Overwatch, it's always been how do you create this place for players to escape to." The Legend of California represents the latest iteration of this obsession with immersive world design, now applied to a setting that feels both familiar and otherworldly.

World Design: Handcrafted Detail Meets Procedural Flexibility

One of the most technically interesting aspects Kaplan discusses is the game's world generation system. The development team has taken a hybrid approach that combines handcrafted elements with procedural variation. "As a world, we handcrafted the world," Kaplan explains. "So like the shape of California is always the familiar shape of California, except it's an island. So, you know, there's no Nevada on the eastern side. Um, we handcrafted all of that. It looks gorgeous. And places like Yosemite are where you would expect Yosemite to be."

However, within this familiar framework, the game introduces significant variability. "We have like dozens of points of interest and those move around the map depending on the map seed," Kaplan continues. "And the map is also tiered in terms of difficulty. We don't really have levels in this game. We have tiers and there's only four tiers right now. Maybe that will change."

The implications of this design philosophy are substantial. Kaplan describes a system where not only do points of interest move around procedurally, but the difficulty progression itself varies by seed: "So, Mojave might be the easiest newbie area on your server, but on my server, it's endgame tier four area." This creates enormous replayability potential, as players exploring different servers—or returning to the game after updates—will encounter fundamentally different spatial relationships and progression challenges.

As a specific example, Kaplan points to a point of interest called Dread Rock, inspired by Alcatraz: "And we have a really amazing point of interest that we call Dread Rock that's inspired by Alcatraz. And like sure sometimes it's in San Francisco, but sometimes it can be sitting in the middle of the Mojave Desert also." This flexibility allows the development team to create a consistent aesthetic vocabulary while ensuring that exploration feels genuinely rewarding across multiple playthroughs.

Visual Philosophy: The Intersection of Art and Atmosphere

The conversation reveals Kaplan's deep investment in the game's visual presentation, particularly regarding lighting and atmosphere. Lex Fridman specifically notes that he experienced "probably the most beautiful sunset and sunrise I've seen in a game," high praise that Kaplan attributes to the work of lighting artist Mike Mara.

Kaplan's approach to visual design clearly draws from specific artistic inspirations. He cites the work of painter Albert Bierstadt, whose epic landscape paintings of California and the American West provided conceptual reference points for the game's aesthetic: "There's a lot of inspirations for this game, but there's a painter named Albert Bierstadt who I discovered while researching California and he painted these just epic landscape pieces of, you know, Yosemite and a lot of other gorgeous parts of California." Kaplan describes seeing these paintings and immediately wanting to walk around in those worlds—a desire that directly informs the development approach.

The connection between painting and video game as art forms represents something Kaplan feels strongly about. "I want I mean when I see that painting this is this is what video games brings to the table. So every art form that evolves after another gets to incorporate previous art forms. Movies got to take sound and you know fine art. We get to take everything including movies," he explains. This interdisciplinary thinking manifests in the team's commitment to creating visual environments that reward exploration and evoke genuine emotional responses.

The emphasis on lighting goes beyond mere aesthetics. Kaplan acknowledges that he's "not an artist" but recognizes intuitively when atmospheric elements work: "I just know I want to live in that world and these are the types of worlds that we want to make." This perspective—that game worlds should feel habitable and worth escaping to—underlies the entire development philosophy.

Tone and Atmosphere: Embracing Darkness and Solitude

Perhaps the most significant departure from Kaplan's previous work lies in the game's tonal register. Where Overwatch emphasized bright, aspirational heroism and team-based cooperation, The Legend of California aims for something markedly different. "This is really different," Kaplan acknowledges. "It's been hard for people when people were talking to us about, you know, they know me and Tim and they're, 'Oh, the Blizzard guys, the Overwatch guys. You're making like a bright, aspirational future team based hero shooter, right?' And I'm like, why would I want to do that?"

The game's tone is explicitly described as "edgier" with an emphasis on solitude and mystery. Kaplan paints a vivid picture of the intended player experience: "You're going to enter this world. It's going to feel lonier. It's going to feel mysterious, larger than you. You're going to feel small until you earn the right to feel big. It's going to feel really dangerous. You're going to want to see what's over that next hill. A little bit if the sun is setting like get to shelter. Can't wait to get back to my ranch and put my cozy fireplace on and wait till morning."

This emphasis on survival horror elements—finding shelter before dark, building safe spaces, gradually earning the right to feel powerful—represents a dramatic tonal shift. Kaplan explicitly contrasts this with Blizzard's approach: "That's what we would have done at Blizzard. We're going to be a little different here." The description evokes games like The Long Dark or Green Hell, where the environment itself functions as an antagonist and player vulnerability is central to the experience.

The phrase "solitary, solitary, solitary, almost scary, almost scary, uh, but beautiful" encapsulates this tension between danger and aesthetic appeal. Kaplan clearly views this atmospheric darkness as a feature rather than a bug—an opportunity to explore themes of isolation and survival that weren't appropriate for his previous projects but now represent the creative direction he finds most compelling.

Release Strategy: A Radical Departure from Corporate Convention

Perhaps the most unconventional aspect of Kaplan's announcement involves not the game itself but the distribution strategy. In a departure from traditional AAA marketing, Kaplan describes plans to release with minimal fanfare: "We're just gonna kind of quietly put it up on Steam and see what happens. You know, no like big corporate marketing group would ever think to do that in a million years without like some, you know, $10 million announce or whatever. We'll just kind of put it on Steam and be cool if people wishlisted it."

This approach represents a significant gamble—one that reflects both the freedom of operating outside corporate structures and Kaplan's apparent desire to let the game's quality speak for itself. The mention of wishlisting serves as a clear call to action, with Kaplan acknowledging the need for audience-building while rejecting expensive marketing campaigns.

The timeline for release involves several phases. Kaplan mentions shooting for "some sort of publicish alpha in March," followed by an Early Access launch on Steam. The Early Access model, while controversial among some玩家, represents an opportunity for the development team to iterate based on player feedback while maintaining financial sustainability. "Some people hate early access and won't touch it and I understand it. And then some people are like, I want to be in on the ground floor and see the thing from day one and watch it evolve," Kaplan acknowledges, demonstrating awareness of the model's polarised reception.

Kaplan also embraces the vulnerability inherent in this approach: "This game has more rough edges probably like the most rough edges we would have at Blizzard is like showing you at Blizzcon which was heavily polished and controlled. This is going to be more, you know, like in development than anything else I've ever worked on." This honesty about the game's unfinished state represents both a practical reality and a philosophical commitment to authenticity over polish.

Key Insights: What This Means for the Industry and Players

The value of creative reinvention in game development: Jeff Kaplan's departure from Blizzard to found Kinugyama demonstrates that even highly successful developers can benefit from stepping outside their comfort zones. Rather than repeating proven formulas, Kaplan chose to explore themes and genres that personally excited him, suggesting that authentic creative passion may ultimately produce more compelling results than commercial calculation.

Alternate history as a design tool: By explicitly rejecting historical accuracy in favor of an "alternate history" framework, Kaplan's team gains creative freedom while maintaining atmospheric authenticity. This approach—creating fictionalized versions of real places rather than strict simulations—offers a model for developers seeking to balance familiarity with imaginative freedom.

Procedural variation as a means of replayability: The tiered, seed-based world generation system described in the interview suggests that meaningful procedural variation can extend playtime and discovery beyond what static, handcrafted worlds offer. By moving both points of interest and difficulty tiers across seeds, the game ensures that no two server experiences feel identical.

Atmosphere as a primary design pillar: The interview reveals a development team that prioritizes emotional response—specifically the feelings of danger, solitude, and wonder—over mechanical complexity. This suggests that for certain types of games, atmospheric design may be more important than innovative gameplay systems.

Independent distribution as a philosophical choice: The decision to release quietly on Steam without traditional marketing reflects both practical constraints and genuine philosophical alignment with indie development values. This approach may resonate with players weary of aggressive AAA marketing while appealing to those who enjoy discovering games organically.

The Legend of California and the Kinugyama project represent something compelling for several distinct audiences. The game clearly targets players who enjoy survival crafting experiences like Rust, The Long Dark, or Subnautica, where environmental challenges and atmospheric tension take precedence over narrative or competitive multiplayer elements.

The interview also holds significant value for those interested in game development business models. Kaplan's unconventional release strategy—quiet Steam launch, Early Access, minimal marketing—provides a case study in how established developers can leverage their reputations without relying on traditional publishing structures. As the gaming industry continues to evolve toward creator-driven models, Kaplan's approach offers a template worth examining.

Finally, anyone interested in the intersection of visual art and interactive media will find the discussion of artistic inspiration—from Albert Bierstadt's paintings to Mike Mara's lighting work—illuminating. The conversation reveals a developer who thinks deeply about how games can function as experiential art forms, translating the emotional impact of static visual art into navigable, habitable digital spaces.

For readers seeking to understand where one of gaming's most influential creators is heading next, this interview provides essential context. The Legend of California may represent Kaplan's most personal project yet—a game designed not to meet market expectations but to realize a specific creative vision that has been developing throughout his career.
