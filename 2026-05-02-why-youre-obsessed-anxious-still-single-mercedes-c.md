## 1. Introduction

The modern dating landscape has become a minefield for emotionally available people, and therapist Mercedes Coffman has a compelling explanation for why so many capable, well-intentioned individuals find themselves stuck in patterns of heartbreak, confusion, and chronic loneliness.

In this episode, Coffman—a relationship therapist who works primarily with emotionally available clients—explains how what she calls "avoidant culture" has systematically rewired our expectations around relationships. The episode unpacks why dating apps disproportionately reward emotional unavailability, how being involved with an emotionally unavailable person creates measurable damage to your nervous system, and what genuine emotional alignment actually looks like beyond surface-level compatibility markers.

Coffman introduces a practical framework for navigating early dating without losing yourself to biochemical attachment, and she reveals she's actively building a dating app designed specifically for emotionally available people—born from watching her clients become increasingly disillusioned with a system that seems built to punish them.

## 2. Key Concepts & Arguments

### The Architecture of Avoidant Culture

Coffman defines avoidant culture not as clinical attachment avoidance, but as a broader cultural orientation toward minimizing discomfort, inconvenience, and effort in relationships. This includes avoiding anything that requires time, consistency, or follow-through. She argues this has become the default operating mode of modern dating, largely because the digital infrastructure supporting it—particularly dating apps—is designed to reinforce these behaviors.

The apps optimize for novelty and dopamine, creating systems where people are perpetually chasing the excitement of new matches rather than investing in existing connections. Coffman describes how this has fundamentally shifted what's possible in dating: emotionally available people increasingly lower their standards just to maintain any relationship at all, believing there's "no hope left" if they hold firm to their original requirements.

### The Neurological Cost of Dating Avoidantly

One of the most striking aspects of Coffman's analysis is how she frames the impact of dating an emotionally unavailable person on the nervous system. She explains that these partners typically present with intense, love-bombing behavior initially—creating rapid attachment—before gradually revealing their inability to sustain relationship responsibilities.

This pattern creates what she calls "micro grief": a cycle of dopamine spikes during exciting early stages, followed by crashes when the unavailable partner withdraws. The nervous system responds with elevated cortisol (stress hormones), and over time, people experience measurable effects including fatigue, mood disorders, sleep disturbances, and appetite changes. Coffman emphasizes this damage is "much more harmful than we think it actually is."

### Why Modern Dating Punishes Availability

Coffman makes a pointed observation: dating apps don't intentionally punish emotionally available people, but their structure inherently reinforces emotional unavailability. Unavailable people are looking for dopamine hits, comfort, and minimal effort—exactly what the apps provide. Emotionally available people, by contrast, seek consistency, follow-through, and singular focus on one person at a time.

The paradox is that the more options dating apps provide, the less invested people become in any single option. So the emotionally unavailable person thrives in an environment designed for their behavior patterns, while the emotionally available person struggles to find anything that resembles what they're actually looking for.

### The Entropy of the Dating Pool

Coffman describes what she sees as a systemic "race to the bottom" in modern dating. A small number of emotionally unavailable people may become "fixed" by dating someone emotionally available. But the opposite dynamic occurs far more frequently: emotionally available people get damaged, leave the dating pool, or become closed off themselves.

This creates entropy in the system. The people who most want genuine connection become the most likely to get hurt and exit. Those who are comfortable with avoidance remain available in the pool but offer little of value to those who stay. The result is growing loneliness even among people actively seeking relationships.

### What True Emotional Alignment Requires

Coffman identifies a hierarchy of qualities that constitute genuine compatibility, starting with willingness and time availability. A person can possess emotional intelligence, capacity, and maturity, but if they lack time to invest in a relationship, they cannot be aligned with someone who has those things.

Second is capacity: the ability to hold their own emotions and their partner's emotions simultaneously, particularly during uncomfortable conversations. Can they sit with discomfort without retreating, withdrawing, or becoming defensive? Third is emotional maturity: how they manage rejection, whether they become aggressive or reactive, or if they can remain responsive and regulated.

Crucially, Coffman notes this framework isn't really about compatibility in the romantic sense—it's about emotional green flags. Two people can meet all these criteria and still be incompatible for countless other reasons. But without these foundational emotional qualities, compatibility becomes irrelevant.

### The MOP Framework: Protecting Yourself from Biochemical Hijacking

Coffman introduces a three-part framework designed to help people maintain clarity while dating:

**Match Effort**: When chemistry hits and you feel intensely attached, the instinct is to overgive. This over-investment accelerates the addiction cycle and clouds mental clarity. Match what the other person is putting in—not more, not less.

**Observe for Patterns**: People often don't wait to see a person's patterns before becoming attached. Within a couple of weeks or months, patterns emerge. Maintaining clarity during this observation period is crucial.

**Pace Access**: Physical access to a person dramatically increases your likelihood of getting into what Coffman calls a "dopamine fix." Clarity deteriorates rapidly once physical intimacy enters the picture.

The framework is designed to protect against what Coffman describes as the natural biochemical hijacking that occurs in early attachment—a period when dopamine increases and serotonin decreases, systematically undermining your ability to make good decisions.

### The Neuroscience of Rationalization

Coffman explains how the excuse-making process works neurologically: as attachment deepens, your capacity for discernment deteriorates. Behaviors you would normally find unacceptable get reframed and justified. The unavailable partner who was "just busy" becomes acceptable. The text that should have been a red flag gets explained away.

She notes that women particularly tend to trust words over behaviors, which makes them especially vulnerable to this dynamic—evolutionary reasons likely play a role, but in modern dating contexts, this can become a liability. Meanwhile, the emotionally unavailable person hasn't actually changed; your ability to see them clearly has simply been compromised by your own neurochemistry.

## 3. Detailed Takeaways

**Dating apps require a fundamentally different assessment than traditional dating.** Coffman points out that profiles focus on age, interests, and surface-level hobbies—but say nothing about conflict repair ability, love language, emotional availability, emotional capacity, or emotional maturity. People bond over shared colleges or food preferences, then discover deep incompatibilities in the relational fundamentals. Before attaching, assess for the things the apps don't measure.

**Treat yourself like a future addict who hasn't yet taken the drug.** The early dating period—when you're in the passionate attachment phase—is designed by evolution to make you think you've found your soulmate. Your pre-frontal cortex is being chemically compromised. Use this period to observe, not just to feel. Your future self will thank you for maintaining clarity now.

**Match effort, not intensity.** When you feel that rush of connection, the temptation is to give more, try harder, invest more heavily. This is the biochemical addiction response talking. The healthier approach is to give reciprocally—what they give, you give back. This keeps you grounded in reality rather than drifting into the dopamine fantasy.

**Watch for patterns, not potential.** Instead of focusing on who someone might become or how good the connection feels in its peak moments, observe how they respond when there's no reward at the end. How do they treat service workers? What's their patience like when things go wrong? Can they handle feedback without withdrawing? These patterns reveal actual capacity far more reliably than chemistry.

**Pace access protects clarity.** The more physical access you grant someone early in dating, the faster your ability to see them clearly deteriorates. This doesn't mean avoiding intimacy entirely, but it means being intentional about the pace, recognizing that each level of access reduces your objectivity.

**Loneliness can be just as damaging as bad relationships.** Coffman notes that many of her emotionally available clients have simply stopped dating entirely out of exhaustion and discouragement. But chronic loneliness also damages the nervous system. Neither option—staying in situationships nor isolating completely—is healthy. The answer isn't to accept either extreme, but to find environments and approaches that actually support emotionally available people.

## 4. Who This Is For

This episode is essential listening for anyone who considers themselves emotionally available and has found modern dating demoralizing, confusing, or exhausting. If you've noticed yourself lowering standards, getting attached too quickly to people who ultimately couldn't meet your needs, or feeling increasingly cynical about the possibility of finding genuine connection—you'll likely find Coffman's analysis both validating and illuminating.

It's particularly valuable for people who have experienced the pattern of intense early attraction followed by gradual withdrawal and ghosting, leaving them questioning themselves rather than the people who created the dynamic. Coffman provides a framework for understanding that pattern as a systemic issue, not a personal failing.

The episode is also useful for people entering the dating world after a long relationship or period of absence, as it offers concrete tools for protecting yourself from biochemical hijacking while remaining open to genuine connection. And for those who recognize their own avoidant patterns and want to understand how they affect the people they're dating, this provides crucial perspective on the ripple effects of emotional unavailability.</p>
