# Scott Galloway: AI Wasn't Built for You. The Rich Don't Need You Anymore!

Key Insights

  • The US and AI are the two brands that have "fallen fastest" in the last 18 months — Gallup data shows, for the first time in history, more people globally now view China as a force for good in the world than view the United States, while AI's brand has swung from "scary but exciting" to "scary and already enriching the wealthiest."
  • Only people earning over $200,000/year have a net positive view of AI — wealthier individuals are the largest users of AI and see it as a portfolio fuel and innovation driver, while middle-class Americans see rising electricity bills and no pathway to invest in the companies driving the technology.
  • The unemployment data directly contradicts the AI apocalypse narrative — US unemployment sits at 4.5% (slightly below historical average), youth unemployment at 8.8%, and new business permits per capita in the US have *doubled* in the last decade, suggesting no "exogenous meteor" is visibly striking the employment market.
  • The very roles declared most endangered are now growing — radiologist job listings are up in 2026 despite predictions of total extinction, and coding job listings are up 11% year-on-year, as AI has made vibe coding and startup creation broadly accessible.
  • Amazon's robotic scale is staggering and underappreciated — Amazon operates *one million* industrial robots, representing two and a half times the number in the rest of the entire United States combined, and the company has stated it will double its largest business by 2032 without a single incremental hire.

    Introduction

    In a wide-ranging and often provocative conversation on *The Diary of a CEO* podcast, Steven Bartlett sits down with Professor Scott Galloway — the NYU marketing professor, author of *The Algebra of Wealth* and *Prof.sc Galloway*, and founder of multiple companies — to examine one of the most consequential questions of our time: what is artificial intelligence *actually* doing to the world, and who is it serving?

    The timing is deliberate. Over the preceding 18 months, public perception of AI has shifted sharply. The technology that was once portrayed as an exciting, if somewhat daunting, frontier — something that would reshape productivity, unlock new businesses, and create unprecedented wealth — has increasingly come to be seen as something being imposed on ordinary people by a coastal tech elite who seem intent on narrating their own obsolescence. Sam Altman has told people to stop complaining about energy costs. Elon Musk has predicted that AI and robots will replace virtually all jobs, rendering work "optional" like "growing your own vegetables." Meanwhile, ordinary households have watched electricity bills rise while being told they should be excited about chatbots.

    Galloway, who describes himself as an AI optimist, is one of the few voices in this space willing to call the bluff of his own industry. He doesn't dismiss AI's significance — he believes it will be enormously consequential — but he insists the catastrophizing coming from tech CEOs is largely fundraising theater, and that the real story is one of a technology being built primarily to serve and enrich the people who already have capital, while the human cost of transition is being woefully underaddressed by policy makers. This conversation is a corrective to a lot of the noise, grounded in data, historical precedent, and the practical experience of someone running real businesses and hiring real people.

    ---

    The Brand Destruction of America and AI

    Galloway opens the conversation by identifying what he sees as the two most significant "brand falls" of the preceding 18 months. The first is the United States itself. He argues that America has historically functioned as the "enforcer of the operating system" of the global order, the power that kept rogue nations in check. Under the Trump administration, he contends, the US has itself become that rogue nation. The consequences are measurable: for the first time in recorded history, more people around the world now view China as a force for good in the world than view the United States. This is not a marginal shift in sentiment — it is a fundamental realignment in global perception, one with profound implications for soft power, alliance-building, and the dollar's role as the world's reserve currency.

    The second great fall, Galloway argues, is AI itself — and Sam Altman in particular, whom he describes as having "gone to the dark side." The AI brand has undergone a remarkable transformation. Eighteen months ago, AI was exciting but uncertain: scary in its potential, but potentially democratizing and wealth-creating. Today, it is scary *and* widely perceived as a wealth-creation engine already captured by the already-wealthy. The mechanism behind this perception shift is not subtle: the only demographic cohort in the United States with a net positive view of AI is people earning more than $200,000 per year. These are the people who are heaviest users of AI tools, whose portfolios are being buoyed by tech stock appreciation, and who have the capital to invest directly in AI companies. Everyone else — the middle-class family watching their electricity bill climb while reading about Sam Altman's plans to put humanity inside data centers — sees AI very differently.

    Galloway is unsparing in his criticism of how the brand has been managed. He cites Sam Altman's now-infamous remark about energy costs — "stop complaining about energy costs. Think about the amount of energy it takes to raise a child" — as emblematic of a profound disconnect between the people building this technology and the people being asked to accept its consequences. "They haven't really managed the brand well," he says. "In the last eighteen months, it's come from something kinda scary, but we can be optimistic and a wealth creator to something that's very scary and something that's a wealth creator for the wealthiest already."

    ---

    Debunking the Job Apocalypse

    The heart of the conversation turns to the most alarming claims emanating from AI company leadership. Bartlett reads two quotes directly: Elon Musk's assertion that "AI and robots will replace all jobs. Working will be optional, like growing your own vegetables instead of buying them from a store. The challenge will be fulfillment — how do you derive fulfillment and meaning in life?" And Sam Altman's more recent suggestion that by 2028, "more of the world's intellectual capacity could reside inside data centers than outside them." Bartlett confesses that when he reads Sam Altman's blog posts carefully, he finds them describing "a fairly dystopian future." So what, he asks Galloway, is the truth versus the marketing?

    Galloway's answer is blunt: "I think it's mostly bullshit." He is careful to frame this as a probabilistic assessment rather than certainty, but his reasoning is grounded in both historical pattern and current data. He draws a distinction between a *real* phenomenon he is concerned about — the potential for a V-shaped disruption so severe that civil society buckles before recovery arrives — and the specific claim that AI will permanently eliminate the need for human work. The catastrophizing, he argues, is "nothing more than thinly veiled attempt to say, my technology is so devastating that it's gonna shift society, and you should invest at this crazy valuation."

    His primary evidence is simple and empirical: if you didn't know that a transformative technology was supposedly on the verge of destroying the employment market, and you looked only at the macroeconomic data, "you wouldn't know there's anything going on." The US unemployment rate stands at 4.5 percent. Youth unemployment is at 8.8 percent — slightly *below* the historical average. New business permits per capita in the United States have *doubled* in the last ten years. These are not numbers consistent with an imminent employment apocalypse.

    Galloway walks through the specific case studies that should have served as ground zero for the AI jobs revolution. Radiologists were supposed to be the first casualty: scan billions of images and no one needs a radiologist anymore. "Kid, mama, don't let your daughter grow up to be a radiologist because that job's going away." And yet: job listings for radiologists in 2026 are *up*. The reason, Galloway explains, is that while AI is genuinely useful at scanning images — a component of the job — the actual value of a radiologist lies in diagnosing the illness and constructing a treatment plan, skills that require contextual judgment and human relationship. Similarly, job listings for coders are up 11 percent year-on-year because AI has made it possible for more people to build software. "Where I will be wrong," Galloway says carefully, "is if there is sustained job destruction and the new jobs created and new businesses and the employment those jobs create doesn't keep up with new jobs."

    The historical analogy he invokes is the agricultural revolution and every subsequent technological leap: some jobs are lost, productivity increases, new opportunities emerge, and the long-term employment picture improves. He does not dismiss the transitional pain. "Do some people be on the wrong side of the trade? Absolutely." But he rejects the framing that this time is categorically different, at least as far as total employment destruction is concerned.

    The one scenario that genuinely concerns him is civilizational in nature rather than economic: "At 20% unemployment, the French had a revolution, and Weimar Germany turned very ugly. At 20% unemployment, especially among youth, especially young men tend to get very angry and take to the streets." You don't need 100 percent unemployment to trigger social breakdown, he notes — just 20 percent sustained among the right demographic. The question, then, is whether the V-shaped disruption could be steep enough, and the recovery slow enough, to produce that kind of political rupture even if the medium-term employment picture ultimately improves.

    Bartlett, for his part, offers a compelling personal data point. He describes himself as "head of recruitment" across his companies, and says he recently noticed himself pausing before hiring candidates he would have eagerly snapped up two months earlier — not because of economic pressures but because new AI tools have emerged that offer alternative solutions. This is a microcosm of the reshaping he sees: AI fluency is becoming a genuine filter in hiring decisions, and people who don't adapt may find themselves on the wrong side of that filter even as the overall employment picture holds.

    ---

    The Reshaping That Is Actually Happening

    Galloway is careful to distinguish between the macro-level debate about total employment and the more granular reality of a labor market in active, significant transformation. One of the most striking data points he offers is structural rather than cyclical: for the last 30 to 40 years, the unemployment rate among college graduates has been lower than among non-college graduates. That relationship *reversed this year*. Non-college graduates now have lower unemployment than college graduates, driven by a boom in vocational work. "If you think about it," Galloway says, "all of these data centers need carpenters, welders, plumbers." The physical infrastructure of the AI economy — the cables, the cooling systems, the buildings — is creating demand for tradespeople that the college-prep pipeline is not serving.

    The new college graduate cohort faces a particularly interesting dynamic. When Galloway graduated from Berkeley in 1992, he tells Bartlett, 40 percent of his class had a job on graduation day — meaning 60 percent did not. He suspects that at Stern, where Bartlett presumably went, the ratio today may be roughly reversed: 40 percent of graduates will start their own business on or around graduation day. "I would bet there's gonna be 30 to 50 businesses that are started on graduation day. There's kids dropping out of their second year of business school to start businesses because they just raised $10,000,000 in a series a." AI, he argues, has dramatically lowered the cost of starting a technology company, making this kind of entrepreneurial acceleration possible for a much wider range of people. In his own class in 1992, only two businesses were started — one of which was his own company with his cofounder.

    This does not mean the transition is painless or fair. Galloway points to Denmark — which spends 2 percent of GDP on retraining and vocational education — as a counterpoint to American policy. "We spent point 2% in The US," he says. "So we're not good at retraining. We're really bad at it, actually. We're not good at taking care of the people on the wrong side." The failure, in his view, is not technological but political: a society that is willing to pour hundreds of billions into AI infrastructure while spending almost nothing on human transition support is making a choice that will concentrate the costs of progress on those least able to bear them.

    ---

    The Real Story: AI and Robotics Colliding

    Midway through the conversation, Bartlett presses Galloway on whether the speed and internet-native distribution of modern AI — which allows new models from Anthropic or ChatGPT to spread globally overnight, unlike the industrial revolution that required physical infrastructure — might make this disruption categorically different from previous technological transitions. Galloway accepts the premise but draws an unexpected conclusion.

    "The more important technology in terms of how it's gonna change the world and what will create more shareholder value is not AI. It's robotics."

    His reasoning is specific and stock-picker driven. He explains that his "big tech stock pick for '26 is Amazon" — not because of AWS or Prime subscriptions, but because of Amazon's industrial robot strategy. He notes that Amazon acquired a robotics company called Kiva over a decade ago and has since built an industrial robot footprint that dwarfs the rest of the country. "There are 400,000 industrialized robots in The US, and there's a million at Amazon. So million Amazon is two and a half times the number of industrialized robots as the rest of the nation combined."

    Amazon has publicly stated it will double its largest business — its retail operation — by 2032 without an incremental hire. Galloway finds this extraordinary. The collision of AI's pattern-recognition capabilities with industrialized robotics is where "rubber meets the road in terms of shareholder value." This is the combination he finds genuinely transformative, not the chatbot interfaces generating most of the headlines.
