The Rest Is History

670. Tom Holland Meets Paul McCartney

Introduction

In a landmark episode of the Rest is History podcast, host Tom Holland sits down with Paul McCartney for an extensive conversation about the legendary Beatle's new album, *The Boys of Dungeon Lane*, and the Liverpool upbringing that shaped one of the twentieth century's most influential musical voices. This interview represents an unprecedented crossover between the popular history podcast and living history itself—McCartney not only witnessed the transformative decade of the 1960s but served as its central creative force alongside John Lennon.

The conversation arrives at a moment when McCartney has returned to deeply personal territory. *The Boys of Dungeon Lane* draws extensively from his childhood and teenage years in Liverpool, examining the specific circumstances and city that produced both The Beatles and the man who would become the world's most celebrated composer. For Tom Holland, whose Rest is History podcast typically explores ancient Rome, the Ottoman Empire, and other historical epochs, this interview represents something different: a chance to excavate the recent past with one of its primary architects.

What emerges is not merely an artist discussing new work but a meditation on place, memory, and the invisible forces that shape genius. McCartney reflects on the Irish roots that made Liverpool feel separate from England, the wartime humor that became The Beatles' signature attitude, the sailors who smuggled American rock and roll into a country starving for it, and the communal creative process that could produce "Yesterday" or "Help!" in a matter of minutes. This is a conversation about how history—the large-scale forces of war, migration, class, and culture—collides with individual talent to produce moments that change the world.

The Character of Liverpool

The conversation opens with a deceptively simple question: could Paul McCartney or The Beatles have emerged from any other city? McCartney's answer is immediate and emphatic. Liverpool's distinctive character, he argues, was essential to everything that followed.

"Liverpool's Irish influence" stands at the center of this identity. Both of McCartney's parents traced their roots to Ireland, and this heritage created a city that never quite identified as fully English. McCartney describes a shared sense among Liverpudlians that "we were special," that Liverpool was a "great grand historic place" with its own importance. The city had been, after all, Britain's second city—second only to London in wealth and prominence—fueled by its position as a major global port. McCartney recalls watching ocean liners depart for Canada and other destinations, a sight that reinforced Liverpool's connection to the wider world.

This Irish heritage carried darker implications that McCartney acknowledges with characteristic honesty. "It was only later," he notes, "that you learn that this was slave trade." The city's wealth came substantially from the transatlantic slave trade, though McCartney describes how what they saw as children was more immediately the presence of Caribbean families—descendants of those who had come through Liverpool's ports, though "we didn't talk about it then."

Yet this dark history coexisted with something vital: Liverpool's openness to the world. The port brought not just goods but sounds, ideas, and influences that bypassed the more insular English interior. This global connection would prove essential when McCartney and his contemporaries began hunting for American music.

War, Humor, and the Making of The Beatles

McCartney was born in 1942, in the middle of the Second World War. All four Beatles were wartime children—Ringo Starr, the eldest, was old enough to have some memories of bombing, while McCartney was not. Yet the war's shadow fell heavily on the city and shaped the generation that would become The Beatles in ways both obvious and subtle.

"What I say is the feeling that the grown-ups had was having to laugh it off," McCartney explains. "This is a world war. Yeah. But they've just gotta carry on." The humor was not evasion but survival mechanism. McCartney describes his uncles as "great joke tellers," and he never heard any of them complain about life's difficulties. "They just came through it, and so it was kind of wasn't allowed" to wallow in hardship. This was Liverpool's gift to The Beatles: the ability to find levity in catastrophe, to face down cynicism with wit.

When the band arrived in America in 1964, facing a New York press corps eager to mock the "long-haired boys from England," this upbringing served them well. "We gave as good as we got," McCartney says, attributing this readiness directly to their Liverpool formation. The Beatles' famous irreverence—their willingness to parry serious criticism with self-deprecating humor—was not affectation but authentic cultural inheritance.

The physical legacy of the war was everywhere. Liverpool's bomb damage took decades to repair, and the bomb sites became unofficial playing fields. McCartney describes playing football on what locals called "the Bombie"—a bomb site that children simply accepted as part of the landscape. "We didn't think anything of it," he says, noting that they only later realized the ominous origin of the name. The war had passed, but its evidence remained, incorporated into daily life.

The timing proved providential. When McCartney and his peers reached military age, national service was winding down. "It was as if god opened the waters and the Israelites could just go through," McCartney says, with evident wonder. "We suddenly all of that had gone." The Beatles escaped military service by approximately one year—a narrow window that allowed them to pursue music during the early 1960s rather than reporting to barracks.

Salesman Saint: A Song About Parents

The centerpiece of the interview is "Salesman Saint," one of the new album's tracks that McCartney premieres during the conversation. The song is about his parents, and its opening lines establish their characters immediately: his father was a salesman; his mother was a saint who worked "every god given minute" while "the war was nearly over / The peace would soon begin."

McCartney's mother Mary was a community midwife, serving Liverpool families across the economic spectrum. She delivered babies in wealthy "leafy homes" and in households "with very little," serving the entire community without distinction. This role carried unexpected benefits: as a midwife, Mary earned housing upgrades. Each time she helped deliver a baby, the family would move to slightly better accommodations.

"We moved around quite a lot and didn't realize till we were much older that, oh, that's why we moved," McCartney recalls. The progression was tangible: the family eventually reached Milton Road, which McCartney remembers with particular affection because it had an indoor toilet. "Wow. An indoor toilet," he says, still amused by the memory. For the young McCartney family, these moves represented genuine aspiration—"we thought we were going somewhere."

Mary McCartney was also aspirational in her ambitions for her children. McCartney reveals that she actively tried to make her sons speak "posh," hoping they might become doctors or other professionals. "She thought she was hoping we'd be doctors or something," McCartney says, noting that his wife Nancy has told him that "people love it when you talk Liverpool." Yet Mary wanted her sons to transcend their working-class roots.

The memories of Mary at work remain vivid. "Going out and home delivering all these babies and the parents being so in love with you—I have one big memory of her," McCartney says. "It was in the winter, and there'd been a heavy snowfall. And she got called up because we had a phone, one phone in the house, and she got called to go to a birth. So she got on her bike because they didn't have cars. She got on her bike in this deep snow with a uniform on with a little suitcase on the back and a little basket on the front." McCartney recalls watching her cycle away through the streetlights, thinking, "wow. That's pretty brave."

Tom Holland notes that the interview coincides with International Midwife Day, and McCartney expresses warm regard for the profession. Holland's own wife, it emerges, is a midwife, and she had specifically requested that Holland ask about Mary's career.

The Informal Transmission of Rock and Roll

One of the interview's most fascinating segments explores how McCartney and his contemporaries accessed American rock and roll in a Britain before streaming, before widespread radio play of American music, and before the internet. The story reveals both the scarcity of the era and the ingenuity of young musicians hungry for something new.

Sailors, particularly those in the merchant navy who traveled to American ports, were the crucial link. "A lot of them that we would know came back from America, whether they'd been to New Orleans or down south," McCartney explains. "And they had records that nobody else had." The system was informal and communal: a sailor would lend his records to local musicians, who would borrow them, copy the songs, and pass both records and knowledge to others. "The record would go around, and we'd all learn it."

McCartney emphasizes how different this was from the present day: "For people of subsequent generations, it's hard to get our heads around how difficult it was to access music." Now, a teenager can stream any recording ever made in seconds. In the 1950s, gaining access to Little Richard or Chuck Berry required navigating networks of sailors, record collectors, and friends willing to share rare 78s. This scarcity had an unexpected effect: it created self-selecting communities of the genuinely committed.

"You would know certain chords, and then someone would know an extra chord," McCartney says. "So you would go to his house and learn this extra chord, and you build up your knowledge through things like that." The learning was entirely oral and aural. "There was none of us ever learned to read or write music," McCartney notes—a striking fact about The Beatles and most groups of their era. Jeff Lynne ofELO later confirmed this was true of his generation as well: "He says, oh, we just made it up."

This method of transmission created something valuable that formal training might have inhibited. "There's a great strength in me showing you a chord or a riff or something, and it's just going from mind to mind," McCartney reflects. "There's no paper involved." He draws a parallel to Irish folk music, which similarly was never written down but passed directly from musician to musician, each version evolving in the sharing. "We had a lot of that," he says. "And that was really nice, for transference of ideas."

The Beatles' Creative Process

The interview concludes with McCartney's vivid description of how The Beatles actually created songs in the studio—a process that seems almost impossibly casual given its results. The scene unfolds in a recording studio on a Monday morning, with the band gathering around 10 or 10:30 AM.

"In the beginning, it would mainly be John and I, and we would've just written something the week before, written some songs," McCartney explains. They'd come in, and everyone would gather. "George Martin would say, oh, okay. What is it? What are you gonna do?" And McCartney and Lennon would play their new song on two acoustic guitars.

"George Harrison would look at it and go, okay," McCartney continues—because Harrison could immediately see and understand the chord structure, having absorbed music the same way they all had. "Ringo would tap out a rhythm kind of thing, and we trusted him to know what to do." Then, remarkably: "Twenty minutes later, we were recording that song."

This was the Beatles' magic: not formal training but total intuitive communion. "All of what we did was that," McCartney says. "It was really immediate transference of ideas." The music existed between them, shared through listening and watching and playing together. There was no notation, no arrangement charts, no formal process. Just musicians who had learned music the same way, thinking the same way, creating together in real time.

"If you look back on all our histories and our legacy, it was kind of Bartek," McCartney says, invoking the medieval European tradition of communal, unwritten musical creation. The parallel is apt: The Beatles operated less like modern recording artists and more like folk musicians, their songs emerging from collective improvisation and shared knowledge rather than written compositions.

Detailed Takeaways

The power of local culture in shaping creative output: McCartney's Liverpool upbringing illustrates how deeply place shapes artists. The city's Irish heritage, its port-city openness to the world, its wartime humor, its culture of self-deprecating wit—all these invisible forces became the raw material of The Beatles' identity. This challenges the myth of the self-made genius. McCartney is clear that his Liverpool formation made him possible; another city might have produced another McCartney, but the specific Liverpool combination was essential.

Scarcity as a teacher: The pre-internet difficulty of accessing music forced McCartney's generation to be active learners rather than passive consumers. The effort required to find records, learn chords, and build musical knowledge created deeper engagement than simply streaming a song. The informal apprenticeship system—someone teaches you a chord, you teach them something else—created tight communities of practice and mutual accountability that modern passive consumption cannot replicate.

The importance of historical timing: The Beatles emerged not just through talent but through favorable circumstance. They missed national service by approximately one year. They came of age in a Britain rebuilding itself after devastation, hungry for joy and new cultural forms. The physical and psychological bomb sites of their childhoods created both the need and the space for creative escape. Understanding these historical currents helps explain why Britain, rather than America (the source of the music that inspired them), produced The Beatles.

Community over individual genius: McCartney consistently credits the collective—the Liverpool community, his bandmates, his parents, the sailors who brought records. His creative process was intensely collaborative, built on trust and shared knowledge. The songwriting happened between people, not within one mind. This suggests that even the most seemingly individual creative achievements rest on networks of support, teaching, and shared culture.

The emotional power of specific memory: McCartney's most moving moments come when he recalls specific images—his mother on a bicycle in the snow, the indoor toilet on Milton Road, the liners departing for Canada. These granular memories, preserved across eight decades, carry the emotional weight of his art. His new album draws on this kind of memory, suggesting that artistic creation often works by recovering and transforming the specific images that lodged in the mind during childhood.

Who This Is For

This conversation will appeal most directly to fans of The Beatles and McCartney's solo work who want to understand the personal and cultural roots of his music. The interview provides rich biographical detail about McCartney's Liverpool upbringing—his parents' occupations, the family's housing progression, the wartime atmosphere of his childhood—that illuminates the emotional texture of his songwriting.

History enthusiasts will appreciate the window into mid-twentieth-century British life, particularly the experience of growing up in a port city shaped by Irish immigration, wartime bombing, and cultural isolation from American music. McCartney's reflections on how difficult it was to access rock and roll in pre-internet Britain offer a valuable perspective on cultural transmission in the analog era.

For writers and musicians, the interview offers a model of how to talk about creative process. McCartney is articulate about the informal transmission of musical knowledge and the collaborative nature of Beatles songwriting, providing insights into how communal creative practice can produce individually brilliant work.

Finally, anyone interested in how place shapes identity will find this conversation valuable. McCartney's sustained reflection on what Liverpool contributed to who he became—its humor, its separateness from England, its openness to the world—provides a thoughtful example of how artists can understand and articulate their own formation.
