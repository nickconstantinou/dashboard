Samsung Galaxy S26 Ultra Review: There's a Catch

A Comprehensive Analysis of Samsung's Latest Flagship

The smartphone world has long operated on a simple promise: each new generation brings meaningful improvements that justify upgrading. But as Samsung's latest flagship arrives in consumers' hands, one tech reviewer is urging buyers to look beyond the marketing fanfare and examine the trade-offs that define this $1,300 device. After spending a week with the Samsung Galaxy S26 Ultra, the review unpacks what actually changed, what was sacrificed to achieve those changes, and whether the phone's signature new feature justifies the compromises. The timing matters because this review arrives as consumers weigh whether to commit to Samsung's vision of an "Ultra" device in an increasingly competitive market where alternatives like the OnePlus 15 offer compelling specifications at potentially lower price points.

What makes this particular review noteworthy is not merely the assessment of a single device, but the philosophical framework the reviewer applies to evaluating any smartphone purchase. The central argument—that every feature represents a trade-off, and understanding those trade-offs matters more than chasing specification sheets—provides a lens through which readers can evaluate not just this phone, but any technology purchase. This perspective becomes particularly valuable when examining the S26 Ultra's headline feature: the Privacy Display.

---

The Privacy Display: Innovation Meets Compromise

How It Works

The Privacy Display represents Samsung's attempt to introduce meaningful hardware innovation in a market where incremental improvements have become the norm. The feature allows users to toggle a privacy filter that dramatically limits viewing angles, preventing anyone from looking over your shoulder at your screen. Unlike third-party screen protectors that achieve similar effects through polarization, Samsung's implementation is built directly into the display itself.

The technical implementation is genuinely innovative. Samsung's display contains two types of pixels: wide-angle pixels viewable from virtually any position, and narrow-angle pixels equipped with focusing lenses that restrict visibility to straight-on viewing. When you activate Privacy Display, the phone essentially turns off the wide-angle pixels while keeping the narrow-angle pixels active, creating that privacy filter effect without requiring any external accessories.

The reviewer notes this is "a genuinely useful innovation" and "really special because it's actually a new hardware feature, which we really don't get that often in the smartphone world these days." The capability to apply the filter selectively—to specific apps like banking or messaging applications, or even just to incoming notifications—represents meaningful flexibility that external solutions cannot match.

The Trade-Offs

However, this innovation comes with significant costs that Samsung's marketing materials do not prominently feature. When Privacy Display activates, it essentially turns off half the pixels on your screen. The reviewer purchased a microscope specifically to demonstrate this effect, showing how the pixel structure visibly changes when the feature is enabled. The practical result is that your resolution is quite literally cut in half, creating noticeably blockier edges around text and reducing detail in smaller visual elements.

Beyond resolution, peak brightness decreases when Privacy Display is active. Samsung has cleverly calibrated the remaining active pixels to maintain perceived brightness in most conditions, but at maximum brightness levels, users will notice a dimming effect when toggling the feature on.

Perhaps most significantly, the presence of these narrow-angle pixels affects the display's behavior even when Privacy Display is turned off. Because half the pixels now have permanently restricted viewing angles due to their focusing lenses, the overall viewing angle performance of the display is slightly worse than its predecessor at all times. The reviewer explicitly states this means "this display is slightly worse all the time" compared to the S25 Ultra.

Additional display compromises include a new coating that the reviewer describes as "not quite as good at the anti-reflective thing that was so good on S25 Ultra," and the continued use of an 8-bit display simulating 10-bit color rather than a native 10-bit panel—something competitors have increasingly adopted.

The Philosophy Behind the Decision

The reviewer acknowledges that Samsung likely understands their target audience well. The phone ships at 1080p resolution by default rather than its native 1440p because, as Samsung presumably determined, most users would prefer slightly better battery life over a perfect screen. "Nerds like me will lock in and immediately switch to 1440p in the settings right out of the box," the reviewer admits, "and we will notice more." This admission illustrates the divide between specification-focused enthusiasts and the broader market Samsung is actually serving.

The Privacy Display represents a calculated bet: Samsung believes this unique feature will sell phones, even if it means the display technically performs worse in several dimensions. The reviewer's assessment is measured: "this is still a really good display" but "if you're a pixel peeper like me, just know what you're getting into because there are better-looking displays that you can get for your $1,300 bucks, ironically probably also manufactured by Samsung."

Design: Evolution or Regression?

The Physical Changes

Samsung has made the S26 Ultra slightly thinner, slightly lighter, and given it rounder corners to align aesthetically with the company's other flagship offerings. On the surface, these seem like welcome refinements. However, the reviewer documents several consequences of these design decisions that prospective buyers should consider.

The most practical issue involves the S Pen. Because the corners are now more rounded, the entry point for the stylus has a slight curve rather than a flat edge. This means the S Pen can only be inserted in one orientation, whereas previous models allowed more forgiving insertion. Users who frequently remove and reinsert the stylus will need to pay attention to alignment.

The camera system presents another usability concern. The camera bump has evolved into what the reviewer describes as a "camera plateau with rings on top of it," which is larger than previous iterations. Combined with the thinner body, this creates a phone that "rocks viciously on a table" when laid flat without a case. The reviewer notes this is particularly noticeable when attempting to type on the device without a protective case, though they acknowledge most users will likely install a case that mitigates this issue.

Material Decisions

The switch from titanium sides back to aluminum generates mixed reactions. Objectively, the reviewer states this "makes no real difference to using the phone" in terms of functionality. However, they observe that it provides ammunition for critics who might "make fun of you for copying the iPhone's titanium for no reason"—a reference to Samsung's earlier adoption of titanium that now appears to have been abandoned.

These design choices collectively suggest Samsung prioritized aesthetics and the signature Privacy Display feature over some practical usability considerations. The reviewer's description of the phone as an "S26 Plus Plus Pro Max" rather than a true successor to the Ultra lineage captures this sense of incrementalism masked as advancement.

Performance: The Expected Improvements

Processing Power

The S26 Ultra arrives with Qualcomm's Snapdragon 8 Elite Gen 5 for Galaxy, a slightly customized version of the standard chip. Benchmark testing against the OnePlus 15, which uses the regular Snapdragon 8 Elite Gen 5, revealed similar scores with the Samsung version slightly ahead. This aligns with expectations of approximately 20-30% CPU improvement over the previous generation—standard annual gains that the reviewer describes as "almost a formality at this point."

The more interesting development is Samsung's apparent focus on the NPU (Neural Processing Unit) rather than pure CPU performance. This emphasis supports the extensive AI features bundled with the device, though the reviewer defers detailed discussion of these capabilities to a later section.

Thermal and Battery Performance

Despite maintaining the same 5,000 milliamp-hour battery capacity as its predecessor, the S26 Ultra achieves slightly better battery life through chip and software efficiency improvements. The redesigned vapor chamber with reduced internal volume performs adequately under load, and the device charges slightly faster at peak rates of 60 watts.

However, the reviewer notes that standby time remains "not quite as magical as some of its competitors"—a limitation that becomes more pronounced when comparing against phones featuring silicon carbon battery technology. This sets up the reviewer's broader criticism of Samsung's conservative approach to battery technology.

---

Missing Features: The Frustrating Omissions

Qi2 Magnets

Perhaps the most puzzling omission, according to the reviewer, is the absence of Qi2 magnetic charging support. This seems particularly strange given that magnetic accessories have become increasingly popular in the smartphone ecosystem, with Apple's MagSafe ecosystem demonstrating consumer appetite for this functionality.

The reviewer directly addresses Samsung: "I know you want to sell lots of cases with magnets in them, Samsung, but wouldn't you also want to maybe win an MVP Phone of the Year award, too?" The implication is clear—while Samsung presumably hopes to profit from selling magnetic cases, refusing to build magnets directly into the phone puts them at a competitive disadvantage for enthusiasts who expect this feature in a $1,300 flagship.

Silicon Carbon Battery

The absence of silicon carbon battery technology represents another conservative choice that the reviewer finds frustrating. Phones using this technology have demonstrated superior battery performance in testing, including comparisons conducted by other tech reviewers. The reviewer references Mrwhosetheboss's battery test results, which showed the S26 Ultra achieving slightly better battery life than its predecessor while still getting "dunked on by some silicon carbon battery phones from last year."

This becomes particularly notable because Samsung has experienced battery reliability concerns in the past, making the decision to avoid newer battery technology seem even more risk-averse. The reviewer suggests that a phone with genuinely larger battery capacity—even at the cost of being slightly thicker—would have been "pretty universally appreciated."

S Pen Limitations

The S Pen still lacks Bluetooth connectivity, meaning it cannot serve as a remote shutter button or perform other wireless functions that would leverage its position as an integrated accessory. While this may not matter to users who simply want a stylus for drawing and note-taking, it represents another area where Samsung's Ultra device falls short of its potential.

Cameras: Incremental Improvements with Software Enhancements

Hardware Changes

The camera system remains largely similar to the S25 Ultra, though there are meaningful refinements. Both the main camera and the 5X telephoto lens now feature wider apertures, allowing more light to reach the sensor. The reviewer, who identifies as a photographer, expresses clear preference for faster lenses: "I feel like I'm always going to take the faster lens when I can get it."

However, this improvement comes with a trade-off: the minimum focus distance on the main camera has increased, meaning users cannot get as close to subjects for macro photography. While the phone still includes a dedicated macro mode, this represents a noticeable downgrade from the previous generation's capability.

Software Additions

The more significant camera improvements come through software rather than hardware. The addition of APV log codec provides greater dynamic range for video professionals who want more flexibility in post-processing. Meanwhile, the new Horizon Lock stabilization feature represents what the reviewer considers "one of the best executions we've seen of it."

This stabilization takes advantage of the massive 200-megapixel sensor by cropping in significantly while maintaining excellent stability, enabling Quad HD video at 60 frames per second that the reviewer describes as "pretty sick."

For users interested in seeing extensive video footage from the S26 Ultra, the reviewer mentions that all talking head footage from their latest AutoFocus episode featuring the Polestar 4 was captured on this device, providing a real-world demonstration of its video capabilities.

---

Software and AI: Quantity Over Quality

The Galaxy AI Suite

Samsung has loaded the S26 Ultra with numerous AI features, accessible through a dedicated Galaxy AI tab in the settings. These include call screening for filtering unknown numbers, Audio Eraser for removing background noise from videos, and Photo Assist for generating entirely new scenes in photographs—capabilities that have generated both interest and controversy.

The reviewer's assessment of these features is notably lukewarm: "I think, honestly, take it or leave it. You can choose to use as much of it as you want. You can go full slop mode and use all of it all the time or you can kind of just never use any of it."

This reflects a broader skepticism about whether AI features should drive purchasing decisions. The reviewer specifically notes that many of these capabilities could and should be delivered to older Samsung devices through software updates, diminishing the argument that they represent a reason to purchase the new flagship.

Feature Reliability

Some specific AI features drew criticism. The reviewer states they "didn't get Now Nudge to work once," and experiments with the AI text-based photo editor revealed limitations—the system "refused many of the things I tried to make it do." While this might represent positive safeguards against misuse, it also suggests inconsistency in feature performance.

---

The Base Models: S26 and S26 Plus

Diminishing Value

The review takes a sharp turn toward criticism when addressing Samsung's non-Ultra models. The reviewer states plainly that "the base S26 and S26 Plus are not good deals anymore. Those phones feel like afterthoughts."

These devices feature essentially unchanged designs from previous generations and have maintained essentially the same camera hardware since the S23. Additional compromises include the removal of millimeter wave connectivity, the use of Exynos processors in many regions outside the United States, and the elimination of the 128 gigabyte storage option while maintaining the same price for the 256 gigabyte base model.

This results in a price of entry that has risen to $900—significantly more than what competing devices offer at similar price points. The reviewer's assessment is blunt: "You can get a lot more phone for $900... but Samsung doesn't really seem to care."

This criticism extends the review's broader theme: Samsung appears to be concentrating meaningful innovation and premium features exclusively in the Ultra tier, leaving the base models as increasingly weak value propositions.

---

Key Insights: What This Review Reveals

The Trade-Off Philosophy

The review's central insight extends beyond the S26 Ultra itself. Every feature in every phone represents a trade-off, and consumers must decide which compromises they can accept. Samsung chose to deliver a unique Privacy Display feature that no competitor offers, accepting display quality compromises across resolution, brightness, and viewing angles to achieve this differentiation. Whether this trade-off makes sense depends entirely on individual priorities.

The Conservative Flagship

Samsung appears to be playing it extremely safe with their Ultra flagship. Despite the premium price point, they have omitted features that competitors offer at lower price points: Qi2 magnets, silicon carbon batteries, and native 10-bit displays. The reviewer explicitly states that "at this point, it's not surprising that they're still playing it safe here, but it's also still kind of frustrating because it feels like the ceiling for this phone could have been so much higher, so much more Ultra."

The Value Question

The $1,300 starting price makes the S26 Ultra one of the most expensive mainstream smartphones available. While the reviewer acknowledges it's "a really good phone with some clever new features," they suggest that the price itself may be the most "Ultra" characteristic of the device. Consumers must weigh whether the Privacy Display justifies the premium, or whether better value exists elsewhere in the market.

Software as the Differentiator

With hardware increasingly standardized across manufacturers, Samsung is betting heavily on software and AI features to justify premium pricing. The reviewer's skepticism about whether these features constitute meaningful reasons to purchase suggests this strategy may not resonate with all consumers—especially when many capabilities could theoretically reach older devices through updates.

---

Who Should Read This Review

This review provides the most value to several distinct audiences. Enthusiast consumers who scrutinize specifications and display quality will find the detailed analysis of the Privacy Display trade-offs essential for making informed purchasing decisions. The review explicitly notes that "pixel peepers" should understand exactly what compromises they're accepting with this display.

Current Samsung owners considering an upgrade will benefit from understanding exactly what has changed and what has remained the same, helping them assess whether the upgrade cost makes sense for their specific situation.

Photography and videography enthusiasts should pay particular attention to the camera analysis, especially the software improvements like Horizon Lock and APV log codec, which may represent meaningful workflow enhancements despite relatively minor hardware changes.

Finally, anyone evaluating smartphones as a category will appreciate the philosophical framework around trade-offs that the reviewer applies throughout—this lens can help evaluate any technology purchase, not just this specific device.

---

Conclusion

The Samsung Galaxy S26 Ultra arrives as a genuinely capable flagship smartphone that succeeds in delivering a unique feature no competitor can match. The Privacy Display represents real innovation in an industry often accused of incrementalism. However, achieving this differentiation required accepting compromises across display quality, design practicality, and feature completeness that some consumers may find difficult to justify at the $1,300 price point.
