<p># FPL Masterclass: How to Navigate the Business End of the Season with FP Harry

Fantasy Premier League managers face their most consequential decisions of the season right now, and one content creator's meticulous approach offers a masterclass in strategic chip management, transfer planning, and calculated risk-taking. FP Harry, a prominent FPL analyst and team manager, recently shared his comprehensive strategy for the critical run-in, revealing the thinking behind his wildcard play in gameweek 32 and his plans for maximizing points through the season's final stages. His approach demonstrates how understanding chip timing, fixture difficulty, and player availability can mean the difference between a mid-table finish and a genuine title push.

The core of Harry's strategy revolves around a carefully orchestrated chip deployment sequence: bench boost in gameweek 33, free hit in gameweek 34, and then husbanding transfers for gameweek 35 onwards when the majority of leagues are decided. This isn't accidental—it's the product of analyzing fixture swings, player form, and the mathematical reality that most other managers will be running on fumes by this point in the season. With five free transfers at his disposal heading into GW33, Harry finds himself in a luxurious position that allows him to be more aggressive than he might otherwise be, taking calculated risks that could yield significant point differentials over his rivals.

## The Wildcard Gamble That Nearly Backfired

The decision to play his wildcard in gameweek 32 rather than saving it represented a calculated pivot from conventional FPL wisdom. Most experienced managers would typically preserve their wildcard for as long as possible, using regular transfers to gradually improve their squad before unleashing the chip in the final stages. Harry inverted this logic deliberately, recognizing that with five free transfers available, he could execute a significant overhaul of his squad while still positioning himself optimally for the upcoming double gameweeks.

The reasoning was strategic rather than impulsive. By wildcarding in GW32, Harry could immediately target the double gameweek 33 fixtures while simultaneously setting up his bench boost. Then, rather than entering the final weeks with depleted free transfers, he would have a full complement of five transfers available when most other managers who wildcarded earlier would be scraping together hits to make minor improvements. "Wild card 32 in order to target 33 and 34 as best I can and then save my transfers as much as possible for gameweek 35 onwards in order to have the advantage later down the line as opposed to most wild card 32 teams not having very many transfers once we get to the final few weeks of the season," Harry explained.

The execution, however, did not go according to plan initially. On Friday evening, Jarrod Bowen—who would have been Harry's transfer-in—delivered a 14-pointer, while on Saturday, Thiago scored 13 and Virgil van Dijk managed a nine. By Saturday night, it appeared Harry had potentially lost 20 to 30 points compared to his non-wildcard team. The Manchester City versus Chelsea match on Monday night became pivotal, and fortunately for Harry, Jeremy Doku delivered a 14-pointer while Bryan Mbeumo and Burnmo either didn't play or failed to register a return. The final damage was just six points lost to his pre-wildcard team, resulting in a modest red arrow from 36,000 to 40,000 overall rank. "Losing out by six points to my pre-wildcard team is definitely not the end of the world," Harry noted, "so I'm pretty happy with it as long as the bench boost in gameweek 33 does well enough to set me up well for the rest of the season."

## The Gabriel Trade: A Short-Term Sacrifice for Leeds Exposure

Harry's first planned transfer involves selling Arsenal defender Gabriel to acquire a Leeds United defender, a move that exemplifies the kind of aggressive double-gameweek exploitation that separates top-tier FPL managers from the pack. Gabriel faces Manchester City away, a fixture that carries significant clean sheet risk, while Leeds faces both Bournemouth and Tottenham across their double gameweek—generally more favorable attacking prospects for the Yorkshire side's defense.

This transfer wouldn't make sense if Harry had limited transfers available, as he acknowledges. "If I only had one, two, or maybe even three transfers, because in gameweek 35 after the bench boost, after the double, after then the blank where I'll be playing my free hit, I will be looking to buy Gabriel straight back into my team," he explained. The move effectively costs two transfers and potentially an eight-point hit if hits are required later in the season, but with five transfers available and no pressing need to preserve them, Harry views this as an acceptable trade-off. "As long as Gabriel concedes away to Manchester City, I will be pretty happy with the decision that I've made there," he added.

The choice between Leeds defenders centers on a fundamental tension in FPL: safe, nailed options versus slightly more attacking but rotation-prone alternatives. Harry has identified Pascal Struijk and Vislar Bijol as his primary targets, with Goodmanson, Bogle, and James Justin as secondary considerations. The key calculation involves whether an attacking defender can accumulate at least two assists or equivalent returns to outweigh Struijk or Bijol's defensive clean sheet potential across two matches.

The rotation risk becomes particularly acute if Leeds secure favorable results. Harry explains: "If Leeds win at home to Wolves and then Spurs lose to Brighton, Leeds will be nine points clear of the relegation zone with an FA Cup semi-final on the Sunday against Chelsea, which means we might see a bit of rotation for Leeds away at Bournemouth." While he doesn't consider this likely, particularly for center backs, the possibility of rotation affects the reliability of any attacking returns from Leeds defenders.

Struijk emerges as the safer long-term option, particularly because Harry might want to deploy his chosen Leeds defender in gameweek 35 at home to Burnley. If Christian Rodon returns from injury by then, Bijol could lose his place in the starting lineup, whereas Struijk remains almost certainly nailed as long as Rodon remains sidelined. "Pascal Struijk is probably slightly safer," Harry concluded. There's also a small possibility that Struijk could take penalties—he performed well in the FA Cup shootout against West Ham—though Harry expects Kalvin Phillips to retain those duties, particularly given that Phillips doesn't play 90 minutes every week.

## The O'Reilly Dilemma: Patience Over Panic

Perhaps the most nuanced decision Harry faces involves whether to retain or sell Manchester City's Rúben Dias, who remains a doubt for the Arsenal match in GW33. Rather than making a hasty decision, Harry is adopting a patient approach that prioritizes long-term value over short-term point swings.

O'Reilly represents, in Harry's assessment, the second-best Manchester City asset to own and potentially a better pick than Erling Haaland himself. "I actually think O'Reilly is better than both [Kevin] De Bruyne and [Bernardo] Silva in their midfield," Harry noted, highlighting the defender's remarkable consistency and clean sheet potential in a City shirt. Given that City are chasing down the Premier League title and have a second double gameweek against Crystal Palace potentially in GW36, retaining O'Reilly seems strategically sound.

The uncertainty around his availability creates a complex decision matrix. If positive updates emerge or if O'Reilly is pictured in training, Harry will almost certainly retain him rather than selling to an inferior City defender only to buy him back later. The historical parallel Harry draws is instructive: "It feels like a couple of years ago... there was a big time where people didn't know whether they were going to go for Ederson for being nailed or they were going to go for Kyle Walker of Manchester City. The differential gains from having him when others don't, combined with his importance to City's title challenge, outweigh the short-term uncertainty. Harry expects to receive clarity from Pep Guardiola's press conference scheduled for Friday lunchtime, which will provide approximately 24 hours to assess the situation before Saturday's deadline.

The alternative City defenders under consideration—Matheus Nunes versus Joško Gvardiol—present their own trade-offs. Gvardiol's defensive numbers appear inflated by his time at Crystal Palace, and since joining City, his underlying attacking returns haven't matched expectations. ## Chip Strategy: The Mathematics of Depletion

Harry's approach to chip deployment reveals a sophisticated understanding of how the FPL calendar creates windows of opportunity that can be exploited or missed depending on when managers deploy their resources. The bench boost in GW33 represents the first domino in a carefully planned sequence, with the free hit in GW34 designed to navigate what promises to be a challenging blank gameweek.

The critical insight underlying this strategy is recognizing that most managers who wildcarded in earlier gameweeks will have exhausted their transfer flexibility by the business end of the season. By front-loading his wildcard to target the doubles in GW33 and GW34, Harry preserves maximum optionality for GW35 onwards, when the fixtures thin out but the stakes—literally, in terms of league positions—increase dramatically. "I need to deal with some of these things to also hold my team up better for the long term, but also really trying to maximize the double in 33 on my bench boost as best I can," Harry explained regarding his priorities heading into GW33.

The bench boost itself represents a significant point-scoring opportunity if executed correctly. Harry estimates that a successful bench boost could yield somewhere between 15 and 20 points over his non-boosted total, easily recovering the six points lost through wildcarding and potentially providing a significant advantage heading into the final stretch. The key is having sufficient depth in the right positions—particularly among City and Leeds assets with favorable double fixtures—to capitalize on this opportunity.

## Key Insights for FPL Managers

**Strategic chip sequencing matters more than individual chip timing.** Rather than simply asking "when should I play my wildcard?" or "when is the best time for bench boost?", successful managers should consider the entire calendar and how chip deployments interact with each other. Harry's approach—bench boost, free hit, then preserve transfers—creates a logical flow that addresses each challenge in sequence while maintaining maximum flexibility for the run-in.

**Five transfers is a resource to be spent, not hoarded.** Many FPL managers become overly conservative with their transfers, always preserving one or two for emergencies. Harry's willingness to "waste" two transfers on a Gabriel-out, Leeds-defender-in move demonstrates that in the right circumstances, using transfers aggressively can yield better returns than hoarding them. The key is recognizing when you have sufficient surplus resources to absorb the cost.

**Player availability uncertainty requires patience, not reaction.** The temptation when a player is listed as a doubt is to sell immediately and buy a guaranteed starter. Harry's analysis suggests this approach often costs more than it gains, particularly for premium assets in favorable fixture situations. Waiting for confirmation, even if it means taking a small hit, frequently proves cheaper than unnecessary selling and re-buying.

**Contextual factors like cup competitions affect rotation risk.** Leeds' potential FA Cup commitments, Manchester City's title chase, and other external factors can influence how managers deploy their players. Successful FPL managers track these schedules and adjust their expectations accordingly, understanding that a double gameweek doesn't always mean guaranteed minutes for all relevant players.

**Historical comparisons illuminate decision frameworks.** The Ederson versus Kyle Walker example Harry references demonstrates how comparing similar past situations can inform current decisions. When multiple options seem equally viable, examining how similar decisions played out previously can provide guidance that raw statistics cannot.
