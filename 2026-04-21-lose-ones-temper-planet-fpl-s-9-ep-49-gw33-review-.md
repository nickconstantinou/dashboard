# Lost One's Temper: Inside the Wild Card vs. Bench Boost Showdown of Gameweek 33

## The Biggest Double Gameweek of the Season Arrives

The world of Fantasy Premier League has reached its most critical juncture, and the hosts of Planet FPL find themselves in the thick of it. Serge and James—who have been guiding the FPL community through the complexities of the game for nine seasons now—meet for Gameweek 33's episode, a moment that represents the fulcrum upon which hundreds of thousands of seasons will pivot. This isn't just any gameweek; it's the largest double gameweek of the entire season, one that James describes as "914 boss"—a reference to the sometimes cryptic FPL community shorthand for the biggest chip-playing week of the campaign.

With roughly half of all fixtures still to be played as the episode records, the stakes couldn't be higher. Serge notes that most managers still have approximately 12 players remaining on their squads who have yet to feature this gameweek, meaning captaincy decisions on players like Erling Haaland—still to play against Bournemouth—remain very much up in the air. The episode captures both hosts in the midst of executing completely opposite chip strategies, creating a natural experiment in FPL decision-making that the Planet FPL audience can learn from.

The atmosphere crackles with the energy of live strategy execution. James has committed to a Wild Card, piling into eleven players with double gameweeks in what he describes as "a slightly different strategy to some people on free hit this week." Serge, meanwhile, has gone with Bench Boost—the chip that activates all fifteen squad members for maximum points potential. Their different approaches offer a masterclass in FPL philosophy, revealing how the same gameweek can be approached from entirely different angles depending on squad composition, upcoming fixtures, and long-term planning horizons.

## The Chip Landscape: How Millions of Managers Are Playing GW33

The official FPL account revealed that a staggering 2.7 million chips were played this gameweek, a number that both hosts find revealing. "Tells you how many dead teams there are, doesn't it?" James observes with a wry smile, noting that the sheer volume of chip activations suggests many managers have already abandoned their seasons but continue to tinker with their squads. Among the active managers still competing, Bench Boost emerged as the dominant choice with approximately 800,000 activations, though the hosts note there was "quite a spread across wild cards and free hits as well."

Serge makes an important observation about the engaged community versus the broader player base. "I think in terms of the engaged community, the majority are obviously on bench boost and it's gone pretty well for them so far," he explains. "There wasn't for the majority there wasn't massive loss." This distinction matters because it suggests that while Wild Card and Free Hit strategies certainly have their place, the Bench Boost approach has been the prudent choice for those still genuinely competing—not the reckless gamble that some might assume.

The Live FPL data from the top 10K provides the most concrete evidence of chip strategy effectiveness. Teams in the elite ranking band utilizing Bench Boost are averaging approximately 69 points at the halfway stage of the gameweek. Teams on Wild Card? A more modest 53 points—a 16-point differential that James points out "is a bench roughly," meaning the difference essentially comes down to which bench players are available to activate. Serge immediately qualifies this observation by noting that "we're only halfway through the game week, right? So the majority of those with the bench boosters still got more players to go than wild card." The picture could still change significantly before the gameweek concludes.

## James's Wild Card Strategy: Trusting the Process

James opens up about his Wild Card approach, revealing a squad built around what he calls "Treble City"—his three premium forwards being Pedro Neto, Morgan Gibbs-White, and Erling Haaland as captain. "I haven't included Semenyo versus Chaundry in that cuz in all honesty, I was never going to play Ray and Chaundry," he admits, clarifying that certain players simply don't fit his structure regardless of their form. His attacking core also includes Chrisb in the midfield alongside Gibbs-White, with Haaland anchoring the forward line.

His defensive structure revolves around Chelsea and Bournemouth assets. "Went double Chelsea, Palmer Xiao Pedro," he explains, referencing Cole Palmer and Noni Madueke as his Chelsea double-up. "Double Bournemouth, Hill versus Tavernier," he continues, noting his preference for Antonee Robinson over Kiernan Trippier at Bournemouth. The hosts then discuss James's other decisions, with James revealing he carries Bruno Fernandes despite the Manchester United midfielder being "kind of the biggest loser in my book at the moment," plus Harry Wilson as a value hold and Dean Henderson waiting for Crystal Palace's expected double gameweek in GW36.

The small decisions, James explains, have been the difference between a respectable score and a disaster. "O'Reilly versus Gay, Vanek versus Braggen, Calvitt Leuen versus Wbeck, Hill versus Sessi—all those little bits, they're only small little bits, but they've helped to accumulate," he notes. "That means I'm swimming okay this week as it stands." His philosophy demonstrates that in FPL, particularly during double gameweeks, the cumulative effect of marginal gains across multiple positions can be the difference between green and red arrows.

## The Harry Wilson Investment: Long-Term Thinking

James dedicates significant attention to his decision to carry Harry Wilson, revealing the multi-layered thinking behind what might seem like a simple player hold. "I carried Harry Wilson, couple of reasons for carrying him," he begins. "A big value in him that would that's impacted by the next transfer that I could end up making in gameweek 35 and also impacts what I might be able to squeeze out in gameweek 34 on a free hit as well because I bought him at 5.2 and have no problem keeping him and playing him for gameweek 34."

This represents sophisticated FPL thinking at its finest. James recognizes that Wilson's price point makes him valuable beyond just his on-pitch contributions. His continued presence in the squad affects his transfer options in GW35 and, perhaps more importantly, gives him flexibility on the GW34 free hit where he might need budget flexibility to execute a complete template transformation. "And also I really like the last three fixtures," James adds, noting that Wilson's favorable run-in means he won't need to transfer him out down the line either—a secondary benefit that preserves future transfer budget.

## Serge's Bench Boost Dilemma: The Saturday Morning Sleeplessness

For Serge, the decision to bench boost proved agonizing, and he shares the full internal debate that took place Saturday morning. His bench at the time consisted of Diogo Dalot, Bruno Fernandes, Jarrod Bowen, and Gabriel—players he had zero intention of selling. "I didn't want to sell Bruno Fernandez or Jared Bowen or Gabrielle," he admits. "Like all of them. Gabrielle definitely I think is going to go on a bit of a run of clean sheets between now and the end of the season. Bruno Fernandez we know. And Jared Bowen I think is going to do all right."

The fundamental problem Serge identifies is philosophical: if he's bench boosting with three single gameweek players, "then there's got to be a week where I've got three single gameweek players that have better fixtures than Chelsea away, Man City away, and Crystal Palace away." Those are exactly the fixtures his bench players would face—horrible matchups that would make bench boosting feel like a waste. "It just doesn't make sense that those are the three fixtures I'm bench boosting into."

Yet despite these reservations, Serge ultimately pulled the trigger. "I kind of just bit the bullet," he says. "I didn't know anything about Pedro Pedro. I sold Jared Bowen for Pedro Pedro." The decision cost him his Brentford asset but gained him a player with double gameweek potential. The gamble appeared to pay off—he was sitting at 67 points at the time of recording, "swimming" as he puts it, though he acknowledges that bench boosting inherently means accepting a score that will dip relative to the extra players wild card managers can deploy.

## The Rotation Warning: What Managers Should Watch

Both hosts identify rotation risks that could define the second half of this double gameweek. Serge expresses particular concern about Liverpool's assets. "Aaronson has only had only just come back the previous game," he explains, "so maybe he'll get rotated or maybe he'll just get trotted out again." The implication is clear: coming back from injury makes a player more likely to be rested, and in a double gameweek, losing a player to rotation is doubly painful.

Danny Welbeck presents another dilemma. "Rut's obviously come off the bench and scored at the weekend and could easily start ahead of him," Serge notes, suggesting that Brighton might bench their more expensive forward option in favor of the in-form Danny Welbeck. "I have no expectations," he concludes, maintaining realistic expectations about rotation-prone assets.

Manchester City, James suggests, represents a different philosophy entirely. "I think they play all their players," he observes. "I think they smell blood like to go top of the table if they"—the sentence trails off, but the message is clear. When a title race heats up, elite teams resist rotation, making their assets more reliable for FPL purposes. Dalot and Bol, James believes, will similarly play both games for Liverpool, while Rodon sits on the bench as cover.

The episode also serves as an education in the psychology of FPL decision-making. Serge's Saturday morning bench boost dilemma—the internal struggle between wanting to keep premium assets and recognizing the mathematical case for bench boosting—will ring true for any manager who's ever agonized over chip timing. With approximately nine gameweeks remaining and the biggest double gameweek of the season only half complete, managers who stay calm, trust their processes, and avoid panic decisions will find themselves best positioned when the season reaches its conclusion.
