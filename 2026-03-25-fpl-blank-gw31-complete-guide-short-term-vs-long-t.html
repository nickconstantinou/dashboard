FPL Blank Gameweek 31 Complete Guide: Chip Strategy, Captaincy Picks, and Transfer Advice for the Season Run-In

Introduction

Crystal Palace's European success earlier this week finally settled weeks of speculation about which teams would miss fixtures, and with the deadline now less than 24 hours away, FPL managers are scrambling to finalize their strategies for what promises to be one of the most complex periods of the season.

In this comprehensive guide, FP Harry breaks down everything managers need to know about navigating the blank, including the critical chip strategies available, which players to target for captaincy, and why patience—rather than panicked transfers—will likely be the winning approach. With Arsenal, Wolves, Manchester City, and Crystal Palace all blanking in gameweek 31, the flow of fixtures across the remaining weeks has become clearer, but significant uncertainty remains around how the postponed matches will be rescheduled and what that means for future doubles and blanks.

The timing of this video is particularly crucial because we're approaching a fork in the road for chip strategy. The FA Cup results—specifically whether Liverpool or Manchester City progresses—will be the deciding factor in which path managers should take. Understanding this and acting at the right time could mean the difference between a strong finish and a frustrating end to the season.

Understanding the Blank Gameweek 31 Confirmation

The delay in confirming gameweek 31's blank status came down to Crystal Palace's European commitments. When Crystal Palace progressed in European competition, it finally locked in what many had suspected: Arsenal, Wolves, Manchester City, and Crystal Palace would all blank in gameweek 31. This confirmation means that the Manchester City versus Crystal Palace fixture cannot be played in either the midweek of gameweek 31 or gameweek 32, meaning it won't be rescheduled until at least the midweek of gameweek 33 at the earliest.

This development is significant because it eliminates one potential variable from the equation, but it also raises important questions about how the postponed fixtures will be distributed across the remaining weeks. FP Harry emphasizes that gameweek 32 will be a normal gameweek with all teams in action, while gameweek 33 will likely be the biggest double gameweek of the season—a prospect that has many managers planning their chip deployments around that blank.

The key takeaway here is that we now know with certainty which teams are blanking this week, but the ripple effects on future weeks remain somewhat unclear. This is precisely why FP Harry advises against making any dramatic transfers until the FA Cup results play out and the schedule for those postponed matches becomes clearer.

Chip Strategy: The Two Main Paths Forward

With the blank confirmed, managers now face a critical decision about how to deploy their chips over the coming weeks. FP Harry outlines two primary strategies that are likely to dominate the landscape, with the FA Cup semi-final between Liverpool and Manchester City serving as the key pivot point.

Option One: Wildcard 32, Bench Boost 33, Free Hit 34

This path becomes more likely if Liverpool beats Manchester City in the FA Cup. Under this strategy, managers would use their wildcard in gameweek 32—which will be a normal gameweek with full fixtures—to restructure their squad ahead of the anticipated double in gameweek 33. The bench boost would then be deployed in gameweek 33 to maximize returns from that double, while the free hit would cover gameweek 34, which would be blank under this scenario.

Option Two: Free Hit 33, Wildcard 35, Bench Boost 36

This alternative becomes more attractive if Manchester City beats Liverpool in the FA Cup. Here, managers would use their free hit in gameweek 33 to capitalize on whichever teams have doubles that week, then deploy the wildcard in gameweek 35 to reset the squad for the final run-in, with bench boost in gameweek 36.

FP Harry is careful to note that this isn't an exact science—even if Manchester City wins, some managers might still prefer Option One, and vice versa. The general framework provides guidance, but individual team circumstances and existing player selections will influence the final decision. This is why maintaining flexibility in your squad is so critical at this stage of the season.

Why Early Transfers Are a Trap

One of the most important messages in this video is FP Harry's strong recommendation against making early transfers into gameweek 32 before the FA Cup results are known. He pleads with viewers to "please please please do not make any early wildcard 32 transfers until after the FA Cup."

The reasoning is straightforward: with the international break followed by the FA Cup, we won't get clarity on the double and blank schedule until the midweek between the FA Cup and gameweek 32. Making transfers now based on current predictions could lock you into a strategy that becomes obsolete once those results come in. Yes, you might gain 0.2 to 0.5 in value by moving early, but that small gain isn't worth the risk of committing to the wrong chip strategy.

The comparison site FPL.team and Planet FPPL provides predicted doubles based on current information, and while their combination work is "fantastic" according to FP Harry, we're still in guessing territory. Teams like Arsenal, Chelsea (with fixtures against Newcastle and Brighton), and Manchester City plus Crystal Palace are predicted to double in gameweek 33—but this won't be confirmed until before the gameweek 32 deadline.

The message is clear: maintain as much flexibility as possible. Don't pigeonhole yourself into one specific scenario. Use transfers sparingly this week unless you absolutely have to, and focus on players who have long-term value regardless of which chip strategy you eventually choose.

The Newcastle Player Dilemma

A specific example illustrates the complexity of current decision-making. Many managers have been looking at Newcastle players this week, and while FP Harry acknowledges they're good picks, he explains why they might not fit every strategy.

Newcastle has favorable fixtures—Sunderland at home in gameweek 31, Crystal Palace away in gameweek 32—but here's the catch: under Option Two (Free Hit 33, Wildcard 35, Bench Boost 36), Newcastle players become much less valuable. If you're free hitting in gameweek 33, Newcastle's double that week doesn't matter because you're using the chip to bring in different players. Then in gameweek 34, Newcastle blank, and gameweek 35 is when you'd use your wildcard.

FP Harry admits he's not forcing himself to buy Newcastle players this week "because actually they don't really fit that well unless you go with strategy one." This highlights the broader principle: try to buy players who are good for the long term no matter what happens with your chip strategy, rather than chasing short-term gains that might not align with your eventual approach.

Captaincy Analysis for Gameweek 31

Moving to captaincy, FP Harry identifies Bruno Fernandes as his number one pick for gameweek 31. Approximately 75% of managers are considering going for the Manchester United star on the Friday night, and FP Harry explains why this makes sense: he's the highest chance of a goal or assist, he's most in form, and he's the joint highest projected point scorer on the Fant Scout members area.

However, there's a wrinkle: Bruno's away form is significantly worse than his home form. Bournemouth have been one of the best home defenses this season, which provides some justification for looking elsewhere. His assist threat is definitely better at home, making the away fixture a less attractive proposition despite his overall form.

For managers looking to go differential, Harry Wilson emerges as the best alternative. Burnley have conceded a lot of chances to right wingers this season, and this data point supports Wilson as a strong captaincy option outside of the obvious choice. Anthony Gordon is another consideration—he's on penalties, playing at home to Sunderland, and while Newcastle lost in the Champions League and the derby last time out, they'll be motivated to get a result. Cole Palmer remains an option away at Everton despite Chelsea's poor recent performances, and while FP Harry notes he expects Ekitike to start for Liverpool, there's a Saturday morning update on Salah that could impact that situation—though FP Harry still considers Ekitike "nailed" and a one to consider.

The broader context for captaincy this week is that every team is predicted to score less than two goals. Fulham are at the top at home to Burnley with 1.83 expected goals, Manchester United are just below with 1.72, and Newcastle are around 1.76. Even the big hitters like Chelsea and Liverpool are in the same range. This suggests we're in for a relatively low-scoring gameweek where the safest choices—rather than the most explosive ones—might be the way to go.

Key Takeaways for FPL Managers

First, patience is paramount. Don't make transfers based on predictions until the FA Cup results are known. The small value gains from early transfers aren't worth the risk of locking yourself into the wrong strategy. Wait for the doubles and blanks to be confirmed before making major moves.

Second, maintain maximum flexibility. Keep your options open by avoiding commits to specific chip strategies until you know which path makes more sense. Players who have value regardless of which strategy you choose are better purchases than those who only work with one specific approach.

Third, understand how Newcastle players fit into the puzzle. They're good options but only if you're on Strategy One. If you're leaning toward Strategy Two, their value diminishes significantly because their double week becomes less relevant to your plans.

Fourth, for captaincy, Bruno Fernandes remains the safe choice despite the away fixture concerns. The form and chances justify the popular pick, while Harry Wilson emerges as the best differential for those looking to differentiate their team.

Fifth, gameweek 31 is relatively simple in terms of benching decisions. With most managers struggling to field a full XI, the complexity is in transfers and chip planning rather than lineup selection.
