Pras' Gameweek 31 Team Selection | FPL Tips for Blank Gameweeks

Introduction: The First Blank Gameweek Arrives

The Fantasy Premier League season has reached its most treacherous stretch. In a new video from Pras—known to his audience on the FPL Wire—viewers are welcomed to gameweek 31, the first blank gameweek of the season. This is the moment Pras describes as when "things start to take shape" and when players across the FPL community begin making moves that may conflict with their long-term planning. The psychological pressure of blank gameweeks creates a uniquely challenging environment where emotional decision-making can undermine carefully constructed strategies.

Pras opens by reflecting on a successful previous gameweek, one that saw him deliver a strong gameweek rank thanks largely to captaining Bruno Fernandes, who delivered despite Pras admitting he had considered switching to Mohamed Salah. Beyond Bruno, players like Thiago and Dango contributed positively, while the double Arsenal clean sheet—exclusive to Ryan and Gabriel defense owners due to Timber going off injured—provided unexpected value. Despite these gains, Pras finds himself hovering around the 500,000 rank, having experienced the volatility typical of FPL at this stage: a jump from 2 million to 500k, then back to 700k, now returning to 500k. He remains optimistic, noting he's approximately 50 points from the top 100k, a gap he believes remains "doable" though requiring some luck.

The video was recorded following a significant evening for English clubs in European competition. Crystal Palace's progression in their competition carries particular weight for FPL planning, while Nottingham Forest advanced via penalties and Aston Villa also secured advancement. More teams progressing in European competition, Pras explains with evident satisfaction, means more fixture congestion and more blank gameweek carnage ahead—developments he views as favorable for those who have planned accordingly.

The Crystal Palace Fixture Matrix: What the Results Mean

Perhaps the most technically detailed portion of the video involves Pras walking viewers through his updated fixture chart following Crystal Palace's European advancement. Using a visualization he first presented the previous week, Pras explains how he has now "boxed out" the midweeks in which Palace are in European action, revealing that the fixture against Manchester City cannot fit into gameweek 31—making it a guaranteed blank for any Palace assets.

This has direct implications for one of FPL's most popular assets: Eberechi Eze. Anyone who had sold Eze with the hope of reinvesting him in gameweek 31 can now rest easy knowing that move won't backfire through a fortunate fixture reschedule. More significantly for Pras's own planning, the Crystal Palace result means the Manchester City fixture, which he had tentatively expected might shift to gameweek 32, also cannot be accommodated there. Palace now have the second leg of their next European round scheduled during that window, eliminating any possibility of a double gameweek in gameweek 32.

The confirmed scenario, then, is that gameweek 33 will serve as the double gameweek for Palace that the community has been anticipating, with gameweek 36 likely providing another Palace double. This aligns with the "base case" that Pras and others have been projecting from sources like FPL.team and discussed extensively across various FPL podcasts. The evening's results have actually strengthened confidence in this outcome, as the reduced likelihood of a double in gameweek 32 makes the gameweek 33 double more probable by elimination.

Pras acknowledges that "carnage can still happen" in this scenario, as Manchester City must still beat Liverpool in the Champions League, Chelsea must win their FA Cup tie, Arsenal must advance in their competition, and television broadcasters must schedule fixtures in the expected manner. Multiple variables remain in flux, but the trajectory has become clearer.

The Three Chip Strategies: A Framework for the Blank Gameweek Run

Rather than prescribing a single "correct" approach, Pras outlines the three primary chip strategies that FPL managers are likely pursuing during this critical period. Each strategy has distinct advantages and suits different squad states, and Pras offers his perspective on the viability of each while acknowledging that reasonable managers can disagree.

Strategy One: Wildcard in Gameweek 32, Bench Boost in 33, Free Hit in 34

This represents the most popular approach, according to Pras. The logic is straightforward: use the wildcard during gameweek 32 (with a Friday deadline, meaning managers have very limited time to act), immediately pivot to bench boost in gameweek 33 to capitalize on whatever double gameweek materializes, and then deploy the free hit in gameweek 34 for maximum flexibility. Pras has discussed this extensively in previous content and believes many managers have already committed to this path.

Strategy Two: Free Hit in 33, Bench Boost in 36, Wildcard in 35

Pras reveals this is the strategy he is personally leaning toward, provided the fixtures ultimately align with current projections. His reasoning is revealing: "I just like it because my team looks nice in 32 and 34," he explains. This approach sacrifices the gameweek 32 wildcard in favor of waiting for greater certainty about which fixtures will be doubled and which will be moved. By delaying the wildcard until gameweek 35, managers gain the advantage of the international break—which will have passed by then—knowing "100% which fixture is going into 33 and which is staying in 34." The bench boost shift to gameweek 36 accommodates the likely Palace double in that week.

Strategy Three: Wildcard in 33, Hit in 34, Modified Bench Boost Timeline

A more niche approach discussed by FPL creator LR in his main podcast, this strategy involves wildcarding in gameweek 33 and accepting a hit in gameweek 34. Some managers pursuing this route may have already used their bench boost earlier, having evaluated their gameweek 32 squad and determined that a wildcard in 33 makes more sense than holding steady. They might then deploy bench boost in gameweek 35 or 36, depending on their squad composition and the fixtures available.

Pras offers a notably pragmatic argument for Strategy Two that crystallizes the broader strategic tension: "The fact that there are three strategies and only one of them has a wildcard in gameweek 32 makes me think—why don't you just wait? Within this international break, we will know 100% which fixture is going into 33 and which is staying in 34. So might as well wait." This argument hinges on the value of information: with relatively little price movement expected before the FA Cup quarterfinals are resolved, managers sacrifice little by waiting and gain significant certainty about their planning inputs.

Fixture Analysis: The Solio Tool and Strategic Team Targeting

Pras then pivots to detailed fixture analysis using a visualization from Solio (an FPL analytics tool), which projects expected goals for teams across the upcoming gameweeks. The chart is presented in columns representing different strategic approaches, allowing managers to quickly identify which teams to target based on their chosen chip deployment timeline.

For managers planning a Free Hit in Gameweek 33, the analysis is clear: Liverpool represents the highest-value team to target. Pras emphasizes Liverpool's favorable run of home fixtures—Fulham in gameweek 32 and Crystal Palace in gameweek 34 flank the blank gameweek 33, making them ideal for a one-week hit. Mohamed Salah remains the obvious premium entry point, though Pras flags that managers should monitor whether Salah is injured ahead of the upcoming fixtures; his status should become clearer following Liverpool manager Arne Slot's press conference.

Manchester United projects as the second-highest attacking option for both gameweek 32 and gameweek 34, meaning their assets—particularly Bruno Fernandes—fit well within this strategy. Fulham also shows up strongly, which Pras connects to the popularity of Alex Iwobi this week, along with the emerging appeal of Andreas Pereira or Raul Jimenez depending on whether managers prefer midfielder or forward coverage.

For managers planning a Free Hit in Gameweek 34, the right-hand column of the Solio chart reveals Chelsea as the primary target. Players like Nicolas Jackson, Pedro Neto, and especially Cole Palmer emerge as excellent options for this horizon. Newcastle also features prominently, making them valuable for managers taking a longer-term view.

Pras uses this analysis to resolve a common current debate: should managers invest in a Newcastle player this week or a Fulham player? His framework provides a clear answer based on strategy: if a manager plans to wildcard in gameweek 32 and doesn't intend to free hit in gameweek 34, they should take the Newcastle option and potentially bench the player if fixtures don't materialize as expected. Conversely, if a manager plans to free hit in gameweek 34, Fulham becomes the better immediate investment since they'll play in that gameweek regardless.

Player Recommendations and Strategic Nuances

Beyond team-level targeting, Pras offers several specific player observations that reflect deeper strategic thinking.

Adama Traore (Fulham) emerges as a compelling option because his underlying statistics suggest he's underperforming his xG and xGI, creating potential for positive regression. For managers planning a Free Hit in 33, Traore's combination of good fixtures and statistical upside makes him an attractive mid-price option.

For Manchester United coverage, Pras mentions players like Kobbie Mainoo and Alejandro Garnacho as viable options, suggesting he may be looking to add one of these players in his own team soon. The reasoning ties back to the fixture projections showing United as a high-value attacking team across the relevant gameweeks.

In defense, the recommendations split by strategy. For Free Hit 33, Liverpool, Manchester United, West Ham, Tottenham, and Nottingham Forest all project well. For Free Hit 34, Arsenal (despite their blank in gameweek 31), Newcastle, Brighton, and Aston Villa are the targets. Pras notes that while Arsenal blank this gameweek, their eventual double gameweek status makes them essential for the longer-term plan.

The nuance Pras provides throughout is valuable precisely because it resists one-size-fits-all thinking. He repeatedly emphasizes that strategy depends on individual squad composition, which chips have already been used, and what a manager's rank situation requires. A player chasing a million positions has different optimal plays than someone in the top 10,000 who needs to protect a strong position.

Key Takeaways

Takeaway 1: Blank Gameweek Planning Requires Strategy Commitment, Not Constant Reaction

The blank gameweeks are not an isolated event—they're a multi-week strategic arc that rewards managers who commit to a coherent plan. Pras's recommendation to wait on the gameweek 32 wildcard until after the FA Cup quarterfinals reflects a core principle: resist the urge to react to each new data point and instead wait for the information that will make your decisions lower-risk. The international break provides exactly this window, and managers who patient wait will make better-informed wildcard decisions than those who panic-press on Friday deadline.

Takeaway 2: Crystal Palace's European Run Locks in a Core Fixture Assumption

With Crystal Palace now confirmed to blank in gameweek 31 and unable to provide a double in gameweek 32, managers can plan with greater confidence around the expected gameweek 33 double. Eze owners can stop worrying about missing a fixture window, while managers considering Palace assets should target gameweek 33 and gameweek 36 as their deployment windows.

Takeaway 3: The "Wait and See" Chip Strategy Has Mathematical Merit

Pras's preference for Strategy Two (wildcard in 35 rather than 32) reflects an underappreciated truth: the information value of waiting exceeds the opportunity cost of delayed action. With minimal price changes expected before FA Cup results are known, waiting provides certainty about fixture outcomes while sacrificing little in terms of market movement.

Takeaway 4: Team Targeting Should Flow Directly From Your Chip Plan

The Solio fixture analysis demonstrates a crucial principle: don't ask "who are the best players this week" in isolation. Instead, ask "who are the best players for my specific chip timeline." Liverpool assets make sense for Free Hit 33 managers; Chelsea assets make sense for Free Hit 34 managers. This framework eliminates decision paralysis and ensures each transfer serves the broader strategy.

Takeaway 5: Mid-Price Options Like Fulham Players Offer Strategic Flexibility

With many managers locked into premium options like Salah, Palmer, and Bruno, Fulham assets represent an underexplored pathway to strong returns. Wilson, Iwobi, and Traore all have favorable fixtures and price points that allow managers to field premium attackers without sacrificing budget elsewhere.

Who This Content Is For

This video will prove most valuable for FPL managers currently ranked between approximately 100,000 and 2 million who have not yet committed to a specific chip strategy for the blank gameweeks ahead. Managers in this band face the most volatile decision space: they're close enough to meaningful ranks to care about optimization but far enough from the top to have flexibility in their approach.

For managers in the top 100k, the content provides validation for strategies already committed to while offering tactical nuances about specific player selection. For those below 2 million, the framework helps prioritize information over panic, though they may need more aggressive chip deployment to close gaps.

What makes Pras's approach particularly valuable is his refusal to pretend there's a single correct answer. By laying out three viable strategies with their respective trade-offs, he empowers managers to evaluate their own squad states and choose accordingly. The specific player recommendations then flow from that strategic foundation, making the video not just a list of transfers but a coherent philosophy for navigating one of FPL's most challenging periods.
