TikTok Tech Tips are TERRIBLE

In an era where TikTok has become the primary information source for hundreds of millions of Americans—often supplanting traditional news outlets—a popular tech YouTuber has taken it upon himself to put viral platform "tech tips" on trial. Welcome to Tech Court, where these supposed hacks are examined, tested, and ultimately judged: either free to go or straight to jail (of public opinion, at least).

The premise is simple yet compelling: scroll through TikTok's tech community, find the most viral "hacks" and "tips," then rigorously test them to determine whether they're legitimate innovations or dangerous misinformation dressed up in slick video edits. The creator approaches each case with a mix of skepticism and genuine curiosity, purchasing equipment when necessary and personally executing every step to verify claims. What emerges is a troubling portrait of a platform where engagement often trumps accuracy, where mysterious commands are typed without explanation, and where confident presentations mask fundamental misunderstandings of how technology actually works.

The Peltier Cooling Experiment: When Dangerous Actually Works

The first case before the court involves a TikTok user claiming to have achieved the impossible: a CPU fan spinning and cooling a processor without any power connection to the motherboard. The video shows a massive fan keeping a Thread Ripper CPU cool under 100% load at around 66 degrees Celsius—remarkable given that nothing is plugged into the CPU fan pins or chassis fan pins.

To determine if this is fraud or genuine innovation, the host purchased the exact same model demonstrated in the viral video. The answer, surprisingly, is that it actually works—but not for the reasons the original creator likely believed. The setup uses a thermoelectric cooling element, specifically a Peltier module, which creates a temperature differential between its two sides. The hot side pulls heat from the CPU while the cold side transfers that heat to a heatsink, which the fan then cools. The remarkable twist is that the voltage needed to spin the fan is generated by the temperature difference itself across the Peltier module—the heat from the CPU creates the electrical current that powers the cooling fan.

However, the host quickly identifies several critical problems with this approach. The setup pulls only around 106 watts while attempting to cool a processor that would typically require far more thermal management capacity. The tiny fraction of surface area compared to what a Thread Ripper actually needs means the system is severely undercooled. During testing, temperatures peaked at 94 degrees—the processor was thermal throttling, though the system wasn't properly indicating this. When the host touched the heatsink to check temperatures, he immediately burned his fingers, demonstrating just how dangerous the setup actually is.

The verdict: not guilty of spreading misinformation, because the technique genuinely does work in a limited sense. However, the host emphasizes this is incredibly stupid to actually use, noting that if they had a better GPU pushing the system harder, the CPU would have throttled or potentially been damaged. The very fact that it worked at all under such light load was surprising, leading the host to call it "a win" only in the sense of proving it was technically possible—not that anyone should attempt this.

The "Delete Nvidia Cache" Myth

The second case tackles one of the most persistent pieces of tech mythology floating around TikTok: the claim that opening a "hidden Nvidia folder" via Windows key plus R, entering certain directories, and deleting everything inside will fix random stutters, reduce lag, and boost your FPS. The video demonstrates someone typing the command and making these claims with complete confidence.

The host immediately recognizes this as malicious and fundamentally wrong on multiple levels. Rather than simply dismissing it, however, he enters all three folders to verify what actually happens. The result? Absolutely nothing changes. The game runs exactly as it did before. There was never any performance issue to fix, and deleting these cache files—which the graphics card genuinely doesn't need—provides zero benefit.

The verdict: guilty. The host issues a crucial rule of thumb for the audience: if something instructs you to press Windows key plus R and type in a command, do not do it until you understand what it actually does. This is especially true when the advice involves deleting files. The broader problem across TikTok's tech community, the host notes, is that creators tell viewers to do things without providing any explanation of why. This creates a dangerous cycle where people follow instructions blindly, potentially damaging their systems or security without understanding the consequences.

The Control Panel "God Mode" Deception

The third case involves a TikTok video with 55,000 likes claiming that by creating a new folder on your desktop, renaming it with a specific code, and pressing enter, you can access a secret panel containing all your computer settings in one place. The video presents this as some kind of hidden Windows feature—a supposed "god mode" for the operating system.

The host types out the cumbersome code—which takes more effort than simply accessing the control panel normally—and discovers that it simply opens the traditional Windows control panel. Nothing more, nothing less. The sales pitch that this provides access to all your settings in one place is demonstrably false. Many basic settings, particularly display settings like HDR, are not accessible through this method. The control panel itself is increasingly outdated in Windows 11, with Microsoft pushing users toward the modern Settings app.

The verdict: guilty. While the host admits the control panel's organization in this format is actually somewhat nicer than the default, the fundamental claim that this reveals some hidden capability or provides access to all settings is completely misleading. The creator could have simply said "open the control panel" and achieved the same result without the theatrical folder creation.

The Wi-Fi Password "Hack" That Misses the Point

The fourth case examines a TikTok video demonstrating an elaborate multi-step process to view a saved Wi-Fi password: pressing Windows key plus R, typing "CPL," navigating through network devices, clicking wireless properties, accessing the security page, and finally clicking "show" to reveal the password. The video presents this as some kind of secret technique.

The host immediately identifies multiple problems with this "tip." First, this isn't even the fastest way to access the network control panel—it's unnecessarily convoluted. Second, the video is recorded in a way that suggests the creator couldn't even be bothered to properly frame their screen, having to wait and move the window over. But most importantly, in Windows 11, there's a dramatically simpler method that makes this entire workflow obsolete.

To demonstrate, the host shows that in Windows 11, you simply click on Wi-Fi in the quick settings, select the network, and click "show" next to the password field. That's it. No command line, no navigating through control panel menus. The elaborate workaround is completely unnecessary on modern Windows.

The verdict: guilty. While the technique technically works, it's presented as some hidden knowledge when it's actually an outdated method that was never optimal in the first place. The host notes they'd previously done a whole video on war driving and Wi-Fi password cracking that actually would be a "tech tip that feels illegal to know"—unlike this trivial, unnecessarily complicated approach.

The "Unhack Your PC" Fantasy

The fifth and final case addresses a TikTok claim about what to do if your computer is hacked: press Windows key plus R, type "control," navigate to user accounts, find the list of users with full access, and remove any you don't recognize. The implication is that this will remove the hacker's access to your system.

The host dismantling this argument is devastating in its simplicity: if a hacker has compromised your computer to the point where they've created user accounts, they almost certainly have administrator-level access. This means they can simply re-add themselves or create new accounts whenever they want. The advice assumes the victim somehow has more control over the system than the attacker—a fundamental misunderstanding of what it means to be hacked.

The verdict: guilty. This is described as something a hacker would literally never do—creating an obviously suspicious account that would immediately alert the victim. If you're at the point where you're seeing unexpected user accounts on your system, the appropriate response is to disconnect from the network, wipe the machine, and restore from a clean backup.

Critical Takeaways for Navigating Tech Advice

The first and most important lesson is to never execute commands you don't understand. The Windows key plus R "tip" about deleting Nvidia cache files represents a broader pattern where TikTok creators provide instructions without any explanation of what those instructions actually do. This is dangerous because typing the wrong command or deleting the wrong files can cause real harm to your system. A good rule: if someone tells you to type something into a run window or PowerShell, pause and research what that command actually does first.

The second takeaway is that viral doesn't equal valid. The 55,000 likes on the control panel "god mode" video demonstrate how engagement metrics create perverse incentives. A simple, well-known feature dressed up as a secret performs better than boring accurate content. The most popular tech tips are often the most misleading, because they're optimized for sharing rather than correctness.

Third, context matters enormously in technology. The Peltier cooling experiment works in a limited sense but is genuinely dangerous. It demonstrates that something being physically possible doesn't make it advisable. Similarly, the Wi-Fi password trick technically works but is completely unnecessary in modern Windows—the context of which operating system version you're using completely changes whether something is useful.

Fourth, understanding the "why" is essential for evaluating tech advice. The "unhack your PC" advice fails because it demonstrates no understanding of how system access actually works. User accounts aren't the appropriate layer to address a compromise that's sophisticated enough to create those accounts in the first place. Without understanding the underlying principles, you can't evaluate whether advice makes sense.

Finally, newer isn't always better, but complexity isn't either. The host notes that the modern Windows Settings app has its own problems—particularly around audio and the depth required to reach basic settings like refresh rate—while also acknowledging that the control panel isn't the solution some people think it is. The best approach is pragmatic: use whatever method actually accomplishes your goal with the least friction.

Who Should Watch This

This content serves anyone who consumes tech tips from social media platforms, particularly TikTok, where viral advice spreads faster than fact-checking can occur. Gamers looking to optimize their systems will benefit from understanding which "fixes" actually work and which are wasteful or dangerous. IT professionals and help desk workers will recognize many of these myths as persistent problems they have to debunk repeatedly. General computer users who follow instructions without understanding them will gain crucial critical thinking tools for evaluating future advice.

The value here extends beyond specific tips to a broader media literacy skill: approaching viral content with skepticism, understanding that confidence doesn't equal competence, and recognizing that the goal of most content is engagement rather than genuinely helping you. In an era where social media has become the primary tech knowledge source for millions, this kind of critical examination serves an increasingly important public service function—separating genuine innovation from recycled myths dressed up in vertical video format.
