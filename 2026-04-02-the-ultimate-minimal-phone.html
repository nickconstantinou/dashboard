The Ultimate Minimal Phone: MKBHD's Surprising Review of the $10.99 Bluey Phone

A Tech Reviewer's Unexpected Appreciation for Radical Simplicity

In a tech landscape dominated by ever-larger screens, faster processors, and increasingly sophisticated cameras, Marques Brownlee—better known as MKBHD—finds himself drawn to something radically different: a $10.99 phone that can't make calls, connect to the internet, take photos, or do almost anything most of us would consider essential on a mobile device. His discovery of this device came, appropriately enough, while scrolling through Amazon's top-rated phones, where he noticed one listing with incredible ratings that he hadn't yet reviewed. Apparently not on the manufacturer's review program list, Brownlee took matters into his own hands and ordered one directly. What arrived at his door was the Bluey phone, manufactured by VTEC—a device that represents perhaps the most committed take on the "minimal phone" concept that Brownlee has ever encountered.

The timing of this review speaks to a broader cultural conversation happening around smartphone addiction, digital wellbeing, and the growing appeal of technology that does less rather than more. While flagship smartphones compete to add features and capabilities, devices like the Bluey phone ask a fundamental question: what if we stripped away everything that makes modern smartphones so compelling—and so addictive? Brownlee approaches this question with his characteristic analytical lens, examining not just what the device is but what it represents in the larger ecosystem of mobile technology.

The Unboxing Experience: Where Premium Expectations Meet Budget Reality

The moment Brownlee opens the package, it becomes clear this is not a typical consumer electronics experience. The Bluey phone arrives shipped in an open-faced cardboard box—exposed and visible, a presentation method Brownlee admits he has "never seen before." It's an unconventional choice that immediately signals this product exists outside the polished retail experience most consumers expect from smartphones. Rather than sliding the device out of sleek packaging, getting fully into the box required what Brownlee describes as an "unintuitive and difficult" process. He found himself ripping cardboard and untwisting fasteners that held the phone in place—experiences that "didn't feel very premium," though he quickly acknowledges this is "forgivable at this price."

The cost-conscious approach extends throughout the unboxing. Notably absent is any charging brick or USB cable—increasingly common omissions in the smartphone industry, but still notable when the device costs just $10.99. Even more telling, while the Bluey phone does feature removable batteries (a rarity in 2024-era devices), the tool required to access and replace them is not included in the box. The one small mercy is that the phone arrives from the factory with enough charge to power on immediately, sparing new owners the frustration of a dead device and no way to charge it.

Design Philosophy: A Phone That Commits to Less

Physically, the Bluey phone falls into a category Brownlee describes as "on the big side, but not too big." He compares it to the Nexus 6—Google's enormous 2014 phablet—though notes this device is actually "a little bit smaller." The form factor will be familiar to anyone who's handled feature phones from the pre-smartphone era, and that's entirely the point. The top of the device features a 1.7-inch black and white display, and above that sits a physical antenna. This antenna is purely decorative, however, because the Bluey phone contains no cellular radios whatsoever.

The implications of this design choice cannot be overstated. Brownlee enumerates what the Bluey phone does not have: no cell radios, no internet connection, no cameras at all, and exclusively physical buttons. There isn't even Bluetooth capability—a feature so ubiquitous in modern electronics that its absence feels almost shocking. The device represents what minimalism truly means when taken to its logical conclusion: not a smartphone with fewer features, but a fundamentally different category of device that shares only the most superficial characteristics with contemporary mobile technology.

The Experience of Using a Phone That Does Almost Nothing

Despite—or perhaps because of—its extreme limitations, the Bluey phone offers a user experience that Brownlee finds surprisingly charming. When pressing number buttons, for instance, the phone doesn't attempt to dial anything. Instead, it simply speaks the numbers aloud in a clear voice. Brownlee demonstrates this with a quick sequence of "six, seven," each number announced by the device's synthetic voice. It's a small detail, but it exemplifies the phone's philosophy: this device exists not to connect you to the wider world but to provide a contained, self-contained experience.

The speaker, positioned on the back of the device, features three distinct volume levels. While a single rear-facing speaker would typically be criticized on any modern smartphone, Brownlee appreciates the design consideration here: "the grill and the opening are more than big enough to ever accidentally block it with your fingers." It's thoughtful industrial design that acknowledges the phone's limitations and works within them rather than trying to overcome them.

Perhaps surprisingly for a device marketed as a minimal phone, the Bluey includes several built-in games. Brownlee acknowledges this might seem "counterintuitive," but he frames these games as "much healthier than doom scrolling TikTok or Instagram." His personal favorite involves blowing into the microphone to create virtual bubbles on screen—a simple, almost whimsical interaction that stands in stark contrast to the algorithmic content consumption that defines most smartphone usage. The phone also boots remarkably quickly, with Brownlee noting with visible delight how it powers on almost instantly when he presses the power button.

The chat feature proves particularly noteworthy. In an era of AI assistants and chatbot integrations, one might expect this to be some form of artificial intelligence conversation. It's not. The only characters available to talk to are Bluey and Bingo—the titular blue heeler dogs from the Australian children's animated series. The phone leans fully into its identity as a toy for young fans, creating an experience that is charmingly specific rather than attempting to be universally applicable.

Why This Phone Actually Achieves Minimalism

Brownlee is direct in his assessment of where other minimal phones fall short. He suggests that reviewers who evaluate the Bluey phone "up against other more expensive flagships" are fundamentally misunderstanding the product. "It's not going to have the thin bezels or high benchmark scores to compete with the $13, $15, $20 phones," he observes, genuinely uncertain whether the device's processor could even run a benchmark test. This is not a phone that attempts to compete on traditional smartphone metrics.

Instead, the Bluey phone succeeds precisely because it refuses to compromise on its core concept. Brownlee draws a comparison to other minimal phone efforts, specifically mentioning Light Phone's PALMA device. "EIN's cool and everything," he allows, "but you could technically still find your way over to Instagram on the book's PALMA." The implication is clear: other minimal phones pay lip service to the concept while still providing the pathways that lead back to compulsive smartphone use. The Bluey phone, by contrast, removes those pathways entirely.

With "less than a thousand total pixels across your whole display" and performance that never exceeds "three to four frames per second," combined with literally no cameras and no internet connection, the Bluey phone achieves something remarkable: battery life measured in weeks rather than days, and screen time measured in minutes rather than hours. "Total doom scrolling is effectively reduced to zero," Brownlee notes with evident appreciation. "This is good."

Key Takeaways: Lessons from the Most Minimal Phone

The Minimal Phone Concept Requires Radical Commitment to Work

Brownlee's review reveals that half-measures in digital minimalism tend to fail. Other devices in this category still provide the hooks—internet access, app stores, touchscreens—that enable users to fall back into the same compulsive patterns. The Bluey phone succeeds because it recognizes that meaningful reduction requires eliminating the very mechanisms that make smartphones addictive. The lesson here extends beyond this specific product: anyone seeking to reduce their smartphone dependency might consider that incremental approaches often leave the pathways to overconsumption intact.

Limitation Can Be a Design Feature, Not a Bug

At $10.99, the Bluey phone represents remarkable value for what it is—though that value proposition requires understanding the device on its own terms. The lack of premium unboxing experience, the missing charging cable, and the rudimentary black-and-white display are not failures of engineering but intentional choices that serve a larger purpose. Brownlee forgives these limitations "at this price" while simultaneously recognizing that the price enables a kind of freedom unavailable in more sophisticated devices.

Playful Design Choices Can Serve Serious Goals

The inclusion of Bluey and Bingo characters, the bubble-blowing game, and the voice that speaks numbers aloud all represent design decisions that could seem gimmicky but actually serve important functions. They make the device engaging enough to use while simultaneously preventing the kind of deep engagement that characterizes smartphone addiction. There's a lesson here for product designers: the path to reducing screen time doesn't require making devices boring, just making them differently engaging.

The True Cost of Smartphones Is Measured in More Than Dollars

Brownlee never explicitly states this, but his enthusiasm for the device hints at an important truth: the smartphone industry's race to provide more capability comes with hidden costs. The attention economy, algorithmic content feeds, and always-on connectivity have created devices that compete for user engagement in ways that are increasingly recognized as harmful. At $10.99, the Bluey phone offers an alternative paradigm where the product's value is measured not in features but in what it doesn't do.

Who This Review Is For

This review—and the product it examines—will resonate most with several distinct audiences. Parents searching for a first phone for young children may find the Bluey phone appealing precisely because of its limitations; it provides something that can make sounds, play simple games, and engage a child's imagination without introducing the risks and addictive patterns of a connected device. Adults experiencing smartphone addiction or screen time fatigue may discover in this review a validation of the intuition that something has gone wrong with mobile technology—and a glimpse of what an alternative might look like.

Tech enthusiasts and reviewers will appreciate Brownlee's nuanced analysis of how a device can succeed by failing to compete on conventional metrics. The review demonstrates that product evaluation requires understanding context and intended use, not just raw specifications. And anyone interested in the broader conversation around digital wellbeing will find in this Bluey phone a surprisingly compelling case study in what happens when you take the concept of minimalism seriously rather than treating it as a marketing label.

MKBHD concludes his review with genuine praise for VTEC's execution: "This is a good minimal phone, especially for the price. It's undercutting the competition by, from what I can tell, quite a bit." The comment carries an implicit message that extends beyond this specific product: in a world of smartphones designed to capture and monetize attention, a device that deliberately opts out of that competition represents a form of integrity that deserves recognition. Whether the Bluey phone is right for any given individual depends on their relationship with technology and what they want their phone to actually do. But as a proof of concept for radical digital minimalism, it earns its place as something genuinely different in an increasingly homogeneous market.
