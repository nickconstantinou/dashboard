What to Ask Your Early Customers for Hyper Growth | My First Million

The Hidden Engine Behind Twitch's Billion-Dollar Success

In the high-stakes world of startup growth, conventional wisdom often suggests that founders should focus on building polished products, scaling automated systems, and perfecting their go-to-market strategy before ever launching. But according to a revealing conversation on the *My First Million* podcast, the most successful companies often arrive at their breakthrough insights through a radically different approach—one that involves getting messy, talking to customers directly, and embracing the unglamorous work that doesn't scale.

The podcast explores a fundamental truth about building companies that achieve escape velocity: the answers to your biggest product challenges are hiding in plain sight, sitting inside the minds of your earliest users. The key is knowing how to ask the right questions, listening with genuine curiosity, and having the conviction to act on what you hear—even when the feedback seems counterintuitive or impossible to build.

---

The Three Questions That Built Twitch

When Twitch (then Justin.tv) was acquired by Amazon, the company had grown into the fourth most-trafficked site on the internet with around 2,000 employees. But in those critical early months, the founders faced a daunting challenge: how do you get streamers to abandon established platforms and come to yours? The answer came not from sophisticated market research or complex growth hacking, but from a surprisingly simple practice of direct customer interviews.

The co-founder, Emmett Shear (referred to as EMTT in the conversation), went out and personally interviewed one hundred streamers who were currently using competing platforms. Now, this is particularly noteworthy because Shear is not described as someone with a natural inclination toward what the host calls "the anthropologist approach"—the stereotypical founder who observes users with meticulous research interest. Yet he pushed himself to have these conversations anyway, and he did so in a remarkably disciplined way.

Each interview lasted only seven minutes, and he asked exactly the same three questions every single time. First, he asked what they liked about their current platform. Second, he asked what they disliked about it. Third, he asked what it would take for them to switch to Twitch. Most hadn't even considered switching—why would they? They were comfortable where they were. But a few people showed genuine interest, and they identified specific features or capabilities that were missing from Twitch. The pattern was clear: build what they want, and they will come.

---

The Subscription Feature That Generated Nearly $1 Billion

Acting on this feedback, Shear would go back to the people who had expressed interest and tell them exactly what was happening: "Hey, we built it. That was the thing you said you would switch for." The host describes Shear's approach as having a certain "ferocity"—he was relentless in following up, and eventually, people would cave to his persistence and give Twitch a try.

But the most significant insight came from a single conversation with one streamer who had a relatively small audience. When asked what it would take to switch, the streamer said he wanted to be able to make money. Shear pushed back—given the small audience, the ad revenue would be negligible. But the streamer had a specific vision: he wanted his fans to be able to pay him five dollars a month directly. Shear initially thought this was unrealistic. "People don't subscribe to individual creators," he thought. "That's not really a thing at the time." He even listed ten reasons why it wouldn't work.

But the streamer was clear: that's what it would take. So Shear built the feature. He expected it to fail. The guy moved over to Twitch, and yes, he wasn't making big money at first. But then came the revelation that would change Twitch's entire business model. Even earning just five, ten, or seventeen dollars a month felt meaningful to these streamers. It wasn't about the money itself—it was about the sense that there was a path forward, that they could potentially make a living doing this. The host describes the reaction as "unbelievable"—five dollars completely changed these people's behavior and sense of possibility.

Today, subscriptions drive close to a billion dollars in revenue for Twitch. It was the single most important feature, the primary revenue engine, and it never would have been built had Shear not had those conversations. The lesson is stark: the most valuable feature in the company's history came from listening to a single user with a small audience, not from internal product planning or competitive analysis.

---

The Perfection Over Progress Trap

The conversation then pivots to a broader pattern that trips up many founders. The host describes what he calls "the perfection over progress trap"—the tendency to look at successful companies and competitors and assume they "just showed up that way," emerging from the womb already polished and successful. In reality, those companies made countless mistakes, launched half-formed products, and figured things out through iteration.

One of the most important mindsets the host has developed is being comfortable shipping things that are only 80 to 90% of the way there. The goal is getting products into users' hands as quickly as possible so you can collect feedback and iterate. The reasoning is straightforward: if your company is going to work, you have to assume that where you are right now is the smallest you'll ever be. Therefore, making a less-than-ideal first impression on your first hundred users is actually inconsequential if it means you can take their feedback and make the product ten times better for the next hundred and the thousand after that.

Yet so many founders get stuck, unwilling to release anything until it's "exactly right." They spend months perfecting features that might not even be what users want, when they could have launched something imperfect, learned what resonated, and built from there. The host frames this as a philosophical shift: accept that early users are informants, not customers expecting a finished product.

---

Turning a Disadvantage Into a Growth Engine

One of the most practical examples shared comes from the host's own experience building PHive (likely a cold email or outreach tool). In the early days, they faced a significant problem: their platform involved sending emails, which attracted spammers and abuse. If they let anyone sign up and start blasting emails immediately, they'd be overrun with bad actors creating security nightmares and reputation damage.

The conventional solution would have been to spend three to four months building an automated security verification system. But that would have delayed launching the core product and pulled engineering resources away from building features they already knew were missing. So they made a seemingly crazy decision: they created the highest-friction signup process imaginable.

New users had to submit their name, what platform they were using, their Twitter handle, their LinkedIn profile, and various other information. They couldn't do anything on the platform once they signed up—they were essentially locked out until manually approved. On the back end, the host would go through every signup line by line, clicking on their Twitter profile, their LinkedIn, checking out their newsletter to verify they were legitimate, then manually clicking a button to approve them.

This approach seems to fly in the face of everything about hypergrowth startups—you're actively preventing people from using your product in the name of security. But here's where the creative thinking comes in: the host realized he could turn this disadvantage into a relationship-building opportunity. While he was checking out their profiles anyway, he would follow every single person who signed up and send them a direct message on Twitter: "Hey, I'm the co-founder at PHive. Thanks so much for signing up. Here's what we're working on. Let me know if there's anything I can do to improve your experience."

People who were initially frustrated that they couldn't immediately use the platform suddenly found themselves being personally welcomed by the CEO. They were being followed by the founder on Twitter. The reaction was consistently amazement—this kind of personal touch was completely unexpected. And as the company launched new features and shared updates about users having success, the host already had a network of people who had followed him back, who were now super fans following the journey and engaging with the content.

---

Finding the Advantage Buried Inside the Disadvantage

The host frames this as a specific mental model: whenever you face a disadvantage or constraint, ask yourself what's buried inside it. In this case, manual approval was painful, but it forced him into direct contact with every early user. He was already looking at their profiles anyway, so following them and sending a personal message required almost no extra effort. The result was that he built genuine relationships with hundreds of early adopters who became evangelists for the product.

This connects to a broader theme the host emphasizes about "things that don't scale"—the unglamorous tasks that no founder wants to do but that compound over time and generate real momentum. In the early days, these things feel like obstacles, but they're actually the secret weapons that separate companies that take off from those that stall. The host suggests that this approach becomes even more impactful later in a company's life, when maintaining that direct connection with users becomes a genuine competitive advantage.

He references a recent insight he read: in a world where many features are commoditized, the stories and narratives and the people behind the company become the actual competitive advantage. This personal touch, this willingness to do what doesn't scale, is something that competitors can't easily copy because it requires actual time and effort, not just money or technology.

---

Pet the Dog: The Service Business Philosophy

The podcast closes with a story about Tommy Melo, a guest who built a billion-dollar company (A1 Garage) starting from painting garage doors himself, then becoming an installer, and eventually building one of the largest garage door companies in the country. When Melo's team goes to a customer's house, they have a specific rule: "You got to pet the dog."

The host loves this phrase so much he wants to put it on a poster. The concept is simple but profound: when you go to someone's house to give them a quote or do a job, don't just transact and leave. Pet the dog. Say hello. Make eye contact. Smile. On the way over, ask if you can pick them up a coffee—find out if they like it black or with sugar. The point is that people remember how you made them feel, not just what you did for them.

This philosophy translates directly to building products and companies: the early users who stick with you through rough patches and help you figure things out are the ones who become lifelong advocates. They're the ones who tell others about you, who give you honest feedback when everyone else is just being polite. Investing in those relationships—not because it scales, but because it's right—is what builds the foundation for explosive growth.

---

Key Takeaways

The first major insight is that your best product ideas will come from direct conversations with early users, not from internal brainstorming or competitive analysis. Emmett Shear asked three simple questions to a hundred streamers and discovered the feature that would eventually generate nearly a billion dollars in revenue. The specific phrasing matters: ask people what they'd switch for, not just what they like or want. Focus on the specific conditions that would cause behavior change.

The second takeaway is about embracing imperfection and speed. Shipping products that are 80 to 90% ready, rather than waiting for perfection, allows you to collect real feedback and iterate quickly. The cost of a poor first impression on early users is far lower than the cost of building the wrong thing for too long. Assume that where you are now is the smallest you'll ever be, and act accordingly.

Third, look for the advantage hidden inside every disadvantage. When the host faced the "problem" of manual approval, he found a way to turn it into a relationship-building opportunity that generated super-fans. Every constraint or obstacle contains within it an opportunity if you're willing to think creatively about how to use it.

Fourth, invest in relationships with early users even when it doesn't scale. Following up personally, sending direct messages, making people feel valued—these things take time but compound into something competitors can't replicate. In a world of commoditized features, the human connection becomes your competitive moat.

Finally, the "pet the dog" principle applies to everyone: whether you're building software or painting garage doors, the personal touch matters. People become loyal not to products but to the people and experiences behind them.

Who This Is For

This content is most valuable for early-stage founders, product managers, and startup operators who are in the trenches of building something from scratch. If you're currently wrestling with questions about when to launch, how to find product-market fit, or how to generate growth without massive marketing budgets, this episode offers a framework for thinking about those challenges differently.

The specific audience that will benefit most includes founders who are too focused on building the perfect product before launching, founders who are struggling to get meaningful user feedback, and anyone looking for practical examples of how to turn constraints into competitive advantages. The stories are concrete enough to inspire immediate action—there's nothing theoretical about manually approving users and following them on Twitter to start a conversation.

The overarching message is both reassuring and challenging: you don't need to have everything figured out to start. You need to be willing to talk to people, listen to what they say, act on it with urgency, and build genuine relationships along the way. That's the actual playbook for hypergrowth, hidden inside all the messy, unscalable work that nobody wants to do but everyone who succeeds has done.
