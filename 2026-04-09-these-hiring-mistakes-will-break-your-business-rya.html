These Hiring Mistakes Will Break Your Business | Ryan Deiss

Introduction

In the journey of building a scaling business, few decisions carry more weight than who sits beside you in the trenches. Yet for most entrepreneurs, hiring remains one of the most consistently mismanaged functions—often because the very traits that make someone a successful founderwork against them when it comes time to build a team. Ryan Deiss, founder and CEO of Digital Marketer, has spent years observing the patterns that separate businesses that stall from those that break through to eight and nine figures of revenue. In this candid discussion, he pulls back the curtain on the hiring myths that keep entrepreneurs trapped in operational chaos, the critical shift from idea generation to constraint identification, and the functional leadership model that transforms a scattered collection of helpers into a compounding engine of growth.

The stakes could not be higher. As Deiss explains, the difference between a business that caps out at a few million in revenue and one that reaches to ten, twenty, or even a hundred million often comes down to hiring decisions made in the first place—not later, when resources abound and the path seems clearer. Most entrepreneurs arrive at a critical juncture around the four to six million dollar mark—what Deiss calls "no man's land"—where they desperately need threeVP-level leaders they feel they cannot possibly afford. Yet those very roles, if obtained, would carry the business to twenty million and beyond. The gap between where they are and where they want to be can only be bridged through systems and exceptional functional leadership. Understanding what to hire for, when to hire it, and how to avoid the two most catastrophic hiring mistakes forms the foundation of every scaling business.

The Two Hiring Mistakes That Destroy Scalability

Deiss identifies two polar opposite mistakes that entrepreneurs make when building their teams, and he argues that both stem from the same fundamental misdiagnosis of the problem. The first involves hiring what he bluntly calls "helpers"—people brought on simply to execute tasks that the founder finds tedious or unpleasant. The second mistake represents the opposite extreme: the search for some mythical "integrator" who can magically handle all the operational work that the visionary founder resents. Both approaches, according to Deiss, represent "the biggest lies ever perpetrated on the entrepreneurial community."

The helper approach creates what Deiss describes as a particularly insidious trap. When you hire helpers to handle work you don't want to do, what you actually accomplish is giving yourself a new job called management—which is objectively harder than the work you were originally doing. Your helper doesn't build systems, doesn't solve strategic problems, and certainly doesn't free you to focus on higher-leverage activities. Instead, they create a constant need for your direction and oversight. As long as you keep directing everything, they help you get stuff done. But that means you always have to keep your eye on that area because you're the one coming up with the plan and driving the execution. The helper is just there to help—which means you remain perpetually trapped in the work you tried to escape.

The integrator fantasy proves equally destructive, though it seduces entrepreneurs with a different promise. The concept goes something like this: the visionary founder can simply hire this one magical person—an integrator—and they'll just do all the crap that I don't want to do. Deiss has seen this play out countless times, particularly among students of certain business frameworks that heavily promote the visionary-integrator dynamic. It makes for a very good book, he acknowledges, but it just doesn't work ever in practice. The fundamental flaw lies in the assumption that someone can replicate your unique combination of domain expertise, network relationships, institutional knowledge, and business intuition. When you try to hire somebody just like you, what typically happens is that they don't want to work for you—not when they could be out building their own thing. You end up with either a diluted version of yourself or someone constantly chafing against the constraints of the founder's vision.

The Real Constraint: Shifting from Ideas to Identification

Before discussing what entrepreneurs should actually hire for, Deiss introduces a conceptual framework that transforms how they approach their entire business. He explains that most founders begin their journey with idea generation as their primary engine of progress—I got an idea, and so that's the hammer I take to every single nail. It's the tool that got them started, and it feels productive and exciting. But at some point, your business doesn't need another new idea or another new thing to implement. What it needs is to figure out what is the thing that is holding us back from the level of growth that you know you need.

This shift—from idea generation to what Deiss calls "constraint identification"—represents perhaps the most important mental model in the entire conversation. Rather than asking "what new thing could I add?" the scaling entrepreneur must learn to ask "what is the single biggest constraint right now in my business today for us to grow to the next level?" The answer, Deiss argues, is almost never ideas. Ideas are the one thing most entrepreneurs never run out of. The real constraints are almost always system-related or people-related, and the solution almost never involves hiring a bunch of helpers. If you hire helpers, you've simply added another management burden. If you try to hire someone just like you, you've created a relationship that won't last.

What the constraint actually requires is typically a functional leader—and this distinction matters enormously. A functional leader differs fundamentally from both a helper and an integrator. Instead of doing the work you don't want to do or handling all operations on your behalf, a functional leader owns an entire domain of the business: the head of sales, the head of marketing, the head of product. They bring genuine expertise, leadership capability, and the capacity to build a team beneath them to execute all your ideas. Deiss emphasizes that most entrepreneurs aren't nearly as good at hiring functional leaders as they become at scaling. They're good at hiring a bunch of little helpers. They think they're good at hiring a quote unquote COO, although nobody actually knows what that is. Getting really good at hiring functional leaders and building a team of those means that team leadership becomes a lot easier when you have three or four actually talented skilled people working for you, because they'll build the teams under them to execute.

Helpers vs. Drivers: The Transformation Test

Deiss elaborates on a critical distinction that emerged during the conversation with Sean, suggesting that understanding this concept separates businesses that scale from those that stall. He describes what he calls the helper-driver dynamic, explaining that it's very tempting to hire other entrepreneurial people like yourself because you feel like you finally have another sort of shiny object syndrome guy in the room that doesn't help. Or you hire a helper who, as long as I'm directing everything, helps me get stuff done. But that means I always have to keep my eye on that area because I'm the one coming up with the plan.

The helper approaches their role as someone who executes instructions—take this email, forward it, delete the original, handle whatever comes back. These are the "send and delete people" that John Morgan, who appeared on Digital Marketer the previous week, describes. They can handle the email without your involvement, and they do it competently but mechanically. There's nothing wrong with this at certain stages of business development, but it creates a ceiling. When you need someone to actually drive—to take ownership of outcomes rather than just execute tasks—they simply can't make that adjustment. They're still acting like a helper even though you've now asked them to drive. The question becomes whether that person can make that adjustment or not, and Deiss suggests this is always just about that person and their own kind of goals and self-improvement and self-development and their self-image.

The real test for whether someone represents a good hire comes down to this: you switch from hoping this would happen or wanting this to happen to fearing that this person will leave. When you hire someone so good and so transformative to the business that if they ever left you would have to worry about that area of the business again—that's when you know you've hired a driver. It's also when you face your most difficult retention challenge: these people are so talented that other opportunities constantly beckon. Building systems that create leverage so they can see the impact of their work, compensating them fairly, and allowing them to lead rather than directing them micromanages all become essential retention strategies.

Process Mapping Mistakes: What You Wish Were True

The conversation shifts to a related topic that Deiss identifies as equally destructive to scaling: how businesses map their processes. The mistake he sees most often involves mapping what they wish were true rather than what actually happens in the business today. You need to see all of those as points of future optimization because you've got to get a picture of what it is today because that's what's going to inform what are all of the people actually doing or what should they be doing. The difference matters enormously for building accurate scorecards and identifying the metrics that actually drive growth.

Deiss provides a compelling example from his own organization. When Digital Marketer rolled out scorecards to the team, someone pointed out that they didn't have any metrics for their email newsletter. It seemed like an obvious gap—if they're tracking everything else, why not this? When Deiss asked where the newsletter feeds into anything, the answer revealed the problem. The newsletter existed for awareness and engagement of the list, but it never linked off to anything that was meaningful. It never showed up as a sticky note on any of their growth engines, which is why it never showed up on the scorecard. Because the newsletter didn't drive measurable outcomes at any stage of their business process, it was essentially an orphaned activity—something that kept getting done because it felt like it should be done, not because it actually moved the business forward.

This reveals a critical principle: metrics should flow from process maps, not the other way around. You don't want to just say I'm going to build out my scorecard because that looks cool. The question becomes: what are the metrics that you're picking? Because if the metrics don't all link back to a step or stage on your business process maps, then you could very well be tracking something that doesn't matter. Many orphaned activities that companies are doing exist simply because they're doing things that don't have a sticky note represented anywhere. When you map what actually is happening and identify the stages where value is created or lost, the right metrics become obvious.

Bridging the Gap: Systems and the No Man's Land

The conversation touches on a universal challenge that Deiss frames around the threshold between $10 million and $100 million in revenue, referencing something he read from Jason Lumpkin: if you get to 10 million in revenue, your business can 100% get to 100 million in revenue. Deiss agrees but locates the real bottleneck earlier—in what he calls the swamp of scale that happens at four to six million. That's no man's land. That's when you probably need three people that you absolutely can't possibly afford, right? Three VP-level roles that you desperately need to grow but can't justify financially yet.

The mathematics reveal the trap. At four to six million in revenue, the business generates enough margin to survive but not enough to comfortably fund three functional leaders who could take it to twenty million. At that point, you would have the margin and the cash flow to be able to get the rest of the people and then be able to scale to the next level. But you can't get there without them, and you can't afford them without getting there first. The only way to bridge that gap is through systems. You're not going to be able to bridge it through just brute force through just chewing rocks. That's what gets you from a million to two million, maybe for really motivated people up to seven or ten, but it's not going to get you beyond that.

Deiss is careful to distinguish between two types of scale. Brute force—working harder, longer, with more intensity—works for a while. It will get a motivated entrepreneur with a solid offer from one million to perhaps ten million. But beyond that threshold, you're going to have to get really talented people, and those people are expensive. The only way you're going to be able to create that margin is through systems. Systems that allow a functional leader to leverage their time across a team rather than individually executing everything. Systems that create predictable, repeatable processes for acquiring customers, converting leads, and delivering outcomes. Systems that make the business less dependent on any single individual and more dependent on the compound interest of well-designed processes and talented people working together.

Who This Content Is For

This discussion speaks most directly to entrepreneurs who have launched their businesses, found initial product-market fit, and now face the disorienting challenge of building teams that can scale beyond their personal capacity. If you're currently handling every important task personally, if you feel like you can't get anything done without your direct involvement, or if you've hired helpers but still feel trapped in the day-to-day operations—this content provides a diagnostic framework for understanding why.

The audience most likely to benefit includes business owners currently in the one to ten million dollar range who sense that their current approach has reached its limits, founders who have tried the "hire an integrator" approach and found it didn't work, and entrepreneurs who recognize they're excellent at generating ideas but less skilled at identifying and removing the actual constraints blocking their growth. Even business owners above ten million will find value in understanding why process mapping matters and how to avoid orphaned activities that consume resources without driving meaningful outcomes.

The specific value comes from understanding both what to avoid—the two hiring mistakes of helpers and integrative fantasies—and what to pursue: functional leaders who own domains, build teams beneath them, and create compound leverage. It also offers a useful recalibration around metrics: if your scorecard isn't derived from your actual business process maps, you may be tracking numbers that feel important but don't drive growth. And perhaps most importantly, it provides a mental model—constraint identification rather than idea generation—that shifts your entire approach to growth from adding new things to removing the actual blockers standing between you and your next level of revenue.
