'Radical acceleration' in targenting cycle through AI points to lack of human oversight

The Anthropic-Pentagon Dispute: AI, Autonomous Weapons, and the Crisis of Human Oversight

Introduction

In what represents one of the most consequential confrontations between a private technology company and the United States military, Anthropic—the AI startup behind the Claude language model—has launched legal proceedings against the Pentagon after being designated a national security risk. The dispute, which has escalated rapidly in recent months, centers on Anthropic's refusal to remove safety guardrails that would permit its AI technology to be used for autonomous weapons systems and domestic mass surveillance of American citizens.

The conflict comes amid disturbing revelations about how artificial intelligence is already being deployed in military operations. According to reports from the Wall Street Journal, the US military has been using AI to identify targets for an operation called Epic Fury—including in an incident that reportedly resulted in the deaths of 175 people, many of them children, when a school near a naval base in Iran was mistakenly targeted. The Pentagon has refused to confirm or deny whether AI was involved in that particular strike.

This confrontation pits corporate power against state might in an unusually public fashion, raising profound questions about who gets to determine how powerful AI systems are used—and whether sufficient human oversight exists when algorithms are making life-or-death decisions at unprecedented speeds.

The Dispute: What Started This Conflict?

Professor Akash Schwarz, a scholar of political theory at Queen Mary University of London, has been tracking this dispute closely. Speaking on the program, he explained that the conflict had been brewing for approximately a year, though the public nature of the confrontation is relatively recent.

The relationship between Anthropic and the Pentagon began with a contract dating back to July 2025. However, Anthropic had also entered into a partnership with Palantir, the data analytics company that provides the Maven Smart Systems—which the Department of Defense uses for AI targeting and decision-support systems. That contract was established in 2024, meaning the underlying relationship predates the current public dispute by some time.

The turning point came when the Department of Defense recently issued a new AI strategy for the military domain. This strategy, as Schwarz explained, clarified that the Pentagon understands it should be able to use "any AI model or product for any lawful use"—a stipulation that is notably open to broad interpretation.

Anthropic's discomfort stemmed from the company's insistence on maintaining two specific red lines: it would not permit its models to be used for mass surveillance of US citizens, nor would it allow its technology to power fully autonomous weapons systems. When the company communicated these positions to the Pentagon, the response was swift and uncompromising.

Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth went so far as to label Anthropic a "supply chain risk," prompting the company to file two lawsuits on Monday arguing that the designation violated its First Amendment free speech rights and due process rights under the Fifth Amendment. The company stated it would not be "swayed by intimidation or punishment," adding that "Anthropic's reputation and core first amendment freedoms are under attack."

President Trump then announced he was ordering federal agencies to stop using Anthropic's technology, giving them six months to phase it out—though reports suggest CEO Dario Amodei remains in talks with the Pentagon to potentially work out some kind of arrangement. Meanwhile, Sam Altman's OpenAI, backed by Microsoft, has announced its own deal with the Defense Department to provide technology for the government's classified systems.

The Deeper Problem: Radical Acceleration in Targeting

Beyond the immediate legal battle, Professor Schwarz highlighted a more fundamental concern: the way artificial intelligence is transforming the targeting cycle in military operations represents what he termed a "radical acceleration" that raises profound questions about human oversight.

AI integration into military operations is not new. For the past decade, as Schwarz noted, various AI tools and narrow models have been increasingly incorporated to help with supply chains, logistics, and translations. However, the introduction of large language models like Claude represents something qualitatively different—a much broader, expanded, and scaled-up integration of AI into all aspects of military operations.

The most consequential shift is occurring in the targeting cycle. Schwarz explained that what we are now witnessing is "a quite a radical acceleration in terms of how quickly targets are identified or indeed discovered and then how quickly targets get actioned." He cited reports from the Iran conflict in which approximately 1,000 targets were identified in the first 24 hours of operations—a staggering volume that raises immediate concerns about the adequacy of human review.

The fundamental question, Schwarz emphasized, is where human oversight exists in this process. International humanitarian law requires that targets being attacked are valid military objectives and not civilian infrastructure. It mandates that the data and information on which target decisions rest must be sound, and that precautions must be taken to protect civilian populations and civilian objects.

"That's really, really difficult when you're dealing with 41 targets an hour or a thousand targets in a 24-hour period," Schwarz observed. "That is a challenge that we're seeing with this sped-up, broader scale of targeting that becomes possible with artificial intelligence and specifically with large language models."

The Reliability Problem

Adding to these concerns is the documented reliability issues with large language models. Schwarz noted that some of these models have reliability rates between 25 and 50 percent—"which means they're wrong very often, or they put things together that don't necessarily make sense because these models can't understand meaning or they don't really have an understanding like we do."

This reliability problem becomes especially grave when the "temporal horizons" of decision-making shift so dramatically and the "scale of possible action" expands so dramatically. When algorithms can generate thousands of potential targets in a single day, the capacity for human reviewers to meaningfully check each one becomes severely compromised—and the consequences of errors can be catastrophic, as the reported deaths of 175 people in Iran appear to demonstrate.

The Inversion of Responsibility

Perhaps most striking about this dispute is what Schwarz described as an "interesting inversion" in the typical relationship between government and private industry.

Normally, it is the democratically elected government that puts guard rails, restrictions, and safeguards onto the private sector and its interests in order to ensure that citizens don't come to harm. In this instance, we see the opposite: a private company attempting to impose ethical restrictions on government use of its technology, while the government pushes back against those self-imposed limitations.

Yet Schwarz was careful not to portray Anthropic as purely heroic in this narrative. "I would venture to say there aren't really any good guys," he said, "because we have now seen quite quickly how the bar, the ethical bar and legal bar has been lowered quite significantly to stipulations about mass surveillance and fully autonomous weapon systems."

The deeper problem, in Schwarz's assessment, is that dual-use technologies—those originally designed for civilian purposes but now finding military applications—are entering the military sector without the robust testing, evaluation, verification, and validation processes that normally apply to military technologies. The safeguards, regulations, and stipulations that would typically govern weapons systems are often absent when digital technologies developed for commercial markets are repurposed for warfare.

Key Takeaways

The AI targeting revolution is already here, with or without guardrails. The US military has been actively using AI systems for target identification in active combat operations, including the reported identification of 1,000 targets in the first 24 hours of operations in Iran. This represents a fundamental shift in how warfare is conducted, one that is happening faster than regulatory frameworks can adapt.

Human oversight is being outpaced by algorithmic speed. The volume and velocity of AI-driven target identification raises serious questions about whether meaningful human review can keep pace. When targets are being identified and actioned at rates of 41 per hour or more, the capacity for human judgment to serve as a meaningful check on machine recommendations is fundamentally compromised.

Large language models have significant reliability limitations. With documented reliability rates as low as 25 to 50 percent, these systems frequently generate incorrect outputs or connect information in ways that don't reflect actual meaning. Deploying such systems for life-and-death targeting decisions without robust human verification creates substantial risk of civilian casualties.

The Anthropic-Pentagon dispute reflects a broader regulatory vacuum. The conflict represents an unusual situation where a private company is attempting to impose ethical restrictions on government use of its technology while the government resists. This points to a systemic failure: no established frameworks exist for determining how dual-use AI technologies should be deployed in military contexts.

The normalization of autonomous weapons and mass surveillance is accelerating. Even as Anthropic has fought against these uses, the broader trend in the defense establishment is toward accepting wider deployment of AI for both autonomous weapons and domestic surveillance. The ethical and legal bar has been "lowered quite significantly," in Schwarz's words.

Who This Matters To

This discussion is essential for policymakers wrestling with AI governance, defense professionals concerned with the ethics and effectiveness of AI integration into military operations, technology executives navigating the responsibilities of dual-use AI development, legal scholars examining the intersection of constitutional rights and national security, and citizens concerned about the increasing automation of warfare and surveillance.

The Anthropic-Pentagon dispute represents a critical inflection point in determining whether artificial intelligence will be deployed in military contexts with appropriate safeguards, meaningful human oversight, and adherence to international humanitarian law—or whether the technology will outpace our capacity for responsible governance, with potentially catastrophic consequences for civilian populations.
