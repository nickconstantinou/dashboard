# Your Kids Asked the Artemis Astronauts Questions. They Answered.

Key Insights

  • Orientale Basin visibility: Human eyes had never before seen the Orientale Basin on the moon's far side—the same basin the Artemis II crew witnessed firsthand, marking the first direct visual observation of this marquee crater of the solar system.
  • Distance milestone: The Artemis II mission traveled farther than any human has ever been from Earth during the 10-day lunar orbital flight.
  • Carbonated beverage failure: Bubbles in carbonated drinks do not rise to the top in microgravity—instead they disperse throughout the liquid, causing bottles to "spray like a shaken can" when opened, making soda effectively undrinkable in space.
  • No "upside down" in space: With no gravitational reference, any surface can function as floor or ceiling, and astronauts reported feeling like they were floating even after returning home to Earth.
  • Nutella tortillas as space dessert: Astronaut Jeremy Hansen popularized spreading Nutella on tortillas and folding them into makeshift chocolate crepes, which became a beloved cabin treat during the mission.

    Introduction

    When The Daily podcast team tallied responses to their call for children's questions directed at the Artemis II astronauts, the volume exceeded anything the show had seen in its entire run. That fact alone speaks to something deeper than mere curiosity—children remain uniquely captivated by the possibility of space travel in ways that adults, often jaded by fiscal pragmatism, rarely sustain into adulthood. Host Rachel Abrams gathered the four astronauts—Reid Wiseman, Victor Glover, Christina Koch, and Jeremy Hansen—to pose these genuine questions directly, creating an episode that oscillates between the profound and the delightfully mundane.

    Artemis II represented a milestone in humanity's return to lunar exploration. The crew spent ten days orbiting the moon, traveling farther from Earth than any humans before them. They circumnavigated the far side, the hemisphere permanently hidden from our planet's view due to tidal locking. They conducted scientific observations and, perhaps most importantly for this podcast episode, they fielded questions ranging from the existential ("Why spend billions on space?") to the endearingly practical ("Can you drink soda in space?"). The resulting conversation offers readers a rare window into both the philosophical weight of space travel and its surprisingly domestic realities.

    The episode matters because it humanizes an endeavor that can easily feel abstract and government-funded from a distance. These four astronauts, each with distinct backgrounds and personalities, returned from their journey with perspectives fundamentally altered—not into mystical cosmic wanderers, but into people who now carry a palpable sense of scale, humility, and gratitude back into ordinary life.

    The Making of an Astronaut: Advice from the Crew

    The conversation opened with a question that countless children have likely asked themselves: what does it actually take to become an astronaut? Rather than offering rehearsed motivational clichés, each astronaut identified one quality they consider essential, revealing distinct but complementary philosophies.

    Reid Wiseman emphasized curiosity and wonder—the capacity to look at a tree, watch a bird in flight, or observe water cascading over rocks and recognize that same miraculous nature exists everywhere, including beyond our atmosphere. His advice was essentially to practice paying attention to the natural world before attempting to chart courses through it.

    Jeremy Hansen zeroed in on persistence, acknowledging directly that challenges will arrive between any aspiring astronaut and their goals. "There are going to be times where you do not believe you're going to make it," he said, advising young people to communicate their aspirations to others so that support systems can materialize. The implicit message: talent alone does not sustain missions, whether lunar or otherwise.

    Victor Glover offered perhaps the most counterintuitive answer, prioritizing teamwork above technical skill. "The number one thing I would say to a young person is being a good teammate," he explained. The crucial insight was that you cannot independently declare yourself a good teammate—your teammates make that determination. Children can begin practicing this immediately, by learning how to contribute to group efforts and considering others' needs.

    Christina Koch, the final responder, encompassed her answer with three words: humility, hard work, and supporting those around you. Together, the four responses painted a portrait of astronaut-readiness as fundamentally relational and attitudinal rather than strictly technical.

    The Far Side Experience: Awe Without Words

    When eight-year-old Colin from California asked whether being on the moon's dark side was scary or adventurous, the astronauts revealed how profoundly difficult it is to articulate what they witnessed. Christina Koch described the moment as "almost solemn," noting that despite their demanding schedules, the four crew members deliberately paused to acknowledge the historic nature of their position.

    Reid Wiseman admitted he had no adequate words for the experience, explaining that human brains "have not evolved to see what we're seeing." The astronauts found themselves in a unique astronomical position: whenever Earth experiences a new moon, the far side is fully illuminated. They were gazing at terrain never directly observed by human eyes—even those who walked on the near side during Apollo never witnessed what Artemis II photographed and recorded.

    Victor Glover shared that his unofficial role during the mission became "pulling Reid away from the window," a task he hadn't anticipated but that proved continuously necessary. They observed the Orientale Basin, described as the solar system's marquee crater, along with formations like the Vavelof crater and the distinctive ejecta rays extending from the far side around to the visible hemisphere.

    Jeremy Hansen described a particularly overwhelming moment when the sun went behind the moon, causing a sudden shift from extreme brightness to darkness while they looked out at the large orb. "There were so many different emotions at the same time," he recalled, noting that his frequent exclamations of "oh my god" were, if anything, understated responses to what was occurring.

    For Hansen specifically, the entire experience carried spiritual weight. "The whole thing to me was about God's glory," he explained, describing the mission as a reminder of forces far beyond human creation. "We're next to the moon, which dwarfs us, and we didn't do that. That's just happened over billions of years." The perspective shift—heavy on humility and gratitude—was central to how this mission changed him.

    Defending the Cost: An Astronaut's Response to Skepticism

    Thirteen-year-old Isaac from Arlington, Virginia, asked the question that often lurks behind public enthusiasm for space exploration: why are we spending billions of dollars when we're not even landing, just looking? This represented the episode's most direct challenge, and Jeremy Hansen took it seriously.

    Hansen acknowledged that Earth faces legitimate problems requiring attention while also defending the investment's indirect benefits. His core argument centered on what he called "the genius that we bring together under the focus of a common goal." When humanity commits to solving the engineering problems required for deep space travel, the collaborative pressure generates innovations that benefit people on the ground—miniaturized medical devices, water purification advances, and materials science breakthroughs among them.

    The crucial insight was that extraordinary innovation emerges specifically when "a thousand minds try to solve a problem together," not from isolated research efforts. Space exploration functions as a forcing function for human collaboration, and the returns on that collaboration exceed the direct scientific knowledge gained from lunar observation alone.

    Life Aboard: Food, Sleep, and the Mundane Marvels

    Children's questions about daily life in space revealed how thoroughly they had considered logistics the adults might overlook. The food questions alone could fill a cookbook.

    Christina Koch addressed the universal childhood assumption that astronauts must suffer through bland meals: "The food is magnificent," she declared. The crew enjoyed brisket on tortillas, chicken and beans, and broccoli au gratin that Koch insisted was "actually delicious" despite host Rachel Abrams's skeptical raised eyebrow. Spicy green beans emerged as a crew favorite, along with beet and kale salads. The kitchen setup required adding water and heat for rehydrated items, but the variety exceeded what many Earth-bound individuals eat regularly.

    The surprise hit of the mission came from Jeremy Hansen's accidental invention: Nutella spread on tortillas, folded into a half-moon shape resembling a chocolate crepe. He "distributed those around the cabin," becoming the provider of what everyone agreed was an ideal dessert. The floating-food physics added an element of play—tortilla pieces regularly drifted free during meals, prompting the crew to grab whatever passed nearby and verify it was edible before consuming.

    Victor's story about Reid's oatmeal mistake illustrated how mishaps became bonding opportunities. When Reid added insufficient water to rehydrate his oatmeal, thousands of crumbs launched into the cabin. Rather than expressing frustration, Victor floated over and declared the scene "my favorite game," proceeding to consume the floating oatmeal particles. This moment, which Viktor noted resembles a famous Simpsons episode, captured how astronauts learn to adapt to microgravity's chaos without losing their sense of humor.

    The soda question received the most deflating answer: carbonated beverages are effectively impossible to consume in space. Bubbles don't rise to the top when gravity is absent—they distribute evenly throughout the liquid. Any attempt to open a sealed bottle causes immediate spraying, like shaking a can. The crew tried drinking Sprite on a previous mission and eventually surrendered in defeat.

    On sleep arrangements, Jeremy Hansen explained that without gravity, the concept of "upside down" disappears entirely. Any orientation feels equally natural; the crew simply designated some surfaces as floors and others as ceilings through convention. They arranged themselves somewhat like bunk beds, with two astronauts oriented opposite to the other two. Hansen discovered that mentioning his preference for floating triggered his crewmates to kick him, sending him drifting across the cabin—entertainment he grew to appreciate even as it interrupted his rest.

    Perhaps most remarkably, all four astronauts reported that upon returning home and sleeping in beds for the first time, they experienced phantom floating sensations. Their bodies had adapted to weightlessness so thoroughly that Earth-normal sleep felt unusual.

    Detailed Takeaways

    Space travel reframes perspective on human insignificance—but that reframing is generative, not depressing. Jeremy Hansen's description of the mission as "about God's glory" and Reid's observation that the moon "dwarfs us, and we didn't do that" suggest that encounters with cosmic scale can produce humility rather than despair. The astronauts seem to have absorbed a healthy sense of human smallness combined with pride in what collaboration can nonetheless accomplish. This implies that exposure to awe-inspiring scale, whether through space travel or other means, might be psychologically valuable precisely because it detaches ego from outcome.

    The most important astronauts are good teammates who let others determine their value. Victor Glover's insight that you cannot independently assess your own teamwork quality deserves wide circulation beyond aspiring astronauts. The principle—that external reputation in collaborative contexts is determined by those you work with—applies to virtually every professional domain. Young people practicing teamwork, then, are building the evaluative framework they'll need throughout their careers.

    Persistence matters more than talent for long-term achievement. Jeremy Hansen's direct acknowledgment that "there are going to be times where you do not believe you're going to make it" offers realistic encouragement rather than false promise. The corollary—that communicating your goals to others enables them to help you—provides both a practical strategy and a social framework for handling discouragement.

    Innovation under pressure produces side benefits that isolated research cannot match. The justification for space spending centered not on direct lunar knowledge but on the collaborative forcing function that ambitious goals create. This suggests that organizations and societies should consider not just the direct outputs of ambitious projects but the human capacity-building that pursuing them requires.

    Adaptability and humor are operational necessities in extreme environments. The oatmeal incident and floating food chaos illustrate that technical competence alone cannot sustain astronauts. Their capacity to reframe mishaps as entertainment—and to support each other through embarrassing mistakes—made the mission functional. This principle extends: any high-stakes environment rewards practitioners who can maintain perspective through chaos.

    Who This Is For

    This episode serves parents seeking to share genuine STEM enthusiasm with children—not through lectures but through authentic conversations that validate kids' questions as worthy of serious adult attention. It also benefits anyone questioning the value of space investment, as the astronauts offer articulate, humble defenses of exploration spending that avoid both jingoism and detached technophilia. Educators working with curious middle-schoolers will find the astronauts' advice on cultivating qualities like persistence, teamwork, and curiosity directly applicable to classroom culture-building. And for adults processing their own sense of smallness in the face of overwhelming problems, the astronauts' accounts of how cosmic perspective generated gratitude rather than despair offer a model for maintaining appropriate humility without succumbing to helplessness. The episode succeeds because it treats children as capable of engaging with both the philosophical weight and the domestic details of space travel, refusing to condescend while embracing what makes the topic wonderful.
