FBI Negotiator: The 5 Steps to Becoming a Better Listener | Christopher Voss

The Art of True Listening: What FBI Hostage Negotiators Teach Us About Connection

*Christopher Voss reveals the counterintuitive secrets to becoming a better negotiator—and a better human being*

---

Introduction

If there's one skill that governs our professional and personal lives more than any other, most of us never learned it in school. We picked it up haphazardly, through trial and error, and we probably got it wrong far more often than we realized. That skill is negotiation—and according to former FBI hostage negotiator Christopher Voss, it touches every aspect of our existence, whether we're aware of it or not.

"Life is full of negotiations," Voss explains in a revealing conversation about the art of listening. From conversations with employees and customers to high-stakes deals with investors, we're constantly negotiating—often without even realizing it. Yet despite how fundamental this skill is to human connection and professional success, most of us were never formally taught how to do it well.

Voss has spent decades honing these skills in the most extreme environment imaginable: face-to-face with hostage takers, armed criminals, and individuals in the depths of crisis. What he discovered through that work challenges nearly everything we assume about negotiation—and it starts with a truth that's far more profound than any negotiation tactic. The key to success, he argues, isn't about being more persuasive, more powerful, or more charismatic. It's about learning to listen in a way that most of us have never truly experienced.

---

The Hijack Moment: Where Good Listeners Go Wrong

According to Voss, becoming a better listener involves roughly five or six distinct steps, but the real challenge begins at step two. It's what he calls the "hijack moment"—that critical point in any conversation when the other person is speaking and your mind suddenly leaps to what you want to say next.

"Most people get past that stage," Voss notes, referring to the initial hurdle of actually paying attention to another person rather than just waiting for your turn to speak. But if you're listening at all, you're typically doing one of two things: you're either listening to rebut or listening to relate. Both represent hijack points where genuine connection falls apart.

When we listen to rebut, we're formulating our counterargument while the other person is still speaking. The urge to correct is, in Voss's words, "insanely impossible to overcome." It's so powerful that Voss and his colleagues actually weaponize it as a negotiation technique. He'll deliberately say something wrong on purpose—and watch as his counterpart cannot resist the urge to correct him with the truth. This "triggering hidden information" works, Voss explains, because "the urge to correct is so satisfying that you'll never regret telling me something you shouldn't have told me because it felt so good in the moment."

There's an old saying that encapsulates this phenomenon: "We don't remember what was said, we remember how we felt in the moment." That satisfaction of correcting someone is so powerful that it overrides our usual caution about revealing sensitive information. It's a vulnerability that skilled negotiators exploit—and a trap that anyone seeking genuine connection must learn to avoid.

---

Story Stealing: The Fallacy of Common Ground

The second major hijack point is what Voss calls "hijacking to relate," which he describes as "story stealing." When someone shares an experience with us, it often triggers an incredible memory of something similar that happened to us. The urge to share that story becomes irresistible—and we do it without meaning any harm.

You know the feeling: someone tells you about their difficult week, and before you know it, you're saying, "Oh my god, the same thing happened to me!" You feel incredibly good in that moment, completely unaware of how the other person feels. This is what Voss refers to as one of the "fallacies of common ground."

"You can't imagine that what I feel is like you had to one-up me," Voss explains. "You had to do better than me. And you can't imagine that that's the case because the memory it triggered in you was so good."

The person who just shared their experience feels squashed. Their story was hijacked, their moment of vulnerability redirected toward someone else's experience. They may not even consciously register this feeling, but the connection they sought has been undermined. And here's the counterintuitive reality: the person doing the story stealing can't imagine this is happening because the memory they triggered was so good for them. The experience felt bonding to them, but it was actually alienating the person they were trying to connect with.

This phenomenon represents "probably the single biggest challenge of becoming a better negotiator," Voss suggests, because it requires listening through these hijack moments—actually listening to somebody all the way through rather than jumping in with your own parallel experience.

---

Being Heard vs. Feeling Heard

Here's where Voss's insights take a turn that fundamentally changes how we think about communication. There's a crucial difference between being heard and feeling heard. Just because someone understands what you're saying doesn't mean you feel understood. Just because someone has received your words doesn't mean they've connected with what you were trying to communicate.

Voss illustrates this with a powerful personal story from a wedding in Ireland. He found himself in his first substantive conversation with the bride on the night after the wedding ceremony—a moment when she was "unconscious on her feet," making the rounds at the reception because that's what brides are obligated to do. She was exhausted, running on fumes, and had probably given the same polite responses to dozens of people already that evening.

What made Voss's approach different was that he didn't tell her anything. Instead, he reflected back to her what she had experienced that day. He told her how she had probably found it easier to run a small country in South America than to pull off this wedding. He acknowledged that she was there to celebrate the union of two families, and that her husband was present "cuz he's got to be." He demonstrated completely that he understood everything she had gone through to reach that moment.

She ran into him the next night at dinner and gave him a big hug. Her words to him were remarkable: "I have no memory of what you said to me last night. I just remember how good it made me feel."

That is the power of making somebody feel understood. She literally couldn't remember the words he used, but she felt bonded to him because he had thoroughly understood her. This is the challenge, the mission, and the goal of making somebody feel completely heard and understood—and it's a skill that transcends negotiation into the realm of genuine human connection.

---

Rethinking Negotiation: From Battleground to Bridge

The conversation then turns to a question that challenges our default assumptions about what negotiation actually is. Most people, when they imagine a great negotiator, picture someone powerful, perhaps slightly intimidating, or intensely charismatic—someone who can manipulate and win the other side over. The mental model is adversarial: "I'm going to get the upper hand on you. I'm going to have you over a barrel. I'm going to force you to do what I want."

This approach, what Voss calls "power negotiations," puts you out of business in the long run. You lose friends, collaborators, and colleagues. It may win individual battles, but it loses the war of sustained relationships and ongoing success.

The alternative isn't about being weak or giving in. It's about recognizing that negotiation, at its best, is relationship-building. Voss frames it starkly: "If your objective is to have a long-term relationship of trust where we both prosper, now if you're a complete sociopath, that should be your objective because it's low maintenance, it's the least amount of effort, and it's the maximum amount of money."

Even for those who are purely mercenary—only caring about money—the collaborative approach is more effective. Voss shares that one of his top-level coaching clients told him, "I have made so much more money being collaborative than I ever made being cutthroat." The relationships flow more smoothly, deals happen faster, and the outcomes are better for everyone involved.

This challenges the conventional wisdom that negotiation is a zero-sum game where one person's gain must be another's loss. In Voss's framework, the best negotiation is one where both parties feel heard, understood, and satisfied—and where the relationship is strengthened rather than damaged.

---

Tactical Empathy: The First Move in Any Negotiation

So how does this actually work in practice, especially in high-stakes situations like hostage negotiations? Voss traces his approach back to his work on a crisis hotline, where he learned what he now calls "tactical empathy"—demonstrating understanding in a way that makes the other side feel heard and draws them into telling you more.

The first move in any negotiation, Voss explains, is to remove yourself as a threat. This is counterintuitive: in most people's mental model of negotiation, you want to project power and strength. But why would someone make a great deal with you if you're a threat to them? They might make a deal with you out of fear, but they won't make a great deal with you. They won't collaborate, they won't open up, and they won't trust you enough to reach the best possible outcome for both sides.

Hostage negotiators accomplish this removal of threat through several techniques. They use a "non-threatening voice"—what Voss describes as a "calming, soothing, late-night FM DJ voice." They express genuine concern for everyone involved, including the person who might be considered "the bad guy." Voss puts it this way: "I'm here to get everybody out. That includes you, Mr. Bad Guy. Now, whether or not you let me get you out is going to be up to you, but that's my objective first of all, and I need to find out whether or not you're good with that."

This approach accomplishes two things simultaneously. First, it removes the negotiator as a threat, making it safe for the other person to engage. Second, it begins the process of making the other person feel understood—establishing a connection that can then be built upon to reach a resolution that works for everyone.

Who This Is For

This content is essential viewing for anyone who communicates with other humans—which is to say, everyone. Sales professionals will find Voss's insights particularly valuable, as the principles of making customers feel heard directly translate to closing deals and building long-term client relationships. Leaders and managers at all levels will benefit from understanding how to truly listen to employees, direct reports, and peers. Anyone involved in business development, partnerships, or client-facing roles will discover practical frameworks for building trust and achieving better outcomes through collaboration rather than confrontation.

Beyond the professional realm, anyone who has ever felt unheard in a conversation—the spouse who doesn't feel understood, the friend whose problems get overshadowed by someone else's similar story—will find Voss's framework transformative. The skills he describes aren't just negotiation tactics; they're the foundation of every meaningful relationship we have.

The value here is profound but practical. Voss doesn't offer abstract philosophy; he offers concrete observations about why we fail to connect and specific techniques for doing better. Whether you're negotiating a multi-million dollar deal or trying to have a meaningful conversation with your partner, the hijack moments he describes are the same, and the path through them is the same: listen all the way through, resist the urge to hijack the conversation, and focus not on being heard but on making the other person feel understood.

That's the secret that hostage negotiators know and the rest of us are still learning: the fastest way to get what you want is to help the other person feel like they've already been heard.
