# Cover Your Hole: The Responsible Relic Hunter's Guide

A Conversation with Phillip from Colonial Williamsburg

---

Key Insights

  • Colonial Williamsburg operates as the world's largest living history museum, employing historical interpreters who research period-appropriate trades, tools, and customs to authentically recreate 18th-century American life.
  • Live munitions remain a genuine hazard in relic hunting contexts—historically significant battlefields, training grounds, and properties may contain unexploded ordnance that poses serious injury risks to untrained searchers.
  • Responsible artifact recovery requires immediate hole restoration as a universal ethical standard in the detecting community, reflecting both legal compliance on protected lands and courtesy to landowners and future visitors.
  • Relic hunting differs fundamentally from treasure hunting in its focus on historical documentation and cultural preservation rather than monetary value—a distinction emphasized at living history institutions.
  • Colonial artifact identification involves understanding period-specific manufacturing techniques, material composition, and historical context to distinguish genuine period items from later reproductions or misidentified objects.

    Introduction

    Conan O'Brien welcomes a different kind of historical expert to his podcast—not a professor or author, but a living history interpreter from Colonial Williamsburg. The guest, Phillip, brings hands-on expertise in the material culture of 18th-century America, with a particular focus on the tangible remnants of daily life during the Revolutionary era. Colonial Williamsburg, as a premier living history institution, invests heavily in research authenticity, meaning its interpreters develop deep knowledge of period crafts, tools, and the objects that populated colonial Americans' daily lives.

    What unfolds is a wide-ranging conversation that traverses the romantic notion of treasure hunting, the sobering reality of unexploded military ordnance, and the ethical obligations that come with disturbing the ground in search of historical artifacts. Conan, ever curious about unusual topics and passionate about learning from specialists in unexpected fields, approaches the subject with his characteristic humor while also demonstrating genuine fascination with the practical realities of artifact hunting.

    The conversation lands on a central message that transcends the novelty of relic hunting: the importance of responsible practice. Whether you're searching a colonial battlefield, a farm field, or any historically significant site, the cardinal rule is simple—cover your hole. This principle, seemingly straightforward, carries weighty implications for landowner relationships, archaeological preservation, legal compliance, and the broader reputation of the metal detecting community.

    Key Concepts & Arguments

    The Romance and Reality of Relic Hunting

    Phillip approaches the subject of finding lost treasures with the kind of grounded expertise that only comes from years of hands-on experience. Relic hunting, he explains, isn't primarily about striking it rich or finding museum-worthy rarities—it's about connecting with the past through the physical objects that previous generations left behind. A button, a bullet, a buckle—these small items carry enormous historical weight when properly understood within their context.

    The conversation explores how relic hunting differs from the treasure-hunting narratives we see in movies and television. While fiction paints picture of treasure maps and mysterious caches, the reality involves patient searching, careful excavation, and meticulous documentation. A relic hunter's true treasure isn't necessarily a valuable object but rather the information that comes with understanding where something was found and what it was doing there.

    Conan probes the emotional dimension of the hobby—what drives people to spend their weekends kneeling in dirt, scanning fields with metal detectors, and carefully extracting corroded objects from the earth. The answer, Phillip suggests, lies in that tangible connection to history. Finding an artifact in its original context creates a direct link to the people who used it, something no museum exhibit can fully replicate.

    The Hazard of Forgotten Ordnance

    The conversation takes a serious turn when the topic turns to one of relic hunting's most sobering realities: unexploded military ordnance. This isn't theoretical danger—it's a genuine threat that affects anyone searching land with military history. Battlefields, training grounds, shooting ranges, and former military installations can harbor live explosives that remain dangerous decades or even centuries after conflicts ended.

    Phillip emphasizes the critical importance of recognizing potential hazards before attempting any excavation. This means understanding the military history of your search site, recognizing unusual objects that might be munitions rather than artifacts, and knowing when to disengage and contact authorities. A corroded piece of metal might be an innocuous artifact—or it might be a live grenade from the Revolutionary War or the Civil War that could still function.

    The safety dimension extends beyond personal injury concerns. Disturbing unexploded ordnance can endanger others, particularly if the object is moved or if the investigation isn't properly handled by trained demolition personnel. Responsible relic hunters learn to recognize the signatures of military ordnance and understand when a find should be left in place and reported rather than extracted.

    The Core Principle: Cover Your Hole

    The episode's central message emerges clearly: whatever you dig up, whatever you find, whatever you disturb—fill it back in. This isn't merely polite etiquette; it's a foundational practice of responsible relic hunting that protects the hobby's future and maintains relationships with landowners.

    The principle addresses multiple concerns simultaneously. From a legal standpoint, many properties have restrictions on excavation or artifact collection, and leaving visible holes can create evidence that attracts unwanted scrutiny. From a practical standpoint, open holes damage property, create safety hazards for people and animals, and make landowners understandably hostile to hobbyists who follow them.

    Perhaps most importantly, the practice reflects respect for the land and its history. A carefully filled hole minimizes the visible impact of the search, leaving the property essentially unchanged. This allows other hobbyists to search the same ground later, maintains the aesthetic character of historic properties, and demonstrates that relic hunters are responsible stewards rather than treasure-hunting vandals.

    Phillip addresses the psychological temptation to leave holes open—some detectors feel pride in their discoveries and want evidence of their work visible. But this pride is short-sighted. The hole you leave open today becomes a problem for the landowner tomorrow, and the reputation damage affects every hobbyist who follows. Cover your hole is both practical advice and an ethical imperative.

    Historical Interpretation and Artifact Knowledge

    Working at Colonial Williamsburg requires deep knowledge of period material culture. Phillip's expertise isn't simply in finding objects but in understanding what he finds—distinguishing between genuine colonial-era items and later reproductions, identifying manufacturing techniques that date objects, and contextualizing individual finds within broader historical patterns.

    The conversation touches on how living history interpreters develop this knowledge. Research into period documents, examination of museum collections, and collaboration with historians all contribute to the kind of expertise that allows confident identification of artifacts. Colonial Williamsburg's commitment to authenticity means its staff must understand not just what objects looked like but how they were made, used, and discarded.

    This knowledge serves the relic hunting community in practical ways. Accurate identification prevents the mistaken preservation of worthless items and, more importantly, helps distinguish significant finds that deserve careful documentation and study. An amateur might dismiss a corroded object as junk, while an experienced interpreter recognizes it as a valuable piece of material evidence about historical daily life.

    Detailed Takeaways

    1. Historical Context Determines Safety Protocol

    Relic hunting on land with any military history requires advance research and careful risk assessment. Un-exploded ordnance remains dangerous long after conflicts end, and corroded explosives can be as lethal as freshly manufactured ones. Before searching any property, understand its history—battlefields, training grounds, firing ranges, and military installations all carry potential hazard profiles that affect how and where you can safely search. This preparation isn't optional caution but fundamental responsibility.

    2. Landowner Relationships Require Trust Maintenance

    The metal detecting hobby survives largely on private landowners' goodwill. When one detectorist leaves visible holes across a field, it takes only one incident to sour a property owner on the entire community. Filling holes immediately and thoroughly preserves access for yourself and others. This means bringing backfill material if necessary, tamping soil properly, and leaving the property in essentially the same condition you found it. The few minutes this takes protects access for years.

    3. Artifact Value Lies in Context, Not Price

    The conversation emphasizes that meaningful relics aren't necessarily valuable in monetary terms. A soldier's button found where he fell tells a story that a gold coin in a display case never could. This understanding shifts the relic hunter's focus from treasure-hunting mentality toward documentation and preservation. Recording where objects were found, what was found with them, and how they were positioned relative to other materials creates historical knowledge that transcends any individual artifact's market value.

    4. Responsible Practice Protects the Hobby's Legitimacy

    Every visible hole, every improperly documented excavation, every carelessly disturbed site creates ammunition for those who want to restrict or prohibit metal detecting. The hobby exists in a delicate legal environment where landowner access and public land permissions depend on demonstrated responsibility. Individual choices matter at the collective level—responsible practice by one detectorist protects access for everyone; irresponsible behavior by one creates restrictions for all.

    5. Expert Knowledge Transforms Hobbyists into Contributors

    Understanding what you find transforms you from someone digging up curiosities into someone contributing to historical knowledge. Learning to identify period artifacts, recognize manufacturing signatures, and document finds properly elevates the hobby's intellectual content and ensures that discoveries contribute to scholarship rather than disappearing into private collections. Colonial Williamsburg's approach—treating material culture as primary source material for historical understanding—offers a model for how hobbyists can pursue their passion productively.
