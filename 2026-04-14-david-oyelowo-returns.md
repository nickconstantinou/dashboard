FPL content creator Harry, known to his growing YouTube audience as "FPL Harry," returns with his trademark Monday morning debrief following a tumultuous Gameweek 32 that saw him deploy his wild card chip. ## The Wild Card Decision: Logic, Regret, and the Numbers

Harry's decision to play his wild card in Gameweek 32 was driven by a specific strategic calculation rather than impulse. With five free transfers accumulated, he recognized that not deploying the chip would leave those transfers with "no real benefit to putting myself in that position." The key insight driving his decision was understanding the timing limitations: managers who wild card in Gameweek 35 will be able to make team changes but won't be able to effectively target the immediate gameweeks that follow. Conversely, Harry saw an opportunity to both capitalize on his existing free transfers AND position himself for the run-in through Gameweek 35 and beyond.

The strategy involved maintaining flexibility while maximizing his immediate opportunities. Harry wanted to execute a wild card, bench boost, and free hit strategy while preserving four or five free transfers heading into the crucial run-in period to target assets like Arsenal defenders, Crystal Palace for their potential double, and other runners who could make a difference. This multi-chip approach, combined with retained transfer ability, represented his ideal path to closing the gap on rivals.

Yet Harry acknowledges the anxiety surrounding the decision: "I was wild carding out of a team that looked very good and over the course of Friday evening and Saturday, it performed very well." The timing felt precarious—he was wild-carding out of a team that was already delivering points, creating the uncomfortable sensation of potentially sacrificing momentum for a strategic repositioning.

## Gameweek 32 Results: Damage Contained, Not Avoided

The post-deadline performance delivered mixed emotions. Harry's wild card team currently sits at 45 points with Bruno Fernandes as captain—a choice that would have been identical in his pre-wildcard draft, meaning the captaincy provides no meaningful comparison. He should have invested in either a Thiago Silva or a Jarrod Bowen rather than keeping the Calvin Lewen slot, a decision that stings given that Thursday was still to play Monday night.

The numbers tell a story of near-catastrophe narrowly averted. Technically, his pre-wildcard team would be on 47 points (receiving Jubei Hall's nine-pointer off the bench for Estique), but with five free transfers and a planned Echitique-to-Bowen transfer, the realistic comparison shows his pre-wildcard team at 52 points with Brown and Burmo still to play. The wild card cost him at least seven points going into the weekend, though Harry was 25 points down before the matches began. O'Reilly's 14-point haul proved transformative, while Verrugan's nine-pointer from Brighton's clean sheet provided additional salvation. The fact that Harry finds himself only seven points down represents a recovery he credits entirely to his two best-performing assets.

The bench boost strategy also incorporates Verrugan, Van Hecken, and James Hill—players Harry feels "pretty happy" with in terms of coverage.

## Transfer Plans and Mistakes Acknowledged

Harry is transparent about his regrets regarding the pre-planned Gabrielle transfer. "I shouldn't have used a defender as the one transfer that I booked in. I should have gone for a more exciting probably forward because I thought my midfield looked fine and in the end it hasn't performed particularly well." Gabrielle earned four points and could have contributed more with a clean sheet and assist, but Harry's self-assessment is harsh: he should have targeted a lead defender in hopes of getting Defcon on Monday night, then simply started players like Bowen or Thiago from the beginning.

This regret stems from misreading his own squad's needs. He believed his midfield was settled but ended up with underperforming assets, whereas an additional forward option might have provided the ceiling he needed.

The five available transfers represent an opportunity to address defensive issues, though Harry doesn't plan "any sideways swaps between any double gameweek players." He could theoretically upgrade Hinchwood to Mtoma or Pascal Groves but doesn't see value in such moves given Hinchwood's performance metrics.

## Key Takeaways for FPL Managers

**Timing flexibility matters more than chip stacking.** Harry's decision to wild card in Gameweek 32 rather than waiting for Gameweek 35 preserves transfer flexibility for the crucial run-in. Managers should recognize that deploying chips earlier can maintain strategic optionality rather than feeling obligated to synchronize all chips into single gameweeks.

**Accumulated free transfers create their own pressure.** Having five free transfers going into Gameweek 32 created urgency to act, even though Harry admits feeling nervous about wild-carding out of a performing team. The existence of valuable unused transfers can force decisions that wouldn't otherwise be necessary.

**Defense often becomes the weak link after wild cards.** Harry's regrets center on defensive booking decisions and an inability to start multiple strong options. When wild-carding, managers should verify defensive coverage is actually improved rather than assuming attacking assets can compensate.

**Individual player performance can disguise team-wide failures.** Without O'Reilly's 14 points and Verrugan's 9, Harry's wild card would have been catastrophic. The lesson: single high-scoring assets can mask underlying squad problems, and managers shouldn't confuse one player's returns with overall strategy success.

**Double gameweek attachment doesn't mean automatic ownership.** Despite Chelsea's defensive struggles, Harry remains uncertain about Palmer while maintaining his position because "I don't think that selling Bruno is a very good idea." The combination of fixture certainty and potential returns justifies holding assets even when confidence in their performance wavers.
