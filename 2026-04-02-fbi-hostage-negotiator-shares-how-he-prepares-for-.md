# The FBI Hostage Negotiator's Guide to Negotiation: How Christopher Voss Prepares for Every Deal and Why Making Others Feel Heard Is Your Most Powerful Tool

## Introduction

Christopher Voss spent twenty-four years with the FBI, seventeen of which were dedicated to crisis and hostage negotiation. He was one of the bureau's lead international kidnapping negotiators, working cases that ranged from corporate kidnappings abroad to terrorism incidents. After retiring from the FBI, Voss founded The Black Swan Group, a consulting firm that applies the psychological and tactical frameworks from high-stakes hostage negotiation to business and personal negotiations. He is also the author of *Never Split the Difference*, a bestselling book that has influenced how executives, lawyers, salespeople, and ordinary people approach negotiations.

In this interview, Voss addresses a question that many professionals overlook: how do you actually prepare for a negotiation beyond researching the other party or calculating your BATNA (Best Alternative to a Negotiated Agreement)? His answer draws directly from his FBI experience and reveals a counterintuitive approach that prioritizes understanding the other person's psychological state before you ever say a word. Voss also discusses the neuroscience behind why making someone feel heard is perhaps the single most effective thing you can do in any negotiation, and he offers practical insights for applying these techniques in everyday situations—from buying a car on Craigslist to negotiating with a stubborn five-year-old.

## The Problem with Common Preparation Methods

Voss begins by acknowledging that most people approach negotiation preparation in one of two ways. The first group relies heavily on their BATNA—calculating their best alternative if the deal falls through, their desired outcome, and the worst case scenario they would accept. While this provides a useful framework for knowing when to walk away, Voss suggests it doesn't adequately address the human element of negotiation. The second group does extensive research on the other party, attempting to walk into the negotiation "fully armed" with information. This approach has merit, but Voss argues it still misses something crucial.

Then there are those who rely on power moves and hardball tactics. Voss acknowledges that leverage has its place, but he suggests these approaches often damage the relationship and can backfire in situations where you might need the other party again. The key insight he offers is that preparation should begin with a deep understanding of what negatives the other party is likely harboring about you before you even meet. This isn't about researching them—it's about introspection and preemptive relationship management.

## The Negative Preemption Strategy

Voss reveals that before any negotiation, he thinks about what negatives the other party is likely harboring about him. "Everybody's got that, actually," he says. "What are the things you want to deny? I don't want you to think that I'm going to be greedy. I don't want you to think I'm another slimy salesperson. I don't want you to think that I'm here to maximize my opportunity. I don't want you to think I'm wasting your time."

The technique is to take those denials and transform them into straight observations. Instead of simply denying that you're like every other salesperson, you voice the concern openly: "Look, I'm probably going to seem greedy. I'm probably just going to seem like another slimy salesperson. I'm probably going to seem like somebody that's wasting your time." Voss explains that this transformation from denial to observation "instantly makes me the straight shooter, the honest person."

The reasoning behind this approach is grounded in neuroscience. Voss notes that "the brain is 75% negative," meaning that humans are wired to focus on potential threats, risks, and negative outcomes. By preemptively addressing the negative assumptions the other party likely holds, you neutralize these concerns before they can fester and undermine trust. "That's the fastest way for me to build a relationship, deactivate the negatives, and make me look like a straight shooter, which increases your trust in me," Voss explains.

## The Appreciation Layer: Beyond Flattery

The second component of Voss's preparation involves anticipating what the other party would be offended by if you failed to appreciate it. This includes their hard work, their expertise, and the legitimate reasons they have for being at the negotiating table. "The fact that I'm talking to you at all means you have value and that you have legitimate reasons in your own right to be respected and admired," Voss says.

He is careful to distinguish this from empty flattery. "I'm taking what a lot of people would refer to as flattery, but then articulating that it's not vacuous flattery. It's genuine appreciation from where you're coming from." He offers an example of how this might sound in practice: "You worked very hard to get to where you are today. You have been in this business for a very long time and you know what you're doing. Otherwise, if you didn't, you wouldn't be sitting here in front of me at all. And you have things that are valuable and worthy of my attention and of my respect."

When combined, the negative preemption and genuine appreciation create what Voss calls "tactical empathy"—a deliberate, structured approach to understanding and acknowledging the other party's emotional and psychological state. This isn't about being nice or avoiding conflict; it's about using empathy as a tactical tool to build trust and create conditions favorable to agreement.

## The Neuroscience of Feeling Heard

The interviewer asks Voss to expand on why it is so important for someone to feel understood and heard. "This is going to sound like a dumb question," the interviewer admits, "but it just seems like it's the most important thing." Voss affirms this instinct, noting that "people come to the table with such a desire to be heard and to make their case, they can't imagine that's what's driving them. It's blinding."

Voss explains that when you make someone feel heard, you trigger the release of specific neurochemicals. First, oxytocin is released—this is the hormone of bonding and trust, and it also promotes honesty. "If you get a hit of oxytocin in interacting with me, you're going to bond with me and you're going to be far more honest with me," Voss explains. Second, serotonin is released, which he describes as "a drug of satisfaction," meaning the person becomes less demanding and more open to finding mutually acceptable solutions.

The practical implications are profound. If you can make the other party feel heard, you simultaneously increase their bonding with you, their honesty toward you, and their willingness to be flexible. "That's a pretty good start for a negotiation," Voss says with understated emphasis.

The key insight the interviewer offers—and Voss confirms—is that most people default to wanting to make their own case and feel heard themselves. "It seems like the biggest swing I could make as a negotiator is to let go of my need of that, or at least initial need of that, and really hone in on your need for that," the interviewer observes. "If I just did that one switch, I'm a much better trust builder and therefore a much better negotiator." Voss affirms this is exactly right.

## Everyday Negotiations: From Craigslist to Car Dealerships

When asked how he applies these techniques in low-stakes everyday situations—buying a couch on Craigslist or negotiating at a market—Voss offers a revealing window into his approach. "I make their case for them," he says, "because that's what they're going to say to me. So what I've done is I've just now left them with nothing to say."

The technique involves articulating the seller's position so completely that they have no反驳 left. "I can pound you down on price," Voss admits, "but I better never need to come back to you for anything. There are few deals that are complete one-offs." This philosophy reflects his understanding that even casual transactions can have long-term implications.

Voss is refreshingly candid about the distinction he makes between private sellers and businesses. He describes having no regard for car dealerships across the board: "I no longer ever get any vehicle I own serviced at a car dealership. I don't care what the warranty says because I don't trust them." With car dealerships, he employs a more aggressive approach. "I will make their case and then I will become very passive aggressive," he explains, "and my favorite is: how am I supposed to pay that? You would have said all the things that I just got done saying if I hadn't said them first."

The tactical logic is elegant. "If I took away all the wind out of your sails, took all the bullets out of your gun, then when I give you the opportunity to point the gun at me, you got empty chambers. You got nothing to say." Voss then drives to the seller's bottom line quickly, and if he likes the outcome, he makes a deal. If not, he walks away.

Crucially, he acknowledges the trade-off: "Once I've killed you on price, you are not going to forget that and you are not going to lift a finger to help me ever again." This is why he is selective about when to deploy maximum pressure versus when to preserve the relationship.

## Parenting as Negotiation: Raising Thinkers, Not Followers

When the interviewer—himself a father of a five-year-old, four-year-old, and one-year-old—asks about negotiating with children, Voss offers both practical advice and a philosophical framework. His starting point is an age-based heuristic: "Up to age five, treat a kid like a king. Five to 15, you know, work them like an indentured servant. At 15, let him go." He notes that this aligns remarkably well with brain development science.

The core principle throughout childhood is that your job as a parent is to help your kids think. "What a lot of parents want to do is just order kids around like go to bed because I'm—why? Because I said so. Because I'm the father," Voss observes. "That's not really helping them think. It's demonstrating to them that the only way you can get stuff done is by through the use of force."

At the same time, Voss is clear that this doesn't mean tolerating bad behavior or failing to maintain boundaries. He shares an anecdote about a video he admires: a mom trying to get her five-year-old son into the backseat of an SUV while he cries and resists. Her nine-year-old daughter eventually just picks him up and throws him in. "At some point in time, you got to toss the kid in the car," Voss acknowledges. The point is not permissiveness but rather recognizing that the ultimate goal is to challenge and develop your children, not shield them from difficulty.

Around age four to six, children begin developing the cognitive capacity to engage with reason rather than just authority. Voss suggests that parents should recognize this transition and gradually shift from simply giving orders to helping kids process and think through situations.

## The Easiest Framework to Implement

When asked what single framework is easiest to implement for someone looking to improve their negotiation skills, Voss identifies the family of techniques involving repetition: "It really is going to be the different ways to repeat back to somebody what they just said. Labeling, focusing on their emotions or affect, mirrors and paraphrases that focus specifically on what they said."

He acknowledges that this approach "seems like it's a waste of time and it ends up being"—but the thought trails off, implying that the initial skepticism dissolves once you experience the results. This is a technique that Voss has taught extensively: repeating back key words or phrases the other person has used, labeling their emotions ("It sounds like you're frustrated"), or paraphrasing their position to confirm understanding. These simple acts of reflection signal that you are listening, that you understand, and that you take their perspective seriously.

## Detailed Takeaways

**Negative preemption is faster than persuasion.** Rather than waiting for the other party to express their doubts about you and then having to overcome them, proactively acknowledge what they are likely thinking. This approach draws on the fact that the human brain is inherently risk-focused, and by addressing negative assumptions before they are voiced, you short-circuit the skepticism before it can take root. Transform your denials into straight observations: instead of denying you're greedy, acknowledge that you probably seem greedy.

**Making others feel heard triggers biological bonding.** When you successfully make someone feel understood, you trigger the release of oxytocin (bonding and honesty) and serotonin (satisfaction and reduced demands). This isn't soft or sentimental—it is a neurochemical advantage that makes the other party more cooperative, more truthful, and more flexible. The counterintuitive insight is that the greatest swing you can make is to delay your own need to be heard and instead focus on fulfilling the other person's need.

**Match your tactics to the relationship's future.** Voss draws a clear distinction between one-off interactions and relationships you may need again. With car dealerships, where you likely will never see the same salesperson twice, aggressive price negotiation is fair game. But even then, he makes the seller's case first before applying pressure. With private sellers, Craigslist purchases, or anyone you might encounter again, preserving goodwill matters. The question to ask yourself is whether you might need this person in the future—and calibrate your tactics accordingly.

**The goal of parenting negotiations is to develop thinking, not compliance.** Rather than defaulting to authority ("Because I said so"), Voss suggests parents should use negotiations as opportunities to help children think through problems and develop judgment. This doesn't mean abandoning structure or allowing misbehavior, but it does mean recognizing that the long-term goal is raising children who can reason through situations rather than simply following orders.

**Repetition and reflection are the entry point for beginners.** If you remember nothing else from negotiation training, remember this: learn to mirror, label, and paraphrase. Repeat back key words. Acknowledge emotions. Confirm understanding. These techniques seem deceptively simple, almost too easy to be effective, but they consistently create the foundation for trust-building that every successful negotiation requires.
