Introduction

Fantasy Premier League managers up and down the country are facing one of the most challenging periods of the season as blank gameweek 31 arrives with a vengeance. Four teams—Arsenal, Wolves, Manchester City, and Crystal Palace—will not be in action this weekend, leaving FPL managers scrambling to field their best possible sides while managing limited options. For those with heavy investments in players from these four clubs, the pressure is particularly acute, as they must decide whether to hold onto expensive assets hoping for a fixtures reshuffle or cut their losses and reinvest in playing assets.

In this comprehensive analysis, FPL Harry walks through his personal team for the blank gameweek, examining the specific challenges he faces with four non-playing assets in his squad. More importantly, he provides a framework for thinking about the broader strategic picture—one that extends far beyond this single gameweek. With the FA Cup quarterfinals looming in gameweek 32, the fixture landscape is about to shift dramatically, and understanding how to position yourself for those upcoming doubles and blanks could be the difference between a successful season and a disappointing finish. Harry emphasizes that while there are certainly opportunities to exploit this blank gameweek with one-week punts, the smartest managers will be thinking several moves ahead, preserving transfer flexibility for the pivotal weeks that lie ahead.

The Current State of Gameweek 31

As things stand, gameweek 31 is officially a blank gameweek with four teams not playing: Arsenal, Wolves, Manchester City, and Crystal Palace. However, FPL Harry notes that this situation is not yet locked in stone, and managers would be wise to avoid making hasty early transfers. The reason for this uncertainty stems from the European competitions—both Manchester City and Crystal Palace remain in their respective European tournaments, and there's a theoretical chance that if both teams crash out of their fixtures this week, their postponed match could be rearranged back into gameweek 31.

Manchester City currently hold a 3-0 deficit against Real Madrid in the Champions League, while Crystal Palace are level at nil-nil in their Europa Conference League away fixture. According to Harry, there's an article on the Premier League website confirming that if both teams were to exit their European competitions, there is a chance their fixture could be reinstated into gameweek 31. However, Harry assesses this likelihood at only around 10%, meaning managers should not be making major strategic decisions based on this outcome. The situation will become clearer by the gameweek 31 deadline, but for now, Harry advises caution—particularly when it comes to Manchester City players, whose fixture away at Crystal Palace could suddenly become very attractive if the European exits occur.

Harry's Team Analysis

Looking at Harry's squad for this blank gameweek reveals both the challenges and opportunities that many FPL managers will be facing. In goal, Harry has David Raya, who faces an away trip to Fulham—not the worst fixture in a blank gameweek, but certainly not ideal either. The Fulham defense has been decent this season, and this fixture represents something of a banana skin for the Arsenal goalkeeper.

In defense, Harry has lined up with Anderson from Fulham, Tarkowski from Everton, and Virgil van Dijk from Liverpool. Anderson returned to the Fulham starting XI in their nil-nil draw against Nottingham Forest in gameweek 30, keeping a clean sheet and earning a Defenccon (clean sheet bonus) in the process—making him a very useful bench option sitting in Harry's first bench slot. Tarkowski, however, presents a problem. He was not in the Everton squad that traveled to Arsenal, having picked up a slight knock late in training either Thursday or Friday of the previous week. This is particularly problematic because Harry has no other player on his bench with a fixture this week—all his other non-playing assets (Haaland, Gabriel, and Richarlison) are among his starters, meaning if Tarkowski is unavailable, Harry would be forced into making a defensive transfer or playing a player who isn't guaranteed to feature.

Van Dijk travels to Brighton with Liverpool, a fixture that the Reds will be expected to dominate despite Liverpool's somewhat rotated side in gameweek 30 against Tottenham. Harry still considers this a probable good fixture for the defending champions.

In midfield, Harry has Wilson, Bruno Fernandes, Bryan Burmo, and Elliot Anderson. There are concerns here too—Bryan Burmo has been disappointing for Manchester United this season, and Harry notes there is a small chance he limped off in their game against Aston Villa. Elliot Anderson travels to Tottenham in what Harry describes as a massive six-pointer for Champions League positioning, but he failed to even hit a clean sheet in gameweek 30, making this a risky fixture for clean sheet points.

Up front, Harry has Haaland, Gabriel, and Richarlison—all three players from teams that are blanking this gameweek. This represents a significant problem, as Harry effectively has only eight playing assets across his eleven positions, with three players who will definitely not score points this gameweek. Technically, Harry has four transfers in the bank and a full eleven on the pitch, but he readily admits that his team doesn't look that great and there are definite areas he would like to improve.

Chip Strategy and Long-Term Planning

The most valuable insight Harry provides in this video is not about specific player recommendations but rather about the strategic framework for approaching this blank gameweek in the context of what lies ahead. The key question managers need to ask themselves is whether they are definitely going to use their wildcard in gameweek 32, might wildcard later in gameweek 35, or have already used their wildcard entirely.

This distinction matters enormously because it fundamentally changes what kind of players you should be targeting this week. If you are definitely wildcarding in gameweek 32, then focusing on one-week punts—players who will only be useful for this single gameweek before being transferred out—makes perfect sense. However, if you are planning to wildcard later or have already used your wildcard, then you need to be thinking about longer-term investments that will serve your team well through the upcoming doubles and blanks.

The reason gameweek 32 is so pivotal is that it coincides with the FA Cup quarterfinals. This round of the FA Cup will confirm which teams have doubles in gameweek 33 and 34, and will give us much better estimates of what is going to happen later in the season in gameweeks 35 and 36. Will there be any more doubles or blanks after gameweek 34? We will know the answer to this almost certainly by the gameweek 32 deadline, giving managers a clear picture of the fixture landscape for the remainder of the season.

Harry argues that preserving transfer flexibility into gameweek 32 is probably the most valuable approach for most managers. Between gameweek 31 and gameweek 32, the FA Cup quarterfinals will reshape the fixture list dramatically, and being able to react to those changes with transfers in the bank could be worth far more than squeezing an extra few points from a one-week punt this week. This is particularly true for managers who have already used their wildcard and need to navigate the remainder of the season with careful transfer management.

Fixture Analysis and One-Week Punts

For those managers who are looking at one-week punts this gameweek, Harry provides a useful analysis of the available fixtures. The standout fixture for targeting is undoubtedly Fulham at home to Burnley. Whether you're looking for a defender, midfielder, forward, or goalkeeper from Fulham, there are plenty of options available, and Fulham's home form makes them an attractive proposition across all positions. Harry notes that Fulham kept a clean sheet in gameweek 30 against Nottingham Forest—actually their first clean sheet of 2026—which shows that even for them, clean sheets aren't guaranteed, but the fixture is nevertheless the most attractive on paper.

Liverpool away at Brighton is another fixture Harry recommends targeting. Despite Liverpool rotating heavily in gameweek 30 against Tottenham, their trip to Brighton should still be a profitable fixture for the title contenders. Chelsea away at Everton is not a great fixture but not a terrible one either, while Newcastle at home to Sunderland represents another good opportunity for those looking to take punts on players with favorable fixtures.

Beyond the obvious highly-owned players that everyone will be considering—such as Ekitike or anyone without a good option—Harry also mentions some nice differential options worth considering. Raul Jimenez, Callum Styles, and even Jarrod Bowen could represent value for managers looking to take a slightly more adventurous approach.

Harry does caution that defensively, this week is quite difficult to predict clean sheets. The Bournemouth versus Manchester United fixture on Friday night is particularly tricky to call—Bournemouth have been excellent defensively this season, but Manchester United have been impressive going forward in recent weeks, making this a difficult fixture to predict. Brentford versus Leeds is similarly unpredictable, while Newcastle at home to Sunderland is probably where managers should be looking to target their main punts.

Long-Term Fixture Considerations

For managers thinking beyond this single gameweek, Harry emphasizes that the fixture landscape looks very different. Bournemouth, for example, are right down at the bottom of the fixture difficulty table over the next six weeks, with matches against Manchester United, Arsenal, and Newcastle making them a team to avoid for long-term investment despite their attractive attacking play.

The key insight here is that managers need to be honest with themselves about their chip strategy. If you're definitely wildcarding in gameweek 32, then by all means, load up on Fulham assets for this week. But if you're keeping your wildcard for later or have already used it, then you should be thinking about players who will be useful not just in gameweek 31 but also in the double gameweeks that will follow the FA Cup quarterfinals.

Harry uses the example of Chelsea and Newcastle as teams with decent fixtures in gameweek 31 that could also be useful further down the line, making them more attractive options for managers not planning an immediate wildcard. This approach allows you to play in the blank gameweek while also building toward the more lucrative double gameweeks that are just around the corner.

Key Strategic Takeaways

The first and most important takeaway from this analysis is that patience is paramount when it comes to gameweek 31 transfers. With the European competitions still in flux and a possibility—however small—that Manchester City and Crystal Palace could have their fixture reinstated, making early transfers could prove costly. Wait for the press conference updates later in the week, particularly regarding Tarkowski's injury status, before committing to any transfers.

The second takeaway is that transfer flexibility going into gameweek 32 is more valuable than maximizing points in gameweek 31 for most managers. The FA Cup quarterfinals will reshape the fixture list dramatically, and being able to react to those changes with transfers in the bank could be the difference between success and failure in the crucial double gameweeks that follow.

The third takeaway concerns the specific fixture targeting for those who do need to make transfers. Fulham at home to Burnley is the best fixture across all positions, with Liverpool away at Brighton and Newcastle at home to Sunderland also offering good opportunities for one-week punts.

The fourth takeaway is about the importance of understanding your own chip strategy. Are you wildcarding in gameweek 32 or later? Have you already used your wildcard? Your answer to these questions should determine whether you focus on one-week punts or longer-term investments this week.

Finally, managers should be cautious about loading up on players from teams with terrible long-term fixtures, even if they look attractive for this specific gameweek. Teams like Bournemouth may have good fixtures this week but terrible fixtures over the next six weeks, making them poor long-term investments unless you plan to wildcard soon.
