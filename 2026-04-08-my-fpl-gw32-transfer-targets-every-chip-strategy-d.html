FPL Gameweek 32 Transfer Targets and Chip Strategy: A Complete Guide

Introduction

Fantasy Premier League managers entering Gameweek 32 face one of the most consequential decisions of the season: when to deploy their wild card chip. With the season winding down and several double gameweeks on the horizon, the strategic use of chips could mean the difference between finishing in the top 10,000 or much deeper in the rankings. In this detailed analysis, FPL Harry walks through two distinct chip strategies—the traditional Wildcard 32, Bench Boost 33, Free Hit 34 pathway against the alternative Free Hit 33, Wildcard 35, Bench Boost 36/37 approach—and evaluates which offers the better expected value.

The video comes at a critical juncture in the FPL calendar. With the international break freshly concluded and the business end of the season approaching, managers need clarity on their transfer plans. Harry's current squad sits with five free transfers and just 0.1 million in the bank, meaning at least one transfer is mandatory this week. The question becomes whether to use this as an opportunity to significantly restructure the team or simply make minimal adjustments while preserving transfers for future gameweeks. This analysis examines both pathways in depth, using the planning platform FPL.team to visualize how each strategy plays out through the remainder of the season.

Understanding the Two Chip Strategies

The first strategy Harry examines involves activating the wild card in Gameweek 32, followed by bench boost in Gameweek 33 and a free hit in Gameweek 34. This represents the more traditional approach that many managers have been planning for throughout the season. The alternative pathway defers the wild card to Gameweek 35, uses a free hit in Gameweek 33 instead, and delays the bench boost until either Gameweek 36 or Gameweek 37.

Harry notes that he currently has five free transfers available and 0.1 million in the bank—a relatively comfortable position that allows flexibility in decision-making. He emphasizes that the wild card decision shouldn't be made in isolation but should consider whether your current team actually needs significant restructuring. Looking at his squad, he sees "luxury changes" he could make, such as upgrading between players like Juju Bhour and Anderson, or improving on Taroski, but doesn't feel he "definitely need[s] a wild card." This is a crucial observation: the mere existence of potential improvements doesn't justify using a limited resource.

The analysis then moves to examining what a potential Gameweek 35 wild card draft might look like. Harry projects a triple-up on Arsenal defense with Fulham at home, plus a triple-up on Crystal Palace for their double in Gameweek 36. He includes players like Calbertt Lewen, Jar Pedro, Gordon, Semeno, Bruno, and Anthony Gordon in this projection. Importantly, he notes that many managers are looking at Wildcard 35 with Bench Boost 36, but his planning suggests the bench boost actually looks "slightly better in 37" when factoring in potential fixtures.

Transfer Options for Gameweek 32

For managers staying with the Wildcard 35 strategy, Harry outlines several specific transfer considerations for Gameweek 32. His primary planned transfer involves moving Echitique out to bring in Jared Burn, citing that "Ekit's minutes either side of the PSG games... is a slight risk" and that Burn has "Wolves at home and he also has Everton at home in gameweek 34"—both attractive fixtures for the two weeks where the wild card would be deployed.

The fixture difficulty analysis places West Ham near the top of the rankings for Gameweeks 32 and 34 combined, making Jared Burn a particularly appealing target. Harry also mentions considering transfers like Richards to Muki, who has "Spots at home and Forest at home in the two fixtures" that would be relevant. Another option he discusses is Burmo to Kuna, noting that while "Brian and Burmo will start for Manchester United at home to Leeds, [but] he could once again be hooked early, get reduced minutes," making Mattz Kuna "probably slightly safer."

The alternative transfer Harry mentions is Echitique to Bowen if Echitique isn't expected to start at home to Fulham. He notes that if Echitique "is going to start, I actually think he's equally good to Bowen," and importantly, "he has Palace at home in the blank in gameweek 34"—meaning Bowen provides coverage for an upcoming blank.

The Free Hit 33 Analysis

Harry provides an early look at what a Gameweek 33 free hit draft would resemble, acknowledging this represents the alternative pathway if deferring the wild card. His initial draft includes Darlow Stra and Cal Leuen in goal, with Antoine Stack appearing as a doubt. He opts for a triple-up on Leeds, reasoning that "their double is slightly better than that of Bournemouth with Leeds at home, Newcastle away versus Bournemouth away, Wolves at home."

For the midfield, Harry selects O'Reilly, Seno, and Harland as a triple-up for Manchester City, with Cole Palmer and Erling Harland as captaincy options. The final midfielder slot remains "quite up in the air," with Pascal Gross currently selected but Ryan and potentially Antoine Stack (if available) as alternatives.

This analysis reveals a critical insight: if you're planning to free hit in Gameweek 33, "you're not getting that much benefit" compared to those who wild carded in Gameweek 32. The starting eleven would be largely similar, with the primary difference being potentially having an extra Leeds player or additional flexibility in case of injuries. Harry astutely observes that some players like Palmer "don't want longterm, but you might want him from the double," while also questioning how long managers might want to hold triple Bournemouth or Brighton assets.

Key Strategic Insights

One of the most valuable observations Harry provides concerns the value of waiting to wild card. He explains that the Wildcard 35 strategy is "definitely better if you don't have lots of transfers available" because it "allows you to sort of cut the remainder of the season" and focus on the crucial double gameweeks. The strategy preserves the wild card for when it can maximum impact—specifically targeting the double gameweeks in 35 and 36.

Harry also demonstrates how his current position with five spare transfers allows him to be more aggressive. He mentions potentially "buying Salorin 34 if I wanted to" or making "bigger upgrades on the likes of Tarovski, Echitik this week" because he has "lots of spare transfers further down the line" if going Wildcard 35. This represents a key strategic advantage: managers with abundant transfers can make immediate improvements while still having chips in reserve.

The analysis also touches on the tricky situation around Manchester United assets. Harry considers "selling Erling Harland" to "buy MoSalah" as a potential move, potentially "downgrading Erling Harland to a forward" like Gimenez "and upgrade Anderson all the way to MoSalah." However, he acknowledges "I probably wouldn't want to use two free transfers, probably would only want to use one of them to take five transfers into gameweek 35."

Final Recommendations and Takeaways

Harry concludes by summarizing his likely approach: "I'll probably still only make one, maybe two transfers in gameweek 32 and continue to carry the rest of them as far forward as possible." This reflects his belief that the current squad is competitive enough without major intervention, and that saving transfers provides more optionality than using them immediately.

For the bench boost consideration, Harry notes that a potential Gameweek 37 play could involve "Darlow at home to Brighton" with potential transfers like "Richards to Harry Maguire" or bringing in "Jbury Hall and Daniels." The flexibility to make such moves in the final weeks depends entirely on preserving transfers through the earlier gameweeks.

The core strategic tension remains: use the wild card now to fix immediate weaknesses but sacrifice the ability to target the later double gameweeks optimally, or preserve the wild card and accept potentially inferior teams in 32-34 with the flexibility to make major changes in 35. Harry's analysis suggests he's leaning toward the latter, but acknowledges "it's really close to the end of the season" and the decision remains genuinely difficult.
