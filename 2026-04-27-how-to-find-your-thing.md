<p># How to Find Your Thing: A Comprehensive Guide to Discovering Work That Matters

---

## Key Insights

- **Joseph Campbell originally said "follow your bliss" but later corrected to "follow your blisters"** — emphasizing that genuine love for something manifests as willingness to suffer for it, not as constant euphoria. Blisters are "receipts" proving sustained effort that couldn't have been forced through willpower alone.

- **"Passion" etymology comes from the Latin "passio," meaning suffering** — as in the Passion of the Christ, the story of crucifixion. The modern concept of passion as pleasurable bliss is historically recent, emerging from the Gilded Age (late 1800s) when leisure became a status symbol for the wealthy. Before that, your trade was inherited from your parents.

- **Paul Graham's framework: let enthusiasm be both the motor AND the rudder of your boat** — it provides fuel to move and direction to navigate. Following this leads you to the frontier of any field, where you'll notice gaps that become business opportunities. The speaker identified the low-testosterone gap, leading to investment in Hone Health, now at 9-figure run rate.

- **Pick sales/growth mechanisms you enjoy, not industries** — because once a company succeeds, roughly 85% of time goes to growth (team building, sales, management), not to the actual product. The industry almost "fades to oblivion" while the growth mechanism is what you live with daily.

- **Enthusiasm chains into mastery chains into passion** — Cal Newport's "passion is a byproduct of mastery" plus the speaker's extension: mastery requires enduring enthusiasm (you practice piano at night when tired), and mastery produces the deep satisfaction that becomes passion.

## Introduction

The conversation opens with a scene at a restaurant in Austin: a 24-year-old fan approaches the hosts, confessing a crisis that millions recognize. "I'm smart. I'm hardworking. I just don't know which lane to go in. I'm ready to swim, but I don't know which lane." He describes the transition from structured education—take these classes, pass these tests, earn these marks—to complete freedom. Teachers and parents told him what to do for two decades. Now they're silent.

His question cuts to the heart of modern career anxiety: everyone says "follow your passion," but how? What would the hosts do if they could go back?

This episode becomes an exploration of why "follow your passion" is actually terrible advice, what to do instead, and how to navigate the fog of uncertainty that characterizes most people's twenties and thirties. Drawing on Joseph Campbell (the man behind the hero's journey), Paul Graham, Mark Manson, and Cal Newport, the conversation dismantles the feel-good mythology around passion and replaces it with a more sustainable, historically grounded framework built on enthusiasm, blisters, and the reality of how startups actually consume your time.

## The Problem with "Follow Your Passion"

The hosts argue that "follow your passion" fails for a fundamental reason: most people don't know what their passion is. One host confesses he's 36, arguably successful, and still doesn't know his passion. He describes perpetually reinventing himself, questioning everything, starting from scratch—and while this has produced "some amazing changes," it's also "a period of great uncertainty." He admits he sometimes envies people who just pick a thing and do it for fifty years.

When you don't know your passion, you default to what you know. You follow the familiar—your parents' path, your friends' trajectory, what you've already done. You mistake familiarity for passion. You ask, "What's wrong with me that I don't feel ignited?" when the truth is that passion isn't a pre-existing beacon; it's something discovered through engagement.

The hosts emphasize that searching feels like being lost, which makes people feel like they're doing something wrong. But searching IS the process. The fog of uncertainty isn't a failure state—it's the diagnostic environment for the problem.

---

## Joseph Campbell's Evolution: Bliss vs. Blisters

The conversation turns to Joseph Campbell, the mythological scholar who discovered the "hero's journey" structure that explains Star Wars, Harry Potter, and countless great stories. Campbell gave many interviews about his philosophy and originally said "follow your bliss." Later, he changed it to "follow your blisters."

Campbell's own journey illustrates the point. As a child and teenager, he was obsessed with indigenous tribes and Native American myths. First as a consumer, then as a curious investigator: "Why do I love these? What's the structure?" That question led to his discovery of the hero's journey. He followed his enthusiasm into unfamiliar territory, faced the dragons, paid the tolls—and arrived at something deeply satisfying.

The hosts interpret "bliss" not as pure joy or instantaneous pleasure, but as enthusiasm: something naturally drawn to you, of ongoing interest, where you feel alive doing it. You lose track of time. You do it in your off-hours, when others would see it as work. These are signals.

But the "blisters" correction matters. Real engagement with your enthusiasm leads you off familiar paths. You leave safety. You face hardships. The blisters on Campbell's hands were evidence of sustained effort—receipts proving price paid. If you find evidence that you've suffered willingly for something, that's a strong signal that you genuinely want it.

The framework: Bliss isn't the destination; it's the direction. Blisters are proof you're on the path.

---

## The Etymology and History of "Passion"

One host offers a historical bombshell: the word "passion" comes from suffering. The Passion of the Christ is the story of crucifixion—pain, endurance, being called to something despite cost.

This etymology reveals something important. Up until recently, "following your passion" didn't mean what it means today. The modern interpretation—follow what makes you happy, find bliss, do what you love—emerged from the Gilded Age (late 1800s), when leisure time first became a status symbol. "A man of leisure" was a phrase invented then. If you were wealthy, you could spend days in pleasure. For everyone else, your trade was assigned by your parents: "My father was this thing, therefore I will become this thing."

The hosts argue people were probably happier then—not because the work was better, but because they weren't plagued by existential questions about whether they were living their purpose.

The concept of weekends, by the way, only emerged in the 1930s. Henry Ford, employing massive workforces, determined through research that weekends off created more loyalty and productivity than six-day weeks. The GI Bill after World War II created a golden era of leisure—returning soldiers went to college, bought houses, enjoyed rising prosperity. This is when "following your passion" became culturally available.

But here's the paradox: we now work harder than ever. The research shows we're working significantly more hours than any previous generation. Yet we simultaneously believe we should find work that feels like passion—effortless, joyful, perfectly aligned. This impossible standard creates misery.

The host cites a book called *Bad Therapy*: people who constantly ask "Why am I not happy? Why am I not following my life's work?" are often significantly more discontent with their lives than people who simply engage with their work.

## Enthusiasm as Motor and Rudder

Paul Graham (Y Combinator founder) wrote an essay called "How to Do Great Work" that takes two days to read. One phrase the host remembers: "Let enthusiasm be not just the motor, but the rudder of your boat."

Enthusiasm provides fuel—the engine that moves you forward. But it also guides direction. When you're enthusiastic about something, you naturally go deeper. You learn more. You notice gaps others miss.

The hosts use the example of fitness. One started with roughly 50th-percentile knowledge about health and fitness. But enthusiasm drove him to train for the MRF (Muscle Failure Research), doing pull-ups until his hands developed blisters. "Why do you need these abs? You're married. You're already healthy." The interest was irrational, but something pulled him forward. He went to the frontier of fitness—measuring body fat, injecting compounds, doing NFL combine drills as a civilian, experimenting with best practices and new methods.

At that frontier, he noticed a gap: men with low testosterone wanting to take exogenous testosterone. "There was no trusted brand for doing this." He and his partner invested in Hone Health, which now operates at over 9-figure run rate.

The same pattern occurred with Ozempic. Before it was branded, the host was taking semaglutide and recognized the opportunity—but didn't act on it.

The lesson: go to the frontier, and you'll see gaps. The gaps are where opportunities live.

---

## The Chain: Enthusiasm → Mastery → Passion

Cal Newport, author of *Deep Work*, argued that passion is a byproduct of mastery. The hosts extend this: mastery comes from enduring enthusiasm.

Consider the piano example: the host had a lesson in the morning. At night, tired, walking to bed, he passed the piano room and thought, "Let me get in and play this sea shanty." Half-asleep, he practiced a pirate song. He got one step closer to mastery.

The only way to accumulate the 10,000 hours required for mastery is through enduring enthusiasm—showing up when you're tired, when there's no immediate reward, because you genuinely want to. That's different from motivation or willpower. That's sustained engagement.

The chain becomes clear: Enduring enthusiasm leads to sustained practice, which leads to mastery, which produces deep satisfaction, which feels like passion. Passion isn't the starting point—it's the destination you reach by following enthusiasm through difficulty.

## The Critical Insight: Pick Your Sales Motion, Not Your Industry

Here's where the conversation gets tactical. How do you actually apply this framework?

The host realized he used to pick projects based on industry—"I'm gonna do healthcare because I'm passionate about healthcare" or "Let's do fashion, that sounds fun." He picked industries or products that seemed enjoyable.

What he learned: the actual product almost fades to oblivion. In any successful company, you spend very little time on the product itself. The majority goes to people—hiring, managing, organizing, leading. And beyond that, most time goes to growth.

The host estimates that in successful companies—Hampton Creek, The Hustle, whatever he's built—less than 15% of time is spent on the actual product. More than 85% goes to growth mechanisms: team building, sales, management, running ads, sending emails, optimizing SEO, closing enterprise deals.

So his reframe: Don't pick an industry you love. Pick a sales or growth mechanism you love.

If it's enterprise sales, most of your time will be hiring, managing, and doing enterprise sales—taking people to dinner, promising to circle back, wearing sport coats in regional VP offices. If it's e-commerce, you live behind a laptop running ads and sending emails. If it's SEO, you're playing the SEO game constantly.

"Do you wanna work with spreadsheets? Or do you wanna work with guys that wear bright brown shoes and sports jackets and call themselves the regional VP of the Southeast Region?"

The growth mechanism is the constraint. The industry is almost irrelevant. You can apply any growth mechanism to any niche.

## Who This Is For

This content is essential for anyone in their 20s or 30s navigating the gap between structured education and unstructured adult life—who feels "ready to swim but doesn't know which lane." It's for people who have been told to follow their passion but find that advice hollow and paralyzing.

It's also for people who feel stuck in careers they chose based on external prestige (banking, consulting) but don't find fulfilling. And for anyone questioning their path, wondering if they're supposed to feel more certain than they do.

The value: a framework that replaces vague inspiration with actionable logic. You don't need to find your passion—you need to find what you're enthusiastic about, notice what you're willing to suffer for (blisters as receipts), and follow that enthusiasm to the frontier of a field, where gaps become opportunities. Pick the growth mechanism you can sustain, and let the industry take care of itself.

The hosts admit uncertainty is unavoidable—it's not a bug but the feature. You don't need to know the destination. You just need to follow what excites you through the difficulty, notice where it leads, and have the courage to stay on the path.</p>
