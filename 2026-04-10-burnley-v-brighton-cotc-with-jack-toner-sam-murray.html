Burnley v Brighton | CotC with Jack Toner & Sam Murray | Planet FPL 2025/26

Introduction

The latest episode of Planet FPL's Clash of the Correspondents provides a comprehensive look ahead to a double gameweek involving two clubs at opposite ends of the Premier League spectrum. Host James previews the upcoming double by bringing together Jack Toner, the Burnley correspondent, and Sam Murray, the Brighton and Hove Albion correspondent, to dissect the fortunes of their respective clubs and what FPL managers should be considering ahead of the double.

The timing of this episode is particularly relevant as only six teams are doubling in the upcoming gameweek, making every asset choice critical for managers navigating their squads. Burnley, currently languishing near the bottom of the table, face a daunting double against Manchester City and Nottingham Forest, while Brighton have emerged from a mid-season crisis to win four of their last five matches, positioning themselves as the more attractive FPL option despite not doubling in this particular gameweek.

The correspondents also touch on the broader managerial landscape, comparing Scott Parker's struggles at Burnley with Vincent Kompany's surprising success at Bayern Munich, while also examining Brighton's remarkable turnaround under their current manager and what it means for the club's immediate future.

Burnley's Double Gameweek Dilemma

Jack Toner opens the discussion by acknowledging that Leeds United's victory on Sunday essentially killed much of the enthusiasm around Burnley assets for this double gameweek. The correspondents explore why Burnley has become something of a forgotten asset in the FPL landscape, with Jack noting that having a double featuring Manchester City "just feels like it's not really a double, is it?" The fixture against the Premier League champions represents perhaps the toughest assignment in English football, making any Burnley player an extremely risky pick regardless of their price point.

The first match of the double comes against Nottingham Forest at the City Ground, a fixture Jack describes as one his team will be "targeting a win in." However, he immediately tempers expectations by acknowledging that Forest will likely want the victory just as much, if not more. Jack reflects that "we're all a bit down, aren't we?" at Burnley right now, suggesting the psychological burden of a poor season may play into Forest's hands. The match could represent an opportunity for Burnley to salvage some pride, but the underlying performance levels suggest this is far from a guaranteed outcome for either side.

When pressed on whether he would recommend any Burnley defenders, Jack isemphatic: "I wouldn't really want to touch our defense. It's chalk and cheese from what we were like defensively last year." This is a significant statement from the Burnley correspondent, indicating that while the team may have been more defensive under previous regimes, the current squad has fundamental defensive issues that make their defenders unappealing even in favorable matchups.

The State of Burnley Football Club

The conversation shifts to the broader mood at Turf Moor, with Jack providing a candid assessment of the club's current situation. He describes the recent weeks as "grim" and admits his typically more positive outlook has been "waning" in the face of consistent poor results. The correspondents discuss a drawn match against Bournemouth and a lead thrown away at Fulham, patterns that have defined Burnley's season and left fans searching for answers.

Jack articulates the frustration facing Burnley supporters: "It just feels like next year is going to be a real challenge for us again." With only three teams able to gain promotion from the Championship and several well-funded clubs likely to compete for those spots, Burnley faces an uphill battle to return to the Premier League even if they are relegated. The club's strategy of major squad overhaul each season, with numerous incomings and outgoings, creates instability that compounds the challenge.

The discussion takes a humorous turn when Jack notes that Burnley are "only four points off making the Vincent Kompany total of the two seasons ago," a reference to the current Bayern Munich manager's tenure at Burnley. This sets up a deeper comparison between the two managers that reveals much about Burnley's current predicament and the broader questions facing the club.

The Kompany Comparison

The correspondents spend considerable time discussing Vincent Kompany's remarkable transformation at Bayern Munich, with the episode pre-recorded on Wednesday evening shortly after Bayern's impressive performance. Jack acknowledges that when the two first discussed Burnley before the season started, he was genuinely positive because "that season in the Championship under Kompany was the best season I've ever watched us."

Jack traces the arc of his thinking: under Kompany, Burnley played attacking football that "just suffocated teams" and was a joy to watch, scoring numerous goals in a style that made them "the big fish in a small pond in the Championship." When Bayern came calling, Jack felt that given the right tools and patience, Kompany could replicate that success at a top club.

The contrast with Scott Parker's approach is stark. Jack explains that while Kompany's style didn't translate to the Premier League tools available to him, "he obviously didn't want to change his style. He was going to play his way or no way." This stubbornness, which cost Kompany his job at Burnley, has proven exactly what Bayern Munich needed, with the German giants playing some of the most attacking football in Europe under his guidance.

The comparison leads naturally to questions about Parker's future and what Burnley fans want from their next manager.

Scott Parker's Future

Jack provides nuanced analysis of the managerial situation at Burnley, acknowledging that while he personally would "be inclined to give Parker a chance" given his record of "three promotions out of three chances in the Championship," he recognizes that most fans have already made up their minds. He observes that "the board would want to get some of the feel-good factor back in" after a difficult season.

The key concern among Burnley supporters, as Jack explains it, is that "let's say we managed to come back up and we stick with Parker, they just feel like we'd be at the same crossroads again in two years' time." This reflects a desire to "roll the dice" and see what a new manager might achieve, rather than continuing with a system that appears to have reached its ceiling.

Jack is uncertain whether Parker will actually depart, noting "I don't think he— I'm not necessarily sure he will go. I think he's probably at the point where I think he should." His more significant concern is that if the club waits too long to make a change and "start next season and it doesn't start well, then we've already wasted a window."

The discussion reveals a club at a crossroads, uncertain whether to stick with a manager who has proven credentials in the Championship or risk a change that could either revive their fortunes or create further instability. What is clear is that the fans want change, and that sentiment likely "tells you the decision that needs to be made."

Brighton's Remarkable Resurgence

Sam Murray provides a dramatically different perspective on his club's situation. When asked about Brighton's recent form—four wins in five matches with the only defeat coming against the league leaders—Sam describes it as "typical Brighton, really." This characteristic inconsistency has defined their season: "One win in 13 and then four wins in five."

The turning point, according to Sam, was the humiliating defeat to Crystal Palace. He admits that even as someone who "kept the faith" and was "pretty patient" with the manager, the Palace performance was so bad that "I don't think you could have had many complaints if he was sacked." The problem wasn't just results, but the fundamental lack of attacking intent: "We never looked like scoring. There was no intent there at all. A lot of the fans had turned."

What followed was a tactical reset that Sam describes as "going back to basics a little bit, bit of stability, bit of experience with Milner in there, Welbeck, and sort of picking a team that he knew could dig in and defend." The approach has delivered results even if it hasn't always been pretty: "You've had to grind out a few wins, Brentford and Sunderland a few weeks ago, but we look a bit more solid defensively without losing too much fluidity going forward."

Sam highlights the performance against Liverpool before the international break as "our best this season, to be honest," praising "the combinations of the front four" and noting that "we could have scored more" than the single goal they managed in a narrow defeat.

Key Takeaways for FPL Managers

First, Burnley's double gameweek against Manchester City and Forest is essentially a non-starter for FPL purposes. Even the most budget-conscious managers should look elsewhere for their doubles, as City's attacking threat makes any Burnley defender a liability while Forest away represents a difficult fixture regardless of Burnley's position in the table.

Second, Brighton's form reversal demonstrates the value of patience with managerial situations, but also shows how quickly things can change in the Premier League. Managers who stuck with Brighton assets through their 13-game winless run would have been rewarded handsomely over the past five matches, though the timing of this turnaround means it doesn't coincide with a double gameweek.

Third, the Burnley situation extends far beyond this season's results. With major squad overhaul every season and a psychological mood described as "grim," the club faces significant challenges regardless of whether they stay up or return to the Championship. FPL managers should not expect Burnley to be a reliable source of assets even in future seasons.

Fourth, the Kompany-Parker comparison offers broader lessons about managerial philosophy. Kompany's refusal to compromise his principles eventually led to success at Bayern, while Parker's more pragmatic approach has produced poor results at the Premier League level. This doesn't necessarily mean one approach is better, but it illustrates the different risks managers face when sticking to their philosophy versus adapting to circumstances.

Fifth, Brighton's "grind out wins" approach has made them defensively more solid without sacrificing attacking fluidity, a balance that may make their players more consistently valuable in FPL terms going forward, even if the ceiling isn't as high as under more expansive systems.

Who This Episode Is For

This Planet FPL Clash of the Correspondents episode is essential listening for FPL managers navigating the double gameweek, particularly those looking to maximize their bench value with budget options from doubling teams. The detailed analysis of Burnley's fixture situation and the candid assessment of their defensive issues could save managers from making expensive mistakes.

Beyond immediate FPL considerations, the episode provides valuable context for anyone interested in the broader managerial landscape of English football. The comparison between Kompany and Parker, while humorous in parts, raises serious questions about how clubs should approach hiring and firing decisions, and what patience looks like in practice.

Finally, for fans of either club, the correspondents offer inside perspectives that go beyond standard match analysis, exploring the psychological and emotional dimensions of supporting teams in challenging circumstances. Whether you're a Burnley fan hoping for change or a Brighton fan basking in recent success, this episode provides the context to understand where your club stands and what the future may hold.
