FPL Gameweek 33 Strategy: Bench Boost Deep Dive and Transfer Planning with FP Harry

Introduction

The Fantasy Premier League season is reaching its critical phase, and for dedicated managers, the chips are down—literally. In this detailed video, FP Harry walks viewers through his Gameweek 33 team selection strategy, revealing a complex web of decisions centered around his active Bench Boost chip, five available transfers, and several injury doubts that could dramatically reshape his squad. With the double gameweek presenting both opportunity and risk, Harry's approach offers a masterclass in FPL chip management and transfer planning during the business end of the season.

Harry begins by outlining his immediate concerns: three of his players are facing difficult away fixtures that could significantly limit their points potential. Gabriel travels to Manchester City with Arsenal, Bruno Fernandes heads to Stamford Bridge with Manchester United, and perhaps most concerning, young starlet O'Reilly remains a doubt for Manchester City's fixtures. These uncertainties come at a crucial moment when Harry needs to maximize his Bench Boost activation, which theoretically requires a full squad of playing assets. The strategic depth required here—balancing short-term point accumulation against long-term squad sustainability—demonstrates why FPL management at this level requires careful deliberation rather than reactive transfers.

What makes this video particularly valuable is Harry's transparency about his recent decision-making process, including a detailed breakdown of why he chose to play his Wild Card in Gameweek 32 despite initial hesitation. By sharing his thought process, the outcomes he experienced, and his reasoning for upcoming moves, Harry provides viewers with a framework they can adapt to their own squads and strategic approaches.

The Wild Card Reconsideration: A Calculated Risk

The Decision That Almost Wasn't Made

Harry reveals that his decision to play the Wild Card in Gameweek 32 was not set in stone until Friday afternoon. The original plan had been to save the chip for later in the season, but a confluence of factors prompted a last-minute change of heart. The primary driver was his possession of five free transfers going into the gameweek—a luxury that most managers wouldn't have had after playing their Wild Card earlier in the season. Harry recognized that by wild-carding in GW32, he could strategically target the upcoming double gameweeks in GW33 and GW34, then preserve his remaining transfers for the run-in when other managers who had already played their Wild Cards would be limited to fewer available moves.

The logic here is rooted in game theory and resource allocation. By front-loading the Wild Card to capture points during the double gameweeks, Harry sacrifices any future Wild Card flexibility but gains an advantage in the immediate high-scoring opportunities. Meanwhile, managers who had used their Wild Cards earlier would enter the final weeks of the season with depleted transfer resources and less ability to react to form changes or injuries. Harry calculated that this asymmetry would more than compensate for the points he might sacrifice by not having a fresh Wild Card later.

The Outcome: Six Points Lost, But Strategically Worth It

The execution of the Wild Card didn't go perfectly according to plan. Harry had initially intended to bring in Jarrod Bowen, but the timing of his decision meant he missed out on the West Ham man's 14-pointer, which came on Friday evening. Further disappointment followed when Thiago scored 13 points and Van Dijk delivered a nine-pointer on Saturday—all assets Harry had transferred out. By the time Manchester City faced Chelsea on Monday night, it appeared Harry had potentially surrendered 20 to 30 points relative to his non-wildcard team.

However, fortune intervened in unexpected ways. Burn and Burnham's combined XI didn't perform as expected on Monday night, and crucially, O'Reilly delivered a 14-point haul that salvaged what could have been a catastrophic week. By the end of the gameweek, Harry had lost just six points compared to what his pre-wildcard team would have scored—a remarkably narrow margin given the apparent disaster on Saturday. The final tally was 55 points, with Villa's nine-pointer and O'Reilly's 14-pointer providing the necessary rescue act.

In terms of rank movement, Harry dropped from 36,000 to approximately 40,000, representing a small red arrow that he characterizes as "definitely not the end of the world." The key insight here is that losing six points on a Wild Card week, while not ideal, represents a reasonable cost when the alternative is entering the double gameweeks with a sub-optimal squad. Harry emphasizes that he would have bought Bowen anyway for the injured Ekit, who didn't even start for Liverpool, further validating that the Wild Card execution, while imperfect, didn't materially damage his season trajectory.

The Bench Boost Blueprint: Maximizing the Double Gameweek

Why This Week Demands Full Attention

With the Bench Boost chip active in Gameweek 33, Harry faces his most consequential team selection of the season. The double gameweek presents an opportunity to accumulate significant points by loading his bench with players who have two matches scheduled. However, the injury doubts complicate this strategy considerably. Gabriel at Manchester City, Bruno Fernandes at Chelsea, and the uncertain O'Reilly represent a concerning cluster of potential non-returns.

Harry's approach prioritizes understanding which players are likely to return points despite difficult fixtures, versus which ones might be better served by being benched in favor of alternatives with more favorable matchups. The five free transfers he possesses give him unusual flexibility to reshuffle his squad without taking hits, but he must still be strategic about which changes are worth making versus which ones create more problems than they solve.

The Long-Term Chip Strategy: A Three-Gameweek Arc

Harry's planning extends well beyond this single gameweek. His broader strategy involves sequencing his remaining chips—Bench Boost, Free Hit, and potentially others—in a way that maximizes points across the crucial run-in period. Specifically, he plans to use Bench Boost in GW33, Free Hit in GW34 (which is expected to be a blank gameweek with limited options), and then reassess his position for GW35 and beyond.

This sequencing requires careful squad construction. Harry must ensure that his bench for GW33 is stacked with players who have double fixtures, while simultaneously maintaining enough quality in his starting XI to score competitive points without taking unnecessary hits on transfers. The complexity of managing multiple chips across consecutive gameweeks while maintaining a coherent squad structure represents one of the most challenging aspects of advanced FPL management.

Transfer Strategy Deep Dive: Gabriel and the Leeds Defender Dilemma

The Case for Selling Gabriel

Harry identifies his first potential transfer as selling Gabriel to acquire a Leeds United defender. This is not a move he would recommend with fewer than four or five transfers available, but the abundance of resources changes the calculus considerably. The reasoning is straightforward: Gabriel faces a difficult away fixture at Manchester City, where Arsenal's defense is unlikely to keep a clean sheet, and Gabriel himself may struggle for attacking returns against one of the league's strongest home sides.

By selling Gabriel and buying a Leeds defender, Harry aims to exploit the double gameweek structure. Leeds are expected to have favorable fixtures in GW33, and their defenders have demonstrated the ability to accumulate points through clean sheets, assists, and defensive bonuses. Even if the Leeds defender only outscores Gabriel by four or five points across the two matches, the transfer would be worthwhile given Harry's available resources.

Evaluating Leeds Defender Options: Pascal Stra vs. Bijl

The decision between Pascal Stra and Bijl (possibly referring to Wijnaldum or another Leeds defender) represents one of the more nuanced selection decisions in Harry's analysis. He presents several factors that influence this choice:

Defensive Clean Sheet Potential: The Leeds defenders need to accumulate defensive clean sheets across their double fixtures to justify the transfer. If both Stra and Bijl would be expected to deliver similar defensive returns, the decision shifts to other factors.

Rotation Risk: Harry raises a critical concern about potential rotation for Leeds in GW35. If Leeds win their home match against Wolves and Tottenham lose to Brighton, Leeds would move nine points clear of the relegation zone with an FA Cup semi-final against Chelsea scheduled for the following Sunday. This scenario might prompt Leeds manager to rotate his squad for the away fixture at Bournemouth, potentially resting center-backs and reducing the reliability of those defenders for managers who have invested in them.

Rodon's Return: The injury status of Leeds defender Cooper is a key variable. Pascal Stra currently starts ahead of Rodon, but if Rodon returns from injury in GW35, Bijl might lose his starting spot entirely. Stra, by contrast, appears to be a more established starter who would maintain his position even with Rodon's return.

If this were to occur, Stra's value would increase substantially, making him the clear choice over Bijl.

Harry ultimately leans toward Pascal Stra as the safer long-term option, valuing his apparent nailedness and the potential penalty upside over Bijl's slightly better defensive numbers this season.

The O'Reilly Question: To Sell or Not to Sell?

Understanding the Stakes

The situation with O'Reilly represents the most complex decision Harry faces heading into GW33. The Manchester City player is currently a doubt for the upcoming fixtures, and without a confirmed update from manager Pep Guardiola's Friday press conference, Harry must decide whether to retain him in his squad or sell him to a more certain option.

Harry's assessment is unequivocal: O'Reilly is "by far probably the second-best Manchester City player to own at the moment" and might even represent better value than star striker Erling Haaland. The reasoning extends beyond simple goal involvement. O'Reilly has established himself as a key component of City's attacking structure, ranking higher than both Bernardo Silva and İlkay Gündoğan in the team's midfield hierarchy. His combination of consistent starting appearances, goal involvement, and underlying metrics positions him as one of the most valuable defenders in the game.

The Historical Parallel: Ederson vs. Stones

To illustrate the risk of selling O'Reilly prematurely, Harry draws a parallel to a scenario from a few seasons ago when FPL managers faced a similar decision about Manchester City defenders. At that time, many managers opted for Ederson (the goalkeeper) over Rúben Dias or Kyle Walker, based on assumptions about nailedness and clean sheet potential. The decision backfired spectacularly when Stones scored multiple goals in the final matches, swinging the points differential significantly in favor of those who had kept him.

Harry sees a similar potential swing between O'Reilly and alternatives like Matheus Nunes or Márques. If O'Reilly returns to fitness and delivers strong returns across City's remaining fixtures—including a potential second double gameweek against Crystal Palace in GW36—managers who sold him would miss substantial value. Conversely, if Harry keeps O'Reilly and he remains injured, the opportunity cost of holding an unavailable player through the Bench Boost could be significant.

The Decision Framework

Harry outlines his approach: if O'Reilly is pictured in training or receives positive updates from the Friday press conference, he will almost certainly retain him despite the doubt. The risk-reward calculation favors keeping a potentially injured but high-upside asset over swapping him for a healthier but lower-ceiling defender. Harry specifically mentions that selling O'Reilly to a City defender he considers inferior, only to repurchase O'Reilly in GW35 or later, doesn't make strategic sense given the transfer costs involved.

If, however, O'Reilly's injury appears more serious or his return is significantly delayed, Harry would be forced to sell him as part of the Bench Boost strategy. The alternatives he considers—Nunes and Márques—are close in terms of overall quality, though Márques's set-piece threat from his Crystal Palace days gives him slight appeal, while Nunes offers better underlying baseline bonus points once City's attacking structure is considered.

Beyond GW33: Future Transfer Considerations

The Gabriel Repurchase Plan

One of the key elements of Harry's long-term strategy is the planned repurchase of Gabriel after the Bench Boost chip is deployed. This creates a two-transfer cost for what is effectively a temporary switch: selling Gabriel this week and buying him back next week. With five transfers available, Harry can absorb this cost without taking a hit, but he acknowledges that with fewer resources, this approach wouldn't be viable.

The logic depends on Gabriel's performance over the double gameweek. If Arsenal keep clean sheets or Gabriel contributes to attacking returns, the temporary switch to a Leeds defender should net positive points. If Gabriel performs poorly, the transfer still provides a buffer against the worst-case scenario while maintaining long-term squad flexibility.

Key Insights and Strategic Takeaways

Insight 1: Chip Sequencing Creates Lasting Advantages

Harry's decision to Wild Card in GW32 rather than saving it reveals a fundamental principle of advanced FPL strategy: the timing of chip usage affects not just immediate point accumulation but long-term competitive positioning. By using his Wild Card to capture double gameweek points, Harry accepted a small immediate loss (six points) in exchange for maintaining transfer flexibility during the crucial run-in. Managers who used their Wild Cards earlier would enter the final weeks with fewer resources, potentially creating a compounding disadvantage as the season concludes.

Insight 2: Resource Abundance Enables Aggressive Transfers

The availability of five free transfers fundamentally changes Harry's transfer calculus. Moves like selling Gabriel to a Leeds defender, which would represent poor value with limited transfers, become reasonable when the cost can be absorbed without hits. This highlights an important FPL principle: the strategic value of a transfer isn't fixed but depends on the resources available to execute it.

Insight 3: Injury Doubt Management Requires Probability Weighting

Harry's approach to O'Reilly's injury doubt demonstrates sophisticated risk management. Rather than treating the injury as binary (play or don't play), he weights the probability of various outcomes and their respective point impacts. Keeping an injured player might cost bench points during the double gameweek, but selling him risks missing a significant positive swing if he returns to form. The expected value calculation favors keeping him in most scenarios.

Insight 4: Long-Term Squad Construction Over Short-Term Gains

Throughout the analysis, Harry consistently prioritizes squad sustainability over maximizing any single gameweek. His preference for Pascal Stra over Bijl stems partly from Stra's apparent nailedness even when Rodon returns. His decision to keep O'Reilly rather than cycle through alternatives reflects confidence in the long-term value of holding premium assets. This approach sacrifices some flexibility but reduces the risk of being forced into suboptimal transfers later in the season.

Insight 5: Context Matters More Than Raw Numbers

The video emphasizes repeatedly that FPL decisions cannot be made based solely on points projections or form tables. Harry's analysis considers fixture difficulty, rotation risk, historical parallels, manager tendencies, and competitive context. The decision about Leeds defenders, for example, depends not just on their defensive numbers but on whether Leeds will prioritize the league or rest players for the FA Cup. This contextual awareness separates successful long-term FPL management from reactive, number-chasing approaches.
