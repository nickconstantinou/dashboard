The Art of Deep Listening: What FBI Hostage Negotiators Teach Us About Connection

Introduction: The Skill Nobody Taught Us

Christopher Voss spent twenty-four years as an FBI hostage negotiator, navigating some of the most high-stakes conversations imaginable. But in his telling, the most transformative insight from his career wasn't about coercion, leverage, or psychological manipulation—it was about listening. Deep, genuine, strategically patient listening.

"Negotiation is this skill we all need that I feel like I personally never got taught," Voss admits in a conversation that blends practical wisdom with hard-won experience. "I never. It's all self-taught. Anything you learn." This framing sets the stage for what follows: a candid exploration of how often we navigate life without recognizing we're negotiating, and how profoundly our inability to truly listen undermines our relationships, our deals, and our influence.

The context matters here. Voss isn't speaking from the detached perspective of an academic theorist. He's a practitioner who learned his craft in situations where the stakes were absolute—where failed communication meant lives lost. The principles he shares emerge from that crucible, and their applicability to everyday conversations about salaries, car purchases, and wedding receptions reveals something important about human communication: the dynamics of high-stakes negotiation aren't fundamentally different from the dynamics of ordinary life. We're always negotiating, whether we recognize it or not. With investors and employees, with customers and spouses, with the stranger who cut us off in traffic and the colleague who keeps interrupting our meetings. Understanding this universality is the first step toward becoming better at it.

---

The Five Steps to Becoming a Better Listener

Voss begins with what sounds like a straightforward framework: there are five or six steps to becoming a better listener. But he immediately pivots to the critical insight—the real challenge comes at step two. He calls it the "hijack point."

Most people, when they hear this framework, assume they're already past the basics. They consider themselves listeners. They don't interrupt (much). They're paying attention. But Voss's experience with hostage negotiators, and his observation of human behavior more broadly, reveals a more nuanced reality. The hijack point is where listening stops being about the other person and becomes about preparing your response. Your attention shifts from understanding to rebutting, from absorbing to strategizing. You're technically present, but your mind has already moved on to what you're going to say next.

"The real challenge comes at step two," Voss explains. "It's a hijack point if you're listening at all as opposed to completely focusing on your internal dialogue where you're waiting to speak." This is the threshold most people never cross. They believe they're listening while actually rehearsing, formulating objections, or simply enduring the other person's words until it's their turn to talk.

---

The Hijack to Correct: Why We Can't Resist Being Right

The first form this hijacking takes is what Voss calls "hijack to correct." It manifests as that irresistible urge to jump into a conversation and set the record straight. Someone misspeaks about a date, gets a historical fact wrong, or makes an assumption that you know is inaccurate—and your hand twitches toward the interrupt button.

"The urge to correct is insanely impossible to overcome," Voss says, speaking from extensive observation of human behavior under stress. "I mean, like it's almost impossible to resist." He's not speculating here. This observation has operational implications. In fact, he and his colleagues weaponize this tendency deliberately: "We actually use it as one of our negotiation skills. I'll say something wrong on purpose because you will not be able to resist the urge to correct me with the truth."

This technique accomplishes something remarkable. It extracts hidden information from people without them even realizing they've given it away. They feel satisfaction in correcting you—they feel smart, engaged, and heard in that moment—and that positive feeling masks the fact that they've revealed something they probably shouldn't have. "We don't remember what was said, we remember how we felt in the moment," Voss notes. "The urge to correct is so satisfying that you'll never regret telling me something you shouldn't have told me because it felt so good in the moment."

The implications for everyday conversation are significant. How often have you revealed information in the heat of a correcting moment? How often have you let slip an assumption, a concern, or a constraint because someone misspoke and you couldn't resist the urge to demonstrate your superior knowledge? The hijack to correct is a vulnerability we all share, and recognizing it is the first step toward protecting ourselves—and exploiting it—during negotiations.

---

The Hijack to Relate: When Empathy Becomes Selfishness

The second form of hijacking is subtler but arguably more damaging to relationships. Voss calls it "hijack to relate," and it operates through what he terms "the fallacy of common ground."

Here's how it works. Someone shares a story or describes an experience that triggers an incredible memory in you. Suddenly, you can't wait to tell your own story—the time something similar happened, the parallel experience you had, the way you can completely relate because of your own history. "Oh my god, the same thing happened to me," you interject, and in that moment, you feel connected. You feel like you're building rapport, demonstrating empathy, showing the other person they're not alone.

But Voss offers a different perspective on what's actually happening: "You'll feel incredibly good in that moment, not realizing how squashed I feel." The person sharing their experience—your friend, your employee, your partner—isn't looking for a comparison. They're looking for acknowledgment. And when you hijack the conversation to share your own story, you're inadvertently signaling that your experience was more significant, more worth hearing, more deserving of airtime than theirs.

Voss is unsparing in his characterization of this dynamic. "You can't imagine that what I feel is like, how you had to have a better story than me. You had to one up me. You had to do better than me. And you can't imagine that that's the case because the memory it triggered in you was so good." This is what makes it "story stealing"—the theft happens because of how good sharing your story feels, which blinds you to the other person's experience of being overshadowed.

The challenge here is significant because the hijack to relate often masquerades as empathy. You believe you're demonstrating connection. You believe you're showing the other person they can relate to you, that you share common ground. But genuine empathy requires something different: it requires making the other person's experience about them, not about you.

The Gap Between Hearing and Feeling Heard

This distinction sits at the heart of Voss's framework: just because you hear someone doesn't mean they feel heard. Just because you understand doesn't mean they feel understood. This is perhaps the most important—and most counterintuitive—insight in his entire approach.

"Making somebody feel heard is one of the big challenges, one of probably the single biggest challenges of becoming a better negotiator," Voss explains, "because you got to listen somebody all the way through." The listening must be complete. It must be generous. And it must be other-focused in a way that most conversation rarely achieves.

To illustrate this principle, Voss shares a personal story from a wedding in Ireland. It was the night after the ceremony, and he found himself in his first substantive conversation with the bride. She was exhausted—"unconscious on her feet"—making the required rounds at the reception dinner. The bride and groom are obligated to speak to everyone, to make guests feel valued and thanked for attending. And Voss recognized that her mind was elsewhere, that she was performing a social function rather than engaging in genuine conversation.

"So what did you do?" he asks rhetorically. "I'm telling her what she went through that day. I'm telling her what she went through. I said, you know, this is probably easier to run a small country in South America than it was to pull off this wedding." He continued in this vein, laying out an understanding of everything she'd experienced—the stress of planning, the coordination, the weight of uniting two families, even the slightly obligatory presence of her husband who "got to be there." He wasn't talking about himself. He wasn't sharing his own stories. He was reflecting back to her an understanding of her own experience.

The result was profound. The next night at dinner, the bride approached Voss and gave him a big hug. Her words captured the essence of what he had accomplished: "I have no memory of what you said to me last night. I just remember how good it made me feel."

This is the power Voss is trying to convey. She couldn't remember his words—she was too exhausted for that—but she remembered the feeling. She felt bonded to him because he had thoroughly understood her. "She literally couldn't remember the words, but she felt bonded to me because I thoroughly understood her." The goal isn't to be remembered for what you said. The goal is to create a felt sense of being understood, and that felt sense is what builds trust, deepens relationships, and enables successful negotiation.

The Collaborative Approach: Why Cutting throats Gets You Fired

The conversation shifts to a question that lurks beneath the surface of every negotiation discussion: what's the point? Is negotiation about winning? About getting the best deal? About dominating the other side?

Voss's answer is unequivocal. "Negotiation, if your objective is to have a long-term relationship of trust where we both prosper—now, if you're a complete sociopath, that should be your objective because it's low maintenance and it's the least amount of effort and it's the maximum amount of money."

But he continues, noting that even mercenary-minded people should consider the collaborative approach. One of his top-level coaching clients put it this way: "I have made so much more money being collaborative than I ever made being cutthroat." The reason is straightforward: collaborative relationships flow more smoothly, deals happen faster, and you don't lose collaborators, colleagues, and friends in the process.

"Power negotiations, if you will, that puts you out of business," Voss warns. "You lose friends, you lose collaborators, you lose colleagues." The person who dominates every negotiation, who gets their way through intimidation or positional pressure, may win individual battles. But they're building a reputation that precedes them into every future interaction. People avoid those negotiators. They sandbag those negotiators. They prepare for battle every time they enter a room with them.

The alternative—a collaborative approach that prioritizes mutual benefit—isn't just ethically superior. It's strategically superior. It creates relationships that generate ongoing value, referrals that compound over time, and a reputation that opens doors rather than closing them.

Tactical Empathy: The Hostage Negotiator's Secret Weapon

The conversation reaches its most dramatic point when the discussion turns to hostage negotiations specifically. How does someone build a relationship with a person they've never met, who may be in the middle of committing a crime, who might be experiencing mental health crisis, anger, fear, or all of the above?

Voss begins by correcting a premature assumption: "Let's not put them in the off-the-rocker category." This matters because the worst thing a negotiator can do is dehumanize the person on the other end of the line. Everyone has reasons for what they're doing. Understanding those reasons—without endorsing them—is the foundation of effective intervention.

He then introduces the concept of "tactical empathy," which he distinguishes from ordinary empathy. Regular empathy is about understanding how someone feels. Tactical empathy is about demonstrating that understanding in a way that makes the other person feel heard and draws them into sharing more. It removes you as a threat.

"First move in a negotiation is to remove yourself as a threat," Voss explains, "because why would they make a great deal with you if you're a threat? Why would they—they might make a deal with you if you're a threat, but they're not going to make a great deal with you."

The technique involves a specific approach: a non-threatening voice, what Voss calls the "calming, soothing, late-night FM DJ voice," combined with expressing genuine concern for the other person's wellbeing—including, remarkably, the "bad guy." The messaging is explicit: "I'm here to get everybody out. That includes you, Mr. Bad Guy. Now, whether or not you let me get you out is going to be up to you, but that's my objective first of all, and I need to find out whether or not you're good with that."

This approach is counterintuitive in the extreme. Most people assume that hostage negotiators rely on leverage, pressure, and threats. But Voss's account reveals something different: the power comes from empathy, from genuine concern, from removing the threat perception so that the person on the other end can think clearly about their options. When someone feels threatened, they retreat into their lizard brain—they fight, flee, or freeze. They're not capable of the rational decision-making that resolution requires. So the first job of the negotiator is to create safety, to signal non-threat, to express care for everyone involved, even the person causing the harm.

Detailed Takeaways: Five Principles for Better Listening and Negotiation

The Hijack Point Is Your Real Enemy. Most of us believe we're listening when we're actually waiting for our turn to speak. The moment your attention shifts from understanding the other person to preparing your response, you've been hijacked. Recognizing this tendency in yourself—particularly the urge to correct and the urge to relate your own stories—is the foundation for improvement. You can't fix what you can't see.

The Urge to Correct Is a Lever You Can Use—and Protect Against. When you deliberately say something incorrect, you trigger an almost irresistible response in the other person. They will correct you, and in doing so, they'll reveal information they might not have shared intentionally. Use this technique strategically in negotiations. But also recognize when others are using it on you, and be aware that your satisfaction in correcting them may be masking an inadvertent disclosure.

Feeling Heard Is Different From Being Heard. You can understand someone completely without them feeling understood. The gap between cognitive understanding and felt experience is where most communication fails. The goal isn't to demonstrate your understanding—it's to make the other person feel that their experience has been fully acknowledged. This requires listening all the way through, resisting the urge to interject, and reflecting back their reality rather than comparing it to your own.

Collaboration Outperforms Competition Over Time. Even if your only goal is making money, collaborative negotiation typically produces better results than cutthroat tactics. Relationships built on trust generate ongoing value. Deals made between friends complete faster and more smoothly. The reputation you build through every interaction precedes you into every future negotiation. Win-lose tactics may win battles; they lose wars.

Remove Yourself as a Threat First. In any negotiation, the other person's perception of you as a threat will limit what they're willing to share, consider, and agree to. Before you can persuade, influence, or reach a great deal, you need to create safety. This applies whether you're negotiating with a hostage-taker or a car salesperson. The voice you use, the language you choose, and the genuine concern you express all contribute to whether the other person sees you as someone they can work with.

Who This Is For: Audiences and Applications

This content serves anyone who communicates with other people—which is to say, everyone. But certain audiences will find particularly immediate value.

Business professionals engaged in regular negotiation—salespeople, procurement specialists, executives, attorneys, real estate agents—will find direct applications to their daily work. The distinction between collaborative and positional approaches, and the recognition that tactics like deliberate misstatements can extract information, provide practical tools for the negotiating table.

Leaders managing teams and relationships will benefit from the listening frameworks. The hijack to relate, in particular, represents a trap that many well-intentioned managers fall into. They believe they're showing empathy by sharing their own similar experiences, when they're actually inadvertently centering themselves and missing the opportunity to make their direct reports feel truly heard.

Sales and customer-facing professionals will recognize the power of making people feel understood rather than merely understood. The wedding story illustrates this principle perfectly: the goal isn't to demonstrate your knowledge or your eloquence. It's to create a felt sense of being valued and comprehended.

Anyone experiencing relationship difficulties—personal or professional—will find that the listening principles apply across contexts. The failure to make someone feel heard is at the root of countless conflicts, resentments, and disconnection. Learning to listen all the way through, without hijacking to correct or relate, can transform the quality of any relationship.
