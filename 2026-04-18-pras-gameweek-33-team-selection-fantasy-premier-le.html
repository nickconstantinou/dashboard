Pras' Gameweek 33 Team Selection: Fantasy Premier League Tips 2025/26

A Strategic Deep-Dive into the Season's Pivotal Double Gameweek

---

Introduction: The Biggest Bonanza of the Season

For Fantasy Premier League managers, Gameweek 33 represents what countless players have been building toward all season—a massive double gameweek opportunity that could make or break rankings. In this detailed team selection video, Pras (PR), a content creator for FPL Wire, walks viewers through his decision-making process as he approaches this crucial juncture in the 2025/26 season.

What makes this particular video compelling is Pras' candid admission that despite being in his best-ever FPL position—breaking into the top 500k for the first time—he finds himself without the full complement of double gameweek players that many other managers are leveraging. "I'm sadly not having 15 doublers," he explains, noting that while he can see other teams with excellent doubling assets, his own squad composition leaves something to be desired. This sets up the central dilemma that will dominate the video: should Pras deploy his wild card now, or save it for a future gameweek while using a free hit to navigate the current double?

The context matters enormously here. Last gameweek (GW32), Pras made the strategic decision not to wild card, instead preserving his chips in anticipation of this very double gameweek. That decision initially paid dividends when players like Bowen delivered a triple return, Thiago scored a brace, and Van Dijk kept a clean sheet. However, the gains were partially clawed back by Brighton's unexpected clean sheet and Manchester United's underwhelming performance against his assets. "In the end it was still a good green week," Pras reflects, though he acknowledges the frustration of losing approximately 20 points to bench boost while only gaining 10 points on the week—his first entry into the top 500k.

---

The Central Dilemma: Free Hit or Wild Card

The heart of Pras' analysis centers on a fundamental FPL question: when both options yield similar team compositions, which chip should be deployed? His reasoning reveals the intricate mathematical and strategic thinking that separates successful FPL managers from the pack.

If Pras chooses to free hit this gameweek, he would essentially field the same team he currently possesses, with the significant advantage of keeping those players through Gameweek 34, which features exceptionally favorable fixtures across his squad. "Man United are home to Brentford. Bowen's playing Everton at home. Our Liverpool guys are playing Crystal Palace at home. And then of course Arsenal always have a good fixture at home who are playing Newcastle. Not the easiest, but Newcastle are not looking great." The confluence of these favorable matchups means Pras would be leaving a strong team intact for the following week without spending a valuable chip.

Alternatively, a wild card would allow Pras to restructure his squad more fundamentally, potentially freeing up funds or addressing weaknesses. However, when he compares the wild card team to his free hit team, he finds the differences are minimal. "I'm like, you know what? If I can wild card this week to a pretty much a free hit team, play a free hit next week and then in gameweek 35 have a nice bench boost that also works."

Sneak peek: Pras is leaning toward wild card. But the video builds toward this revelation methodically, walking viewers through the factors that inform this decision.

Understanding the Fixture Landscape: Gameweeks 33-35

#### The Symmetry of the Schedule

Pras provides crucial context about what he calls "the biggest Bonanza gameweek of the season" by explaining the broader scheduling pattern. Between Gameweeks 33 and 35, every Premier League team will play twice—some teams, particularly those still involved in cup competitions, will play three times. However, the critical insight is that whoever blanks in GW33 will double in GW34, and vice versa. This symmetry creates both opportunities and traps for FPL managers.

The fixture load, Pras argues, isn't as punishing as it might appear, largely because the FA Cup draws have been relatively kind to top teams. "It's not like Man City and Chelsea have got very difficult FA Cup games and they tend to rotate a little bit on those anyway." This means the disruption to regular Premier League rotation patterns may be less severe than in previous seasons.

#### The European Teams Complication

Here is where Pras' analysis becomes particularly valuable for serious FPL players. Four teams remain involved in European competition: Arsenal, Aston Villa, Nottingham Forest, and Crystal Palace. These teams will have midweek fixtures that complicate their GW35 planning significantly.

Aston Villa and Nottingham Forest are locked in a Europa League semi-final, creating a "mess in terms of rotation" for both clubs. Nottingham Forest plays Chelsea away on the Monday before their Thursday Europa League fixture against Villa. The question becomes: will Forest rotate against Chelsea to preserve energy for the European tie? Pras suggests this depends on their Premier League situation. "If they get a win and they're looking safer and let's say they get a draw, then you know it could be a semi-rotated team against Chelsea." This creates a "high upside game for Chelsea," as a potentially weakened Forest defense faces a motivated Blues side fighting for Champions League qualification.

Crystal Palace represents another fascinating case study. Pras notes that Palace "last night played again, took Waton off, took Lacro off" in a rotation that some believe was precautionary. The deeper concern is Palace's Conference League final aspirations—while the actual final occurs after GW38, the psychological and physical preparation for that match will influence selection decisions in the coming weeks. "We're going to have to calibrate very closer to Gameweek 36 on which picks we actually get for them. And more importantly, Gameweek 35, the Bournemouth away game that also fits within the two Europa League ties."

This creates a golden opportunity for Bournemouth, who host Palace at home in GW35. "That's a derby, right? Bournemouth Crystal Palace. So it's not like they can completely throw that game away. But these are things that we have to keep in mind as we look at the horizon."

Player-by-Player Analysis and Injury Concerns

#### The Nicco Riley Situation

One of the most instructive moments in the video demonstrates the unpredictable nature of FPL decision-making. Pras reveals that had he wild carded in GW32, he would have been able to "free hit not Nicco Riley and then wild card him back where everybody will have to sell. Well, now nobody's selling." The twist? Riley picked up an injury during the same gameweek when Pras' original team relied on him. Guardiola's comments suggest Riley is fine, but the uncertainty highlights how difficult it is to plan around player fitness.

#### Gordon's Motivation Question

Newcastle's Anthony Gordon represents a different kind of risk—not physical injury but motivational question marks. Pras explains that Eddie Howe "has come out and said if you're only fully committed then you will play, and apparently he was flirting with Bayern Munich a little bit." This revelation, combined with the observation that Gordon "was not playing striker in the previous Premier League game" and "was also subbed off earlier," creates genuine concern about his GW35 viability. "Gordon looks weaker," Pras observes, factoring this into his broader strategic calculations.

#### Saka's Confirmed Absence

Perhaps the most definitive information in the video concerns Arsenal's Bukayo Saka. "He's out of Man City," Pras confirms, explaining that Saka will miss Arsenal's fixture against Manchester City in GW35. This creates a two-game consideration: Saka faces Atletico Madrid in a Champions League tie with Fulham lurking on either side. "Are you going to be picking Saka on Game 35 wild card?" becomes a rhetorical question—the answer clearly being no for the foreseeable future.

#### Timber and Arsenal's Broader Situation

Omar Timber, who has been absent from recent Arsenal lineups, presents another complication. Pras questions whether Timber will "start the two Atletico Madrid games and the Fulham at home game" given Arsenal's compressed schedule. The bigger question is Arsenal's title ambitions: "Especially if Arsenal don't lose to Man City again, GW35 becomes a little tricky on how you set it up." The interplay between domestic and European priorities creates rotation risks that make Arsenal picks less reliable than they might initially appear.

Pras' Strategic Framework: A Four-Gameweek Plan

Rather than making impulsive decisions based on single gameweeks, Pras presents a cohesive strategy spanning multiple gameweeks—a hallmark of elite FPL management.

Step One: Navigate GW33 with a strong team. Whether through free hit or wild card, Pras needs competitive assets for this double gameweek.

Step Two: Treat GW34 as a free hit. His current team already features favorable fixtures—Manchester United at home to Brentford, West Ham hosting Everton, Liverpool facing Crystal Palace, Arsenal at home against Newcastle. "This team looks solid for 34 as well," Pras notes, implying that minimal changes will keep him competitive without spending a chip.

Step Three: Deploy bench boost in GW35. Pras reveals his intended bench boost structure, suggesting he has identified players who will be available in sufficient numbers to make this chip worthwhile. The Bournemouth fixtures and Liverpool's home match against Palace would anchor this strategy.

Step Four: Three free transfers into GW36 to acquire Palace players. This is the most forward-looking element of Pras' plan. "Buy three Palace" represents a calculated bet that Palace's rotation concerns will resolve favorably and that their fixtures will improve. By GW36, the Conference League picture may have clarified, making Palace more reliable options.

Detailed Takeaways

1. The Chip Opportunity Cost Principle

Pras demonstrates a crucial FPL concept: chips should only be deployed when they provide meaningful differentiation from alternatives. When comparing a wild card team to a free hit team, "the differences are minimal," which makes the wild card less valuable than it might appear. The takeaway is that FPL managers should always run the comparison—calculate what your team looks like under each chip scenario before committing. If the results are similar, the chip might be better saved for a situation where it creates genuine advantage.

2. European Competition Creates Two-Way Value

The semi-final between Aston Villa and Nottingham Forest creates asymmetric opportunities. Forest, fighting for Premier League survival while facing Chelsea in the league, may rotate against Chelsea, making Chelsea an excellent GW35 option. Simultaneously, Villa's focus on the Europa League might weaken their league performance, creating value against opponents. Managers should identify these European fixtures not just as risks for their owned assets, but as opportunities for opposing players.

3. Rotational Teams Require Dynamic Assessment

Pras emphasizes that decisions about Palace, Forest, and other rotation-risk teams should be made "very close" to the relevant gameweeks. GW35 selections should wait until after GW34 results clarify the situation. The lesson: don't lock in selections for rotational teams too early. Maintain flexibility and gather information before committing to assets whose playing time is uncertain.

4. Motivation and Contract Situations Affect Performance

Gordon's Bayern Munich interest exemplifies a FPL factor that often receives insufficient attention: player motivation. When a player is contemplating a move, their performance can suffer, and their relationship with the manager may become complicated. Eddie Howe's "fully committed" comments suggest tension that could affect Gordon's selection chances. Successful FPL managers track these situations and may avoid players in uncertain circumstances, even when their underlying ability suggests they should be owned.

5. The Schedule Symmetry Lets You Plan in Pairs

By understanding that doublers and blankers alternate between GW33 and GW34, managers can plan chip deployments more efficiently. Pras plans to "have a good team in 33, have a good team in 34, which is a free hit, have a good team in 35." This sequencing—strong in both gameweeks of a mini-double—demonstrates sophisticated long-term thinking. The practical application: when building a team for a double gameweek, also ensure you have strong options for the following blank gameweek, as those assets often become cheap and available.

Conclusion: Adaptability as the Ultimate FPL Skill

Pras concludes by reinforcing a theme that runs throughout the video: FPL success requires continuous adaptation to new information. "Saka not in the squad. It has been confirmed he's out of Man City... Gordon looks weaker... Timber who's still not playing." Each piece of news changes the strategic calculus, and managers who rigidly adhere to pre-formed plans often find themselves outperformed by those who react intelligently to changing circumstances.

His own experience in GW32 illustrates this perfectly. By not wild carding, he missed opportunities to address squad weaknesses while gaining the benefit of preserving his chips. "I made 10 points this week, but I will lose like 20 points this week to bench boost." The trade-off hurt, but "it's not the end of the world" because Pras remains "10 points up to Wildcard 32." The lesson isn't that his GW32 decision was wrong—it's that the process matters more than any single outcome. Make good decisions based on available information, and over a large sample, the results will take care of themselves.

The wild card decision for GW33 will be made with similar reasoning: not trying to maximize every point right now, but building a team structure that creates maximum advantage across the critical season-end stretch. Whether Pras ultimately wild cards or free hits, viewers now have the framework to make their own informed decisions about this pivotal gameweek.
