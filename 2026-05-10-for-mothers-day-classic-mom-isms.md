# For Mother's Day, Classic Mom-isms: The Wisdom We Carry Without Knowing Why

On Mother's Day 2020, *The Daily* podcast—a flagship production of *The New York Times* hosted by Rachel Abrams—dedicated a special episode to exploring the enduring phrases, mantras, and expressions that mothers repeat throughout their lives. What begins as a warm conversation between Abrams and her own mother, Alice Chesler Abrams, evolves into a rich collection of voice memos from listeners across North America, each sharing the particular saying that has stuck with them for years, decades, sometimes a lifetime. The episode works as both a celebration of maternal wisdom and an inadvertent meditation on how language carries values across generations.

The episode format follows the signature *Daily* structure: Rachel introduces herself as "from the New York Times," followed by the date and a promise to return with the main content after the break. This ritual framing gives the Mom Mantras collection a sense of occasion, elevating these domestic sayings to the level of serious cultural artifact. Throughout the episode, listeners hear from callers spanning from Edmonton, Alberta, to Austin, Texas, from Brooklyn to Miami, each bringing their own mother's distinctive voice and philosophy. What emerges is a surprisingly diverse catalog of practical wisdom, emotional guidance, and humorous observations—all filtered through the universal experience of hearing the same phrase repeated so often it becomes embedded in a person's identity.

---

## The Origins of Maternal Wisdom

Rachel's mother, Alice Chesler Abrams, offers what becomes the episode's philosophical anchor: **"These are the good old days."** The expression carries a specific meaning that transcends its surface-level positivity. The idea is that regardless of age or circumstance, people will always look back at the present moment and recognize it as precious. The failure, as Alice articulates it, is that we rarely appreciate these moments while they are happening. By imagining how we will view today from some future vantage point, we can recognize how precious our current circumstances truly are.

When Rachel asks where this phrase originated, Alice admits uncertainty—she doesn't believe she invented it—but traces it to lived experience. She describes her own realization that when looking at old photographs, she once thought she looked "so this and so that," only to recognize years later how wonderful she actually looked in that moment. "I wish I could have appreciated it then," she reflects. The phrase emerged, she suggests, as a way to pass this hard-won wisdom to her daughter before time runs out. "I did want to pass that on to you before it's too late," she says—a maternal urgency that encapsulates the episode's emotional core.

This exchange establishes the pattern that will repeat throughout the episode: moms expressing their values not through direct instruction but through repeated phrases that their children absorb without initially understanding their depth. The sayings become vessels for philosophy, carrying complex emotional wisdom in memorable, repeatable form.

## Practical Wisdom: When Logic Meets Survival

Beyond philosophical guidance, some mom mantras address practical survival. A caller named Jane Dee, originally from Somerset, Massachusetts, shares her mother's refrain whenever her children were about to leave the house: **"All tragedy starts out in fun."** The saying functions as a gentle warning—trouble often begins as entertainment, risk often masquerades as pleasure. It's the kind of parental caution that teenagers dismiss as joy-killing but that carries retrospective weight when remembered during moments of potential recklessness.

From Central Texas, a caller named Bobbi shares an anecdote that transforms her mother's cleaning obsession into philosophy. She was pulling into her parents' driveway when she spotted her mother with "a half bottle of Windex in one hand and a cleaning rag in the other," gazing at the water heater with evident satisfaction. When Bobbi suggested it was time to get a hobby, her mother replied with what became a family saying: **"I love the smell of Windows in the morning. It smells like victory."** The phrase captures something essential about how mothers find meaning in activities that others might dismiss as meaningless—the victory of a clean surface, the satisfaction of domestic accomplishment.

From Portland, Oregon, caller Gwen Harvey offers **"When in doubt, clean,"** a mantra that her mother deployed when life felt overwhelming. The expression carries sophisticated emotional logic: if something is bothering you, put yourself to work cleaning a closet, a cupboard, the car, or the dog. Even if cleaning doesn't solve the underlying problem, "at least you come away with some evidence of things being better." This practical wisdom acknowledges that sometimes action is more therapeutic than rumination—that making something visibly cleaner can restore a sense of control when other domains of life feel ungovernable.

## The Poetics of Maternal Persuasion

Several mom mantras demonstrate how mothers use vivid imagery to make their points memorable. From Miami, Jesse Kay shares **"With one behind, you can't sit on the whole world,"** a phrase passed down from his grandmother. The expression carries a pragmatic message about limited capacity: you cannot do everything at once, and pretending otherwise leads to burnout. The anthropomorphized image—of a single backside trying to occupy the entire globe—makes the abstract concept of overwhelm concrete and slightly absurd, which is perhaps why it stuck.

Richard Martin from Austin, Texas, learned a distinctly Texan expression from his grandmother: **"Take an onion out of that stew."** In the regional dialect, this means "I know you're lying to me—straighten up and tell the truth." The agricultural metaphor suggests that deception introduces an undesirable element that spoils the whole mixture, and the remedy requires identifying and removing it. The expression represents a gentler approach to calling out dishonesty than direct confrontation, wrapping truth-telling in folksy imagery.

From Paso Robles, California, Toby Crockett shares **"Don't teach your grandmother how to suck eggs,"** an expression that puzzled him as a child. He found himself confused by multiple dimensions: why would anyone need instruction in sucking eggs, and what connection did grandmothers have to this peculiar skill? Only as an adult did he understand the meaning: don't tell experienced people how to perform tasks they have mastered countless times. Toby connects this to contemporary concerns about condescension, describing it as "the all-purpose version of mansplaining"—a way to shut down unwanted instruction with generations of maternal authority behind it.

## Emotional Guidance for Uncertain Times

Several mantras address the challenge of maintaining perspective when circumstances feel dire. From Fredericton, New Brunswick, Giselle Gauguin shares her mother Gail's expression: **"There's many a slip between the cup and the lip."** The traditional meaning warns against premature celebration—don't count your chickens before they hatch, don't assume success before it arrives. But Gail reinterprets the phrase for comforting purposes: the world is in constant flux, and situations that seem certain to turn badly probably will not, or will not be as catastrophic as imagined. "That situation at work that you can't control, that you're convinced is going to end badly," Giselle explains her mother's logic, "it probably won't." The phrase becomes a reminder that uncertainty cuts both ways—things can improve as readily as they can deteriorate.

From Alamo, California, Danielle shares a mantra from her "tiny, fiery, extremely loving little Sicilian mother": **"Just because someone throws you a football doesn't mean you have to catch it."** The expression addresses the way others' opinions and criticisms can feel obligatory—if someone offers feedback, it seems rude not to accept it. But her mother's wisdom suggests a different relationship to external judgment: you can receive the ball, inspect it, and decline to engage. "You can just say thank you for the feedback and just not take it in because it wasn't yours," Danielle explains. The athletic metaphor transforms criticism into something external and optional rather than an obligation requiring response.

From Brooklyn, Hillary Smith shares a story about a middle school meltdown over a school project that felt "too big and too overwhelming." In the middle of her panic, her mother asked, **"How do you eat an elephant?"** After a confused moment, Hillary guessed "in a giant gulp?" Her mother's response—"one bite at a time"—became a framework for approaching any overwhelming task. The absurdist imagery of an elephant as food makes the wisdom memorable while the practical message applies universally: any massive undertaking becomes manageable when broken into component pieces.

## Identity, Presence, and Self-Expression

Some mantras address how to occupy space in the world. From Greensboro, North Carolina, Jonathan Caberta shares his mother Pauline Caberta's expression: **"Sing out, Louise."** The phrase references a famous line from the musical *Gypsy*, and Jonathan describes his mother as "Mama Rose Incarnate"—a force of life, a tornado in every room she entered. "Sing out, Louise" meant to his mother that one should always let one's presence be known in every room entered, make choices decisively, "damn the torpedoes" on whatever choice was made, and embrace *carpe diem*. The theatrical reference transforms a simple instruction into dramatic self-presentation, suggesting that presence is something performed rather than merely experienced.

From Fairfield, Connecticut, Phoebe shares an expression tied to her Syrian heritage: **"We have a Syrian look."** Coming from "a long line of Syrian women who have intense, unfriendly, frowning faces," Phoebe explains that strangers often assume they are unapproachable. But her mother taught her that this intimidating expression could be deployed deliberately: whenever someone has wronged you or whenever you need to stand up for yourself, "give them the Syrian look"—cut your eyes, deliver the stank eye, let them know "you can't be messed with." The expression reframes a cultural stereotype as a tool for self-protection, transforming a perceived weakness into an asset.

## Fashion, Appearance, and Emotional Regulation

From Manhattan, Carrie O'Grady shares what she describes as her mother's counterintuitive advice: **"If you feel bad, look better."** The expression contradicts typical impulses to hide during emotional distress—sweatpants, pajamas, comfort food. But her mother, who is "always perfectly put together," would encourage getting up, putting on something cute, and doing makeup even when feeling terrible. At the time, Carrie found this "insanely annoying," and admits she still does.

However, as an adult, now "almost 43" and a professor who commutes "nearly four and a half hours to and from her university multiple days per week," Carrie finds herself incorporating her mother's wisdom unconsciously. She packs "something a little extra" when traveling—zebra print, bedazzled headbands, high heels—and has "perfected applying eyeliner on the Peter Pan bus." When students compliment her outfit, she immediately feels "more awake" and "more ready to conquer my day." She remains unwilling to fully admit her mother was right, but acknowledges "she was onto something." The expression captures a psychological truth about the relationship between appearance and mood—that caring for external presentation can influence internal states.

## Philosophy of Accumulation and Adventure

Several mantras address material goods and life's adventures. From New York (raised in New Jersey), Nisan shares a phrase his mother deployed whenever the family went shopping: **"We can make that."** Whether examining candles, jewelry boxes, or even ripped jeans, his mother would turn the item over, pause, and declare that they could recreate it themselves, then return the item to the shelf. The expression carries economic wisdom wrapped in self-sufficiency, suggesting that consumer goods need not be purchased when creativity can produce equivalent or superior results.

From Irvine, California, Erin shares **"The grass will soon turn to milk,"** an expression her mother uses frequently. The metaphor references a cow's complex digestive process: a cow eats grass, which goes through a labyrinthine biological transformation, eventually becoming milk. "It's this idea that it might take a while but your hard work will pay off and result in this beautiful, delicious good thing, the milk." The expression acknowledges that results often come through slow, invisible processes—that the value created may not resemble the effort invested.

From Edmonton, Alberta, Anna Curtis shares her mother's staying: **"There isn't a shroud with pockets big enough."** This slightly morbid expression offers a Canadian and slightly darker variant of "you can't take it with you when you go." But Anna emphasizes that her mother didn't merely say this—she lived it. Her mother pursued adventures, explored the world, raised six children with intensity, and embraced the philosophy that life is "for the now and the living." Anna recounts an epic road trip from Winnipeg, Manitoba, to Circle, Alaska, traveled on an unpaved Alaskan highway in a Volkswagen Beetle with "nothing but a tent on the top." Her parents didn't ask whether they had enough money—they asked how to make it happen. "When my mother passed away many years ago, there wasn't any money left. That's okay. There wasn't a shroud with pockets big enough."

## Humor, Mashups, and Family Idiosyncrasy

Some of the most charming mantras emerge from mothers' unintentional creativity. From Seattle, Sean Statham shares his mother's expression: **"Roll with the cookie."** His mother would use this when the household needed to adapt quickly—when plants had "gone awry" or when five children needed to mobilize in a hurry. Only later did Sean realize this was his mother's unconscious mashup of "roll with the punches" and "that's the way the cookie crumbles." The blended expression made perfect sense to him as a child; he assumed every family used it. The anecdote reveals how family language creates its own logic, how children absorb phrases without recognizing their constituent parts, and how maternal efficiency can generate new idioms through conflation.

From Philadelphia, Janine shares her mother's consistent send-off for teenage social outings: **"No sex, no drugs, only rock and roll."** The deliberately flippant tone suggests a mother who understood that direct moral lecturing would be ineffective, so she deployed humor instead—a way of covering the serious warning with comedic packaging, ensuring the message would be remembered precisely because of its absurdity.

From Bloomington, Indiana, Kelsey shares her mother's resigned expression: **"Some things just are."** Her mother originally deployed this phrase in response to a child who perpetually demanded explanations—"why, why, why, why"—and eventually reached the limit of maternal patience. Some questions have no answers, some circumstances have no causes, and accepting this fact is not defeat but wisdom. The expression models tolerance for life's irreducible mysteries.
