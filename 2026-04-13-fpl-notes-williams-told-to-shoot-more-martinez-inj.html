Neco Williams Has Permission to Shoot—and That Changes Everything

The moment arrived in the 72nd minute on Sunday. Neco Williams received the ball outside the penalty area, took one touch to set himself, and unleashed a strike that left Martinez rooted to his line. Nottingham Forest had their equaliser against Aston Villa. More importantly, Williams had vindicated everything his manager has been telling him.

"The manager has been telling me to shoot outside the box more and that's what I did today," Williams said afterward. "I took a few and one went in, so I'm pleased with that."

It's a small sentence that carries significant Fantasy weight.

---

Williams' goal wasn't a fluke. It was the culmination of a pattern that has quietly made him one of the most intriguing differential picks in the game. His four shots against Villa were the most of any player on the pitch. His season-long tally now stands at 39 attempts—at least seven more than any other defender in the division. For context, the next-closest defender has managed somewhere in the low thirties, leaving Williams in a category of his own when it comes to volume from deep positions.

But here's what makes the Sunday assist from Callum Hudson-Odoi particularly interesting: Williams is now taking corners too. That dual threat—set-piece involvement layered on top of an already shots-heavy profile—transforms his ceiling considerably. A £4.7m defender with corner duties and a manager actively encouraging him to shoot from range is precisely the kind of profile that separates budget enablers from genuine point-stealers.

Forest host Burnley in Gameweek 33. The Clarets sit second-bottom of the table. For Williams, it's an opportunity to compound the good work.

---

Wood Back, Jesus Shifts

Sunday's draw brought more than just a Williams revelation. Chris Wood, the New Zealand striker who had spent six months on the sidelines, made his return from the bench in the 65th minute. It was a significant moment for Vitor Pereira.

"His experience, quality, scoring goals, he smells the goals in the box," Pereira said. "His physical and mental condition. He has the ability to help us."

Wood's reintroduction forced Igor Jesus into a No. 10 role, where he continued his gradual build-up of sharpness after his own injury layoff. Whether Pereira starts Wood against Burnley remains to be seen—65 minutes in a competitive draw suggests he's not quite ready for a full 90—but his presence changes Forest's attacking dynamics considerably. At £7.1m, Wood is a reminder that Forest's forward options shouldn't be ignored even as the Williams story captures the spotlight.

Elliot Anderson also returned from his midweek European suspension, contributing defensively as Forest ground out a result. Morgan Gibbs-White, by his own standards, was quiet—but nearly scored when his second-half effort was deflected and saved.

---

Rogers Reminds Us He's Human

On the Villa side, Morgan Rogers served another reminder that elite potential and consistent output don't always align in the same paragraph.

The England international claimed his second assist in as many Gameweeks when his low cross was deflected into his own net by Murillo. It was the kind of fortunate strike that pads returns without requiring the usual craft. But Rogers also clipped the bar shortly after and, most glaringly, blazed a 'big chance' over from about eight yards out when he really should have done better.

Unai Emery removed him on 77 minutes—just one DefCon point shy of the threshold. The Villa boss was asked about the substitution after the match.

"He is able to play three matches in a week," Emery said. "Sometimes he is being clinical, or not. Sometimes as well maybe he is more tired than other matches. Today I felt him, on Thursday as well I changed him after 85, I was thinking we needed extra work and fresh legs through Buendia and he did it."

The sub-optimal finishing and the early withdrawal are worth noting for Fantasy managers who have banked on Rogers' involvement. At £7.4m, he's not expensive, but he's not delivering the clinical consistency his underlying numbers suggest he should. The talent is undeniable. The production has gaps.

---

The Bottom Line

If there's one name from Sunday's City Ground encounter that deserves a second look heading into Gameweek 33, it's Williams. A manager who wants him shooting. Corners in his locker. A fixture against a relegation-threatened side. At £4.7m, he's the kind of pick that doesn't cost much and could return significantly—exactly what a differential is supposed to be.
