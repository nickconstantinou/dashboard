# FPL Gameweek 34: Free Hit Strategy and Fixture Analysis

Comprehensive Guide to the Biggest Blank Gameweek of the Season

---

Introduction

For Fantasy Premier League managers navigating the final stretch of the season, Gameweek 34 presents a unique tactical challenge: six teams who doubled in Gameweek 33 are now blanking this week, creating the season's largest blank gameweek. For those still holding the Free Hit chip, this represents the final opportunity to deploy unlimited transfers for a single gameweek—a strategic pivot point that could make or break FPL seasons.

FPL Harry, a prominent content creator in the FPL community, walked through his strategic approach to GW34 in a detailed analysis video. The timing is particularly relevant because many managers who played their Bench Boost in GW33 are currently sweating on players like Evanilson (Haaland's effective replacement) and Cole Palmer, hoping for favorable results across the remaining fixtures that include triple Brighton assets at home against Chelsea, Manchester City's trip to Burnley, and Tottenham's challenging match against Wolves. Harry himself is on 70 points with his bench boost active, currently sitting at 28,000th overall with a nine-point green arrow above his safety score—meaning his strategy is proving effective despite the uncertainty of so many unplayed fixtures.

The core tension in this gameweek revolves around whether to target attacking players in matches that promise goals, or focus on defensive assets that offer clean sheet potential. With seven fixtures to analyze and multiple premium options available, Harry's detailed breakdown provides a framework for managers wrestling with these decisions on their own Free Hit chips.

Before diving into GW34 strategy, Harry spent time explaining where things currently stand for those who played their Bench Boost chip in GW33. Friday Night Fixture: Southampton vs Nottingham Forest

The GW34 action begins with Southampton hosting Nottingham Forest on Friday evening, and Harry approached this matchup with cautious optimism about some assets while expressing reservations about others. For Southampton, he still views them as a reasonable pick, particularly for defenders like Elderete, Mukielli, or even goalkeeper Ramsdale—players who offer solid defensive contributions and reasonable clean sheet potential against a Forest side that should create chances.

However, Harry pivoted to acknowledge that Nottingham Forest impressed him in their recent fixtures, particularly noting Morgan Gibbs-White's exceptional form during a crucial period for the club. Forest's survival ambitions remain pressing despite a five-point buffer above the relegation zone, meaning they'll be fighting hard for every point. Gibbs-White, fresh off a hat-trick against Burnley, will likely be heavily targeted by FPL managers this week—but Harry expressed caution about stacking Southampton defenders against Forest's attacking threats.

The reasoning here is nuanced: while Southampton have proven defensively organized this season, targeting any defense against a motivated Forest side with Gibbs-White in form presents genuine risk. Harry would prefer to avoid Gibbs-White at his elevated ownership, viewing him as someone likely to see high selection rates following his hat-trick, but he acknowledged the temptation of fading popular picks while targeting Forest's attacking potential is real for differential hunters.

Fulham vs Aston Villa: An Attacking Affair

Harry characterized the Fulham versus Aston Villa matchup as more of an attacking proposition than a defensive one, and his reasoning drew from both teams' recent form. Aston Villa demolished Sunderland 4-1 in their previous fixture—a result that seems impressive until noting Sunderland also scored, highlighting Villa's vulnerability at the back. Harry observed that Southampton actually scored three goals against Aston Villa in a recent match, which suggests Villa aren't as defensively secure as their attacking reputation might imply.

This defensive uncertainty makes Harry lean toward attacking assets over defenders. For Aston Villa, Ollie Watkins is finding form alongside Morgan Rogers, both presenting appealing options for Free Hit builders. Harry specifically flagged Harry Wilson as a potential Fulham asset worth considering, suggesting he's a better pick than Fulham defenders like Tim Ream orAntonee Robinson if managers want exposure to Fulham's attacking output.

The logic follows a clear pattern: when both teams present attacking potential, FPL managers should generally prioritize forwards and midfielders over defenders. Clean sheets become less likely when both sides have scoring intent, making the risk-reward calculation favor goal-scoring assets over defensive options that might keep a clean sheet but offer limited ceiling.

Liverpool vs Crystal Palace: Multiple Angles for Attack

The Liverpool versus Crystal Palace matchup generated substantial discussion, with Harry identifying both obvious premium picks and interesting differential options. For Liverpool, Mohamed Salah is beginning to find form after a quieter spell, making him an attractive captaincy candidate. Dominic Szoboszlai offers consistent reliability through set pieces, open play threat, and meaningful goal danger, while Virgil van Dijk provides goal threat from dead ball situations despite Liverpool's defensive injury concerns.

Harry noted that Liverpool will likely be without goalkeeper Alisson and defender Ibrahima Konaté, which complicates their defensive options and makes clean sheet predictions more uncertain. However, the available attacking assets present enough firepower to compensate for defensive unreliability, particularly if Salah continues his resurgent goal-scoring form.

The Crystal Palace side presents a more complex picture. While Harry expressed low confidence about Palace's general appeal, he made an interesting observation about Eberechi Eze's record against Liverpool—specifically referencing last season's Gameweek 38 when many managers sold Eze before a fixture that seemed unfavorable, only for him to score anyway. This historical context suggests Eze might be undervalued as a differential option despite the apparent mismatch between Palace and Liverpool.

One particularly intriguing differential Harry flagged was Cody Gakpo. With Alexander Isak unable to play 90 minutes regularly, Gakpo has been starting games on the left flank but finishing matches through the middle as the number nine. This positional flexibility and role in an attacking Liverpool side makes him a potentially valuable under-the-radar pick for Free Hit builders looking for differentiation from popular selections like Salah and Szoboszlai.

West Ham vs Everton: Contrasting Motivations

West Ham versus Everton features two teams with significantly different motivational contexts, according to Harry's analysis. West Ham continue fighting for their Premier League survival, making assets like Mohammed Kudus and Jarrod Bowen interesting punts for Free Hit builders seeking value. Everton, meanwhile, still have much to play for this season, with players like Iliman Ndiaye and Dwight McNeil presenting legitimate attacking options despite Everton's uncertain defensive outlook.

Harry specifically mentioned Jesper Lindstrøm as a player who could feature in his final Free Hit squad by the deadline, reflecting his uncertainty about the optimal second forward position. The discussion highlighted a common FPL dilemma: finding a reliable budget option that doesn't sacrifice too much ceiling compared to premium forwards but doesn't break the bank either.

The matchup's key uncertainty is whether Everton can keep a clean sheet. Harry suggested he's not expecting one from Everton in this fixture, which pushes managers toward West Ham attacking assets and Everton's forwards rather than Everton's defenders. This represents a strategic choice to prioritize attacking potential over clean sheet points in a match where both sides appear capable of scoring.

Wolves vs Tottenham: The High-Risk Differential Zone

Harry identified the Wolves versus Tottenham match as the "punt of the week" for Free Hit builders, and his reasoning revealed the strategic complexity behind this assessment. Tottenham impressed Harry in their recent victory against Brighton, suggesting they're playing better than many FPL managers might realize. However, they let emotion affect them at the end of that match, with Brighton equalizing to draw 2-2—highlighting Tottenham's vulnerability to high-pressure situations.

For FPL purposes, this creates two distinct strategies. Managers could target Tottenham attackers like Pedro Porro, James Maddison, or Dominic Solanke as high-ceiling options on a team that absolutely must win this match to avoid deeper trouble in their fight for European qualification. Alternatively, managers skeptical about Tottenham's mental resilience might consider Wolves players as contrarian picks, banking on Tottenham's pressure leading to mistakes.

Harry expressed reluctance to actively recommend Wolves defenders, suggesting the expectation gap favors Tottenham sufficiently to make Wolves an unappealing target despite the narrative around Tottenham's vulnerability. Instead, he viewed Tottenham attackers as the optimal play—their need to win combined with improved underlying performance makes them valuable assets even at elevated ownership levels.

Newcastle vs Arsenal: Defensive Appeal Over Attacking

The Newcastle versus Arsenal matchup presented Harry with a more nuanced challenge, as Arsenal's recent form suggests defensive assets are more trustworthy than attacking options. Arsenal performed adequately defensively against Manchester City in their recent fixture, a result that Harry interpreted as a positive sign for their clean sheet potential against Newcastle.

However, identifying reliable attacking Arsenal options feels difficult according to Harry, who cited concerns about Bukayo Saka's fitness, Martin Ødegaard's consistent involvement, and Kai Havertz's combination of minutes risk with currently uninspiring attacking form. The lack of clear attacking confidence, combined with Havertz's uncertain game time, makes Arsenal forwards unappealing despite their theoretical quality.

Instead, Harry pointed toward Arsenal's defensive assets as the optimal strategy for this fixture. William Saliba, Gabriel, and Ben White will likely be heavily owned picks given Arsenal's favorable defensive matchup against a Newcastle side that Harry described as "all over the place" in recent performances. Timber is also approaching a return, adding another defensive option to the mix. The clean sheet probability, combined with attacking potential from set pieces, makes Arsenal defenders the safer play compared to their inconsistent forwards.

Brentford vs Manchester United: Monday Night Uncertainty

The final fixture—Monday night's match between Brentford and Manchester United—generated particularly interesting discussion about the strategic implications for Free Hit builders. Harry noted that many managers not on Free Hit will be heavily loaded with Brentford assets, particularly defenders and attacking options like Bryan Mbeumo and Yoann Wissa. This creates a natural temptation for Free Hit players to fade Brentford entirely by loading up on Manchester United assets.

Despite recognizing Brentford's appeal to non-Free Hit managers, Harry currently doesn't have Brentford players in his own team, though he expressed openness to adding Thiago Silva as a potential inclusion before the deadline. His reasoning reflects the peculiar challenge of constructing a Free Hit squad: premium options are obvious and widely owned, but finding reliable second-tier picks that don't sacrifice too much ceiling is genuinely difficult.

For Manchester United, Harry identified several competing options without clear resolution. Mainoo, Garnacho, and Amad Diallo present a rotation puzzle where it's unclear exactly which two will start, making all three somewhat risky. This ambiguity pushes Harry toward more reliable picks like Casemiro, Bruno Fernandes, or Matthijs de Ligt—a defensive option with meaningful goal-scoring potential given his ability to deliver 15-point performances.

The Monday night timing creates additional strategic uncertainty: managers can only make transfers before deadline, meaning they'll have less information about team news and late fitness doubts compared to earlier fixtures. Harry's acknowledgment that Thiago might end up in his team by deadline reflects this information asymmetry and the need to remain flexible when constructing Free Hit squads.
