# Programming in the Age of AI: What You Need to Know

## The New Reality of Software Development

The landscape of programming is undergoing a seismic shift, and Hitesh—a recognized GitHub Star—wants you to understand exactly what's happening and why it matters. In a candid discussion drawn from his experiences attending GitHub Constellation, one of the largest events in the developer ecosystem, Hitesh offers a clear-eyed perspective on how artificial intelligence is fundamentally reshaping how we learn to code, how we write software, and how the entire industry will function in the coming years.

Having had the opportunity to speak directly with GitHub's COO and senior VPs at Microsoft during the event, Hitesh gained unique insight into how the major players driving this ecosystem view the transformation underway. His message is both reassuring and challenging: the change is inevitable, the genie is out of the bottle, and those who adapt will thrive while those who resist may find themselves left behind.

This isn't a video about fear-mongering or suggesting that programming is dying. Rather, it's an honest examination of how the craft is evolving and what that means for aspiring developers, current practitioners, and the creators of educational content who shape how the next generation learns to build software.

## The LEGO Paradigm: Understanding Modern Programming

At the heart of Hitesh's argument lies a powerful metaphor that reframes how we should think about software development. **Programming, he explains, is essentially like building with LEGO bricks.** Each module—whether it's an authentication system, an authorization framework, a product listing component for an e-commerce platform, or a payment gateway—represents a discrete piece of functionality that can be combined with others to create complete applications.

The critical insight here isn't just that modules exist; it's understanding how they're assembled. Sometimes module one connects to module two, which connects to module three. Other times, module one connects directly to module three, which connects to module four. The art of programming, Hitesh argues, lies not in creating each brick from scratch but in understanding how to arrange and connect them to build functional products.

This perspective is especially relevant for application engineers—the developers who work on web applications, mobile apps, and software that serves end users. Hitesh describes these developers as "craftsmans" who assemble components much like a tailor assembles fabric pieces into clothing. The work involves taking pre-built modules, combining them according to business logic, and creating systems that enable people to accomplish tasks like ordering food delivery, booking cab rides, or scheduling dental appointments.

What makes this paradigm shift so significant is its implications for how we think about education and practice. The core foundations of platforms like Flipkart, Amazon, or any quick commerce service are remarkably similar. What differentiates them is how they arrange their modules and which additional modules they need. This means the conversation around software development is shifting away from "how do we write this code?" and toward "why does this module exist? How does it scale? How does it fit into our system design?"

## The Death of Traditional Learning Resources

One of Hitesh's most provocative claims is that **Stack Overflow no longer exists as a practical resource for most developers.** This isn't a gradual decline but an almost complete transformation of how programmers find solutions to problems. The forum-based system where humans wrote questions and other humans provided answers has been superseded by AI-powered assistance that can generate solutions directly within the development environment.

Even more striking is what Hitesh reveals about documentation. **Documentation is increasingly being written not for human programmers but for AI agents.** Companies are creating LLM.txt and LLM.mds files—documentation formats specifically designed to be consumed and understood by large language models. The traditional API documentation meant to guide human developers through reference materials is being restructured to train and inform the AI systems that will increasingly be doing the actual coding.

This creates a paradoxical situation for learners. The traditional path of reading documentation, searching Stack Overflow for solutions, and gradually building knowledge through reference materials has been disrupted. Hitesh's advice is direct and somewhat counterintuitive: **"Build it. That's all you can do."** The new way to learn isn't through passive consumption of tutorials or reference materials but through active construction—actually building projects and learning through the process of creation.

For educational content creators, this represents both a challenge and an opportunity. Hitesh makes clear that tutorials aren't going away, but they must adapt. The style and delivery of programming education needs to evolve to match how the ecosystem itself is changing. Rather than focusing on syntax and code-writing mechanics, future tutorials will need to emphasize system design, module selection, scalability considerations, and the strategic decisions that go into assembling software products.

## The Evolution of Code Editors

Hitesh demonstrates how the very tools developers use are being transformed by AI integration. **Modern code editors are now split into three distinct sections:** the file explorer on the left, the traditional code writing area in the center that developers will "touch very less now from onwards," and a chat session panel on the right where developers can issue commands to AI agents and receive suggestions.

This right-side chat panel, Hitesh predicts, **"is going to stay here for at least the next 5 to 6 years. It's not going anywhere."** This represents a fundamental shift in the developer experience. Rather than typing every line of code manually, programmers will increasingly work with AI assistants that can generate code, suggest improvements, and handle routine implementation tasks.

To illustrate this evolution, Hitesh walks through practical examples that demonstrate how AI-powered suggestions work. He shows HTML development, where typing an exclamation mark and pressing tab automatically generates the complete document structure—an Emmet shortcut that mirrors what AI suggestions now do for more complex code. The key point is that this auto-completion capability is "now on with superpowers," and AI is what provides those enhanced capabilities.

He also demonstrates React functional component creation. Previously, developers would type abbreviations like "RFCE" to generate component templates. With AI assistance, the suggestions look remarkably similar but come from a more intelligent system that can understand context and intent. The goal isn't to replace developers but to handle the mechanical aspects of code generation so human attention can focus on architectural decisions and problem-solving.

## A Strategic Learning Path for the AI Era

Hitesh offers specific guidance for those just beginning their programming journey, acknowledging that there's still value in learning fundamentals but emphasizing that the approach must change. **He recommends a 3 to 4 month window for exploring syntax, learning basics, and understanding foundational concepts like loops and functions.** This initial investment remains important because it provides the conceptual foundation necessary to work effectively with higher-level tools.

However, after this initial period, the entire ecosystem changes. Rather than manually writing code, new developers should **"rely more on suggestions and correct those suggestions wherever they are wrong."** This represents a fundamentally different relationship with code—one where AI generates much of the implementation and humans focus on review, correction, and strategic direction.

Once developers become comfortable with suggestion-based workflows, they can then **"rely a lot on the agents"** that power chat-based interactions within their development environments. This progression—from manual writing to suggestion acceptance to agent delegation—represents the new learning curve for programmers entering the field.

The resistance many developers feel toward AI suggestions is understandable but ultimately counterproductive, according to Hitesh. His assertion is direct: **"It is not wrong. Now, AI is so much better that this is how the things are moving forward."** The concern that AI suggestions might be incorrect, while valid in earlier stages of AI development, has become less relevant as these systems have matured.

## Key Insights for Navigating the AI-Powered Development Landscape

**First, the paradigm has fundamentally shifted.** Hitesh is unambiguous: "The programming era is not exactly the same as it used to be 5 years ago. It's a complete new shift in the paradigm." This isn't incremental improvement but a wholesale transformation of how software gets built. Professionals who approach programming with a 2019 mindset will find themselves increasingly out of step with industry realities.

**Second, embrace the change rather than resisting it.** The speaker explicitly states that "we need to accept this. We need to embrace this and get adopted with that." The tools and workflows that AI enables aren't temporary fads that will pass—they represent the new reality of software development. Adaptation isn't optional; it's survival.

**Third, focus your learning on higher-level thinking.** Since AI can handle much of the routine code generation, the valuable skills are those that AI struggles with: understanding why particular modules fit in a system, evaluating scalability implications, analyzing pros and cons of architectural decisions, and making strategic choices about system design. The questions worth asking are about modules, not about syntax.

**Fourth, understand that documentation has a new audience.** When Hitesh states that "docs are for agents," he's describing a fundamental shift in how technical knowledge is organized and communicated. Developers need to understand that the resources they encounter may be designed primarily for AI consumption, which changes how they should approach learning and problem-solving.

**Fifth, active building trumps passive learning.** With traditional resources diminished in utility, the most effective path forward is project-based learning. Actually constructing applications, making decisions about module selection and arrangement, and solving real problems provides the education that matters in this new environment.

## Who Should Pay Attention to This Message

This discussion is essential reading for several distinct audiences. **Aspiring developers** who are just beginning to learn programming will benefit from understanding that the path forward looks different than it did even a few years ago. Rather than following a purely traditional educational trajectory, they can structure their learning to take advantage of AI tools from an early stage while still building the foundational knowledge necessary for strategic thinking.

**Current software developers** who may be skeptical of or resistant to AI integration in their workflows will find Hitesh's perspective thought-provoking. Whether or not one agrees with all his assertions, understanding the direction the industry is moving helps developers make informed decisions about which skills to develop and which workflows to adopt.

**Programming educators and content creators** represent another critical audience. Hitesh's insights about how tutorials must adapt and how the focus of education should shift provide a roadmap for creating content that remains relevant in an AI-powered development environment. The creators who recognize this transformation early will be better positioned to serve their audiences effectively.

**Technical leaders and decision-makers** at companies building software products will also find value in understanding how the development ecosystem is evolving. Hiring decisions, technology choices, and team structures may all need to evolve in response to the changes Hitesh describes.

The core message that ties all these audiences together is this: the transformation is real, it's happening now, and the most effective response is not to fight against AI-powered development but to understand it, embrace its capabilities, and focus human energy on the uniquely human aspects of software creation—strategic thinking, system design, and the creative problem-solving that will define the next era of programming.
