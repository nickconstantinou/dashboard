How to Validate Great Business Ideas in the Age of AI: A Founder's Framework for Filtering Opportunities

The entrepreneurial journey is rarely linear, but perhaps nothing illustrates its complexity more than the challenge of knowing which ideas are worth pursuing. In a candid conversation on the *My First Million* podcast, host Sean sits down with Zach—founder of Calie, a calorie tracking application—to discuss one of the most persistent questions in entrepreneurship: how do you separate good ideas from bad ones? What emerges is not a magic formula but rather a practical framework built on lived experience, market observation, and a counterintuitive philosophy that challenges how most aspiring founders evaluate opportunities. The conversation takes unexpected turns into discussions about artificial intelligence's trajectory, the hardware problems that may slow AI's advance, and even a recent dinner with billionaire investor Steve Cohen, offering listeners rare insight into how experienced operators think about uncertainty.

Zach begins by acknowledging that idea generation is often treated as the holy grail of entrepreneurship—but he argues this focus is misplaced. "The first question I always get when I tell someone about Calie is how do you come up with that? And everyone's trying to think like I want to come up with these huge ideas," he explains. "I think that a better way of learning how to come up with ideas is a skill, but I think the more important skill and the better skill is a framework to validate what is a good idea." This distinction between ideation and validation forms the foundation of everything that follows. Zach's central thesis is simple but powerful: you can generate ideas constantly, but without a filtering mechanism, you'll waste enormous amounts of time and energy pursuing opportunities that were never viable. The real leverage lies in knowing quickly which concepts deserve your attention.

The Validation Framework: Personal Experience, Market Signals, and Adjacent Benchmarks

Zach's approach to validation is deeply personal and tailored to his own strengths and interests. He doesn't believe in universal checklists that work for everyone. Instead, he looks for three specific qualities in any potential venture. First, the idea must be highly marketable and capable of going viral. This isn't accidental—Zach has built his career on consumer products that spread through word of mouth and organic discovery, and he gravitates toward concepts that have inherent viral mechanics built into their design. Second, he requires firsthand experience or direct connection to the problem being solved. Either he has personally experienced the pain point, or he knows people who have, ensuring he understands his target audience at a granular level rather than building based on assumptions. Third, he looks for adjacent companies that have already proven the market exists.

When it comes to that third element—the market validation piece—Zach walks through exactly how he approached building Calie. Rather than conducting extensive market research or calculating theoretical total addressable markets, he simply observed that another calorie tracking application was generating $200 million in annual revenue. "So I know that's like the floor of what we could get to," he says. This approach treats existing successful companies as proof points rather than threats. The logic is straightforward: if someone else has already built a successful product in this space, the market exists, customers are willing to pay, and the problem is real. You're not trying to create demand from nothing—you're positioning yourself to capture a portion of an existing, proven opportunity.

This leads to one of the conversation's most memorable exchanges about the common misconception that competition invalidates an idea. Zach describes sitting in an entrepreneurship class where students would present concepts, only to be discouraged by other students pointing out that the product already exists. "Wait, that already exists," they would say, as if this were a fatal objection. Sean laughs, observing that this essentially means students are looking for non-existent business ideas—a category so small it barely exists. "That's proof," Zach responds flatly. "That's proof it works." The fact that a company is still growing, still acquiring customers, still generating revenue means that gaps remain in the market and that underserved customers are waiting for a better solution. The presence of competition isn't a red flag; it's a green light.

Total Addressable Market: When Gut Instinct Beats Spreadsheets

Sean pushes deeper into the topic of market sizing, asking specifically about Zach's approach to TAM analysis. Zach's answer reveals a pragmatic philosophy that prioritizes speed over precision. "To be honest, TAM is one of those things where I try I kind of always look at my gut for this," he admits. "I'm not like doing Google research." He didn't look up what the total addressable market for calorie tracking applications was before starting Calie. Instead, he looked at a single comparable company, saw its revenue, and used that as his baseline. The reasoning is that trying to calculate the precise size of a market through market research reports often creates false confidence in numbers that may be inaccurate anyway. A single real data point—a company actually generating revenue from serving real customers—is more valuable than a theoretically derived market size.

This approach also reflects Zach's awareness of how large most markets actually are. He notes that "the market is so big that all of these apps can be successful," and the data supports this intuition. Even saturated-seeming categories contain room for new entrants who can serve different segments, offer better experiences, or address unmet needs within the broader customer base. The question isn't whether a market exists but whether you can credibly position yourself to capture a portion of it.

The AI Paradox: Opportunity, Obsolescence, and the Intermediate Future

The conversation takes a significant turn when Sean asks what topics are currently capturing Zach's curiosity. What follows is a surprisingly nuanced discussion of artificial intelligence and its implications for entrepreneurs. Zach expresses genuine ambivalence about the future. He notes that figures like Elon Musk regularly predict coming abundance so significant that entrepreneurship itself may become unnecessary, and that money will lose its meaning. "People are saying you shouldn't save for your retirement," Zach observes, "because the future is going to be so radically different." This framing is both exciting and paralyzing.

Zach then makes a revealing personal admission: the beliefs he was taught in school have been "completely flipped on their head" by his lived experience since then. At the same time, he recognizes that he may be experiencing the same kind of paradigm shift again as AI capabilities advance. The world he lives in now feels "completely different" from the world he knew at age ten or eleven, and he suspects the next decade will bring equally dramatic changes.

Sean immediately identifies the paralysis this creates: "I flip-flop between oh my god there's so much opportunity, I'm going to make so much money. Then an hour later, oh I'm obsolete. Then the next one, wait money is actually obsolete, right?" The tension is real and paralyzing. If AI will automate away both the problems entrepreneurs solve and the need to earn money, what's the point of building anything? Zach's answer draws from an unexpected source—a recent dinner with Steve Cohen, the billionaire owner of the New York Mets.

Steve Cohen's Three-Year Framework: Survival as Strategy

Zach reveals that he recently dined with Steve Cohen, one of the most successful investors in the world, whose hedge fund empire has generated tens of billions of dollars in returns. What made the dinner memorable was Cohen's honest assessment of the current moment. "Right now he's just trying to figure out how to kind of invest or extend the timeline of his companies or ones that he's looking at investing in like 3 years," Zach explains. "Just survive 3 years and then figure it out after that. Like no one knows where the future's heading." This is the advice of someone who has navigated multiple market cycles, technological shifts, and economic disruptions: stop trying to solve for the distant future and focus on surviving the immediate intermediate period.

The logic is elegantly pragmatic. Nobody—including billionaires with vast resources and teams of analysts—knows exactly how AI will develop, which specific capabilities will arrive when, and how society will adapt to rapid technological change. Rather than gambling on a particular vision of the future, Cohen's strategy is to position his investments so they can weather three years of uncertainty, then reassess when more information becomes available. This transforms the paralysis of uncertainty into actionable guidance: stop worrying about what the world looks like in 2030 and focus on building something that can thrive or at least survive through 2027.

The Hardware Problem: Why Software Progress Doesn't Mean Robots Tomorrow

One of the conversation's most substantive tangents involves a point Zach picked up from his dinner with Cohen about the nature of AI progress. "People are thinking about the AI part like okay we solve AGI then overnight everything changes," Zach explains, "but the software is one problem—software is one small problem. The hardware, like building out these humanoid robots that are going to execute these tasks, that's a whole another problem." This observation challenges a common assumption embedded in AI hype: that progress in large language models and artificial general intelligence will immediately translate into physical world automation.

Zach specifically mentions companies like Physical Intelligence that are working on the hardware and robotics layer necessary to actually execute physical tasks. The challenge of building robots that can reliably navigate and manipulate the physical world is potentially just as hard as the software challenge—and in many ways more difficult because it must contend with the physical world's constraints. Sensors, motors, actuators, and mechanical systems don't improve at the same exponential rates as software. "People are underestimating" this complexity, Zach says. For entrepreneurs, this suggests that software-based businesses—particularly those that don't require physical execution—may face more disruption faster than businesses that depend on physical supply chains, manufacturing, or service delivery.

Building for the Long Term: Consumer Passion and Hardware Ambitions

Sean asks a natural question: given these uncertainties, does Zach want to stay in the consumer software space he's already proven in, or does he want to pursue larger, venture-capital-intensive opportunities? Zach's answer reveals his core motivation. "I really enjoy building products for consumers," he says. "I just love—it's the biggest compliment to me—hearing that someone else uses something that I built, especially when they love it." He describes the experience of meeting someone who uses Calie and learning that he built it as "so awesome to see." This emotional reward—creating something that real people use and love—is what drives him, regardless of theoretical payoffs or market size.

That said, Zach expresses genuine interest in expanding beyond pure software. "I definitely want to build a company that does have a hardware component to it," he explains. "Maybe not exclusively hardware, but something that can be either software or hardware, like both that we sell." This suggests an entrepreneur who's thinking about durability and differentiation. Hardware products often create stronger competitive moats than pure software because they involve physical supply chains, manufacturing relationships, and customer relationships that are harder for competitors to replicate quickly. Whether he pursues purely software businesses or hybrid physical-digital products, Zach is clear that the consumer focus will remain central to his ambitions.

Key Takeaways

Validation beats ideation as a skill. Zach argues that the ability to generate business ideas is common and not particularly valuable—what separates successful founders is their ability to quickly filter out bad ideas and identify the few worth pursuing. Rather than spending months developing ideas that have no market, invest your time learning frameworks that help you assess viability faster. The goal isn't to be creative; it's to be discerning.

Competition is proof, not warning. The entrepreneurial class instinct to avoid saturated markets is precisely backwards. If a company is generating significant revenue in a space, it means customers exist, they have real problems, and they're willing to pay. The question for your business isn't whether the market is crowded but whether you can serve customers in that market better than existing options. "That's proof it works," as Zach puts it simply.

Use real revenue benchmarks instead of market research. Rather than relying on TAM reports that may be inaccurate or irrelevant, use the actual revenue of a single comparable company as your minimum floor. If you can identify even one successful competitor, you know the market exists. You can build from there. Observed reality beats theoretical projections every time.

Survive the intermediate period. Steve Cohen's three-year framework offers a template for navigating uncertainty: stop trying to solve for the distant future and focus on building something that can survive the next few years. You can always reassess when more information becomes available. The goal right now is to create a platform from which you can adapt as conditions change.

Hardware is an underestimated bottleneck in AI's trajectory. The popular narrative treats software AI progress as the main challenge, but physical execution—robots, sensors, manufacturing—creates additional massive obstacles that aren't improving at software rates. For entrepreneurs, this suggests that software-first businesses face more near-term disruption, while businesses with physical components may have more durable competitive positions.

Who This Is For

This conversation will be most valuable for aspiring entrepreneurs who feel stuck in the ideation phase, unable to distinguish promising concepts from dead ends. It's also essential listening for current founders who find themselves paralyzed by AI anxiety, uncertain whether their current business model will survive the next wave of technological change. The frameworks shared here offer practical guidance for moving from "should I build this?" to "how do I know if this is worth building?"—a shift that can save months of wasted effort.

Even experienced operators will find value in Zach's honest assessment of how he actually thinks about market validation, which differs substantially from the pitch deck frameworks taught in business schools. And anyone curious about the intersection of entrepreneurship and AI's trajectory will appreciate the candid discussion of both the opportunities and the genuine uncertainties that lie ahead. The conversation ultimately argues for a middle path: take action based on what you can observe today, build for survivability in the intermediate term, and stay humble enough to adapt when the future becomes clearer.
