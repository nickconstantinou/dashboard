# Chips & Strategy: End Game | Planet FPL S. 9 Ep. 47 | Fantasy Premier League

## Introduction

The Fantasy Premier League season is approaching its decisive phase, and managers up and down the country are faced with some of the most consequential decisions they will make all campaign. Welcome to the final Chips and Strategy podcast of the season from Planet FPL, where hosts David and James dissect the end-game strategy landscape and help listeners navigate the complex fixture chaos that has emerged in recent weeks.

This episode arrives at a particularly fraught moment in the FPL calendar. The hosts record this edition on Tuesday, April 7th, and immediately set the scene by noting that the Premier League had just confirmed the rearranged fixtures for gameweek 33 at the conclusion of recording. The news fundamentally alters the strategic picture: Brighton versus Chelsea will be played on the Tuesday of gameweek 33, while Bournemouth versus Leeds and Burnley versus Manchester City will both take place on the Wednesday. This means all six of those teams will double in gameweek 33 and blank in gameweek 34—creating a pivotal swing that every manager must account for.

The timing of this podcast proves crucial because just the previous Saturday evening, Arsenal suffered a shock FA Cup defeat to Southampton, throwing many managers' strategies into disarray. What had appeared "fairly straightforward" suddenly became "an open field" with multiple viable paths to success. The hosts acknowledge that "play it your way is very real"—there are numerous legitimate strategies at this stage, and this episode aims to explore the main options while acknowledging that no single approach is objectively correct.

## Key Concepts & Arguments

### The Fixture Reshuffle: Understanding Gameweeks 33 and 34

The foundation of this episode's discussion centers on the fixture reorganization caused by the FA Cup semi-finals. The hosts explain that any Premier League gameweek 34 fixtures that clash with FA Cup semi-final matches will be rescheduled to take place between Tuesday, April 21st and Thursday, April 23rd—which is, confusingly, gameweek 33 on the FPL calendar. This creates a double gameweek for six teams in gameweek 33 followed by a blank gameweek for those same teams in gameweek 34.

David expresses some measured skepticism about whether all fixtures will definitively be brought forward, placing what he describes as a "two or three percent" chance that one game might not follow the expected pattern. His reasoning centers on the scheduling logistics: with Sky Sports presumably wanting to spread matches across Tuesday, Wednesday, and Thursday to maximize viewership, there could be complications if a team plays Thursday and then faces an FA Cup semi-final on Sunday. "I think the clubs, whoever's got a game on a Thursday and a cup semi-final on a Sunday is not going to be happy about that," David observes. However, both hosts agree that the probability of the rearrangement proceeding as expected is "97-98%," and they urge listeners to wait for official confirmation while planning accordingly.

The scheduling discussion becomes particularly detailed when considering the ideal scenario for team selection. The hosts suggest that for Chelsea versus Leeds, the preferred arrangement would be matches on the Tuesday with a five-day break before the Sunday FA Cup semi-final, allowing both teams to field strong sides in both league fixtures. Manchester City versus Burnley presents a trickier proposition because City must also play Arsenal in the league on the Sunday before their potential semi-final, creating a Sunday-Wednesday-Saturday schedule that "sounds horrendous," as James puts it. However, the hosts note that City's draw against Southampton in the FA Cup semi-final is "good news" because they will likely rotate heavily in the league fixtures, meaning their double gameweek becomes more attractive for FPL purposes.

### The Arsenal Defeat: Strategic Implications

The most significant single event discussed in this episode is Arsenal's unexpected FA Cup quarter-final defeat to Southampton on Saturday evening. James, who watched the match, provides a detailed analysis of what went wrong for the Gunners. "Arsenal picked quite an offensive lineup" featuring Martin Ødegaard and Ethan Nwaneri as number eights, which left them "very very vulnerable off the ball, very unusually for them." He is at pains to emphasize that this was not a case of Arsenal taking the competition lightly: "I don't want to say they took it lightly. It's understandable with Champions League game tonight etc, etc." Instead, the formation and selection left Southampton with opportunities to exploit the space behind Arsenal's midfield.

Southampton's approach was direct but effective, playing "some fun stuff as well" while winning the second balls and creating chances. "They deserved to win," James insists. "It wasn't a fluke." The implication for FPL managers is substantial: Arsenal's double gameweek in gameweek 33 is now off the table, meaning managers who had loaded up on Arsenal assets must recalculate their approach. Previously, Arsenal versus Everton and Arsenal versus Brentford looked like a lucrative double; now, those assets become considerably less attractive for the immediate future.

David summarizes the feeling among Arsenal fans watching the match: even after Arsenal equalized, there was no sense of "oh, it's inevitable now." Southampton continued to push, and in injury time, James found himself "screaming at the TV" wanting Southampton to sit back and defend their lead rather than pursue a third goal. The commentary paints a picture of a genuinely impressive Southampton performance that exposed Arsenal's vulnerabilities when their aggressive pressing was negated.

### The FA Cup Quarter-Final Results and Their FPL Impact

Beyond the Arsenal result, the hosts review all four FA Cup quarter-finals and their implications for the fixture calendar. Manchester City beat Liverpool 4-0 in what James describes as "the first game," followed by Chelsea's 7-0 demolition of Port Vale. These results confirmed the semi-final matchups: Manchester City will face Southampton, while Chelsea will face Leeds.

The Leeds versus West Ham quarter-final proved dramatic, with Leeds eventually winning on penalties after a "wild" Sunday match that featured "lots of twists and turns." James notes that "I've heard some stories of some West Ham fans leaving at 2-0 down" only to return home in time to watch their team lose the penalty shootout—essentially watching their team lose twice in one day at two different venues. This result means Leeds will have the double gameweek in gameweek 33 rather than West Ham, making Leeds players suddenly more attractive for managers positioning for that gameweek.

The Chelsea versus Leeds semi-final and Manchester City versus Southampton semi-final will determine which teams get the double gameweek in gameweek 34, but the hosts note that at the time of recording, the specific dates for those matches had not been confirmed. What is known is that Manchester City will play Southampton on the Saturday, while the hosts tentatively suggest Chelsea versus Leeds will be on the Sunday—though this remains subject to change.

### Chip Strategy: The Hosts' Approaches

One of the episode's most valuable elements is the hosts' candid discussion of their own chip strategies, which differ substantially and illustrate the range of viable approaches at this stage of the season. David reveals that he still has all his chips remaining: "Yeah, so I've got all of them left. Yeah, seven weeks left for chips to use." This places him in an extremely flexible position where he can essentially play the season out on his terms, using chips when the fixture circumstances demand.

James, by contrast, has already used several chips: "I squandered, some would say, my bench boost a number of weeks ago for a number of players who decided not to turn up." Despite this setback, he expresses a degree of contentment with his position: "I'm also somewhat pleased that I don't have to worry about it on this strategy line at the moment because it's influencing people perhaps in a positive way, but also perhaps in a negative way." His remaining chips are the Wildcard and Free Hit, leaving him with considerably less flexibility than David but still with two powerful tools to deploy when conditions suit.

This contrast serves as a useful reminder that chip strategy is deeply personal and depends heavily on one's starting position, rank, and risk tolerance. A manager with all chips remaining can afford to wait and react to developments, while a manager with fewer chips must be more proactive and may need to commit to a strategy earlier.

### The Southampton Fixture Complication

A subtle but important detail emerges regarding Southampton's fixture situation. James explains that Southampton currently have a rearranged Championship fixture against Ipswich scheduled for the week following what would be their double gameweek. This game was originally scheduled for Good Friday but was moved to the Tuesday during the FA Cup quarter-final weekend. "I think to move it back to the Wednesday again is probably unlikely," James notes, suggesting that this extra fixture could affect Southampton's team selection and rotation in their league matches.

For managers considering Southampton players for gameweek 33, this adds another layer of complexity. The Saints will have two league matches that week, but they also have this rearranged Championship fixture (which, while not in the Premier League, still requires squad depth and creates fixture congestion). The hosts suggest this is something to monitor when selecting Southampton assets.

## Detailed Takeaways

**Takeaway 1: The Gameweek 33 Double Is Now Confirmed, But Timing Matters**

The six teams confirmed to double in gameweek 33—Brighton, Chelsea, Bournemouth, Leeds, Burnley, and Manchester City—present both opportunity and risk. The key insight is that these teams blank in gameweek 34, so managers must plan their transfers and chips accordingly. James emphasizes that "all six of those teams confirmed will double in gameweek 33 and blank in gameweek 34," creating a clear two-week swing. The practical application is that managers should identify which of these six teams they want to target based on form,Fixture difficulty, and ownership levels, then ensure they have coverage for gameweek 34 when these teams blank.

**Takeaway 2: Arsenal's FA Cup Exit Changes the Strategic Landscape**

Arsenal's defeat to Southampton removes one of the most attractive double gameweeks from the immediate picture. Managers who had loaded up on Arsenal assets need to pivot. James's analysis makes clear this was not a case of Arsenal fielding a weakened side by choice—their tactical approach left them exposed. "They just picked a team that was too open," James explains. The implication is that Arsenal remain strong when fully focused, but their unexpected exit means the fixture curve has shifted. Managers should not panic-sell Arsenal assets but should recalculate their gameweek 33 strategy to account for this change.

**Takeaway 3: There Is No Single "Correct" Chip Strategy**

The hosts repeatedly emphasize that multiple strategies are viable at this stage. "Play it your way is very real," James notes. "There are lots of different ways to play this." David's position of having all chips remaining gives him maximum flexibility, while James's approach of using chips earlier represents a valid alternative. The key is understanding your own position—your rank, your bench strength, your risk tolerance—and tailoring your chip usage accordingly. Managers should not feel pressured to copy others but should develop a strategy that fits their specific circumstances.

**Takeaway 4: Watch for Scheduling Announcements Before Committing**

While the fixture reorganization is highly likely, David advises caution: "I'm just a little bit suspicious that one of them might not... wait for the official confirmation." The practical advice here is that managers should not make irreversible moves based solely on assumptions. Wait for the Premier League to confirm the exact dates before using valuable chips like the Bench Boost that require specific player selections. The 2-3% uncertainty David mentions is small but not negligible, and it could significantly impact managers who build their entire gameweek 33 strategy around a Bench Boost only to see a fixture move at the last moment.

**Takeaway 5: Leeds Are Now the Attractive Double Gameweek Option**

With Leeds defeating West Ham in the FA Cup, they have secured the double gameweek in gameweek 33 that West Ham would have otherwise had. "Leeds eventually winning on penalties... they are the team that will have the double gameweek in 33 rather than West Ham," James confirms. For managers looking to capitalize on the double gameweek, Leeds assets now represent a viable option alongside the more established picks from Brighton, Chelsea, Bournemouth, Burnley, and Manchester City.
