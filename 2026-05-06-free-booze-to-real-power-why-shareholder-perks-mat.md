## Key Insights

• **Chapel Down wine discounts are tiered by shareholding**: Silver shareholders (1-1,999 shares) receive 25% off wine; Gold (2,000+ shares) gets 30% off all products plus shop discounts; Platinum (10,000+ shares) earns 33% off wine plus an annual guided tour voucher for four people and restaurant perks. Shares trade at just 35p, making the 25% annual voucher essentially free value.

• **Bloomsbury Publishing (Harry Potter's publisher) offers 35% off book RRP**: One share at approximately £6.10 provides perpetual discounts that quickly exceed the purchase price in hardback savings, demonstrating how even modest shareholdings can generate tangible returns for regular consumers.

• **BT Group still requires physical share certificates for its best discounts**: This outdated requirement—visible on Hargreaves Lansdowne's website—illustrates how the transition from paper certificates to nominee accounts created barriers to accessing shareholder perks, and some companies haven't modernized their systems.

• **Euro Tunnel granted original shareholders lifetime free channel tunnel travel**: This perk was legally contested during subsequent restructurings but successfully defended by original shareholders, remaining active into at least the early 2000s.

• **Nuns in West London are actively campaigning against Citibank's fossil fuel investments**: The campaign on indigenous rights and climate impact demonstrates that shareholder activism—enabled by the "perk" of voting rights—remains alive among individual investors who take their ownership seriously.

## Introduction

In an era where most people hold equities through opaque nominee accounts, never attend shareholder meetings, and feel no connection to the companies they technically own, a provocative question emerges: what happened to the relationship between investors and the businesses they fund? That question lies at the heart of this episode of *Maren Talks Your Money*, the personal finance edition of the broader *Maren Talks Money* podcast.

Host Maren Thumbs Up Web—joined by Senior Reporter and Money Digital author John Stepak—engages in a wide-ranging discussion about shareholder perks, the declining culture of AGM attendance, and why these seemingly trivial benefits might actually represent something fundamental about how capitalism is supposed to function. The conversation ranges from British wine companies and luxury handbag discounts to nineteenth-century investment trusts and the peculiar legal status of lifetime Euro Tunnel passes.

What makes this discussion particularly timely is the ongoing erosion of individual shareholder engagement. Proposals to eliminate physical AGMs and restrict individual voting on executive remuneration are currently under consideration, and the hosts argue these changes would further detach investors from any meaningful sense of ownership. The episode argues that shareholder perks—long dismissed as gimmicky giveaways—might actually be one of the last mechanisms keeping individual investors connected to the companies they collectively own.

---

## The Decline of Shareholder Engagement and Why It Matters

The conversation begins with Maren expressing what she describes as genuine concern about the direction of shareholder democracy. She observes that most people hold their equities remotely—whether through auto-enrollment in large funds, shares held on platforms, or accounts with major providers—and consequently lack any tangible connection to the companies in which they invest. "There isn't a certificate, there isn't something to touch," she notes. "And also people don't go to AGMs in the way they used to in the old days."

This disengagement, she argues, is getting worse rather than better. Maren specifically highlights ongoing conversations about potentially ending physical AGMs entirely and restrictions on how individuals can vote on executive remuneration at shareholder meetings. "I'm really nervous about this," she confesses, "because I really think the physical AGM should be compulsory."

Her proposed solutions are characteristically specific: AGMs should be mandatory for all companies, live-streamed so shareholders can either attend virtually or in person, and accompanied by what she describes as essential amenities. "Tea and biscuits should be compulsory at these physical AGMs," Maren declares. Going further, she advocates for compulsory lunch and scheduling meetings at times that allow elderly shareholders to use their discounted travel passes—"Maximum participation," she calls it.

When John gently points out that Maren is typically skeptical of regulation, she acknowledges the apparent contradiction but draws a clear distinction. "I am anti-regulation, but I'm pro participation. I'm pro shareholder democracy. And I'm pro people feeling like they have a sense of ownership." She suggests that companies have been allowed to get away with not treating their individual shareholders appropriately.

To illustrate what has been lost, Maren invokes historical examples of engaged shareholders. She references "the Gilbert brothers" who made fortunes during the California gold rush and subsequently attended every single shareholder meeting of companies in which they invested. When dissatisfied with answers, they would stand and announce: "You work for me." Maren describes this as "activism," and argues that "the world needs more Gilberts."

She also references a contemporary example: an order of nuns in West London (near where Maren herself walks regularly) who have been conducting a campaign against Citibank regarding fossil fuel investments and impacts on indigenous rights. While acknowledging that listeners may have varying views on the substance of such campaigns, Maren emphasizes the procedural point: "If you're a shareholder, you get the perk. You get the perk of being able to use your vote, go to the AGM, have your cake, make your voice known."

## The Mechanics of Shareholder Perks in 2024

The conversation shifts to examining specific shareholder perk programs currently available, with John and Maren working through examples that share a common characteristic: they disproportionately appeal to British sensibilities. "The best ones seem to be all about things that the Brits really like," John observes. "Booze and holidays."

**Chapel Down** receives particular attention as "one of the few companies left that has a really great perk." The English wine producer offers a tiered system based on shareholding:

- **Silver shareholders** (1-1,999 shares at 35p per share): minimum 25% discount via annual voucher
- **Gold shareholders** (2,000+ shares): 30% discount on wine plus additional product discounts
- **Platinum shareholders** (10,000+ shares): 33% off wine purchases, an annual guided tour voucher for four people, restaurant discounts, and shop benefits

For anyone who regularly purchases wine, Maren observes, owning shares "probably is worth just owning shares purely for that" discount alone.

**Bloomsbury Publishing**, J.K. Rowling's longtime publisher, offers 35% off the retail price of any books in print for shareholders owning just one share. With shares trading around £6.10, the discount quickly exceeds the investment cost for anyone who purchases hardback books with any regularity.

The hosts note that many traditional perk programs were originally designed for physical share certificates—a requirement that persists at some companies. "BT Group PLC discounts on a variety of products and services, but only available to shareholders with a share certificate," John notes, though most companies now allow platform-held shares to access perks with appropriate documentation.

Maren observes that measuring whether perks have declined in popularity or availability is difficult because no comprehensive tracking exists, but she suspects the overall number of programs has decreased. She attributes this partly to the transition from paper shares to nominee accounts, which created what she calls "a kind of middle person between you and your shares." However, she notes that technology has improved the situation: "I have an account with one of the big platforms and it's very good at nagging me to vote in the AGM when it's coming up."

**Other examples discussed include:**

- **Renishaw**: A high-tech engineering company offering discounts on packaged holidays through its Wotton Travel subsidiary
- **Norwegian Cruise Line**: Onboard credit for shareholders
- **Michelin Butler's**: 20% off total bills at the restaurant
- **Will Smith and Turner**: A "shareholder indulgence card" providing 50% off food and drink and 10% discounts at hotels
- **Carnival**: Money off cruises
- **Next**: 25% discount once a year with approximately 100 shares
- **Mulberry**: 20% discount on luxury handbags (though requiring a larger shareholding given the share price exceeds £1)

John mentions a correspondent on Twitter highlighting a smaller AIM-listed drinks company that offered shareholders a bottle of 21-year-old whiskey as a one-time bonus for purchasing and holding shares for a specified period—a perk that exemplifies the creative approaches some companies take.

## Historical Context: From Colonial Trusts to Euro Tunnel

Maren traces the lineage of shareholder perks back to the 1870s, specifically citing the **Foreign Colonial Investment Trust**. Unlike modern shares, these were certificates purchased at a discount and redeemed at par at maturity—functioning more like bonds. However, the trust introduced a lottery component: all certificates were entered into an annual random draw, and winners received their money early, effectively crystallizing capital gains ahead of schedule.

"I like that as well," Maren admits, "because it's a bit of elaborate financial engineering. You throw in that lottery element and people just will pay so much over the odds for a lottery." She expresses some frustration that companies haven't figured out how to resurrect this gambling instinct in their cost of capital favor.

The most legendary historical perk the hosts discuss is the **Euro Tunnel** offering. When the Channel Tunnel first listed, original shareholders received something approaching lifetime free travel on the shuttle service. Maren recalls that "they tried to take it away, but it was legally challenged because obviously that went through a load of restructurings." The perk survived, and original shareholders retained their benefits—benefits that Maren says were still being actively used by people she worked with in the early 2000s.

Listeners are invited to write in if they possess such a pass: "Anybody out there who still has that, do write in and let us know. We'd also like to know if you do have it, how often you've used it."

## The Real Purpose of Shareholder Perks

Near the episode's conclusion, Maren reflects on what perks actually accomplish. "When I think about perks," she says, "I think to myself, it's not really about the money, right? It's about the sense that you're part of a group that it sort of cleaves you to the company and it gives you a degree of corporate loyalty." She then immediately undermines her own sentiment with the observation that "whenever anyone says it's not about the money, it's about the principle—it's always about the money."

This apparent contradiction captures something essential about how perks function. For a regular customer who happens to be a shareholder, the discount isn't symbolic—it's real money returned on a minimal investment. "If you can be bothered," John adds, referencing money-saving strategies familiar to followers of comparison sites, "and a lot of people can be bothered with this sort of thing... if you do go to a certain pub a lot and you can buy one share and get 10% off all your bills, then actually, why wouldn't you do it?"

Maren's underlying argument is that perks serve as a bridge between abstract ownership and genuine engagement. The 25% off at Chapel Down or Bloomsbury isn't valuable enough to drive investment decisions, but it creates a small psychological shift that might make someone more likely to attend an AGM, cast a vote on executive pay, or push back when a company acts badly. "I think that your point of ownership is actually really important on this," John tells her, validating the argument.

## Who This Episode Is For

This episode will resonate most strongly with retail investors who hold equities through platforms like Hargreaves Lansdown, Interactive Investor, or Barclays SmartInvestor and have never accessed shareholder perks to which they're entitled. It offers specific, actionable examples of perks available for minimal shareholding requirements, making it immediately useful for readers who might be overlooking real value in their portfolios.

The discussion also appeals to anyone interested in corporate governance and shareholder democracy—particularly those concerned about declining individual engagement with investee companies. Maren's advocacy for compulsory physical AGMs with mandated hospitality provides a clear policy position that will generate strong reactions in both directions.

More broadly, the episode speaks to readers who find the abstraction of modern investing alienating. For those who suspect they're merely "numbers in a database" at companies they technically own, Maren and John offer both historical context for why this happened and practical strategies for reasserting ownership—starting with claiming that free bottle of wine.</p>
