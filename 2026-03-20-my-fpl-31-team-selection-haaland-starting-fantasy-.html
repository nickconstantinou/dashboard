Gameweek 30 Review: Building Momentum

Harry begins by celebrating a third consecutive green arrow, scoring 64 points in gameweek 30—eight points above his safety score as calculated from livefpl.net. For those unfamiliar with the concept, the safety score represents the threshold a manager needs to maintain their rank; anything above it results in a green arrow indicating rank improvement, while anything below produces a red arrow. The magnitude of the arrow depends on how far above or below that safety threshold you score, making it a useful barometer of relative performance.

His gameweek rank of 500,000 might sound modest, but Harry is quick to explain why it matters: achieving a gameweek rank inside the top million is considered a significant achievement, and breaking into the top 500,000 is better still. More impressively, this strong week pushed his overall rank from 64,000 all the way up to 47,000—a substantial jump that places him inside the top 50,000 for the first time this season. After what he describes as a "medium poor start" to the campaign, Harry is now asking himself just how far he can push this ranking before the season concludes.

The captaincy decision proved crucial last week. While many managers defaulted to Erling Haaland or opted for other premium options like Evan Ferguson, Harry backed Bruno Fernandes against Crystal Palace—a choice that paid off reasonably well. However, the real star of his gameweek turned out to be a player who wasn't even his first choice: when Dominic Calvert-Lewin missed a penalty for Everton against Crystal Palace, Richards was brought on as a substitute and delivered a spectacular performance. Beyond the Richards windfall, Harry also collected a goal from Thiago Alcantara and the aforementioned Bruno Fernandes captaincy returns, combining to keep his momentum heading in the right direction.

The Chip Strategy Conundrum

The heart of this video revolves around Harry's chip strategy for the remainder of the season, and he makes clear from the outset that he's approximately 90% committed to one of two distinct approaches—though he acknowledges that FA Cup results, injury news, and fixture announcements could still shift his thinking. The critical deadline is the gameweek 32 cutoff, by which point managers should have a much clearer picture of which teams will have doubles in gameweek 33 and which will blank in gameweek 34.

Harry explains that the first major strategic fork in the road hinges on the FA Cup quarter-final between Liverpool and Manchester City. If Liverpool win that tie, the most popular and likely strategy becomes: Wildcard in gameweek 32, Bench Boost in gameweek 33, and Free Hit in gameweek 34. This approach makes sense because Liverpool's victory would likely secure them a double in gameweek 33 while Manchester City, having exited European competition to Real Madrid midweek, might not double that week—and even if they did, their potential double would come against Burnley, hardly an appealing fixture.

If Manchester City win the FA Cup tie, however, the calculation changes dramatically. In that scenario, Harry is considering: Free Hit in gameweek 33, Wildcard in gameweek 35, and Bench Boost in gameweek 36. The logic here is that a City victory would likely give them a double in gameweek 33 (against a Burnley side that isn't appealing), making the Bench Boost in that week less attractive. Meanwhile, Harry already has ten players likely to blank in gameweek 34, meaning he doesn't necessarily need a Free Hit there—and he can afford to save his Wildcard for later in the season when more teams might have doubles.

The wildcard situation adds another layer of complexity. Harry admits he already wasted his Triple Captain chip earlier in the season on Gabriel, leaving him without that powerful option for the run-in. For managers who still have their Triple Captain available, the conventional wisdom is to save it for either gameweek 35 or 38, when double gameweeks are most likely to occur.

The Chelsea situation introduces additional uncertainty. The postponed Chelsea versus Brighton fixture needs to be rescheduled, and depending on when and where that game gets played, it could affect which teams blank and which double in gameweek 34—another variable that Harry is monitoring closely.

The Taroski Dilemma and Defender Options

The immediate tactical puzzle centers on Tariq Mosques, Harry's Nottingham Forest defender who currently carries a yellow flag. While the flag doesn't automatically rule a player out, it indicates elevated risk of rotation or injury, and managers must decide whether to hold or transfer him out before the gameweek 31 deadline.

What makes this decision particularly fraught is that the replacement Harry might need depends entirely on which chip strategy he ultimately adopts. If he goes with the first strategy (Wildcard 32, Bench Boost 33, Free Hit 34), he needs a defender who performs well in gameweek 31 and ideally has a double in gameweek 33. If he goes with the second strategy (Free Hit 33, Wildcard 35, Bench Boost 36), he needs a defender who blanks in gameweek 33 but has a good fixture in gameweek 34.

Harry walks through the main options in detail. The most obvious single-target replacement is Malik Tchouake of Newcastle United, who has Southampton at home in gameweek 31 and represents the best short-term option. However, if Manchester City win the FA Cup and Harry adopts strategy two, Newcastle are almost certain to blank in gameweek 34 and double in gameweek 33—which means buying Tchouake would actually be counterproductive since Harry would be Free Hitting the week of his double.

This leads Harry to consider alternatives more suited to strategy two: Matty Cash of Aston Villa or Du of West Ham, both budget options around the 4.0 million mark who would serve different purposes. Cash has West Ham at home in gameweek 31 and faces Fulham away in gameweek 34, while Du has a tougher away fixture at Villa this week but gets Everton at home in gameweek 34—which is considerably more appealing than Cash's away day at Fulham.

The problem is that neither Cash nor Du is as obviously good a pick as Tchouake for gameweek 31 itself. Tchouake at home against Southampton is a much more attractive proposition than Du away at Villa or Cash hosting West Ham. So if Harry ends up going with strategy one after the FA Cup results become clear, buying Tchouake would have been the right move—but if he commits to strategy two, he'd have bought the wrong defender and potentially wasted a transfer.

The Haaland Mystery

Perhaps the most eyebrow-raising element of Harry's team selection is his decision to start Erling Haaland in gameweek 31 despite Manchester City not having a fixture this week. Harry acknowledges this directly, explaining that he'll need to bench the Norwegian striker—wasting his entire 14.2 million price tag for the week.

This raises the obvious question: why not just transfer Haaland out and use that budget elsewhere? Harry doesn't fully explain this in the video, but the likely reason is that he's keeping Haaland as a hold for the run-in. With eight gameweeks remaining and Manchester City likely to have at least one double along the way, selling Haaland now would mean buying him back later at potentially higher cost or missing out on his points when City do play. The 4-4.5 million in savings from having Haaland on the bench rather than in the squad might not justify losing access to him for future gameweeks, especially if you're planning to use your Wildcard or Free Hit to completely restructure your team anyway.

It's a reminder that in FPL, sometimes the obvious move isn't the right move—and that holding onto premium assets through blank weeks can pay off if you have the bench depth to sustain it.

Key Takeaways

First, the FA Cup results matter more than almost anything else for FPL strategy right now. The Liverpool versus Manchester City quarter-final will have massive ripple effects on which teams double in gameweek 33 and which blank in gameweek 34. Harry is waiting for that result before committing to either of his two main strategies, and managers who lock in their approach too early risk making transfers that don't fit their eventual chip plan.

Second, chip strategy should drive transfer decisions, not the other way around. Harry is deliberately NOT making a transfer this week (or at least is trying not to) specifically because he doesn't yet know which strategy he'll adopt. The defender he needs to buy changes completely depending on whether he's planning to Free Hit gameweek 33 or gameweek 34. Making transfers before you know your chip plan is how you end up with players who don't fit your overall strategy.

Third, consider how your transfers interact across multiple gameweeks, not just the immediate one. The Malik Tchouake example illustrates this perfectly: he's a great pick for gameweek 31 and would be excellent if you're Bench Boosting gameweek 33, but he's actively counterproductive if you're Free Hitting gameweek 33 and need someone to blank in that week while having a good fixture in 34.

Fourth, gameweek rank matters more than total points for mid-season strategy. Harry's focus on the 500,000 gameweek rank and his movement into the top 50,000 overall highlights how relative performance matters in FPL. A 64-point week is respectable, but scoring it when most other managers are scoring 56 points means a much bigger rank jump than scoring it when everyone else is scoring 62.

Fifth, sometimes holding premium assets through blank weeks makes sense if you have the bench depth. Starting Haaland this week means essentially writing off 14+ million of your budget, but if you're planning to Wildcard or Free Hit soon anyway, keeping him means you don't have to navigate buying him back later at potentially worse value.
