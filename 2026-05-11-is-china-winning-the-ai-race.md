<p># Is China Winning the A.I. Race?

## Key Insights

- **China's strategic goal**: Achieve leading AI power status by 2030, per the 2017 New Generation AI Development Plan—a national roadmap that mobilized government at all levels through industrial parks, tax incentives, and direct funding to attract talent and companies.

- **DeepSeek's breakthrough cost**: The AI model was reportedly trained for approximately $5.6 million—a fraction of American competitors' costs—demonstrating that Chinese labs can achieve frontier-level performance at dramatically lower resource investment.
- **Regulatory duality**: Chinese AI companies face strict government clearance for models that could "mobilize society" (affecting political consciousness), but robotics and non-information-facing applications operate with substantially fewer restrictions—captured by the analyst framing: "control what AI says, unleash what it does."
- **Xi Jinping's pivots after DeepSeek**: Following DeepSeek's January 2025 release, Xi convened entrepreneurs including the unknown 30-something founder and publicly urged them to "go out and show your talents and serve the country"—signaling government recognition that private-sector autonomy must increase for China to remain competitive.

## Introduction

In the spring of 2025, as President Trump prepared to meet Chinese President Xi Jinping for high-stakes diplomatic talks, one question loomed above the negotiating table with particular urgency: who is winning the global race for artificial intelligence dominance? This episode of *The Daily* podcast brings listeners into a deep conversation with Vivian Wong, a New York Times reporter who has covered China for six years and whose recent work has centered almost exclusively on AI development. Her reporting reveals that answering the question—whether China is beating the United States—requires abandoning simplistic win-lose frameworks, because the two superpowers are pursuing fundamentally different visions of what AI is for and how to get there.

What makes this conversation especially timely is a series of recent developments that have shaken the assumptions of both American technologists and policymakers. China, it turns out, is not merely catching up; it is executing a long-term strategy that prioritizes embedding AI throughout its economy in ways that American companies and regulators have barely begun to contemplate. And in January 2025, a little-known Chinese startup called DeepSeek demonstrated that Chinese AI could compete with Silicon Valley's leading models at a fraction of the cost—prompting comparisons to the Sputnik moment and forcing a recalibration of Western confidence in its technological edge. Wong's reporting traces how China arrived at this inflection point, what tensions exist within its approach, and why the answer to "who is winning" depends entirely on which metrics you choose to prioritize.

---

## China's Application-First Approach

The most striking distinction Wong identifies between American and Chinese approaches to AI is philosophical: Silicon Valley and U.S. policymakers largely frame the AI race around the pursuit of Artificial General Intelligence (AGI)—the hypothetical moment when AI matches or surpasses human cognitive capabilities across all domains. China, by contrast, has from the beginning talked about AI as a tool for making the economy operate more efficiently, solving concrete problems, and improving productivity across every sector.

This application-first orientation manifests visibly throughout Chinese daily life. Wong describes encountering autonomous vehicles deployed across numerous Chinese cities, robots serving in restaurants, patrolling streets, and working in factories. The ubiquity of these applications is not accidental—it reflects years of deliberate policy designed to put AI "in people's hands, in factories, everywhere throughout the economy." The goal has been diffusion, not a single moonshot achievement.

One vivid illustration of this cultural comfort with AI comes from Wong's interviews with Chinese parents who have found inventive ways to incorporate AI into their children's education. One mother purchased an AI device shaped like the mask worn by the villain Bane in *The Dark Knight Rises* films—a translation mask she would wear while speaking Chinese, which would then output her voice speaking English. She wore it around the house simply to help her children practice conversational English. Wong's observation is sharp: "You have to really love AI to put on a Bane mask to talk to your kid." Yet this anecdote captures something important about the cultural context in which Chinese AI adoption is occurring—public enthusiasm untainted by the existential anxieties that dominate American AI discourse.

Wong contrasts this attitude with the "doomer conversation" prevalent in American society, where people express widespread nervousness about AI either displacing their jobs or, more dramatically, poses an existential threat to humanity. In China, she found, "that conversation is just a lot quieter." The reasons for this optimism are not naiveté but pragmatism: China faces structural challenges so severe that AI represents not a philosophical curiosity but a practical lifeline.

---

## The Structural Case for AI in China

China's enthusiasm for AI deployment is driven by necessity as much as ambition. Wong explains that China confronts demographic and infrastructural problems that, without AI, appear nearly insoluble. The workforce is aging rapidly, with birth rates falling and the population beginning to contract—a trajectory that threatens the economic model China has relied upon for decades of growth. Young Chinese, facing economic pressures and changing social expectations, are having fewer children, meaning the ratio of workers to retirees will deteriorate sharply in coming decades. AI offers one of the few credible pathways to maintaining economic output as the workforce shrinks: intelligent automation can offset labor shortages by making factories more efficient and enabling greater output per worker.

The healthcare disparity between urban and rural China presents another structural pressure. China has far too few doctors relative to its population, and the concentration of medical talent in major cities means that hundreds of millions of rural residents have limited access to quality care. AI-powered diagnostic tools and telemedicine represent a potential solution—if computer vision systems can analyze medical images and language models can conduct initial patient interviews, the scarcity of human doctors becomes less constraining. China has therefore invested heavily in developing AI systems for medical applications, motivated by the sheer scale of need rather than market opportunity.

This framing—that AI is being counted on to solve existential-scale domestic problems—is what animates the Chinese government's optimism about the technology. Wong notes that "technology has been a big part of what has powered China's amazing economic rise" over the past several decades, and Chinese leadership views AI as simply the latest iteration of that trajectory. Xi Jinping in particular has consistently expressed enthusiasm for technology as a driver of national strength, making AI a logical priority for a government that measures success by economic growth and national prestige.

---

## The 2014 Speech and the 2017 AI Plan

The origins of China's formal AI strategy trace to a pivotal moment early in Xi Jinping's tenure as leader. In 2014, Xi delivered a speech to a gathering of China's top engineers and scientists, having reviewed their submitted recommendations for the country's future scientific direction. Among those recommendations was a report on intelligent robotics, and Xi explicitly highlighted this topic, declaring that he wanted China to become "the number one maker of intelligent robots" and to "dominate all sorts of other technologies that are going to be the future."

This moment established the political will that would coalesce into formal policy three years later. In 2017, China released its *New Generation AI Development Plan*—the nation's first comprehensive national-level roadmap for AI. The document set a clear goal: China would achieve AI leadership by 2030. More importantly, it established the guiding principle that leadership would be pursued through real-world applications integrated throughout the economy, not through basic research alone.

Given how China typically operates, the plan immediately triggered implementation across all levels of government. Provincial and municipal authorities competed to demonstrate compliance, building industrial parks designed to attract AI companies, offering tax breaks, providing subsidized or free office space, and directly funding recruitment of top talent. One researcher Wong profiles, Yong Zhao, was attempting to recreate a Silicon Valley work environment in Beijing's Zhuangguangshan district, explicitly citing that "it's easier to get funding here than in Silicon Valley." The mobilization was, in Wong's words, "this all-in national effort to deliver."

The results were visible within a few years. Chinese companies developed cutting-edge capabilities in facial recognition and voice recognition, areas where they became "some of the best in the world." Beijing police experimented with facial recognition glasses capable of identifying individuals linked to the government's national database, categorizing passersby by age, gender, and other characteristics. For a period in the late 2010s, China genuinely believed it had established a lead in AI development.

---

## The ChatGPT Shock

Everything changed with the arrival of ChatGPT in late 2022. The sudden emergence of a publicly accessible generative AI system that could produce fluent text, answer questions, and engage in open-ended conversation caught Chinese policymakers and technology companies off guard. Wong describes the moment as one that "caught everybody off guard," but the surprise was particularly acute for Chinese leaders who had been focused on narrow applications like facial recognition while the transformative potential of large language models was developing largely outside their strategic attention.

The implications for China's political system were immediately apparent to Beijing. Generative AI, by its nature, processes and synthesizes information from across the internet, producing outputs that are—by design—unpredictable and responsive to user prompts. Chinese authorities recognized this as a direct threat to their information control apparatus. The Communist Party maintains an extensive censorship regime designed to prevent Chinese citizens from accessing information about politically sensitive topics—Tiananmen Square, the treatment of Uyghurs in Xinjiang, criticism of top leaders, and countless other subjects deemed potentially destabilizing. A chatbot that could answer questions freely represented an end run around those controls.

"Here is proof that it also could be a problem," Wong explains, summarizing Beijing's realization. The government's response was swift: ChatGPT was banned in China, and regulators began developing a new framework specifically for generative AI. Within months, authorities released rules requiring that any Chinese AI company building a model capable of "mobilizing society" must first pass a series of government checks and clearances before public release. This language—mobilize society—is deliberately vague, giving authorities wide latitude to apply restrictions.

Wong clarifies what these safety requirements entail in the Chinese context. Western safety testing typically focuses on preventing models from generating advice on self-harm, hate speech, or other harmful content. Chinese safety testing includes those elements but adds the political dimension: models cannot provide answers about June 4th, 1989, or other politically sensitive historical events, nor can they respond to questions about China's top leaders in ways the government might find undesirable. The result is a fundamentally different risk calculus for Chinese AI developers compared to their Western counterparts.

---

## The DeepSeek Inflection Point

The tension between AI ambition and political control might have remained a theoretical concern, but in early 2025, a Chinese startup called DeepSeek forced the issue into the open. DeepSeek was created by a company that virtually no one in the technology world had heard of prior to its model's release. Its AI assistant performed roughly comparably to leading American models from OpenAI, Anthropic, and Google—yet the company claimed the model had been trained for approximately $5.6 million, a tiny fraction of the hundreds of millions invested in competing systems. The model was also released as open-source software, meaning anyone could examine its code, use it freely, and build upon it.

Wong describes the reaction as seismic. "It is mind-blowing and it is shaking this entire industry to its core." Technologists and investors scrambled to understand how a small Chinese company had achieved results competitive with American frontier models at such low cost—suggesting either that Chinese researchers had discovered significant efficiency gains or that the massive capital investments of American companies had been partly wasteful. Venture capitalist Marc Andreessen characterized the moment as "the Sputnik moment for the AI race"—an analogy suggesting that the United States had been comfortable in its presumed lead only to discover a foreign competitor had arrived at something transformative without anyone noticing.

For China, DeepSeek represented something equally significant: validation that Chinese AI could compete at the highest levels. Soon after the model's release, Xi Jinping convened a meeting with business leaders, including the DeepSeek founder—a young man in his 30s who had previously been unknown outside a small circle of AI researchers. At that meeting, Xi delivered a pointed message: "Go out and show your talents and serve the country." Wong interprets this as a significant signal that the government recognized it needed to give AI companies greater autonomy if it wanted them to remain competitive.

This was not merely ceremonial encouragement. Analysts Wong consulted described a fundamental recalibration of the government's stance toward AI development. The Chinese strategy now appears to be something analysts have phrased as: "control what AI says, unleash what it does." The government would maintain strict oversight over information-facing AI products—chatbots, content generators, anything that could directly influence how Chinese citizens form opinions about the world—but would substantially reduce restrictions on applications that do not directly touch political information. Robotics, manufacturing automation, medical diagnosis, agricultural optimization—all of these areas could develop with relatively light regulatory burden.

The concrete results of this shift were already visible by mid-2025. Wong reports that "2025 and this year have really just been huge moments of development in China's AI scene," with numerous new models being registered, startups gaining traction, and AI applications diffusing through the economy at an accelerating pace. The Chinese government's five-year plan, released in March 2025, mentioned AI more than fifty times—making clear that Beijing is staking its entire economic future on AI leadership.

## The Fundamental Tensions

Despite the momentum, Wong is careful not to declare China the unambiguous winner of the AI race, because the race itself remains poorly defined. The question "is China beating us?" depends entirely on which metrics you prioritize. If success is measured by the breadth of AI deployment throughout the economy, the integration of AI into daily life, and the practical application of AI to solve real-world productivity problems, China is arguably ahead. If success is measured by the development of the most powerful large language models, the ability to attract top global AI talent, and the freedom of AI researchers to pursue any direction without political constraints, the United States maintains advantages that may prove decisive.

The central tension Wong identifies is between China's need for openness and its insistence on control. AI development benefits from exposure to diverse data, international collaboration, and the free exchange of ideas—all of which create risks for a government whose legitimacy depends partly on controlling information flows. Wong articulates this as a tension that "exists in China's entire economic development," not just in AI: the government knows that "to allow its economy to flourish, it needs openness, it needs interaction with the rest of the world," but it simultaneously wants that interaction "on its own terms and within its own control." AI simply intensifies this tension because the most powerful models are trained on vast quantities of information, much of which the Chinese government considers dangerous.

The question of whether political restrictions are meaningfully degrading Chinese AI capabilities remains genuinely uncertain.
