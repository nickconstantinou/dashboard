    Why Even Some Democrats Hate California's Billionaire Tax Proposal

    On April 29th, 2025, California's political landscape shifted dramatically when word spread that a historic tax on billionaires had gathered enough signatures to appear on the November ballot. The proposal—a one-time 5% tax on the net worth of California billionaires—has ignited a firestorm of debate, pitting economic populists against the state's powerful tech elite, fracturing the Democratic coalition, and raising profound questions about taxation, residency, and the limits of progressive policymaking.

    The podcast features New York Times reporter Laurel Rosenhall, who has been tracking this measure since its inception, walking host Natalie Kittrell through the origins, mechanics, and political landmines embedded in what supporters call a necessary correction to an unequal system and opponents label as constitutionally dubious and economically dangerous.

    ---

    The Anatomy of an Unusual Tax

    What makes California's proposal so distinctive—and so contentious—stems from its novel approach to taxation. Rather than targeting income, the measure would tax assets: the stocks, jewelry, cars, and fine art that constitute a billionaire's net worth. This represents a fundamental departure from how governments typically extract revenue from the wealthy, who routinely minimize their income through complex financial structures while accumulating vast wealth in appreciating assets.

    The tax would apply to anyone classified as a California resident as of January 1st, 2025, with the revenue flowing into a special fund requiring that 90% be spent specifically on healthcare services. Proponents argue this structure directly addresses the catastrophic cuts to Medicaid and Obamacare subsidies embedded in what Republicans called their "big beautiful bill" signed by President Trump. The supporters—led by SEIU-UHW, a union representing hospital workers including janitors, cooks, and healthcare technicians—say the money is essential to keep hospitals open, emergency rooms operating, and the broader healthcare system functioning at its current level.

    Notably, the measure exempts residential property, creating an interesting carve-out that critics have noted leaves the most visible form of wealth relatively untouched. The union behind the proposal, led by Dave Regan, has a documented history of using ballot initiatives as leverage in labor negotiations—a background that some Democrats cite when explaining their hesitation to embrace the measure wholesale.

    ---

    The Exodus and the Anxiety

    The proposal sent tremors through California's billionaire class beginning late last year, as wealthy residents scrambled to understand how the measure might affect them and, in some cases, to change their official residency before the January 1st deadline. Several high-profile tech founders made well-publicized moves: Sergey Brin relocated to the Nevada side of Lake Tahoe, Larry Page departed for Florida, David Sachs moved to Texas, and Peter Thiel decamped for Miami. While wealthy people routinely threaten to leave whenever tax increases loom, in this case a handful actually followed through—or at least changed their official tax domicile while maintaining California properties.

    Those remaining have engaged in aggressive planning to minimize their tax exposure. At a wealth management conference in Orange County, Laurel Rosenhall observed tax advisors conducting sessions on helping clients reduce their net worth for assessment purposes: moving a Picasso from a Beverly Hills residence to a home in Aspen, or adjusting insurance policies on jewelry to reflect actual market value rather than inflated coverage amounts.

    Yet not all billionaires are fleeing or scheming. Some have adopted a more philosophical stance, accepting that a state that has enabled their prosperity might reasonably ask for additional contributions. "If we have to pay more, we will," represents one response among California's ultra-wealthy, though it appears to be the minority position among that class.

    ---

    The Democratic Fracture

    Perhaps more surprising than Republican opposition is the chorus of prominent Democrats who have come out against the measure—or at least declined to endorse it. Governor Gavin Newsom occupies the most visible and politically complicated position. A potential 2028 presidential contender, Newsom has been vocally opposed to the wealth tax while attempting to preserve his progressive credentials.

    His strategy involves two threads. First, he focuses his objection specifically on state-level wealth taxes, repeatedly stating that California's taxation of the wealthy is already highly progressive through its income tax structure. Second, he advocates for national solutions, leaving open the possibility that he supports wealth taxation in principle while arguing it should be implemented uniformly across the country rather than putting California at a competitive disadvantage.

    This careful positioning allows Newsom to agree with the "moral argument" for taxing billionaires—words he has actually used—while opposing the specific mechanism before voters. It is a genuine tightrope walk, acknowledging that economic inequality demands response while refusing to embrace what he sees as a flawed state-level approach.

    Other influential Democrats have expressed similar concerns. Gubernatorial candidates have hedged their support for taxing the wealthy generally by raising objections to the measure's structure. The California Teachers Union and the umbrella organization for SEIU nationally have not taken public positions, with private expressions of concern focused on the "special fund" requirement that would lock the revenue away from general budget priorities.

    The argument from these skeptics centers on the state's dependence on income taxes from high earners—taxes that fund schools, public safety, roads, and other essential services. Even if the wealth tax generates a short-term revenue bump, they argue, the long-term loss of income tax revenue from those who leave could prove catastrophic. Silicon Valley's technology industry has been particularly vocal, arguing that the measure would devastate California's innovation ecosystem by punishing the risk-takers and job creators who have made the state the world's technology capital.

    ---

    The Bernie Sanders Position

    Counterposing this cautious approach is the energetic support from the progressive wing of the Democratic Party, embodied by Bernie Sanders, who flew to Los Angeles to lead a rally in support of the measure. For Sanders and his allies, the particulars matter less than the principle: billionaires who have accumulated unprecedented wealth should contribute more to the society that enabled their success.

    "Don't let the perfect be the enemy of the good," represents the core progressive argument. The measure might not solve inequality entirely or represent the optimal tax structure, but it represents a meaningful step toward asking more from those who have so much. Sanders doesn't have to govern California or worry about balancing budgets; he can embrace the concept without wrestling with implementation details.

    This philosophical divide exposes a genuine tension within the Democratic coalition between those who prioritize pragmatic governance and those who see tax proposals as moral statements about the kind of society Americans want to live in.

    ---

    The Path Forward

    As the measure moves toward November, multiple scenarios remain possible. California has a long history of using ballot initiatives as leverage to extract concessions from elected officials, and there are already signs that backroom negotiations may attempt to keep the measure off the ballot entirely. Sergey Brin's committee has funded counter-measures that could potentially negate the billionaire tax, creating negotiating leverage that Governor Newsom might exploit in conversations with union leadership.

    If the measure does reach voters, it will trigger an extraordinarily expensive campaign season, with conflicting messages about taxation, healthcare, and California's economic future. The measure's supporters will argue for justice and affordability; opponents will warn of exodus, constitutional overreach, and harm to the innovation economy.

    Laurel Rosenhall identifies three broader lessons for other states watching California's experiment. First, taxing the wealthy remains politically popular—perhaps more popular than ever in an era of economic anxiety and anti-Trump sentiment. Second, actually implementing such taxes proves extraordinarily difficult due to structural complexities and the political influence of wealthy opponents. Third, those seeking to tax concentrated wealth need exceptionally broad coalitions to succeed, because the opposition will be well-funded, sophisticated, and determined.

    ---

    The Deeper Reckoning

    What makes this measure significant extends beyond its specific provisions. It forces a reckoning with fundamental questions about wealth, taxation, and civic obligation that Americans have largely avoided confronting directly. For billionaires and aspiring tech moguls, it represents an "existential threat"—the possibility that government might systematically tax assets rather than just income, potentially establishing precedents that reshape the relationship between citizens and their accumulated wealth.

    For supporters, the measure represents the opposite: an opportunity to finally demand that those who have benefited most from American society contribute proportionally to maintaining the infrastructure and services that made their success possible.

    Whether California voters embrace this vision or recoil from its implications, the debate has already shifted the parameters of acceptable discourse around taxation and inequality. Whatever happens in November, this conversation will continue shaping American politics for years to come.
