# The Greatest Game of All Time: Jensen Huang's Surprising Answer and the Future of Gaming Technology

## An exploration of how id Software's Doom transformed the PC industry and why Jensen Huang believes ray tracing represents gaming's next revolution

---

In a fascinating conversation that bridges the gap between gaming nostalgia and cutting-edge technology, NVIDIA CEO Jensen Huang recently sat down with Lex Fridman to discuss one of the most deceptively simple questions in the industry: what is the greatest or most influential game ever made? The discussion, which spans gaming history from the early days of 3D graphics to the modern era of photorealistic ray tracing, offers remarkable insight into how one of technology's most influential figures thinks about games, innovation, and the cultural forces that shape entire industries. Huang's answer—while perhaps unexpected to some—reveals a deep understanding of how transformative technologies often succeed not through superior specifications alone, but through the perfect convergence of cultural timing, technical innovation, and democratized access.

The conversation takes place against the backdrop of an industry that has undergone radical transformation over the past three decades. From the era when games were primarily played on specialized hardware to today's world where powerful GPUs sit in everything from gaming PCs to data centers powering artificial intelligence, the trajectory of interactive entertainment has been inextricably linked with the evolution of graphics technology. As the leader of the company that has come to define modern GPU technology, Huang offers a uniquely qualified perspective on which games have genuinely moved the needle—not just in terms of entertainment value, but in their ability to fundamentally reshape consumer expectations and industry trajectories.

## Doom: The Unexpected Choice That Changed Everything

When Jensen Huang was asked to identify the most influential game from NVIDIA's perspective, his answer came without hesitation: **Doom**, developed by id Software and released in 1993. The certainty in his response reflects not merely personal preference but a deep appreciation for the historical significance of what id Software accomplished during that pivotal year in gaming history. "Unquestionably, that was the start of the 3D era," Huang explained, framing Doom not as one influential game among many, but as the definitive beginning of an entirely new paradigm in interactive entertainment.

What makes Doom particularly significant in Huang's analysis is not its technical specifications in isolation, but rather the intersection of technological innovation and cultural impact that it achieved. While flight simulation companies had existed before Doom and had demonstrated the potential for compelling interactive experiences on personal computers, these earlier efforts remained confined to relatively niche audiences. "They just didn't have the popularity that Doom did to have made the industry turn the PC from an office automation tool into a personal computer for families and gamers," Huang observed, articulating precisely why Doom deserves its place in gaming history. The game transformed the personal computer from a tool associated primarily with workplace productivity into a device that families would bring into their homes for entertainment purposes—a shift that fundamentally altered the trajectory of the entire consumer electronics industry.

The technological innovations introduced by Doom extended far beyond its impressive-for-the-era 3D graphics engine. The game popularized the concept of **networked multiplayer gaming**, demonstrating that personal computers could serve as platforms for shared social experiences. Its shareware distribution model proved that compelling software could reach audiences through unconventional channels, bypassing traditional retail barriers. The game's distinctive aesthetic—combining industrial environments with hellish supernatural elements—established visual vocabulary that would influence countless subsequent titles. Perhaps most importantly, id Software's approach to engine technology and modular game design created blueprints that developers would follow for years to come, with the Doom engine's source code eventually being released to the public, spawning an entire ecosystem of community-created content and derivatives.

## Virtual Fighter and the Technology Frontier

While Doom represents Huang's choice for the most culturally and industrially significant game, the NVIDIA CEO was careful to distinguish between different categories of gaming achievement. When the conversation turned toward pure technological advancement, Huang's answer shifted to **Virtual Fighter**, the SEGA Saturn launch title developed by Sega AM2 and released in 1993. "From an actual game technology perspective, I would say Virtual Fighter," he noted, drawing a meaningful distinction between games that transformed the industry and games that pushed the boundaries of what interactive entertainment could technically achieve.

This careful categorization reveals Huang's nuanced understanding of gaming history. Virtual Fighter represented a different kind of innovation than Doom—while id Software's title democratized access to 3D graphics and created a mass-market phenomenon, SEGA's fighter pioneered the technical implementation of polygon-based 3D environments that would become standard in subsequent generations of gaming hardware. The game demonstrated that home consoles could deliver experiences previously reserved for expensive arcade machines, raising consumer expectations for what was possible in domestic settings. That Huang counts both Sega and id Software among his friends speaks to his appreciation for the different forms of contribution these companies made to gaming's evolution.

The comparison between these two landmark titles illuminates an important truth about technological progress: innovation rarely follows a single path, and transformative moments in any industry typically involve multiple contributions working in concert. Doom showed what was possible when revolutionary technology met accessible design and effective distribution; Virtual Fighter demonstrated the technical frontier that such innovation would eventually expand to encompass. Together, these games established the dual poles around which gaming's next three decades would oscillate—mass-market accessibility and technical ambition, cultural phenomenon and engineering achievement.

## Modern Gaming: Cyberpunk, Skyrim, and the Art of Replayability

The conversation with Lex Fridman also explored more recent territory, with Huang discussing his personal engagement with contemporary games. His selections reveal a philosophy about what makes games genuinely excellent—one that balances appreciation for technical achievement with recognition of deeper design qualities that transcend raw graphical fidelity. **Cyberpunk 2077** emerged as his primary example of a modern title that showcases the current state of graphics technology, specifically praising the game's implementation of full ray tracing: "really nice GPU accelerated graphics, like fully ray traced, fully ray traced."

Cyberpunk 2077 represents a significant milestone in Huang's view precisely because it demonstrates how far graphics technology has progressed. Ray tracing—the computational technique that simulates the physical behavior of light to create photorealistic images—has been a theoretical goal in computer graphics for decades, but practical implementation at playable frame rates required the kind of parallel processing power that modern GPUs provide. The game serves as a benchmark for what becomes possible when hardware catches up with theoretical possibilities, showcasing reflections, shadows, and global illumination that more closely approximate how light behaves in the physical world than any previous real-time rendering technique.

Yet Huang's most effusive personal praise was reserved not for the most technically advanced title, but for a game that first released in 2011: **The Elder Scrolls V: Skyrim**. "I personally I'm a huge fan of Skyrim," he confessed, before elaborating on precisely what draws him back to Bethesda's open-world RPG repeatedly over more than a decade. "People released mods and it's like a different game. It just allows me to replay the game over and over, and it makes you realize that you can reexperience in a totally new way the world you already love."

This enthusiasm for Skyrim's moddability reflects a sophisticated understanding of what extends a game's lifespan and value. Rather than viewing graphics as the sole determinant of quality, Huang recognizes that the greatest games create worlds compelling enough to return to repeatedly, and that those worlds can be continually refreshed through community creativity. The modding community has transformed Skyrim into virtually countless different experiences, from total conversion projects that overhaul every aspect of the game to subtle enhancements that refine specific elements. This ecosystem of creative participation extends far beyond what the original developers could have accomplished alone, and it represents a model for how games can remain relevant and engaging for years after their initial release.

## RTX Mods: Bringing Modern Technology to Classic Experiences

Huang's discussion of Skyrim naturally led to an announcement of particular significance for both gaming enthusiasts and technology historians: NVIDIA's development of **RTX Mod**, a modding tool specifically designed to inject ray tracing technology into older games that were originally designed without such capabilities. "We created this thing called RTX Mod," Huang explained. "It allows the community to inject the latest technology into an old game."

This initiative reflects a philosophy that bridges nostalgia with innovation. Rather than requiring players to abandon beloved older titles in favor of newer releases, RTX Mod allows communities to enhance classic experiences with contemporary graphical techniques. For Skyrim specifically, this means that players can experience the familiar world they know and love with modern lighting, reflections, and shadows—the kind of visual enhancements that make modern games so immersive, applied retroactively to titles they already treasure.

The technical challenges involved in this process are substantial. Games like Skyrim were built around rendering pipelines fundamentally different from those required for real-time ray tracing. Retrofitting such games to support these techniques requires careful engineering to maintain compatibility with existing assets and gameplay systems while introducing new visual capabilities. Yet Huang's enthusiasm for the project suggests that NVIDIA considers this kind of enhancement valuable enough to justify significant development investment—a testament to the enduring appeal of games that have already been enjoyed by millions of players for years or even decades.

## The Balanced Vision: Graphics, Story, and the Immersive Experience

Throughout the conversation, Huang demonstrated awareness that technical excellence alone does not make a great game. When Fridman asked about the relationship between graphics quality and other game elements, Huang offered an thoughtful synthesis: "What makes a great video game is not just graphics. It's also story and character development. But that's right—beautiful graphics can add to the immersion, the feeling like it's another place you're transported to."

This perspective acknowledges the complementary nature of different game design elements. While graphics cannot salvage a poorly written narrative or unengaging gameplay loop, they can elevate an experience from merely competent to genuinely transportive. The sense of being present in another world, of feeling the atmospheric weight of a midnight rainstorm or the oppressive grandeur of an ancient dungeon, depends significantly on the quality of visual presentation. Modern ray tracing techniques contribute to this immersion by creating lighting conditions that feel natural and consistent with physical reality, eliminating the artificial artifacts that characterized earlier rendering approaches and allowing players to suspend disbelief more completely.

Huang's balanced view suggests someone who has thought carefully about technology's role in creative expression. As the CEO of a company whose hardware enables the creation of ever-more-impressive visual experiences, he could easily have prioritized technical achievement as the supreme virtue in game design. That he explicitly recognizes the equal importance of narrative and character development indicates a sophisticated understanding of how games function as art forms rather than mere technical demonstrations. The greatest games, in his implicit framework, are those that combine all available tools—storytelling craft, character depth, world-building ambition, and visual presentation—into coherent experiences that transport players to places they genuinely want to inhabit.

## Key Insights from Jensen Huang on Gaming History and Technology

**The cultural timing of technological innovation matters as much as the innovation itself.** Doom succeeded not merely because it introduced 3D graphics, but because it did so at a moment when the personal computer industry was ripe for transformation. Understanding when and why audiences are ready to embrace new technologies is essential for anyone seeking to create genuine industry impact.

**Different games contribute to progress in different ways.** Virtual Fighter's technical innovations were no less valuable than Doom's cultural achievements, even though they operated in different registers. The gaming industry's progress depends on the simultaneous advancement of multiple fronts—accessibility and ambition, distribution innovation and engineering achievement, cultural resonance and technical capability.

**Replayability through community creativity extends the life and value of games far beyond initial release.** Skyrim's enduring appeal demonstrates that games which facilitate community modification can remain engaging for decades. This principle suggests important lessons for developers about designing for extensibility and creating worlds compelling enough to justify repeated exploration in new contexts.

**Modern technology can enhance rather than replace classic experiences.** RTX Mod represents a philosophical stance toward technological progress that values continuity alongside innovation. Rather than rendering older games obsolete, modern capabilities can be applied to enhance them, honoring the creative work that went into them while demonstrating the continued relevance of the latest techniques.

**Technical excellence serves expressive purposes rather than existing for its own sake.** Huang's recognition that "beautiful graphics can add to the immersion" frames technological advancement as serving human experience rather than existing as a self-justifying goal. The most successful technology, in his view, is technology that disappears into the experience it enables, transporting users to compelling fictional worlds rather than drawing attention to its own sophistication.
