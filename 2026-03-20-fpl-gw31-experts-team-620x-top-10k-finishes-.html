Transfer Strategy: When to Act and When to Wait

The data reveals that approximately 80% of top managers are making at least one transfer this gameweek, but this doesn't mean transfers are always the right move. The speaker emphasizes that those managers currently fielding a complete XI without major upside available should seriously consider leaving their team as is. The reasoning is straightforward: while a transfer like Audley Anderson to Jensen might gain you four points if Fulham keep a clean sheet and Sunderland concede, the opportunity cost of using that transfer now versus saving it for later double gameweeks could be substantially higher.

The critical insight here is understanding the fixture schedule ahead. With a double gameweek 33 and another double gameweek 36 expected, plus potentially a blank gameweek 34, the business end of the season rewards those who maintain transfer currency. The speaker notes that smaller doubles or blanks might appear elsewhere, making banked transfers potentially even more valuable. This is why the top experts view transfer preservation as "king" during blank weeks—every move saved now could be crucial when the fixture congestion arrives.

However, there's a nuance to this strategy. Andrew Moore himself finds himself in a position where he's fielding fewer than 11 players for the upcoming gameweek, meaning he has no choice but to make at least one move. His specific solution involves transferring out Gabriel and Rice to bring in Bowen and Wilson. This illustrates that while preservation is ideal, sometimes circumstances dictate action, and the best managers adapt rather than stick rigidly to a plan that no longer fits their situation.

The Chip Strategy Debate

Perhaps the most valuable strategic discussion in the video revolves around chip usage, which represents one of the most consequential decisions FPL managers face in the season's final third. The speaker outlines two competing strategies that experts are debating.

The first, and currently more popular approach, involves wild carding in Gameweek 32, using bench boost in Gameweek 33, and potentially taking a hit in Gameweek 34. This strategy aims to maximize points from the immediate fixtures while accepting the complexity of navigating Gameweek 34's blank.

The alternative strategy, which the speaker suggests may actually be optimal, involves saving the wild card entirely and instead using a free hit in Gameweek 33 (which is expected to be a double), then wild-carding in Gameweek 35 before using bench boost in Gameweek 36. This approach becomes particularly attractive if you don't own many players from the teams likely to blank in Gameweek 34—specifically Manchester City, Arsenal, and Chelsea. The speaker admits they're personally in this position, noting they can already feel difficulty fielding a strong outfield team in Gameweek 34 and would need a goalkeeper transfer to make it work.

However, the wildcard 32 route has its own drawbacks. The speaker expresses wariness about filling five or six squad spots with players from Brighton, Newcastle, and Burnley specifically for bench boost in Gameweek 33, then spending the rest of the season trying to fix team structure issues that creates.

The Crystal Palace situation adds another layer of complexity. Their Europa Conference League fixture and potential FA Cup match against Manchester City could significantly alter the fixture calendar. If Palace go out of Europe, their game against Manchester City could move from Gameweek 36 forward to Gameweek 32, potentially creating a double in 32 followed by another double in 33. This would dramatically change the optimal strategy for anyone holding Manchester City or Palace players.

Detailed Takeaways

First, prioritize transfer preservation for the double gameweeks rather than maximizing points in blanks. The fixture schedule over the next several weeks—with expected doubles in 33 and 36 plus blanks in 31 and 34—means every transfer saved now could be critical later. The math often favors waiting, even when immediate moves seem tempting.

Second, Joao Pedro represents the standout forward target this week despite not being a striker. His combination of goal threat (four goals this season, joint-most among defenders), corner-taking responsibility, and better fixture coverage than Anderson beyond Gameweek 31 makes him worth the investment, particularly for those using free hit chips.

Third, chip strategy depends heavily on your current squad composition. If you already own Manchester City, Arsenal, and Chelsea players, the wild card 32 approach may suit you. If not, the free hit 33, wild card 35, bench boost 36 route likely offers better value. The Palace situation could swing this decision significantly depending on European results.

Fourth, don't panic-sell Haaland despite the blank. His ownership remains above 60%, and while there's only approximately a 5% chance Manchester City versus Palace moves to Gameweek 31, if it does, you'd want him in your team. The worst case is he auto-substitutes out, and an announcement would likely come before the deadline if the fixture changes.

Fifth, Bruno Fernandes is undroppable for captaincy this week regardless of formation. The statistics are emphatic—he leads in every meaningful creative metric while maintaining elite goal threat. With 91% of experts backing him, going against this consensus requires extraordinary justification.
