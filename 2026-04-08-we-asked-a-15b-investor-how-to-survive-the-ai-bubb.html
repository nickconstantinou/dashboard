# Mastering the FPL Wildcard: A Strategic Guide for the Season's Critical Final Weeks

Introduction

The Fantasy Premier League season has reached its climactic phase, and for millions of managers worldwide, the decisions made in the coming weeks could determine whether they finish in the glory of the top ranks or languish in the mediocrity of mid-table obscurity. This is precisely the strategic crossroads that FPL Harry addresses in his comprehensive analysis of the two primary wildcard strategies available to managers as they navigate gameweeks 32 through 37.

FPL Harry, a content creator who has returned after a technical break, has refined his presentation—including improved camera work and channel settings—while delivering what he explicitly describes as "a really close call" between the competing strategic paths. The core tension he explores revolves around timing the wildcard chip: should managers deploy their wildcard in gameweek 32 to maximize the bench boost in gameweek 33, or should they preserve it until gameweek 35 to capitalize on favorable fixtures later in the season? This decision, according to Harry, cannot be made in isolation—it requires managers to honestly evaluate their current squad, their remaining transfers, and their appetite for risk.

What makes this analysis particularly valuable is that Harry doesn't merely present one path as superior. Instead, he walks through both strategies in detail, examining the team drafts, the fixture Planning sites like FPL.team, and the specific player movements that would characterize each approach. He acknowledges upfront that his own team currently has five free transfers and just £0.1 million in the bank, which fundamentally shapes his strategic preferences. For managers working with different resources, the calculus will necessarily differ—a point Harry emphasizes throughout his analysis.

The Current Squad Assessment

Before diving into the wildcard strategies themselves, Harry first evaluates his present team situation as it stands heading into gameweek 32. This assessment is crucial because the decision of whether to wildcard depends heavily on whether you believe your current squad needs significant reorganization.

Harry explains that he views his team as "pretty happy" with how it looks at present, which sets the stage for his preference toward preserving the wildcard for later use. He notes that while there are "luxury changes" he could make—perhaps upgrading Júku Bhour or finding more attractive alternatives to players like Echo or Tarasovski—he doesn't look at his squad and feel like he "definitely need[s] a wild card." This distinction between desirable improvements and essential changes forms one of the key decision-making frameworks Harry advocates.

The speaker identifies several specific players whose situations bear watching. The competition between Richard and Taroski in his squad represents "a really close call," while the Wilson versus Juh-Hall debate at the forward position mirrors broader discussions happening across the FPL community. Harry mentions possibly considering a double Everton option, though he acknowledges that Harry Wilson's goal-scoring form makes this "really close" to call.

Harry's single planned transfer for gameweek 32 involves moving Echitique out to bring in Jared Burn, a move justified by multiple factors. First, Ekit's minutes "either side of the PSG games" represent "a slight risk" in Harry's assessment. Second, Burn offers appealing fixtures: Wolves at home in gameweek 32 and Everton at home in gameweek 34—the precise weeks managers using the gameweek 35 wildcard strategy would need to consideration. This dual fixture appeal makes Burn what Harry calls "a good potential buy" positioned in the upper echelons of the fixture difficulty graphic.

Strategy Path One: Wildcard 32, Bench Boost 33, Free Hit 34

The Rationale

The first strategic path Harry examines involves playing the wildcard in gameweek 32, immediately followed by the bench boost in gameweek 33, and then deploying a free hit in gameweek 34. This approach prioritizes immediate squad optimization and banked points from the bench boost, accepting that some fixture disadvantages may arise later in the season when wildcard resources are depleted.

Harry presents what an early free hit draft for gameweek 33 would look like, noting that "Darlow Stra and Cal Leuen" feature prominently with "Antoine Stack looking like he's a doubt." His approach involves a triple-up on Leeds, reasoning that "their double is slightly better than that of Bournemouth" when comparing home and away fixtures. The triple Manchester City coverage includes O'Reilly, Semeno, and Harland, with Cole Palmer as a captaincy option and "Erling Harland for the captaincy" in what appears to be a separate discussion point.

The midfield options Harry explores include Pascal Gross, though he acknowledges this is "quite up in the air at the moment" and could alternatively feature Ryan or Antoine Stack if fit. This flexibility in the draft reflects a broader truth Harry acknowledges: the free hit strategy in gameweek 33 "looks pretty similar to all the teams that people who wildcarded in 32 will have—you're not getting that much benefit."

The Trade-offs

Harry is notably pragmatic about the limitations of the wildcard-32 approach. He states directly that managers using this strategy "basically going to be freehitting the same starting 11 that people who wildcarded in 33 have in 33, but just not play the bench boost on top." The marginal benefits relate primarily to having "an extra Leeds player" or the flexibility to make changes if someone gets injured.

Perhaps more importantly, Harry raises questions about long-term player ownership. He asks whether managers want "Palmer people" from the double, and extends this inquiry to other popular choices like "triple Leeds" or "triple Bournemouth having Brighton players long term." His conclusion is that while some long-term Leeds fixes "are actually not bad," generally "there are better players to pick long term." This suggests that the wildcard-32 strategy may sacrifice longer-term squad quality for immediate points.

Looking ahead to gameweek 34 under this approach, Harry presents an updated squad view with "Van Dijk starting over either Anderson or Taroski"—likely meaning Taroski drops to the bench again. The single transfer available could address what Harry identify as "Chargo away at Manchester United is probably my weakest point." He mentions potentially "selling Erling Harland" to fund "Mo Salah," though this would require careful financial planning across two transfers.

Strategy Path Two: Wildcard 35, Preserve Resources

The Rationale

The second strategic path involves carrying transfers forward and deploying the wildcard in gameweek 35 instead. This approach accepts lower bench boost value in exchange for greater flexibility and potentially superior squad quality in the final weeks of the season.

Harry presents a gameweek 35 wildcard draft featuring "a triple up on the Arsenal defense at the moment with Fulham at home, O'Reilly, Semeno, and again it's taken out here, but it will be Erling Harland." The midfielder selections include "Calbert Lewen, Jar Pedro Gordon, Semeno, Bruno" with what Harry describes as "a triple up on Crystal Palace with their plan double"—referring to Crystal Palace's favorable fixtures in gameweek 36.

This strategy also incorporates planning for gameweek 36, where "Darlow at home to Brighton" would Triple-up alongside Palace coverage, and potentially Manchester City if they receive a double fixture. The squad composition includes "Rya, Gabrielle, Bruno, Juice Hall, Anthony Gordon"—players selected for their fixture runs rather than their immediate appeal.

Harry explicitly states that "the bench boost looks slightly better in 37" compared to gameweek 36, though he acknowledges this is "not something we massively have to decide this week." His specific plan for gameweek 37 involves "Darlow at home to Brighton," with potential transfers including "Richards to Harry Maguire" and maintaining players like "Jbury Hall" and "Daniels" in defense.

The Advantage of Spare Transfers

A critical element of the wildcard-35 strategy, and one Harry emphasizes throughout, is the value of preserved transfers. He notes that "I have a lot of spare transfers" allowing him "to be more aggressive with things like buying Solarin 34 if I wanted to" or making "bigger upgrades on the likes of Tarovski, Echitik this week." These transferred resources provide optionality that managers using the wildcard-32 approach simply won't have.

Harry summarizes that the wildcard-35 strategy "is definitely better if you don't have lots of transfers available" because "it allows you to sort of cut the remainder of the" season more effectively. For managers who have already used several transfers and find themselves short on resources, delaying the wildcard becomes not merely acceptable but optimal.

Key Strategic Insights

Insight One: Evaluate Your Current Team Honestly

The most fundamental principle Harry emphasizes is that wildcard decisions should flow from honest squad assessment rather than abstract strategy preference. "Are there enough potential changes that you would look to make to your team?" Harry asks, noting that his own answer is negative—he doesn't feel he "definitely need a wild card" with his current squad. Managers convinced they require significant changes should wildcard; those with serviceable squads should preserve their wildcard for later.

Insight Two: Fixture Runs Matter More Than Single Gameweeks

Throughout his analysis, Harry demonstrates that fixture difficulty across multiple gameweeks should drive player selection more than individual matchups. The decision to target Burn in gameweek 32 specifically leverages his favorable fixtures in both gameweeks 32 and 34—the critical weeks for wildcard-35 strategists. Similarly, the triple-ups on Leeds and Palace in specific gameweeks reflect fixture runs rather than team quality in isolation.

Insight Three: Preserve Optionality Through Unused Transfers

Harry explicitly values preserved transfers as "optionality"—the ability to respond to emerging situations like injuries or unexpected form changes. Managers using the wildcard-35 strategy can "be more aggressive" because they know they have "lots of spare transfers further down the line." This optionality has real value that shouldn't be discounted when making wildcard timing decisions.

Insight Four: The Bench Boost Trade-off Is Real

Harry acknowledges that the bench boost in gameweek 33 offers tangible value, but he contextualizes this against the alternative approach: "The benefit to that is maybe some of these players like Palmer people don't want longterm, but you might want him from the double." The trade-off between guaranteed bench boost points and preserved wildcard flexibility represents the core strategic tension every manager must resolve based on their specific circumstances.

Insight Five: Late-Season Fixtures Often Favor Certain Teams

The analysis reveals that late-season fixture lists often cluster favorable matches for specific teams, making targeted chips extremely valuable. Harry's focus on Crystal Palace's gameweek 36 double and Manchester City's potential double reflects this reality: certain teams' fixture congestion creates exploitable advantages for managers who preserve their wildcard resources.
