    Introduction

    In a warm and candid closing segment of an extended conversation about software, open source culture, and technology at scale, the conversation turns toward something more personal: the beloved Twitter presence of two foundational figures in video processing — the creators and maintainers of FFmpeg and VLC media player. What begins as a lighthearted question about favorite and embarrassing tweets becomes a meditation on the unexpected reach of free software, the joy of seeing your work run on another planet, and the philosophical underpinnings that have driven decades of volunteer-driven development.

    The exchange captures something rare in technology journalism: the unguarded enthusiasm of engineers who built tools used by billions of people without seeking fame or fortune. Laurent and Jean-Marie — the two figures at the center of this conversation — have spent decades maintaining software that decodes and encodes video for nearly every major platform on Earth and, as we learn, on Mars. The conversation reveals both the absurdity and profundity of open source: the ability for a small group of dedicated people to create infrastructure that powers Formula 1 broadcasts, CERN particle accelerators, and space exploration.

    FFmpeg Beyond Earth: The Mars Rover and the Philosophy of "Send Patches"

    When Lex Fridman asks about favorite tweets associated with either FFmpeg or VideoLAN (the organization behind VLC), the answer comes quickly and with evident pride. The first favorite is the now-iconic phrase "Talk is cheap. Send patches." The speaker explains that this simple statement embodies much of what the open source community represents: the understanding that meaningful work doesn't happen through discussion alone. Things don't get built unless someone does the work. Ideas don't materialize from the ether. The philosophy is that action — writing code, submitting patches, doing the unglamorous work — is what actually moves the world forward.

    The second favorite tweet is one that speaks to the ambition and reach of the project: "FFmpeg — nothing is beyond our reach." This phrase has an interesting origin, drawn from a US military satellite patch where engineers apparently designed some kind of global monitoring system. The connection between military satellite imagery and a community-driven video processing library might seem odd, but it captures something essential about FFmpeg's scope and ambition.

    The conversation then turns to an extraordinary fact that Lex already knows: FFmpeg runs on the Mars 2020 rover. The speaker describes how NASA engineers on the Mars mission "really wanted" to use commercial off-the-shelf technology wherever possible, and FFmpeg was chosen to handle image compression for the rover's cameras. This isn't a footnote or a trivia fact — it's a point of genuine pride. FFmpeg has literally left the planet. It is, as the speaker puts it, "a multi-planetary open source library." The implications are striking: the software that started as a volunteer project to process video now plays a role in humanity's exploration of another world.

    VLC at the World's Largest Science Facility: CERN and the 27-Kilometer Ring

    If FFmpeg on Mars wasn't impressive enough, the conversation turns to an equally remarkable deployment: VLC media player running at CERN, the European Organization for Nuclear Research and home to the Large Hadron Collider, the world's largest and most powerful particle accelerator. The ring itself spans 27 kilometers — a circumference that requires sophisticated monitoring systems to track what happens along its entire length.

    The speaker recounts a visit to CERN in 2010 with Laurent, one of the VLC project founders. CERN was using VLC in a deeply practical way: analog cameras positioned around the accelerator ring were feeding into capture cards, and VLC was receiving those signals to stream across CERN's multicast network. This meant that thousands of scientists and engineers throughout the massive facility could access live feeds from any point in the accelerator ring.     When they visited, there was an issue with the setup — likely related to parameters that weren't well documented at the time. The team, according to the speaker, fixed it "in an hour or something like that." But what made the visit extraordinary wasn't just the technical problem-solving. The team spent the rest of the day touring CERN's facilities, including areas dealing with antimatter research and particle colliders. The speaker describes this as "one of the most amazing days of my physics background." The intersection of open source software development and fundamental physics research — two fields the speaker clearly loves — created a deeply memorable experience.

    From Formula 1 to SpaceX: The Ubiquity of VLC in Unexpected Places

    The conversation expands to reveal just how pervasive these tools have become. In Formula 1 paddocks, engineers use VLC to play live feeds — essential for reviewing telemetry, analyzing races, and coordinating team operations across the sport's global calendar. The European Space Agency relies on it. SpaceX uses VLC to monitor rocket launches, tracking the live video feeds that allow engineers to assess vehicle performance in real time.

    The speaker's reaction to this widespread adoption is telling: "like this like fills you with joy, right?" There's genuine wonder in realizing that software you helped build, often in your spare time with no expectation of widespread adoption, ends up running on the Mars rover, inside the world's most advanced particle accelerator, and at the heart of commercial space exploration. This is the reward that open source developers rarely discuss publicly — the moment you discover your work matters in ways you never anticipated.

    On Regrets, Emotions, and the Tax on the Mind

    Switching gears, Lex asks a more personal question: are there any tweets the speaker regrets? The answer is immediate and philosophical. Referencing the French song "Je ne regrette rien" — "I regret nothing," famously sung by Édith Piaf — the speaker says this philosophy is "very important" to him.

    His reasoning reveals the thinking of someone who has spent decades in a demanding, often thankless field: "Regrets are a tax on your mind." You can't change the past. You can only learn from mistakes. Regret accomplishes nothing except draining mental energy that could be better spent on future work. The key distinction is between learning from mistakes — which is valuable — and wallowing in regret, which he frames as a kind of tax: a cost you pay for no return.

    The speaker extends this idea by referencing a Johnny Depp quote he encountered: "Hate is a very expensive emotion." The parallel is clear — both regret and hate exact a psychological cost without producing any benefit. The philosophy of open source maintenance, where contributors must weather criticism, manage community conflicts, and absorb the weight of millions of users' expectations, likely informed this perspective. Moving forward matters more than looking back.

    Detailed Takeaways

    1. "Talk is cheap. Send patches" is more than a motto — it's a philosophy of value. In an era where tech discourse is dominated by hot takes, speculation, and endless discussion, the FFmpeg community's emphasis on actually writing code reminds us that ideas without implementation are noise. The most impactful software in the world was built by people who sat down and coded, not by people who debated. This applies beyond software: any field where talk is plentiful and action is scarce benefits from this mindset.

    2. Commercial off-the-shelf technology can outperform custom solutions even in extreme environments. NASA's decision to use FFmpeg on the Mars rover rather than building custom image processing software reflects the maturity and reliability of open source tools. When something runs successfully on another planet, it has proven itself under conditions far more demanding than typical use cases. Organizations doing specialized work should evaluate open source tools as viable — often preferable — alternatives to proprietary or custom-built systems.

    3. The distributed nature of open source means unexpected deployments will constantly surprise you. Neither the FFmpeg nor VLC developers anticipated their software being used at CERN or on Mars. This is the nature of free, open software: once released, it can be adopted by anyone for any purpose. Maintainers should see this not as a loss of control but as a measure of impact. If your software is surprising you with where it appears, it's succeeding.

    4. Regret is a choice, and it's always expensive. The speaker's framing of regret as a "tax on your mind" offers a practical framework for anyone in high-pressure work. Learning from mistakes is essential; living in them is not. The parallel to hate as an "expensive emotion" extends this into interpersonal domains. People building things in public — as open source maintainers do — face constant criticism and must develop frameworks for processing it without letting it tax their effectiveness.

    5. The emotional reward of building infrastructure is seeing it survive you. The speaker's joy at learning about VLC at CERN, SpaceX launches, and the Mars rover isn't vanity — it's the recognition that the work has transcended its origins. Infrastructure software lives longer and reaches further than individual developers can predict. The satisfaction of seeing your code operate at the edge of human capability — literally another planet — is the ultimate validation of a life's work.
