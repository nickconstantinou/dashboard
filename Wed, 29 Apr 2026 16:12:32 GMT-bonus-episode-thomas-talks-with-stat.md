<p># The Promise and Limitations of Medicine: A Deep Dive into Drug Story's Thomas Getz

## Key Insights

- **Drug Story's three-act narrative structure** (Diagnosis → Prescription → Side Effects) is directly inspired by *This American Life*'s act-based storytelling, designed to move from explaining diseases historically, to how drugs treat them, to broader social consequences—letting each episode span "ancient Babylonia to the halls of Congress."

- **The Romeo and Juliet drug parallel**: Getz explicitly compares modern pharmaceutical expectations to Romeo's failed apothecary visit—a "magic potion" that "does work, but not used as directed" when he jumped the gun thinking Juliet actually died. This captures the fundamental disconnect between hope and clinical reality.

- **Government agriculture subsidies create a paradox**: Getz points out that if government subsidizes commodity crops making grain "really cheap" and ultra-processed foods ubiquitous, the system simultaneously expects "personal choice" to solve obesity—making GLP-1 drugs a band-aid on a structural wound.

- **The MPH as a journalist's tool**: Getz intentionally pursued a Master of Public Health at Berkeley to gain "passing ability" to understand deeper questions about systems, structures, and context—"invisible structures visible"—transforming how he approaches health journalism.

- **"Mismatched diseases" framework**: Getz uses this term for conditions where human bodies evolved for one environment but now inhabit another, compounded by "commercial determinants of disease" where the food industry and electronics industry manipulate the systems we live within.

## Introduction

In this bonus episode of *Drug Story*, host Thomas Getz shares a conversation with Tori Bosch, editor of *First Opinion* at STAT, originally recorded for STAT's first opinion podcast. The discussion offers an inside look at the philosophical and structural foundations of *Drug Story*, which launched in January 2025 and explores how individual medications reflect larger truths about health, society, business, and human civilization.

Getz, a former executive editor at *Wired* who subsequently earned a Master of Public Health from UC Berkeley, brings a distinctive perspective to medical storytelling—one rooted in understanding systems and structures rather than simply reporting on individual drugs or treatments. His background bridges technology journalism, public health epidemiology, and narrative podcasting, creating a show that uses the "near infinite" stories within medications to help listeners understand their own health decisions and the world around them.

The conversation ranges from the practical (how he structures each episode) to the philosophical (why Americans expect too much from drugs) to the personal (how his MPH transformed his journalism) to the political (the enduring American tension between personal responsibility and the common good in public health). It provides essential context for anyone interested in medical journalism, public health, or the stories behind the pills in our medicine cabinets.

## The Premise and Structure of Drug Story

Getz opens by acknowledging the obvious objection—that the world doesn't need another podcast—but explains that he "could not shake" an idea about the vast world of health and medicine lurking behind the drugs people take. Every medication, he argues, contains "a much larger story" about disease, health, society, business, and economics. Rather than making each episode about a specific drug, he uses that drug as a lens to examine how diseases emerged, how we recognize and diagnose them, and how treatments came to be.

The show's structure reflects Getz's background as an English major drawn to narrative architecture. He describes borrowing "a page from The Best, which in this case is This American Life," which structures every episode as a series of acts. His three-part framework—**Diagnosis, Prescription, and Side Effects**—emerged as "not brilliant" but "pretty obvious."

The Diagnosis section explains the disease itself and how it emerged over time. The Prescription section focuses on the drug's development and how it treats the condition. The Side Effects section is deliberately misnamed—it's not primarily about immediate adverse effects but about "the larger social consequences or contextual consequences of using x drug on y disease." This third section lets Getz range freely: "to an ancient Babylonia or to the halls of Congress, or inside marketing departments at pharma companies." Yet the structure provides enough scaffolding to tell a coherent story.

## The Ivermectin Episode and the Nightmare Tongue Parasite

When Bosch confesses she "did not make it past the diagnosis" of the ivermectin episode due to tales of parasites, Getz discusses that episode as a perfect example of the show's approach. Ivermectin was originally developed for parasitic infections, and the episode explores how repurposing drugs represents "a great idea" and "a great kind of pharmaceutical use"—some repurposing has opened doors to genuinely helpful new treatments.

In ivermectin's case, however, repurposing for COVID represented "the hope for repurposing rather than the actual effective repurposing at least to date." The Side Effects section would inevitably address this gap between expectation and reality.

Bosch recalls the specific parasite that haunted her: one that replaces its host's tongue and then lives in the fish's mouth, acting as a tongue while "taking a little off the top for itself." Getz confirms this is his "favorite parasite" because "it's the nightmare scenario of what a parasite could do and does." (He clarifies for listeners: it's a fish parasite, not a human one.) This exchange captures the show's willingness to venture into genuinely unsettling biological territory to tell stories that illuminate how drugs actually work.

## Americans Expect Too Much from Drugs

Before the podcast launched, Getz wrote a First Opinion essay for STAT titled "Americans Expect Too Much from Drugs." He elaborates on this core thesis during the conversation.

The idea originated from observing patients leaving doctor's offices with prescriptions. They hold that slip of paper (or now, more likely, see it on their phone) "thinking it's gonna solve their problems." But "in reality, it doesn't work that way." The prescription is often "only the first step on this series of trial and error and adjustments and titration and other medications," plus questions of affordability, interactions with other drugs, and numerous other complexities. "The prescription is rarely the solution."

Getz identifies direct-to-consumer pharmaceutical advertising as crystallizing this dynamic. Such ads "kind of come in two parts." First: "the dark clouds parting and the sun shining through, and the patient, the people have a new world of opportunity and promise and health." Then comes the second half: "the very fast talking about all those side effects and all those problems." This duality between our hopes and reality, Getz argues, is something "we, as a society, just are so hopeful and so desirous of a positive outcome, but it's a lot more complicated than that."

To illustrate, Getz invokes Shakespeare: "Romeo and Juliet, when at the end, Romeo goes to the apothecary to get the drug that will put Juliet into a deep trance for a couple days so that they can escape together and run away and live their life happily ever after. Well, it doesn't work that way. Romeo and Juliet is a tragedy." The drug does work as designed, but Romeo "jumps the gun thinking that she's actually died" before Juliet wakes—the classic story of a tool that functions exactly as built but fails due to timing and miscommunication. "The drug does work. Sort of. Not used as directed."

## Modern Ailments and Mismatched Diseases

Bosch observes that Getz's early episodes focus on drugs addressing "the ails of modern life"—insomnia, obesity, addiction—and asks whether this represents a special area of interest.

Getz confirms he is "a public health guy," and these conditions fall under what the field calls "the social determinants of disease" or sometimes "the commercial determinants of disease." He is drawn to what he calls "**mismatched diseases**"—conditions where "our bodies were evolved to do x, y, or z" but "the world we built is not the world that our bodies were evolved to inhabit." This mismatch is then compounded by "commercial forces like the food industry or the electronics industry forcing other things into the equation."

The obesity example illustrates this complexity. The problem "is not just a problem with hyper ultra processed foods" but extends back through history to "the cheapness and ubiquity of grain, which was one of the greatest inventions in the last hundred years—was scientific industrial agriculture—that was allowed to feed billions of people, but it also made grain really cheap." Following this trajectory reveals how deeply embedded structural forces shape disease patterns.

## How the MPH Transformed His Journalism

Bosch notes that Getz was a technology and business journalist before becoming executive editor at *Wired*, then left to pursue his MPH at Berkeley. She asks how the degree changed his approach.

Getz says the MPH "ultimately transformed my career." The core shift was developing "a keenness to understand systems and structures and contexts"—making "those invisible structures visible" so he could "understand the world" and help others do the same. "Public health is understanding the context of disease, the other forces beyond the health care system that manifest in disease or health or illness." Epidemiology specifically "is trying to turn the cacophony of everyday life into something that you can parse enough to understand maybe causation."

These tools proved powerful for storytelling, helping listeners "make sense of their lives, make sense of the challenges they face in terms of their health decisions, their medical options."

Getz is an enthusiastic advocate for the degree. "I encourage everyone to get a masters in public health, especially MDs." MDs who earn an MPH, including his own sister, find it opens "multidimensional universes" where they "don't just think about one patient" but "whole populations of patients, or better yet, not just patients, but people." He describes it as "an incredibly powerful degree and a very powerful kind of frame of reference."

For journalists specifically, public health offers breadth: "it's everything from health care administration, hospital systems, economics, to biotechnology, epidemiology." He recommends pursuing it "as fast as possible" and credits Berkeley's interdisciplinary program with exposing him to "a little bit of everything across the public health menu"—particularly valuable for understanding infectious disease, a domain that "turns out that it matters."

## Evolution of Thinking on Obesity: From Skepticism to Nuance

Bosch references a 2007 *Wired* feature Getz wrote titled "The Thin Pill," which stated that "seventy-five million Americans may have something called metabolic syndrome" and explored "how big pharma turned obesity into a disease then invented the drugs to cure it." She notes that in 2007, it was seven years before the American Medical Association formally recognized obesity as a disease, and she suspects his perspective then was more skeptical than his current coverage of GLP-1 drugs like Wegovy.

Getz confirms he remembers writing it clearly—it was "really the precedent for GLP ones" in many ways. He admits he "was probably more skeptical of the idea of using drugs to treat it than I would be today," though he notes the piece did try to apply a public health lens even then. The story represented "kind of an old saw I've been sawing for a while."

The key shift in his thinking over the twenty years since, he explains, involves appreciating "this tension that is actually a very fundamental American tension between personal responsibility and a common good or a greater good." Twenty years ago, he "was not giving enough heed or paying enough credit to the idea of a common good, the idea that there are these larger systems that are often being manipulated by commercial interests like the food industry, manipulated on an individual basis, basically taking advantage of our ability to make choices, to basically entice us to make what turned out to be bad choices."

---

## The Common Good and the GLP-1 Debate

Getz sees the obesity-GLP-1 debate as a crystallization of this tension. "There's a lot of emphasis on personal responsibility, free will, personal choice, when it comes to what we eat." Diet and exercise are presented as individual choices, but "there are also greater structures and systems at work." Commercial interests exploit human psychology to drive consumption of unhealthy products.

He draws a direct connection to current policy debates: the "attack on the idea of actually, there is a common good, and there is an obligation of government, of regulators, of our health infrastructure to protect our citizens and to create some standards of access to standards of health and medicine." These are "the basic protections of government" that "have massive health implications"—access to healthy food, clean water, safe streets—yet are "under attack and being questioned in ways that they never had been in the last hundred years."

Regarding GLP-1 drugs specifically, Getz's "2026 self" believes they "don't solve the problem. They are not a solution to our social problem of an increasing rate of obesity, our social problem of bad food being so prevalent and so cheap, and being subsidized by the government." He frames this as a systemic contradiction: "If the government is subsidizing agriculture, on the one hand, why do we expect personal choice? We should have some government on the other side too, maybe trying to protect us from some of those effects of those subsidies."

---

## The Real Expectation Problem: Systems Expecting Pills to Fix System-Created Problems

Bosch synthesizes Getz's points beautifully: "It feels like it's less that Americans expect too much from drugs individually, though of course we do, but more that the system expects the drug to fix the system's problems—or the problems created by the system." She uses obesity as an example: "The system creates the problem of obesity and then creates a pill to solve it rather than fixing the big system."

Getz agrees: "Right." He has elaborated elsewhere that the prescription is "the first step into another world of complexity," not a solution. The system creates the conditions for disease—through agricultural policy, food industry practices, marketing, and economic structures—and then looks to medicine to solve problems that medicine cannot address alone.

## Detailed Takeaways

**Takeaway 1: Narrative Structure Makes Complex Medical Stories Accessible**

Getz's three-part structure (Diagnosis → Prescription → Side Effects) serves a rhetorical purpose beyond organization. By making the "side effects" section about social consequences rather than clinical adverse events, he creates "a narrative device" that allows each episode to travel "from ancient Babylonia to the halls of Congress." This proves that compelling storytelling can encompass both ancient history and contemporary policy without losing coherence. For communicators across fields, this demonstrates that even technical or systemic subjects become accessible when given a clear narrative scaffolding.

**Takeaway 2: The Gap Between Drug Marketing and Drug Reality Is Fundamental**

Direct-to-consumer pharmaceutical advertising's two-part structure—sunshine and promise followed by rapid side-effect disclosure—"crystallizes" the enduring tension between what we want from medicine and what medicine delivers. Getz's Romeo and Juliet comparison underscores that this is not a new problem; humans have always hoped for magic solutions and been disappointed when reality intrudes. Understanding this gap helps patients and practitioners set realistic expectations and recognize that prescriptions typically begin rather than conclude treatment journeys.

**Takeaway 3: Public Health Training Reveals Invisible Systems**

Getz's decision to pursue an MPH was motivated by seeing "around the corner to the future of journalism" and wanting "a little more expertise." What he gained was the ability to make "invisible structures visible"—the social, commercial, and political forces that shape health outcomes beyond individual medical decisions. For anyone covering or working in health-related fields, this demonstrates that journalism degrees alone may be insufficient; domain expertise in epidemiology and health systems provides essential frameworks for understanding causation and context.

**Takeaway 4: "Mismatched Diseases" Require Systemic Solutions, Not Just Pharmaceutical Ones**

Conditions like obesity, insomnia, and addiction represent cases where "our bodies were evolved" for environments that no longer exist, now further distorted by commercial interests. GLP-1 drugs may help individuals but cannot address the underlying mismatch. This challenges the assumption that every health problem requires a pharmacological solution, and suggests that effective interventions must include structural changes to food systems, urban planning, work-life balance, and regulatory environments.

**Takeaway 5: The Personal Responsibility vs. Common Good Tension Is the Central American Health Policy Conflict**

Getz frames this as "a very fundamental American tension" that plays out repeatedly across health policy. Obesity illustrates it perfectly: we emphasize individual choice while simultaneously subsidizing commodity agriculture in ways that make unhealthy food cheap and abundant. This creates an impossible double bind—expecting people to exercise choice against systems designed to exploit their vulnerabilities. Recognizing this tension helps explain why purely market-based or purely regulatory approaches both fail, and why sustainable solutions require addressing both individual agency and systemic structures.
