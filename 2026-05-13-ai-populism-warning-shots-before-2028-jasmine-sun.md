<p># AI Populism: Warning Shots Before 2028 | Jasmine Sun

## Key Insights

- **AI ranks 29th out of 39 issues** in voter importance (per Blue Rose Research/David Schwartz polling), yet is **rising faster than any other issue** in salience—outpacing even the Middle East conflict as the #2 fastest-rising concern as of February

- **Dario Amodei publicly stated** that 50% of entry-level white collar jobs will disappear by 2030—industry leaders themselves validating populist fears, unlike crypto where industry and populist narratives diverged

## Introduction

In an era when artificial intelligence increasingly dominates headlines, investment portfolios, and workplace conversations, a new political force is crystallizing across the American landscape—one that transcends traditional left-right divides and threatens to reshape the 2028 election cycle. Jasmine Sun, a technology and politics writer for *The Atlantic* and author of the AI Populism newsletter series at jasmine.news, has been tracking this phenomenon with growing concern. Her work argues that AI is no longer simply a technological topic—it has become what she calls "an elite political project to be resisted."

In a recent conversation on the Bankless podcast, Sun pulled back the curtain on the emerging coalition of unusual political allies, the opportunism of established politicians, and the unique psychological power that AI exerts over the American electorate. The timing of this discussion matters enormously: while AI currently ranks only 29th out of 39 issues in voter importance, it is rising faster than any other concern, and the gap between its stated importance and its visceral emotional resonance may prove to be the most significant political story of the coming years.

This conversation cuts to the heart of questions that will define the next decade: Can AI be regulated? Who will control the narrative around its risks and rewards? And perhaps most urgently, will the growing AI populist movement successfully channel public anxiety into political action—or will it spiral into something more volatile?

---

## The Emergence of AI Populism

### Defining the Phenomenon

Sun's definition of AI populism cuts to the core of what makes this movement distinct from typical technology regulation debates. Rather than viewing AI as simply another innovation requiring standard oversight, AI populists see it as "an elite political project to be resisted." This framing matters because it positions the technology not as neutral infrastructure but as a value-laden enterprise driven by specific interests—typically Silicon Valley companies and their investors—that must be challenged in the public interest.

The significance of this worldview becomes clear when examining the coalitions it has begun to assemble. According to Sun, when she visits Washington, D.C., she observes something remarkable: "family first conservatives sitting with antitrust people, sitting with environmentalists, people who would never be working side by side, but who have united in order to push for AI regulation." This is not a partisan coalition in any traditional sense. It represents a cross-pressured alliance held together not by shared ideology but by shared suspicion of concentrated technological power.

### Measuring the Movement's Strength

To understand where AI populism stands today, Sun cites polling research from David Schwartz at Blue Rose Research. The data reveals an important paradox: AI ranks 29th out of 39 issues in current voter priority—solidly in the middle-to-lower tier of American political concerns. Yet it is rising faster than any other issue in salience, outpacing even geopolitical flashpoints like the Middle East conflict.

This creates a fundamental question for political strategists: how can something rank so low in importance yet be rising so rapidly in visibility? Sun argues that the answer lies in how AI connects to the issues voters already care about most. Her polling shows that the top five American concerns are cost of living, the economy, corruption, inflation, and healthcare—issues that have remained remarkably stable over decades. The critical insight is that AI can be tied to all of them: data center construction affecting local property values and energy costs, AI-driven job displacement affecting economic mobility, concentration of AI power raising corruption concerns, and AI in healthcare raising questions about access and quality.

"When AI is seen as a bogeyman for cost of living, for economic mobility, for whether you're gonna have a job," Sun explains, "then AI will be a much bigger part of the political conversation than we would otherwise expect."

## Political Opportunism and the 2028 Calculus

### Why Sanders and Others Are Betting on AI

The emergence of AI as a political topic has not gone unnoticed by established politicians, and Sun identifies Vermont Senator Bernie Sanders as perhaps the most aggressive adopter of AI populist messaging. When asked why Sanders would invest political capital in an issue ranking 29th in voter importance, Sun offers a two-part explanation that reveals the strategic logic underlying this political bet.

First, AI provides a powerful new hook for existing concerns. Sanders has spent decades arguing about cost of living, economic concentration, and billionaire power. AI doesn't require him to change his message—it provides a new, contemporary justification for policies he has long championed. "Maybe if you feel like your messaging wasn't resonating before in terms of getting people to support universal healthcare or a higher minimum wage," Sun observes, "AI is like a brand new shiny reason to build support for the policies you might have already wanted to pass."

Second, and perhaps more significantly, AI leaders themselves are validating the populist critique. Dario Amodei, CEO of Anthropic, has publicly predicted that 50% of entry-level white collar jobs will disappear by 2030. When the people building the technology acknowledge its disruptive potential, they lend enormous credibility to the critics. Sun notes that this dynamic distinguishes AI populism from other technological debates: "The risks that the populists are talking about are many of the same risks that people in the industry are talking about... That adds a lot of credibility to the message when the people building the technology are saying, actually, that's true."

### Beyond Sanders: A Broader Pattern

Sun is careful to note that Sanders is not alone in this opportunism. The same dynamic is playing out across the ideological spectrum. On the political left, AI provides fresh ammunition for those seeking stronger speech regulation and content moderation on tech platforms. On the right, concerns about data centers in local communities, energy consumption, and cultural disruption resonate with traditional conservative priorities. In the center, antitrust advocates find new ammunition for breaking up technology monopolies.

"I do think AI matters," Sun concludes, "but I also think a lot of politicians are being pretty opportunistic about pointing to the shiny new thing and saying, maybe this is the reason to do what I've been saying all along."

## Why AI Populism Differs from Crypto Populism

### The Elizabeth Warren Parallel

The host in this conversation draws an instructive parallel to Elizabeth Warren's 2023-2024 anti-crypto campaign, which included her widely discussed effort to build an "anti-crypto army" in the aftermath of the FTX collapse. That campaign, while aggressive, largely failed to gain traction beyond crypto enthusiasts and skeptics. The question becomes: will AI populism suffer the same fate?

Sun acknowledges the parallel but identifies crucial differences that may lead to divergent outcomes. The most fundamental is scale. "Crypto was not driving like 30 or 40% of GDP growth over the course of the year," she notes. The economic footprint of AI is simply orders of magnitude larger than anything crypto achieved. Data centers are physically appearing in communities across America, affecting property values, energy grids, and local quality of life. Workers are being asked to integrate AI tools into their daily workflows. These are not niche experiences confined to cryptocurrency enthusiasts.

The consumer adoption gap is equally stark. "Crypto is very hard and confusing to use, and most Americans never really got in the habit of using crypto on a regular basis," Sun observes. By contrast, ChatGPT became "the fastest growing app in human history." The average person has direct, personal experience with AI in a way they never had with cryptocurrency.

### The Credibility Gap

Perhaps most significantly, the narrative dynamics differ fundamentally. During the Warren crypto campaign, industry voices largely defended cryptocurrency and disputed its risks to ordinary Americans. In the AI context, industry leaders themselves are amplifying the warnings. When Dario Amodei publicly discusses AI eliminating entry-level jobs, he validates the populist concerns rather than rebutting them.

"The risks that the populists are talking about are many of the same risks that people in the industry are talking about," Sun emphasizes. "This adds a lot of credibility to the message when the people building the technology are saying, actually, that's true. Like, this stuff is gonna hurt you. It is gonna take your job."

## The Dislocation Between Polls and Passion

### Reading the Warning Signs

The conversation turns to a striking observation: despite AI ranking only 29th in voter priority, the visible manifestations of public concern far exceed what that ranking would suggest. Town halls across America are drawing crowds demanding action against data center construction in their neighborhoods. Physical threats against AI executives have escalated to violence—Sam Altman's home has been targeted with both Molotov cocktail attacks and gunfire. These are not the actions of a population indifferent to an issue.

Sun acknowledges this dislocation but interprets it through a historical lens. She draws a parallel to the way China emerged as a political specter over the past decade: "We gotta do AI because China. We need to reinvest in manufacturing because China. We need to educate our kids better because China. The specter of China competition... was used as an all purpose justification in Washington, D.C."

The pattern, she suggests, reflects human psychology rather than rational policy analysis. When a powerful, seemingly alien force appears on the horizon—whether a foreign competitor or an emerging technology promising to reshape everything—people naturally seek to blame it for their struggles. AI, with its promises of transformation, its concentration of power in a few coastal companies, and its inherent difficulty for non-experts to understand, occupies a similar psychological space.

"There's a big alien force," Sun explains. "Whether that's another country like China, that's very foreign to people, or whether there's this specter of super intelligence and people don't really understand it, but it promises to change everything... It becomes very easy to blame and tie into a really wide range of issues."

### What This Means for 2028

Looking ahead, Sun suggests that the twenty-eight primary season will dramatically accelerate this dynamic. "I think that this opportunism is probably going to accelerate going into the twenty twenty eight primary season. I mean, it's gonna be a crowded primary, most likely, on both the Republican and the Democratic."

The combination of rising salience, cross-partisan coalition building, industry-validated fears, and physical manifestations of public concern creates conditions for AI populism to become a defining issue. Whether that translates into actual policy outcomes or remains primarily a rhetorical device depends on factors that remain uncertain: the trajectory of the economy, actual job displacement data, and the ability of political entrepreneurs to channel anxiety into organized political action.

## Detailed Takeaways

### 1. The Coalitions That Matter Are Being Built Now

The unusual alliances forming around AI regulation—conservatives with environmentalists, antitrust advocates with civil libertarians—represent the building blocks of durable political coalitions. These groups are not temporary bedfellows but are establishing relationships and institutional infrastructure that will outlast any particular election cycle. For those interested in AI policy, understanding these emerging alliances matters more than tracking any single politician's statements.

### 2. Industry Credibility Is the X-Factor

Unlike previous technology debates where industry and critics spoke different languages, AI populism benefits from having its key claims validated by industry leaders themselves. Dario Amodei's job displacement predictions, Sam Altman's Congressional testimony acknowledging risks—these statements provide political cover for critics and rhetorical ammunition for populists. This creates a qualitatively different political dynamic than we saw with cryptocurrency.

### 3. Physical Proximity Changes Politics

Data centers are not abstract financial instruments—they are buildings that appear in neighborhoods, consume enormous amounts of electricity and water, and generate visible truck traffic. This physical presence transforms AI from an abstract policy debate into a local land-use and quality-of-life issue that voters can act on directly. The town hall meetings and community opposition represent a different kind of political engagement than online discourse.

### 4. Twenty-Ninth Is a Lagging Indicator

The polling data Sun cites reflects current attitudes, not future trajectories. Given that AI is rising faster than any other issue in salience, and given its connection to the top five voter priorities (economy, cost of living, inflation, corruption, healthcare), the question is not whether AI becomes more politically important but how quickly and in what form. Twenty-ninth place today may look very different in 2027.

### 5. Violence Is on the Table

The escalation from rhetoric to physical attacks on AI executives represents a dangerous threshold. While still rare, these incidents signal that for some portion of the population, AI has become not just a political issue but a personal cause worth violent action. This dynamic, if it continues or expands, could fundamentally alter how AI companies operate, where they locate, and how their executives engage with public life.

This conversation is essential reading for several distinct audiences. **Technology professionals and executives** will gain crucial insight into how their industry is being perceived by political actors and the public, and why traditional communication strategies may be failing to address populist concerns. **Political observers and operatives** will find Sun's framework for understanding cross-partisan coalition building invaluable for anticipating how AI issues will shape upcoming elections. **Policy advocates and regulators** will benefit from understanding the rhetorical strategies being employed by both populists and their opponents. **Investors and business leaders** should pay particular attention to the economic data points Sun cites—AI's share of GDP growth and its physical presence in communities—because these will drive regulatory and political risk in ways that pure technology coverage misses.

For anyone seeking to understand why a technology ranking 29th in voter importance is generating town hall confrontations and physical attacks on executives, Sun's analysis provides the framework for making sense of a political phenomenon that will almost certainly accelerate through 2028 and beyond.</p>
