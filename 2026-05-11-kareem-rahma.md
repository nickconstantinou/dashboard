# Introduction

The SmartLess podcast has built its reputation on unscripted, free-associative conversations that somehow manage to feel both irreverent and genuinely substantive. In this episode, the hosts—Jason Bateman, Sean Hayes, and Will Arnett—welcome Kareem Rahma, the comedian and media entrepreneur behind "Subway Takes," one of the most-viewed online interview shows on the internet. The episode meanders through several conversational rabbit holes, but its most engaging territory comes from a sprawling, half-joking, half-deadly-serious discussion about space exploration, government secrets, and whether alien disclosure would fundamentally reshape human civilization—or whether people would simply "keep scrolling."

What makes this particular conversation noteworthy is the collision of perspectives it generates. The hosts approach the topic with the casual skepticism of Hollywood insiders who've seen every conspiracy theory and PR campaign imaginable. Kareem, meanwhile, brings the energy of someone who's watched too many documentaries and spent too much time on social media, arriving at conclusions that land somewhere between genuine belief and performance art. The episode works because nobody is entirely sure how seriously to take any of it—including, it seems, Kareem himself.

# The Artemis II Debate: Is Anyone Actually Going to Space?

The conversation opens with the hosts discussing the Artemis II mission, NASA's upcoming crewed lunar flight that will send astronauts on a loop around the Moon without landing. The initial response from Jason is dismissive: "They're pussies. They're pussies." This sets the tone for a running bit about the mission's limited ambitions. Will offers a more generous interpretation, pointing out that this represents the furthest humans have ever traveled from Earth, as the spacecraft will journey around the Moon's far side. Jason's rejoinder is characteristically dry: "That's like not going to Jason's house, but just stopping at Will's."

The hosts consider what a genuinely impressive space mission would look like, with Jason proposing a two-part plan: complete one orbit to establish a record, then land on the subsequent pass and "drive the buggy around again and hit another golf shot." There's also brief mention of Jeff Bezos's commercial space flights, which reportedly cost around $250,000 for participants to experience weightlessness and leave Earth's atmosphere—a price point that prompts genuine bewilderment from the group.

Kareem's contribution to this segment is notably more conspiratorial. When the introduction of his segment arrives, he responds to the earlier mention of Artemis with flat-out skepticism: "That part's true. We're in the mid lap right now. No. I mean, that that, like, we're go like, that we're the the rocket's even in space. Who knows if it's even in space?" The hosts immediately pounce on this, and Kareem clarifies that he saw a social media video suggesting the rocket's arc trajectory indicated it would "plummet" into the ocean rather than continuing into orbit. When pressed about whether he's suggesting the moon landing was filmed on a soundstage, Kareem backpedals slightly, acknowledging "the Earth is round" but maintaining that this particular video raised reasonable doubts.

The conversation then pivots to practical concerns about space travel—specifically, the lack of shower facilities on the spacecraft, which the hosts agree would be unacceptable. Will jokes that he'd want to "feel fresh" before exploring the universe, and there's brief speculation about whether "flushable wipes" might work in zero gravity. It's classic SmartLess banter, treating space exploration as an opportunity for bathroom humor while simultaneously acknowledging the genuine wonder of the endeavor. Jason ultimately sums up his view: "I think it's all pretty cool. I mean, I'm not obsessed with it, but I think it's pretty damn cool."

# The Bob Lazar Documentary and Government UFO Programs

The episode's most substantive segment begins when Will mentions he's been watching a documentary about Bob Lazar, a figure who's claimed for decades to have worked at Area 51's secret S4 facility and reverse-engineered alien spacecraft. The hosts' reactions make clear they're both intrigued and slightly unnerved by the material. "I like getting involved," Will says, sweating visibly. "I mean, I'm sweating. Literally sweating."

Kareem explains the core of the Lazar story: he claims to have worked at a facility containing a massive hangar divided into sections, each housing different components of alien spacecraft that the government had collected over the years. The documentary apparently aligns with recent statements from former intelligence officials—notably James Clapper and John Brennan—who have publicly acknowledged that the government has been conducting "crash site recovery" operations for years. "And Bob Lazar's been talking about it way before they have," Kareem notes, highlighting the apparent credibility that's now accruing to a figure who was long dismissed as a conspiracy theorist.

The group's central question becomes: if this information has been circulating for decades, why hasn't it achieved mainstream acceptance? Kareem's answer is blunt: "I think now it's slowly coming out because I think we can handle it now." The implicit theory is that there's a deliberate rollout strategy—some wise group within government carefully managing the pace of disclosure to prevent societal disruption. Will plays devil's advocate, suggesting this might be the very conditioning process itself: "Do you think we're helping by having, like, little conversations like this on the podcast?" Kareem responds with theatrical gravity: "Absolutely. This is this is having a major impact. Conditioning the public. This is this is helping turn the tide."

The description matches classic alien imagery—green skin, large eyes—though Kareem admits the figure might have been "a weird looking neighbor."

The hosts probe for details about what "zone" might have meant. Did the figure seem to be encouraging him? Telling him to "lock in" or focus on his studies? Kareem can't say for certain, and the group ultimately moves on without resolving whether this was genuine contact or childhood imagination.

The broader philosophical question emerges: would official government disclosure change anything? Would people actually believe it, or would they dismiss it as fake news, government psyops, or CGI? The consensus seems to be that most people have already processed the possibility of extraterrestrial life intellectually, even if they haven't integrated it emotionally. An official announcement might be less of a shock and more of a confirmation of what many already suspected. # Who Is Kareem Rahma?

The episode includes a fairly thorough introduction of the guest, establishing why he's earned the platform of a major podcast. Kareem is described as "a comedian, artist, and media entrepreneur" who was "born in Egypt, lives in New York." His primary claim to fame is "Subway Takes," an online interview show that has generated "billions of views" and earned coverage from the New York Times, GQ, The New Yorker, and Vanity Fair, along with multiple Webby nominations.

Kareem confirms the general biographical facts: he grew up in Minneapolis, completed a journalism degree, attempted (and didn't finish) a business degree, and eventually moved to New York City with the straightforward motivation of "not living in the small town." The hosts joke about his title as "a voice of the hatman"—apparently a reference to a figure associated with internet meme culture—though this goes unexplained.

What's clear is that Kareem has built a media presence by operating outside traditional gatekeeping structures. His success with "Subway Takes" represents a new model of content creation: viral, interview-based, accessible primarily through social media platforms, and appealing to audiences who want personality-driven content that's both entertaining and substantive. The fact that SmartLess—a podcast hosted by established Hollywood actors—positions him as a peer rather than a lesser guest suggests he's achieved genuine cultural penetration.

# Detailed Takeaways

**The democratization of conspiracy theory discourse has changed the information landscape.** What's striking about the episode is how seamlessly the hosts move between genuine scientific discussion and wild speculation. Jason's comment that "a short social media video is all I need" to form an opinion about space physics captures a broader phenomenon: the erosion of epistemic authority. When everyone has access to the same raw footage and when algorithms prioritize engagement over accuracy, the line between informed skepticism and uninformed conspiracy thinking becomes increasingly blurred. The episode implicitly asks whether this is a feature or a bug of the modern information environment.

**The "disclosure" narrative follows a predictable conditioning pattern.** Kareem's Spielberg theory isn't original, but it's significant that it emerged organically in conversation rather than being introduced as a prepared talking point. The idea that governments manipulate popular culture to prepare populations for difficult truths has become normalized in certain discourse communities. What the episode demonstrates is how easily casual conversation can slide into elaborately structured conspiracy thinking—and how difficult it is to distinguish genuine analysis from self-aware performance.

**Commercial space travel is rapidly becoming a cultural touchstone.** The discussion of Bezos's rocket flights and their $250,000 price point reflects a genuine shift in how ordinary people relate to space exploration. What was once exclusively a government undertaking is now accessible to wealthy civilians, creating new categories of aspiration, status, and social stratification. The hosts' reactions—"Would you ever do that?" followed by "I wouldn't pay for it"—capture the ambivalence many people feel about experiences that are theoretically available but practically inaccessible.

**Personal testimony occupies a strange epistemological space in the age of viral media.** Kareem's claim about his childhood alien encounter sits at the intersection of memory, narrative, and cultural influence. Is it significant that the description matches Hollywood's standard alien iconography? Does the fact that "zone" sounds like something from a 1990s sports film undermine or reinforce its authenticity? The episode doesn't answer these questions, but it demonstrates how difficult it is to evaluate personal extraordinary claims when shared in informal settings.

**The relationship between government transparency and social stability remains genuinely uncertain.** When Will asks whether alien disclosure would destroy religion and science, he's not entirely joking. The episode acknowledges that our foundational institutions—religious, scientific, political—rest on assumptions about human uniqueness and terrestrial specialness that extraterrestrial contact would necessarily challenge. Whether this would be catastrophic or simply require adaptation remains unknown, but the conversation suggests we haven't done nearly as much psychological preparation as we might assume.
