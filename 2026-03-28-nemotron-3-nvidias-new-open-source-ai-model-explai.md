# Nemotron 3: NVIDIA's Bold Bet on Open-Source AI and the Future of Computing

## How Jensen Huang's Vision for Democratizing Artificial Intelligence Could Reshape the Technology Landscape

In a wide-ranging conversation that touched on everything from model architecture to pharmaceutical discovery, NVIDIA CEO Jensen Huang sat down with Lex Fridman to discuss the company's groundbreaking release of Nemotron 3, a 120-billion parameter open-source AI model. The discussion, which emerged as a natural continuation of earlier conversation about open-source development, offered a rare glimpse into the strategic thinking behind one of the world's most influential technology companies. For those watching the AI industry closely, the conversation represented something significant: a clear articulation of why a company with enormous proprietary advantages would choose to share its most sophisticated work with the world.

The timing of this discussion matters enormously. The AI industry stands at a crossroads where decisions about openness versus exclusivity will shape how quickly and broadly artificial intelligence transforms society. Huang's perspective carries particular weight because NVIDIA sits at the foundation of virtually every significant AI deployment worldwide through its GPU hardware. What the company chooses to do with its model development efforts signals much about where the industry might be heading. When Perplexity's integration of Nemotron 3 was mentioned during the conversation, it demonstrated exactly the kind of ecosystem behavior that open-source models are designed to enable—powerful AI capabilities becoming accessible through the applications people already use.

## The Architecture of Innovation: Why Nemotron 3 Matters Technically

One of the most technically substantive aspects of the conversation centered on what makes Nemotron 3 distinctive from the sea of large language models flooding the market. Huang was careful to emphasize that Nemotron 3 is not merely another transformer-based model following the same architecture that has dominated AI since the attention mechanism's rise to prominence. Instead, it represents a hybrid approach combining transformer architecture with State Space Models (SSM). This architectural choice reflects NVIDIA's deep investment in fundamental research into model design, not just the application of existing techniques.

Huang drew a historical parallel to illustrate his company's approach to innovation. He referenced NVIDIA's early work developing conditional Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs) and progressive GANs—research that seemed speculative at the time but ultimately contributed to the evolution of modern diffusion models. The implication was clear: NVIDIA invests in architectural research not because it immediately knows the commercial applications, but because understanding how models are built informs how computing systems should be designed. This co-design philosophy sits at the heart of NVIDIA's competitive strategy. By deeply understanding model architecture, the company can build hardware and software ecosystems that will run future models optimally—before those models even exist.

The decision to make Nemotron 3 open-source reflects more than just technical generosity; it serves as a research platform for understanding how hybrid architectures perform at scale. When thousands of developers experiment with the model across different use cases, NVIDIA gains insights that would be impossible to obtain from a closed system. The open weights, the training data, and the methodology behind Nemotron 3's creation—all released publicly—create a feedback loop where the broader AI community helps advance understanding of what works and what doesn't in model design.

## The Three Pillars of NVIDIA's Open-Source Philosophy

Huang articulated a remarkably clear framework for understanding why NVIDIA, a company that could easily monetize its AI research through proprietary licensing, chooses instead to give much of its work away freely. The explanation revealed three interconnected motivations that together form a coherent strategic vision.

The first pillar relates directly to NVIDIA's core business identity. Huang stated plainly that to be a great AI computing company, NVIDIA must understand how AI models are evolving. Open-sourcing models like Nemotron 3 serves as a window into the frontier of AI development. When the company releases a model and the world responds—finding new applications, discovering failure modes, pushing the architecture in unexpected directions—NVIDIA learns. This learning directly informs the design of future GPUs, CUDA optimizations, and the overall computing stack that customers will purchase. The open-source investment pays dividends through improved hardware and software products that wouldn't exist without that community feedback.

The second pillar addresses the economic and social dimensions of AI development. Huang made a distinction that lies at the heart of debates about AI's future: world-class models as products should be proprietary, but the broader diffusion of AI capability requires openness. He argued that if everything in AI remained proprietary, the pace of research would slow dramatically. Graduate students couldn't build on proprietary systems. Researchers at smaller institutions would lack access to state-of-the-art tools. Countries and industries without billion-dollar budgets would be locked out of the AI revolution entirely. By open-sourcing capable models, NVIDIA helps ensure that the transformation AI represents doesn't benefit only the wealthiest organizations.

This philosophy extends from individual developers to entire industries. Huang explicitly mentioned that while NVIDIA doesn't build cars, the company wants every automotive company to have access to excellent AI models. Similarly, NVIDIA doesn't discover drugs, but the company wants pharmaceutical giant Eli Lilly to have access to the world's best biology AI systems. This positioning—providing the infrastructure and tools that enable others to succeed in their domains—represents a deliberate choice to profit from the AI ecosystem rather than compete directly within it. The open-source models become calling cards that demonstrate NVIDIA's technical capabilities while simultaneously growing the market for the computing infrastructure that makes AI possible.

The third pillar addresses something that Huang believes many in the AI industry fundamentally misunderstand: AI is not just language. He argued that future AI systems will increasingly use tools, models, and sub-agents trained on modalities far removed from text. Biology, chemistry, physics, thermodynamics, fluid dynamics—these domains contain vast stores of information that don't fit naturally into language structures. Yet current AI development remains heavily concentrated on language models, partly because language is the most accessible training data and partly because the commercial applications are most obvious.

By open-sourcing models and the methodology behind them, NVIDIA aims to ensure that these other domains receive adequate research attention. Weather prediction, physical AI, AI for drug discovery—these applications matter enormously for human welfare, but they lack the immediate commercial appeal of chatbots and writing assistants. When researchers worldwide have access to capable open-source models, they can adapt and extend those systems for specialized applications that wouldn't justify proprietary development. The open-source release becomes a mechanism for directing AI capability toward problems that transcend language processing.

## Beyond Language: The Broader Vision for AI's Future

The conversation touched on specific examples of how this philosophy translates into practice. Huang mentioned weather prediction as an example of AI that operates on fundamentally different data than text. Climate modeling requires processing vast amounts of numerical sensor data, physical simulations, and historical patterns that have little to do with human language. Yet the techniques developed for language models—attention mechanisms, scaling laws, instruction tuning—may transfer to these domains in unexpected ways. Open models give researchers the freedom to experiment with these transfers without negotiating proprietary licenses or facing usage restrictions.

The pharmaceutical applications represent perhaps the clearest illustration of Huang's vision. Drug discovery involves understanding molecular structures, protein folding, genetic pathways, and thousands of other biological mechanisms. While some of this knowledge exists in scientific literature (and thus in language model training data), the most valuable information lives in biological datasets, experimental results, and proprietary research. An open model that can serve as a foundation for biology-focused AI allows researchers to build specialized systems for drug discovery without NVIDIA having to develop those specialized systems itself.

Huang's framing of this strategy was notably humble for a CEO describing his company's competitive position. Rather than positioning NVIDIA as destined to dominate every corner of AI, he explicitly stated that NVIDIA doesn't build cars, doesn't discover drugs, doesn't operate in most of the industries that will be transformed by AI. The company's ambition is to be the essential infrastructure layer—the computing platform that makes everyone else's AI ambitions possible. Open-sourcing capable models fits this strategy perfectly. It demonstrates capability, builds ecosystem goodwill, and creates the conditions for computing hardware sales without requiring NVIDIA to win every AI application directly.

## Concrete Value for Real-World Applications

The practical impact of this open-source approach was illustrated during the conversation when Perplexity's integration of Nemotron 3 was mentioned. Perplexity, which Huang acknowledged being a fan of for some time, now offers users access to Nemotron 3's capabilities directly through its AI-powered search interface. This represents exactly the kind of distributed AI deployment that open-source models enable. Rather than NVIDIA building a single AI application that competes with everyone else, the model flows outward through thousands of applications and contexts.

The 120-billion parameter scale of Nemotron 3 is notable for its accessibility. While not the largest model ever released, it represents a capability threshold that would be prohibitively expensive for most organizations to train from scratch. By releasing the complete model including weights, training data, and methodology, NVIDIA effectively gives the research community and industry a head start on experiments that would otherwise require enormous investment. Organizations can fine-tune the model for specific applications, study its behavior, identify its weaknesses, and build upon it—all without NVIDIA's direct involvement in every project.

## Key Takeaways for Understanding the AI Landscape

**Open-source AI is becoming a strategic necessity rather than an ideological choice.** Jensen Huang's reasoning makes clear that for companies positioned like NVIDIA, open-sourcing AI models creates feedback loops that improve core business offerings faster than proprietary development ever could. The insight that understanding AI model evolution directly informs computing hardware design suggests that open-source release is as much a research investment as a gift to the community. Organizations building AI strategies should recognize that the most influential players in the ecosystem may increasingly be those who share their work openly.

**Hybrid architectures combining transformers with state space models represent the next frontier beyond pure language models.** Nemotron 3's design signals that the AI research community is actively exploring beyond the transformer architecture that has dominated since 2017. The integration of different model types suggests that future AI systems will be composed of specialized components rather than single monolithic models. Understanding this architectural evolution will be essential for anyone making long-term technology decisions.

**The AI revolution requires openness to achieve broad impact.** Huang's argument that proprietary development would lock out most researchers, industries, and countries from AI's benefits deserves serious consideration. The technology's transformative potential can only be realized if capable tools remain accessible to those beyond the largest technology companies. This suggests that the organizations best positioned to benefit from AI may be those that find ways to build on open foundations rather than those that exclusively pursue proprietary advantage.

**AI's most significant applications may lie outside language processing entirely.** The explicit recognition that biology, physics, chemistry, and other domains contain AI opportunities that pure language models cannot address points toward enormous untapped potential. Industries with specialized data types and domain-specific challenges may find that open-source models provide foundations for breakthroughs that proprietary systems designed primarily for language tasks would miss entirely.
