Why More Americans Are Seeking Religion

After decades of steady decline, faith in America is showing unexpected signs of renewal — and the reasons behind it are more complex than anyone predicted.

Key Insights

  • The "great de-churching" has paused: Pew Research data shows that for the first time since tracking began, Americans have stopped leaving churches — reversing a decades-long trend where 90% of adults identified as Christian, a share that dropped to roughly two-thirds with an estimated 40 million departures.
  • Young men are becoming more religious: Gallup data reveals a dramatic surge among men in their 30s who describe religion as "very important" — jumping from 28% in 2023 to 42% in 2025, upending the historical pattern where young women outpaced men in religiosity.
  • Gen Z shows signs of reversal: 18–23 year olds are now slightly more likely to attend religious services monthly than the cohort just older than them — the first hint the assumption of generational decline may no longer hold.
  • The belief-practice gap is enormous: 92% of Americans believe in God, spirits, souls, or an afterlife, yet only 30% attend a house of worship weekly — suggesting spiritual hunger far beyond what institutions currently serve.
  • Transcendence is appearing everywhere: Journalist Lauren Jackson observed quasi-religious experiences at climate rallies, Black Lives Matter gatherings, and Taylor Swift concerts — places where people seek meaning, community, and connection outside traditional faith.

The Numbers Behind the Shift

The statistical picture, Jackson explains, is more nuanced than headlines suggesting "a religious revival" might imply. The great de-churching has not reversed into a sudden surge of church attendance. Rather, something more subtle but equally significant has occurred: secularization has paused.

In the early 1990s, 90% of American adults identified as Christian. That number dropped dramatically — by some estimates, 40 million people left American churches entirely. Demographers forecast this trend would continue indefinitely. It hasn't.

A Pew report shows that 18–23 year olds are, slightly but meaningfully, more likely to attend religious services at least once a month than the cohort just older than them. And a new Gallup survey documents a sharp rise in the share of men in their 30s who say religion is "very important" — jumping from 28% in 2023 to 42% in 2025.

A Reporter's Personal Journey

Lauren Jackson was raised in Little Rock, Arkansas, as a devout Mormon. She attended a 6 AM Bible study every day in high school and expected to marry within the church. A scholarship to a secular university changed everything — she fell in love with someone outside her faith, encountered Isaiah Berlin's ideas on pluralism, and left the church at 25.

"I stopped going to church, but I didn't stop searching for answers to the big life questions that plague us all," she says. "And that made me like most Americans."

The Search for Meaning Never Stops

According to Pew, 92% of Americans say they believe in God, spirits, souls, or an afterlife — but only 30% attend a house of worship weekly. This staggering gap reveals that spiritual hunger is widespread, but institutional religion satisfies only a fraction of it.

Jackson observed quasi-religious experiences in unexpected places: reverence at a climate rally with Greta Thunberg, moral intensity at Black Lives Matter demonstrations, and what she calls "an extraordinary ecstatic form of communal gathering rarely found outside of religious spaces" at Taylor Swift's Eras Tour.

Why Now?

The data shows something is happening. The explanations remain contested. "Plenty of people have declared this a revival," Jackson notes. "That's a strong word, and plenty of other people have said that is very premature and potentially erroneous."

What's driving young men back? What's bringing Gen Z through church doors? Is this a reaction to pandemic-era isolation? A response to political polarisation? The story is still being written.

Detailed Takeaways

Secularization Is Not Inevitable. The great de-churching has paused. The human need for religious community and meaning may be more durable than previously thought.

Young Men Are the Unexpected New Face of American Religiosity. The 14-point jump among men in their 30s is not a minor fluctuation — it may represent a reversal of a long-established gender gap with significant political ramifications.

The Gap Between Believing and Belonging Is Enormous. 92% believe, 30% attend weekly. This disconnect represents both a challenge and an opportunity for religious institutions.

Transcendence Is Present Everywhere. The human yearning for meaning does not require theological content — people find it at concerts, rallies, and online communities just as readily as in pews.

Personal Religious Journeys Involve Profound Cost. Leaving a faith community is frequently painful, not liberating in the way secular narratives imply. The complexity cuts both ways.
