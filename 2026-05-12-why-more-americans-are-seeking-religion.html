    Why More Americans Are Returning to Faith—And What It Reveals About American Spirituality

    For decades, the narrative seemed settled. American religion was in decline. Church pews emptied. Sundays became just another weekend. Young people especially seemed to be leaving faith behind, trading organized religion for secularism or spiritual individualism. Sociologists had a name for it: the "great de-churching," the largest and fastest shift in American religiosity on record, an exodus they predicted would only accelerate.

    But something unexpected is happening now.

    Across the country—and particularly here in South Central Pennsylvania—church leaders are reporting a noticeable shift. Gen Z is returning to faith, but often in ways that defy easy categorization. Young New Yorkers have made Sunday Mass a "hot spot." Converts are joining churches in the largest numbers seen in recent memory. For the first time in decades, the data suggests that faith in America is not just holding steady—it's showing signs of new life.

    This is the story that New York Times religion reporter Lauren Jackson has been tracking, and it's one that challenges the assumptions that have dominated cultural conversations about spirituality for a generation.

    The Numbers Behind the Shift

    The statistical picture, Jackson explains, is more nuanced than headlines suggesting "a religious revival" might imply. The great de-churching has not reversed into a sudden surge of church attendance. Rather, something more subtle but equally significant has occurred: secularization has paused.

    Pew Research Center has been gathering data on American religion for years, and what they found is that the steady leak from churches that characterized the 1990s through the 2020s has, at least temporarily, stopped. In the early 1990s, 90% of American adults identified as Christian. That number dropped dramatically over the following decades—by some estimates, 40 million people left American churches entirely. Demographers and sociologists had forecast this trend would continue indefinitely.

    It hasn't.

    More recent data has only deepened the picture. A Pew report released in recent weeks shows that if you examine the youngest group of Americans—those aged 18 to 23—there are signs that they are, slightly but meaningfully, more likely to attend religious services at least once a month than the cohort just older than them. This marks the first hint that the assumption each new generation would be less religious than the last might no longer hold.

    Even more striking is a new Gallup survey documenting a sharp rise in the share of men in their 30s who say religion is "very important" to them. That number jumped from 28% in 2023 to 42% in 2025—a 14-percentage-point increase in just two years. Historically, young women have been more religious than young men; that dynamic appears to be shifting.

    As of 2025, the non-religious share of the American population declined yet again. The number of Americans who identify as atheists or agnostics has dropped back to levels not seen since 2014—nearly fifteen years ago.

    The trend appears across the political spectrum, though it is more pronounced on the right, particularly among young men. And it's not confined to Christianity. All major religions in America appear to be experiencing similar patterns.

    A Reporter's Personal Journey Into Faith and Loss

    What makes Jackson's reporting on this topic distinctive is her willingness to bring her own story into the conversation. She describes herself as someone whose life "mirrors the shifts" she has observed in American attitudes toward religion and religious practice over the past three decades.

    Jackson was raised in Little Rock, Arkansas, in what she describes as "a very, very conservative and very religious place." She was a devout member of the Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-Day Saints—a Mormon—and her family was deeply committed to their faith. As a young girl, she writes and sends articles to church magazines because her dream job was to work as a writer for those publications. In high school, she attended a 6 AM Bible study every day before school, driven by the requirement that she graduate from seminary to attend Brigham Young University.

    "I was expected to attend, and I expected myself for my whole life to attend, find someone to marry, have children, and raise them in the church," Jackson recalls.

    The rupture came when a guidance counselor encouraged her to apply for a scholarship to a secular university. She did, was accepted, and found herself unexpectedly moved by the intellectual environment and the desire to learn in a different space. Attending meant leaving behind the tight community she had known—and beginning to question the faith that had shaped her entire worldview.

    "The first week I was there, I fell in love with someone who was not a member of my faith," Jackson says. "It's a very common rupture for a lot of people who then have to question what it is they believe in and how they can negotiate the boundaries of that."

    In a political science class, she encountered the philosopher Isaiah Berlin's ideas on pluralism—the concept that many truths and realities are equally valid and worthy of consideration. "That really cracked my world open," she says, "that idea, that concept that there was not one true church, there could be many possible truths."

    At 25, Jackson formally left the church.

    The personal cost was significant. She describes difficult conversations with her parents, who deeply believed that Mormonism was true and represented the best path forward for their family. "I was desperately wanting my parents to not only understand, but also to approve of the life choices that I've made in leaving my faith," she says. But they were unwilling to offer that validation, because their own commitment to their worldview was absolute. "They deeply believe in the faith that they believe in and that they were raised in and that they live every day."

    Jackson's conversation with her mother—excerpted in the transcript—captures both the tenderness and the tragedy of such ruptures. "I'm just asking if we could talk to you about it," her mother pleaded. "It's not up for debate." Jackson pushed back gently: "If you had ever experienced feeling the holy spirit, then you would get what I'm talking about. But if you don't have room in your heart to just try and listen..."

    The exchange ended inconclusively, as such conversations often do. Jackson describes it as "like a sunset—I feel it. I've got a different view of the sunset."

    The Search for Meaning Never Stops

    Despite formally leaving the church, Jackson never stopped searching for answers to existential questions. "I stopped going to church, but I didn't stop searching for answers to the big life questions that plague us all," she says. "And that made me like most Americans."

    That point bears emphasis. The data bears it out. According to Pew, 92% of Americans say they believe in God, spirits, souls, or an afterlife—but only 30% attend a house of worship weekly. This staggering gap reveals that spiritual hunger is widespread, but institutional religion satisfies only a fraction of it.

    For Jackson personally, meaning came through unconventional channels. She threw herself into work, pursuing the journalism career she had always wanted at The New York Times. She invested heavily in fitness—SoulCycle, CrossFit, whatever promise not just a healthier body but a better life. She understood why young women had downloaded CoStar, the astrology app, even if she didn't use it herself.

    "I get it," she says. "It helps explain the messiness of life. It promises that there's some sort of cosmic alchemy to the chaos, and who doesn't want that?"

    But she also couldn't shake her ingrained awareness of belief and spirituality. As she traveled and spoke to people, she began to notice what she calls "quasi-religious" experiences in unexpected places.

    At a climate rally with Greta Thunberg at COP in Glasgow, she observed "a reverence in the crowd" that felt distinctly religious—a desire for deliverance, for meaning, for collective purpose. At Black Lives Matter demonstrations, she saw the intensity of how people gravitated to social justice and activism movements, driven by a sense of moral right and wrong. At Taylor Swift's Eras Tour, she witnessed what she describes as "an extraordinary ecstatic form of communal gathering rarely found outside of religious spaces."

    "I felt it at the Eros tour," Jackson says. "What are people feeling if not an extraordinary ecstatic form of communal gathering rarely found outside of religious spaces."

    The pattern was unmistakable: people were looking for an outlet for their beliefs, their need for transcendence, their hunger for community and meaning. Organized religion had lost its monopoly, but the underlying human need had not diminished.

    Why Now? The Questions That Remain

    This is the central mystery Jackson's reporting attempts to unravel. Why, after decades of decline, are Americans reconsidering religion or houses of worship as places to explore their spiritual needs?

    The data shows something is happening. The explanations remain contested. "Plenty of people have declared this a revival," Jackson notes. "That's a strong word, and plenty of other people have said that is very premature and potentially erroneous."

    What seems clear is that the story of faith and belief in America is at an inflection point. The old narrative—that religion would inevitably decline as education and prosperity rose—has stalled. The new story is still being written.

    What's driving young men back to religion? What's bringing Gen Z through church doors? Is this a reaction to pandemic-era isolation? A response to political polarization? A broader cultural moment of searching after years of materialism and distraction?

    Jackson's approach, as she describes it, involves deep, intimate conversations that take time and attention. "I feel like I'm part reporter, part therapist, because it takes a lot of attention and a lot of time to attend to these stories," she says. "They're so intimate. They're so personal."

    The reporting continues. And as it does, it offers a window into one of the most significant cultural shifts in contemporary America—questions of meaning, community, and transcendence that millions of people are wrestling with, in churches and beyond.

    Detailed Takeaways

    1. Secularization Is Not Inevitable—It Can Stall and Even Reverse

    The assumption that modernization equals religious decline has dominated social scientific thinking for decades. The American data now challenges that assumption. The great de-churching—the largest and fastest shift in American religiosity on record—has paused. This matters because it suggests that theories about inevitable secularization may need revision, and that the human need for religious community and meaning may be more durable than previously thought. For policymakers, religious leaders, and cultural observers, the implications are significant: institutions that invest in community and meaning-making may find unexpected receptivity.

    2. Young Men Are the Unexpected New Face of American Religiosity

    The 14-percentage-point jump in young men (30s) calling religion "very important" is not a minor fluctuation—it represents a potential reversal of a long-established gender gap in religious participation. This shift has political ramifications, given the association between religious identity and voting patterns. Understanding why young men are returning to faith—whether through churches, online religious communities, or other institutions—may be one of the most important questions of the coming decade. Early hypotheses include a response to loneliness, a desire for structure and identity, and a reaction against cultural trends that feel alienating.

    3. The Gap Between Believing and Belonging Is Enormous—and Growing

    That 92% of Americans believe in something spiritual but only 30% attend weekly services regularly reveals a profound disconnect between private faith and public practice. This gap represents both a challenge and an opportunity for religious institutions: most Americans are spiritually hungry but not served by current forms of organized religion. Understanding what people are looking for—community, meaning, transcendence, moral clarity—and how they seek it outside traditional settings may be the key to whether the current pause in secularization becomes a genuine renewal.

    4. Experiences of Transcendence Are Present Everywhere—Not Just in Churches

    Jackson's observation that quasi-religious experiences occur at political rallies, concerts, and social movements suggests that the human yearning for meaning, community, and transcendence does not require theological content. People standing at a climate rally with Greta Thunberg felt reverence. Concert-goers at the Eras Tour experienced ecstatic communal gathering. The spiritual impulse is robust and寻找新的 outlets. Religious institutions that recognize this—rather than dismissing secular expressions of transcendence as ersatz—may be better positioned to engage those on the margins.

    5. Personal Religious Journeys Often Involve Profound Cost and Growth

    Jackson's willingness to share her story illuminates a truth often obscured in public discourse: leaving a faith community is frequently painful, not liberating in the way secular narratives often imply. The rupture with family, the loss of community, the identity crisis—all of these are real costs that people weigh. Conversely, returning to faith can involve similar complexity. Understanding religion as lived experience—as something people grapple with across their lives, rather than a static identity they either accept or reject—may be essential to making sense of the current spiritual moment.

    Who This Is For

    This reporting is essential reading for anyone trying to understand the cultural currents reshaping American society in the 2020s. Religious leaders will find in these findings both a challenge and an opportunity: the data suggests people are searching, but the forms of religion currently on offer may not satisfy their hunger. Political observers will recognize the significance of young men returning to faith at a moment when religious identity continues to influence voting patterns. Anyone wrestling with questions of meaning, community, and belief—personally or professionally—will see their own experience reflected in Jackson's intimate portrait of American spiritual searching.

    The deeper value of this reporting is its refusal to simplify. Faith in America is not dying. It's not simply resurgent. It's complicated, contested, and evolving—in ways that may ultimately tell us more about what it means to be human than any single answer ever could.
