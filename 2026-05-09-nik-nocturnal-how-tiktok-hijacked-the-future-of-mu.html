# Nik Nocturnal - How TikTok Hijacked the Future of Music - #1095

---

Key Insights

  • Clip-farming mechanics: Metal's inherent structure—sudden breakdowns, vocal gymnastics, extreme technique moments—makes it uniquely suited for TikTok's format, where listeners get "instant payoff" instead of waiting 90 seconds for a song's climactic moment. Speaker compares it to "watching a horror movie and only getting hit with the jump scares."
  • "Short-form first" songwriting inversion: Modern bands increasingly start composing at the breakdown or climactic moment and "work back from there"—writing "how the movie ends" before constructing the rest of the track, fundamentally altering traditional songwriting processes.
  • Bring Me The Horizon's "Can You Feel My Heart" phenomenon: This track exemplifies how an album track can become 10x more popular than other songs solely because it works exceptionally well as a video overlay—demonstrating how platform virality, not musical quality, increasingly drives track selection.
  • The 2008 deathcore era's natural TikTok compatibility: That era's deliberate "do the thing" culture of bass drops, reverse snares, and extreme sound design—created for fun among friends—was never meant for mass consumption, yet contains countless moments perfectly suited for modern short-form algorithms.
  • Bands succeeding through authenticity, not optimization: Knock Loose gained massive traction through pure intensity and pacing—"being loved on social media because it's a window into something outside of it," not through calculated social media targeting. Conversely, attempting to mask that a song was "made for social media" undermines its effectiveness entirely.

    Introduction

    Nik Nocturnal, a prominent YouTube content creator and musician with deep roots in the metal community, has become one of the most thoughtful voices analyzing how internet culture intersects with heavy music. In this episode, he sits down for a wide-ranging conversation about what may be the most significant shift in how music gets created, discovered, and consumed since the advent of the smartphone—the TikTok era and its cascading effects on modern metal.

    The conversation arrives at a critical inflection point for the genre. Metal has historically existed at the margins of mainstream culture, its discovery typically requiring active seeking rather than algorithmic suggestion. But TikTok has changed the equation entirely. Songs can now detonate into cultural relevance based on a single shareable moment—a breakdown, a vocal run, a guitar technique—rather than the holistic listening experience that metal fans traditionally valued. This episode explores the consequences of that shift: how it shapes what gets written, who gets heard, and whether the songs being created today will still matter in ten years.

    What makes this discussion particularly valuable is its refusal to land on a simple verdict. Nik acknowledges TikTok's undeniable power in exposing new audiences to heavy music while simultaneously mourning what gets lost when songs become optimized for fifteen-second windows. The conversation moves from examining specific viral moments to tracing the evolution of songwriting methodology to asking whether metal's greatest era might already be behind it—or whether the genre is merely undergoing one of its periodic reinventions.

    ---

    The Clip-Farming Imperative

    The episode opens with a foundational observation about why metal, of all genres, has found such fertile ground on TikTok. Nik explains that modern metal's structural DNA aligns almost perfectly with what short-form platforms reward. When listeners scroll through endless content, their attention needs to be captured immediately—there's no patience for build-up, no willingness to wait for a payoff that might arrive ninety seconds into a song. Metal, with its characteristic dynamic swings, provides exactly what the algorithm rewards: instant gratification.

    "The breakdown or the elder breakdown or the crazy vocal moment, or even on the guitar, there's a lot of musicianship and unique attributes that kind of go into that skill in terms of, like, metal as a genre," Nik observes. "So when you're able to just kinda clip farm some things like that and be like, oh, here's the best part, and you instantly get a payoff versus most people you have to listen to the whole song or in a minute and a half until the breakdown happens."

    The comparison to jump scares proves particularly apt. Just as horror films can substitute genuine atmosphere and tension for cheap shocks, metal on TikTok risks becoming a collection of isolated moments rather than cohesive artistic statements. Nik doesn't condemn this phenomenon outright—there's genuine excitement in hearing a killer riff or an impossibly technical passage without context—but he does identify it as a fundamental shift in what music gets consumed and how.

    This dynamic has created what might be called the "Can You Feel My Heart" effect. Bring Me The Horizon's track from their acclaimed album should, by Nik's assessment, be roughly equivalent in popularity to other strong songs from that record. Instead, it became a cultural phenomenon orders of magnitude larger—not because it was objectively better, but because it possessed qualities perfectly suited for video overlays. The track worked as a blank canvas for visual content, its emotional resonance and production density making it ideal for lip-syncs, gaming montages, and whatever else the platform's users needed background music for. This represents a new form of natural selection: not the survival of the musically superior, but the survival of the algorithmically adaptable.

    ---

    Writing Songs Backwards

    Perhaps the most consequential shift Nik identifies isn't about discovery but about creation itself. He raises a question that reveals a fundamental transformation in how bands approach songwriting: "How many bands are thinking almost short form first? Like, these are the three segments of this track that work for some reason. And then can we seed a meme early?"

    The acknowledgment that the industry is explicitly doing this—"seeding who was that fuck, who was that band?"—suggests a level of strategic calculation that would have been unthinkable in earlier eras of metal. Bands aren't merely hoping their best moments resonate; they're engineering them from the start. The breakdown isn't the culmination of a song's emotional arc; it might be the entire point around which everything else gets constructed.

    Nik describes this inversion with a striking metaphor: "Let's start writing the song at the breakdown, because that could start writing it at the climactic moment. We'll write how the movie ends, and then we'll work back from there." This represents a radical departure from traditional songwriting logic, where tension gets built through verse and chorus before releasing in a climactic section. Now, the climactic section might come first chronologically, with the rest of the song serving as mere context or expansion.

    The conversation acknowledges that this process isn't universal—Nik is careful to note that "not everybody, but some nonzero number" of bands are thinking this way—but the existence of the phenomenon itself marks a significant change. When writing sessions begin with questions about what makes good TikTok content rather than what makes a good song, the creative priorities have shifted fundamentally. The question becomes not "what feels right?" or "what sounds good?" but "what clips well?"

    ---

    The 2008 Golden Age and Its Modern Resonances

    To understand where metal stands now, Nik and his conversation partner turn backward to what many consider the genre's creative peak: the period between roughly 2004 and 2010. This era—encompassing the birth of deathcore, the maturation of metalcore, and the first wave of bands willing to push extreme music into increasingly experimental territory—produced a body of work that, Nik argues, has yet to receive its due TikTok revival.

    He references the iconic Job for a Cowboy moment—the "girl screen" video that became a touchstone for an entire generation of metal fans—as emblematic of an approach to songwriting that prioritized pure extremity and fun over strategic considerations. "They were just having fun," Nik explains. "They were with their producer, and the producer could hit the button that literally was a bass drop or a reverse snare, and everyone would just laugh. Do the thing. Do the thing. Do the thing. That was the do the thing, but it was a fun thing that they know no one would have cared outside of them."

    That carefree attitude—creating extreme sounds not for mass appeal but for the pleasure of pushing boundaries—resulted in music that contained countless moments perfectly suited for modern platforms. The bass drops of that era, created in car stereos and garage studios for the amusement of teenage friends, possess exactly the kind of immediate impact that TikTok rewards. Nik remembers hearing these tracks in a friend's car, "driving through the mean streets of the Northeast of the UK," with "a proper subwoofer in the boot" delivering bass that "shook my fucking teeth."

    What's striking is that Bring Me The Horizon themselves seem aware of this heritage. Their decision to "do Count Your Blessings again"—referencing their seminal 2005 EP that helped define that era—represents a recognition that the past contains templates for what works. The resurgence of traditional deathcore, combined with Bring Me's willingness to revisit their extreme roots, suggests that the algorithm may be rediscovering what an earlier generation created spontaneously.

    Yet Nik notes a peculiar absence: while Abba and decades-old hip hop have experienced dramatic TikTok revivals, metal from this golden era hasn't achieved comparable rediscovery. He wonders aloud why this might be, acknowledging that everyone knows tracks like "Tears Don't Fall" while questioning whether a systematic revival has occurred. His theory is that the ten-year gap between 2010 and 2020, while producing "a lot of cool music," generated fewer undeniable hits that could anchor a nostalgia movement. The decade was more about consistency than explosive cultural moments—until Architects' "Doomsday" and its massive influence on the modern scene.

    ---

    Authenticity as Competitive Advantage

    The episode's most nuanced section addresses how bands can succeed within the TikTok ecosystem without becoming its prisoners. Nik uses Knock Loose as the primary example of a band that has navigated this challenge successfully—though he immediately adds that their success appears almost accidental relative to their intentions.

    "Knock Loose, what they've managed to do through short form as well just by being kind of the most extreme sound that most people have ever heard... I think they don't care about any of that, but because their music is so intense and so well moment like, I'm sorry, it's just paced so well, they naturally work in that way. That's what it's supposed to be."

    The distinction Nik draws is crucial: Knock Loose isn't making music for TikTok; their music possesses qualities that happen to translate perfectly to TikTok. This "window into something outside of it" approach—where social media becomes a discovery mechanism pointing toward a more substantial artistic reality—stands in direct opposition to the "made for social media" approach, which inevitably fails to convince.

    "Social media is supposed to be something that happens that wasn't meant to fugazi everybody into loving it on social media," Nik explains. "Being loved on social media because it's a window into something outside of it." He continues: "The problem is if you try and make it for social media, you also have to mask over the fact that this isn't supposed to look like it was made for social media, because that then completely undermines it."

    This creates a paradox at the heart of modern music marketing. The only way to succeed on platforms designed for inauthentic engagement is to create work that appears completely indifferent to those platforms—which requires either genuine artistic integrity or extremely sophisticated performance of that integrity. The result, Nik suggests, is often neither: "A lot of time, unfortunately, the song just sucks." The focus on creating moments leads to songs that lack the holistic quality necessary for longevity.

    "You'll maybe get some hype and maybe you'll get a lot of people going like, what was that? But then you won't get replayability. You won't get that timelessness of a song, and then it's again, because that becomes more of the focus. The focus is let's create a moment of a song instead of let's make a good song."

    Bands That Have Sustained

    The episode identifies several bands that have managed to maintain creative momentum while navigating—or simply surviving—the TikTok era. Architects receive particular attention as a case study in consistency, with Nik noting that they're "on album nine or 11 now" and that "each album has just been linearly bigger than the last one." For a band to reach their apparent commercial peak after nearly two decades of releasing music represents an extraordinary achievement, one that Nik describes as "very tricky, for bands to be that consistent."

    The conversation briefly touches on Mick Gordon's influence, noting his work on the Doom soundtrack—a project now ten years old that "is still one of the biggest influences on modern metal"—and suggesting that Bring Me The Horizon's Jordan Fish may have drawn from Gordon's approach when introducing greater complexity and width to their sound. This connection underscores how influences can travel across seemingly unrelated projects, with video game soundtracks ultimately shaping the trajectory of mainstream metal.

    Sleep Token, Bad Omens, and Loathe all receive mentions as bands navigating the contemporary landscape with notable success. The discussion of Loathe proves particularly interesting, tracing how a band that emerged around the COVID-19 era with a sound blending Deftones-style atmospherics with shoegaze textures and djent-like precision gradually built an audience without aggressive release cycles or obvious viral hits. "These songs had something very special to them," Nik observes. "They had to be more of that timeless effort where they feel like... People like, what's going on? Is this metal? Is it shoegaze?"

    This ambiguity—genre fluidity that resists easy categorization—may itself be a survival strategy in an era where algorithms reward clear identity signals. By refusing to occupy a single established category, these bands create space that can't be easily filled by the copycat bands that inevitably emerge whenever something succeeds.

    ---

    The Process Has Changed

    Beyond questions of song structure and viral optimization, Nik addresses how the actual practice of creating music has transformed. The image of a band "hanging in our garage and figuring it out until mom tells us to come up for dinner"—the organic, collaborative process that defined previous generations—has been replaced by something fundamentally different.

    "It's everyone's on their laptops. Everyone's either with their one producer or with, you know, now AI exists too, which is its own fun thing. So it's like the process is also really different." The capture of "that very exciting live, like, start to finish song moment with you and your bandmates" has become rare not merely because of economic or logistical pressures but because the tools and expectations surrounding music production have changed.

    This isn't presented as an inherently negative development—Nik acknowledges that the contemporary process produces real results—but it does suggest that the context in which music gets made has transformed so completely that certain types of songs may no longer be creatable in the same way. The spontaneous joy of the "do the thing" moment, where a producer hits a button and everyone laughs at the absurdity of what they've created, may be fundamentally incompatible with the measured, strategic approach that the TikTok era rewards.

    Detailed Takeaways

    The Clip Moment Is Now the Song's Front Door: Metal's structure has always built toward climactic moments, but TikTok has inverted this completely. The breakdown that previously represented the peak of a song's emotional arc now serves as the entry point for new listeners. This isn't merely a marketing shift; it fundamentally changes how artists conceptualize their work. A band writing a new track today must consider not just whether the breakdown delivers, but whether it delivers immediately and independently of context. The implication for songwriting is significant: if your best material requires five minutes of build-up to land, it may never land at all on these platforms.

    Authenticity Remains the Only Viable Strategy—Even When It Looks Calculated: The apparent contradiction Nik identifies—that bands succeeding on social media are those that seem to ignore it—points to a fundamental truth about platform economics. Algorithms increasingly detect and penalize obvious optimization, rewarding instead work that generates genuine engagement. This means that for artists seeking sustainable careers rather than flash-in-the-pan virality, the only reliable path is creating work with inherent value that happens to translate to short-form contexts. The bands that will matter in ten years are almost certainly not those asking "how do we go viral?" but rather those focused on making the best possible version of whatever they're trying to create.

    Genre Fluidity May Be a Survival Mechanism: The most successful recent metal acts—Loathe, Sleep Token, Bad Omens—share a refusal to be pinned to single categories. In an era where algorithmic recommendation depends on clear genre signals, this might seem like a disadvantage. Yet the opposite appears true: ambiguity creates space that can't be easily categorized, forcing listeners to engage with the music on its own terms rather than through established expectations. As metal continues absorbing influences from R&B, electronic music, shoegaze, and mainstream pop, the artists who thrive will likely be those comfortable existing in the gaps between genres rather than claiming clear territory.

    The 2008-2010 Era Represents Unmined Potential: That period's blend of experimentation, extremity, and genuine fun produced music with remarkable TikTok compatibility that has yet to be rediscovered systematically. For listeners seeking entry points into the genre's history, or for contemporary artists looking for templates, this era offers resources that remain largely untapped. The resurgence of deathcore and the willingness of established acts like Bring Me to revisit their extreme roots suggests the beginning of such a revival—but the full recovery of this era's legacy may still be ahead.

    Long-Term Success Requires Holism: Nik's critique of "moment-first" songwriting centers on its failure to generate replayability and timelessness. Songs optimized for isolated viral moments may generate initial engagement but struggle to sustain careers. The most durable metal acts—Architects approaching their twentieth year with increasing commercial success, Bring Me's decades-long evolution, Knock Loose's grassroots momentum—all share a commitment to making complete songs rather than collectionable moments. For artists making career decisions, the lesson is clear: chasing virality may provide short-term metrics, but only holistic artistic integrity builds lasting audiences.
