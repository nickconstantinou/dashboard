Overcoming depression and alcoholism | Jeff Kaplan and Lex Fridman

Introduction

In a raw and vulnerable conversation that represents some of the most honest dialogue about mental health struggles in the gaming industry, Jeff Kaplan—the renowned game developer formerly known as the face of World of Warcraft and later as President of Blizzard Entertainment—opens up about his battles with depression and alcoholism in a way he has never done before. Speaking with Lex Fridman, Kaplan recounts a journey that took him through the depths of despair, through failed attempts at self-medication, through the clinical world of therapy and antidepressants, and ultimately to a form of treatment so extreme that he had never previously disclosed it to anyone. This conversation arrives at a moment when discussions about mental health in the technology and gaming industries have become increasingly urgent, as the pressure of creative work, the isolation of remote collaboration, and the weight of leading massive gaming communities take their toll on developers who often feel they must project strength and invulnerability. Kaplan's willingness to detail his lowest moments—including drinking an entire bottle of bourbon alone while watching the Oscars and feeling overwhelming shame at the apparent success and polish of celebrities—represents a kind of therapeutic transparency that mental health advocates have long championed but that remains rare among high-profile industry leaders. The conversation not only charts Kaplan's personal path through darkness but also offers concrete wisdom for anyone currently trapped in their own low points, emphasizing that recovery is not linear, that finding the right therapist is essential rather than optional, and that sometimes the combination of meaningful work and genuine human connection can provide the foundation for rebuilding a life.

The Descent: Alcoholism, Depression, and the Oscars Moment

Kaplan traces the beginning of his most difficult period to his twenties, when he began drinking heavily and discovered that alcohol would become something he would "wrestle with until my early 30s." The severity of his drinking reached a point that he now looks back on with a mixture of shame and disbelief, recounting one particular evening when he purchased a bottle of Old Grand Dad bourbon and drank the entire thing by himself while watching the Oscars. This moment has become emblematic of his lowest point—not because of the quantity consumed, but because of the psychological context that surrounded it. As he sat drunk in front of his television, watching what he describes as "such a fake environment" filled with people who seemed "so together and successful and polished," the contrast between their apparent perfection and his own state of intoxication and misery became unbearable. "I just—it made me—it was that contrast that made me feel like such a failure," Kaplan recalls, acknowledging that all of this seems "so stupid and unimportant" to him now but recognizing that at the time, the emotional pain was absolutely real and consuming.

The pattern he describes is one familiar to countless individuals struggling with alcoholism and depression: a constant internal struggle where he would try not to drink, fail in that attempt, and then drink specifically to make the feelings of failure and self-loathing feel more manageable. This circular trap—using the very substance that worsens the underlying condition to temporarily escape that worsening condition—represents one of the most insidious aspects of addiction. Kaplan was fortunate in that his parents remained "very supportive of me even in my 20s, even after I, you know, quote unquote left the house," providing a safety net that many struggling with addiction do not have. However, parental support, while necessary, was not sufficient to pull him out of the spiral he had entered.

The Journey Through Treatment: Therapy, Medication, and Finding What Works

The first major intervention in Kaplan's recovery came through therapy, and his experience with the therapeutic process offers some of the most practical advice in the entire conversation. He emphasizes strongly that simply going to therapy—"checking a box of I went to therapy"—is not enough. Instead, he learned through trial and error that "you have to find the right therapist for you," someone who "sort of helps you get out of whatever rut you're in in a way that's healthy for you." This distinction matters enormously because the therapeutic relationship is deeply personal, and what works for one person may not work for another. Kaplan's willingness to acknowledge that finding the right therapist required effort and that he had to shop around in some sense represents an important counter-narrative to the sometimes simplistic advice to "just go to therapy" that mental health advocates sometimes offer without acknowledging the complexity of the therapeutic matching process.

In addition to therapy, Kaplan also experimented with antidepressant medication, but this approach proved unsuccessful for him. "I tried anti-depressants, but I hated—I just hated taking pills and feeling like something was in me and making me feel different," he explains. "I never responded to it." This aspect of his journey is worth noting because it underscores an important truth about mental health treatment: there is no universal solution, and what works for one person may not work for another. The stigma around medication-assisted treatment for mental health conditions remains significant, and Kaplan's honest admission that he "hated" the feeling of being on medication, while not discouraging others from exploring this avenue, provides validation for those who have had similar experiences and felt alone in their discomfort.

The Extreme Measure: Electroconvulsive Therapy and the Decision to Survive

The most shocking and historically significant revelation in Kaplan's account is his decision to undergo ECT, or electroconvulsive therapy—colloquially known as shock therapy. "The hardest thing, you know, which I've never mentioned to anyone and is hard for me to talk about, but eventually I went through ECT, which is electroconvulsive therapy, and that broke me out," Kaplan reveals. This disclosure is remarkable for multiple reasons. First, ECT remains one of the most stigmatized treatments in psychiatry, despite being one of the most effective interventions for severe depression, particularly in cases where other treatments have failed. The cultural perception of ECT, shaped by decades of misrepresentation in films and television, often evokes images of brutal, punitive procedures rather than the carefully administered, anesthetic-assisted treatment that is standard practice today.

Kaplan is careful not to present this as a miracle solution or to endorse it uncritically: "And I would never endorse that as a miracle." Instead, he contextualizes it within the severity of his situation: "I was at such a low point that people were very worried about me and my well-being and what was going to happen." He describes the decision as "sort of an extreme pull the rip cord like there's nothing else to lose moment," emphasizing that it was not a first or even second resort but rather an act of desperation when all other options had been exhausted. This framing is important because it resists both the temptation to present ECT as a panacea and the temptation to dismiss it as barbaric—it was, in his experience, a necessary intervention at a necessary moment, and it worked.

Finding Purpose: Blizzard, Creative Collaboration, and the Power of Meaningful Work

Following his most acute crisis, Kaplan's recovery was catalyzed by two interconnected factors: finding genuine community and discovering meaningful work. When he joined Blizzard Entertainment, he encountered what he describes as a fundamentally different environment from his previous experience in the film industry. Having spent four years as an undergraduate interning at Universal Pictures—driven by an initial ambition to work in film—Kaplan discovered that "it was such an unhealthy creative collaboration in the film industry." He describes the film industry as operating like "a caste system," with interns like himself at the bottom observing how "the other people who were low cast in the film industry were treated," and found the experience "just horrible."

Blizzard, by contrast, presented a stark contrast. "Games was different. Games was very flat," Kaplan recounts. "It didn't matter if you were the CEO or the boss. The way Mike and Allan carried themselves—with me who was an associate game designer—you felt like an equal." This flattening of hierarchy, this sense of being valued as an equal contributor regardless of title, became foundational to his recovery. But beyond the camaraderie—which was significant—he emphasizes that "the part that shouldn't be overlooked is the work itself and the work ethic. That's what really pulled me out. Hard work on a thing you love."

This insight is particularly powerful coming from someone who had previously been drawn to creative writing precisely because of "the solitude of it and the fact that you didn't have to collaborate and you could just write what you wanted to write and it was all you—you would succeed on your own or you would fail on your own." As an introvert, Kaplan had initially believed that he didn't need other people, but his experience at Blizzard taught him something different: "I think introverts almost need people more, and we don't always know how to engage in the right healthy ways and how to find people and how to connect with people." The combination of meaningful creative work, healthy collaboration, and genuine equality created an environment where he could rebuild himself.

Key Takeaways for Those Still in the Darkness

The Necessity of Finding the Right Therapeutic Match

Kaplan's experience underscores that therapy is not a monolithic solution where any therapist will do. He emphasizes that he "went into therapy and that was very helpful" but only after finding someone who worked for his specific needs. For someone currently seeking help, this suggests that if the first therapist doesn't feel right, trying others is not only acceptable but necessary. The therapeutic relationship is profoundly personal, and the investment in finding the right match can make the difference between productive healing and wasted time.

Medication Is Not One-Size-Fits-All

While many people benefit significantly from antidepressant medication, Kaplan's experience demonstrates that this path does not work for everyone. His honest admission that he "hated" the feeling of being on medication and "never responded to it" provides important validation for those who have had similar experiences. This takeaway is not meant to discourage anyone from exploring medication but rather to normalize the possibility that it might not be the right approach for everyone, and that there are other paths to recovery.

Extreme Interventions Have Their Place

The decision to undergo ECT was not something Kaplan made lightly or recommended casually. It was a "nothing else to lose moment" when his depression had reached a point where "people were very worried about me." The takeaway here is not that everyone should consider ECT but rather that when standard interventions fail, there are more intensive options available. Reducing stigma around effective but historically misunderstood treatments could save lives.

Meaningful Work and Genuine Connection Are Foundational

Perhaps the most inspiring aspect of Kaplan's recovery story is the role that meaningful work and healthy community played in his healing. He attributes his recovery to "hard work on a thing you love" and to finding a workplace where he "felt like an equal." For those struggling, this suggests that rebuilding one's life may involve not only internal work through therapy but also external changes that create opportunities for contribution, collaboration, and community.

Introverts Need Connection Too

Kaplan's reflection on his identity as an introvert is particularly poignant: "We don't always know how to engage in the right healthy ways and how to find people and how to connect with people." This insight challenges the stereotype that introverts are self-sufficient and don't need social connection. In reality, many introverts crave connection but lack the skills or opportunities to form healthy relationships. Recognizing this need is the first step toward addressing it.

Who This Conversation Is For

This conversation is essential viewing—and now essential reading—for anyone currently battling depression, alcoholism, or the crushing weight of low self-worth that can accompany these conditions. Kaplan's story offers hope without false promises, honesty without gratuitous detail, and practical wisdom born from lived experience rather than theoretical knowledge. It is also invaluable for friends and family members of those struggling, helping them understand the complexity of addiction and depression and the various paths toward recovery. Additionally, anyone working in creative industries—whether games, film, writing, or any other field that combines solitary work with commercial pressure—will find particular resonance in Kaplan's reflections on the relationship between meaningful work and mental health, and his critique of unhealthy creative environments. Finally, leaders and managers in any industry can learn from his description of the kind of workplace culture that allowed him to heal: flat hierarchies, genuine equality, and respect for contributors at every level. The conversation stands as a testament to the possibility of recovery and the power of vulnerability in creating authentic connection.
