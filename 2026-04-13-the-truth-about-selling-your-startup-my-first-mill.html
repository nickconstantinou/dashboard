The Truth About Selling Your Startup: What No One Tells You About the Acquisition Process

The announcement came on a Monday, but the deal had closed in December. When the wire finally hit on a Wednesday in early March, the founder found himself experiencing something unexpected: that moment—the first phone call where someone made an offer—had felt better than the actual closing. "When they first made the offer over the phone, the excitement hit me," he recalled. "But then I was like, 'Wait, but it didn't happen yet. We don't have anything signed.' So quell the excitement. And then it never felt as good as that did."

This candid admission from a young entrepreneur marks the opening of a raw conversation on *My First Million*, a podcast hosted by Sam Parr and his co-host, about the messy, emotional, and often misunderstood process of selling a company. What makes this episode particularly valuable is its departure from the typical Silicon Valley narrative—where companies are supposedly "bought, not sold" and founders are besieged by suitors. Instead, we're given an unfiltered look at what actually happens when a founder decides to run a sales process, receives initial lowball offers, doubts whether their company is even sellable, and ultimately lands a successful exit. The conversation exposes the gap between popular mythology and the reality of startup acquisitions, offering hard-won wisdom for anyone who might someday find themselves in a similar position.

The Myth of the Passive Exit

One of the central themes of the conversation challenges a piece of conventional wisdom that circulates through Silicon Valley like received gospel: that great companies are bought, not sold. "Companies are bought, not sold," Sam repeated, noting that big shots in the Valley say this constantly. "And I remember hearing that and I'm like, 'Oh, so people aren't hollering at me all the time that I'm a loser.' And then the reality is that's not the reality. Maybe for some people, but for other people that ain't it. It wasn't it for me. I had to figure out how to sell it."

The co-host, who had actually been a college student when he built and sold his company, echoed this sentiment. His company wasn't failing—it was generating revenue—but it wasn't achieving the goals he had set for it. There weren't huge companies knocking with briefcases full of cash. "I had to sell the company. I didn't get bought just passively," he explained. "And I felt like every story I had ever heard about somebody selling was everybody was knocking on our door, they wanted us and we picked—and I was like, 'Well, that ain't me.'"

This disconnect between mythology and reality creates a dangerous blind spot for founders. When you're building a company and fantasizing about an exit, you imagine a process that looks effortless: interest flooding in, negotiations that feel like a privilege, terms that validate years of sacrifice. The truth, as these entrepreneurs discovered, often involves actively running a sales process, creating competition where none initially exists, and facing rejection that can feel like confirmation of your deepest fears about the business you've built.

The College Deadline That Started Everything

For the younger founder, the decision to sell emerged from a very human pressure: he was about to start college, and he wanted what he called "a normal freshman experience where I don't need to worry about a company at the same time." The timing pushed him to take action rather than letting the business continue operating indefinitely.

Armed with this motivation, he began methodically. He made a list of ten companies that he felt "made the most sense" as potential acquirers—logical fits for his product and market. But rather than simply emailing these companies or calling their business development teams cold, he recognized a crucial strategic insight: cold outreach weakens your negotiating position. "You lose leverage if you're just cold outreaching," he explained. So instead, he pursued warm introductions.

The approach was clever in its framing. He worked through his network to get introductions that presented the meeting as the other person's idea, not a sales call. "I tried to get people that I knew to make warm intros kind of saying something along the lines of, 'Hey, I think you guys should meet. This is a cool company. I just thought of this as if it was his idea, not it was me asking him.'"

This tactic addresses something fundamental about how deals get done: acquirers are far more receptive when they feel like they're discovering an opportunity rather than being sold to. The psychological dynamic matters enormously. A cold email announcing "I want to sell you my company" puts the target on defense immediately. A warm introduction from a trusted mutual contact, framed as "you should check this out," arrives with credibility and curiosity already established.

The First Round: Offers That Hurt

The results were mixed and, in some ways, brutal. "We did a few calls, just met some of the team, and honestly I was surprised that like only after a couple meetings it was like very quick to get an offer," the founder recalled. This part sounded almost too good—but then came the numbers.

"It was like about what we would make in a year in profit," he said. The co-host's jaw dropped: "So 1x profit type of thing." To be clear, this wasn't a company losing money or barely scraping by. It was a business generating real returns. Yet the first-round offers suggested the market valued it at roughly one year of earnings—a multiple that would insult almost any founder who believed in their creation.

The emotional impact was swift. "After that I was kind of like, wait, is my company just not sellable?" The founder described spiraling into doubt, wondering whether the business was simply too small, too niche, or too whatever to attract serious interest. He started looking at companies like Weight Watchers, which had declared bankruptcy, and began questioning whether potential acquirers even had the resources to buy. "Are we just not going to be able to sell? Is this just going to be a cash flowing business forever?"

This moment of doubt represents one of the most psychologically dangerous phases of the acquisition process. The founder had taken a leap, exposed his company to potential buyers, and received valuations that felt like dismissals. He began planning for permanence—looking for a potential CEO to bring on so he could step away and move on to something else. The next several months, he said, "that's really what I believed."

The Breakthrough: A Conversation That Changed Everything

What pivoted the situation wasn't a sudden flood of interest or a changing market. It was advice from another founder who had been through the process. This unnamed entrepreneur explained how M&A bankers operate and, more importantly, pointed out something the younger founder had missed: there were companies he had never even heard of that should be on his list.

The insight was strategic but also psychological. These target companies—often mid-sized businesses preparing for growth, companies about to go public, or roll-up strategies looking to acquire smaller players—were willing to pay fair market value. They weren't waiting for founders to approach them with perfect pitch decks and polished data rooms. They were actively looking for opportunities but lacked the visibility to find them. A banker, or even just a well-connected founder with a systematic approach, could surface these hidden buyers.

The co-host immediately resonated with this experience. "Your experience is very similar to mine except you were 10 years younger," he said. But the emotional journey, he argued, was "similar to many entrepreneurs"—a pattern that repeats across industries, company types, and career stages.

The Emotional Rollercoaster Is the Process

Sam articulated a framework that emerged from comparing their experiences. The entrepreneur who builds a company with aspirations of selling it, goes through a first process, and receives a lowball offer often experiences a specific emotional arc. "You think, oh this company—lowballed the offer—and it's like it's equity at a privately held company that's overvalued, so it's basically worth nothing. Then you go through this process where you got your hopes up, then you're defeated. You're defeated for 10 days, and then you're like, 'All right, I have to go back to work and like maybe I'm just going to own this for forever, so I might as well just like build the company to last forever.'"

What changes, Sam explained, is time combined with adjusted expectations. "Then months later, years later, sometime later, then you get the second round. You care a little bit less, and your company is a little bit better because you've just assumed it's going to last longer."

The analogy he drew is telling: "And it's exactly like dating. The second you don't want to date someone, or you don't really care, they start coming to you."

This reframing—that the emotional detachment that comes from accepting permanence actually improves your position—cuts against every instinct a stressed founder has. When you're desperate to sell, you project neediness, accept worse terms, and respond to offers from a place of scarcity. When you've genuinely accepted that you'll own the business forever, you negotiate from abundance. Buyers sense this, consciously or not, and their behavior shifts accordingly.

The Power Asymmetry Between Founders and Acquirers

One observation that shaped how both founders approached their processes: when you're selling to a company owned by private equity, you're facing professionals who do this regularly. "It's like sitting down with Magnus Carlson for your first game," Sam比喻. "This is what they do, whereas this is your first time doing it."

This asymmetry explains many of the dynamics that feel confusing or unfair during acquisitions. Private equity firms and their portfolio companies have done dozens of deals. They have legal teams, financial models, acquisition playbooks, and most importantly, emotional distance. A first-time founder has none of these advantages—and more importantly, may not even know what questions to ask, what terms to negotiate, or when they're being lowballed versus receiving fair offers.

The younger founder acknowledged that "going into this, it was my first time with everything. So it was honestly scary and stressful, and I kept overthinking things." This honest admission highlights why learning from others' experiences matters so much. You get very few reps at selling a company—possibly only one in your entire career. Hearing stories of how it actually went down, including the failures and disappointments, provides context that no amount of theoretical preparation can replace.

What Actually Worked: Strategies and Tactics

Several specific tactics emerged from their discussion as having made real differences in their outcomes.

Running a systematic process rather than waiting passively. The assumption that buyers will come to you if you build something great is dangerous. Both founders describe having to actively create the conditions for a sale—building target lists, reaching out, creating competition.

Leveraging warm introductions over cold outreach. Framing the conversation as a discovery opportunity rather than a sales pitch preserves leverage and increases engagement from potential buyers.

The banker approach. Finding companies you hadn't considered—often smaller strategic buyers or roll-up candidates preparing for major growth—can surface value that the obvious corporate development teams miss.

Accepting the emotional cycle rather than fighting it. The second round often works better not because anything changed about the business, but because the founder's psychology shifted. Caring less produces better outcomes.

Timing matters. The younger founder specifically avoided announcing or pushing during peak acquisition season (January and February) to avoid being lost in the noise of other deals moving through the market.

Why This Conversation Matters Now

The gap between startup mythology and acquisition reality has real costs. Founders who believe they don't need to prepare for selling—who assume they'll simply "build it and buyers will come"—enter negotiations at a disadvantage. They don't know what questions to ask, what terms are reasonable, or how to create leverage in situations where they feel they have none.

More broadly, the emotional journey these founders describe challenges the stoic exterior that entrepreneurship culture often rewards. The vulnerability of admitting that first offers felt devastating, that doubt crept in, that you considered simply running the business forever—these admissions create space for other founders to be honest about their own experiences rather than performing confidence they don't feel.

The timing of the conversation also matters. With more people than ever starting companies at younger ages, the scenario of a college-age founder trying to exit while beginning higher education will become more common. Understanding that this path is legitimate—that you don't need to choose between building a company and getting an education—requires hearing stories where others made that choice and succeeded.

Key Takeaways for Founders Considering an Exit

The first takeaway addresses the mythology directly: the assumption that great companies are passively bought by eager acquirers is often wrong. Many successful exits require founders to actively run sales processes, create competition, and approach buyers who might never have otherwise considered their company. Waiting for inbound interest is a strategy, but it's often not sufficient.

The second insight concerns leverage in early conversations. Cold outreach signals desperation and weakens your negotiating position before conversations even begin. Warm introductions that frame the meeting as the acquirer's idea—coming from a trusted mutual contact who "thought you should meet"—create far more receptive dynamics. The psychological framing of who wants whom matters enormously.

Third, initial offers almost always feel disappointing, and this is normal rather than a signal of failure. Both founders described first-round offers that seemed insultingly low relative to their expectations and the company's performance. This experience is so common it constitutes almost a rite of passage. The key is not to abandon the process but to adjust expectations and continue building value.

Fourth, the advice from other founders who had sold was transformative in both cases. One conversation about how M&A bankers identify hidden buyers reframed what was possible. Founders have very few opportunities to practice selling companies, making experiential knowledge extremely valuable. Seeking out mentors or peers who had been through the process provided both tactical insights and psychological reassurance.

Finally, the counterintuitive insight that caring less improves outcomes deserves serious attention. The dating analogy captures something real: when you've genuinely accepted that you might own the business forever, you negotiate differently. This isn't about playing games or feigning indifference. It's about the psychological shift that comes from removing the pressure of needing a sale to happen. Buyers respond to this energy, and the deals that close often do so on better terms once founders have moved through their desperation phase.

Who Should Listen to This Conversation

This episode serves founders who are actively building companies and may eventually pursue an exit, whether in the near term or years from now. The tactical insights about running sales processes, creating leverage, and identifying hidden buyers provide concrete preparation for a moment that will arrive with little warning.

It also serves founders currently in the acquisition process, particularly those in the demoralizing phase after receiving first-round offers that feel insulting. Understanding that this experience is nearly universal—that even founders who ultimately close successful deals went through moments of despair—provides both comfort and perspective.

Aspiring entrepreneurs who haven't yet started companies will benefit from understanding the full arc of the entrepreneurial journey, including parts that founder lore tends to omit. The exit, as portrayed here, is not a triumphant finale but another phase of work, with its own challenges, setbacks, and ultimately its own rewards.

Finally, anyone who advises founders—investors, mentors, service providers—will gain appreciation for the emotional complexity of the exit process. Understanding that founders often feel isolated in these experiences, that the mythology they hear doesn't match their reality, allows advisors to provide more genuine support at moments when it's needed most.
