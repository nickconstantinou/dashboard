# Why Chelsea's Double Gameweek 33 Still Makes Sense for FPL Managers

Chelsea's last three Premier League matches have ended 0-0—except they haven't. They've lost all three to nil, failing to score against Manchester City, Everton, and Newcastle. In the underlying numbers, though, something different is happening. Under Liam Rosenior, the Blues are third in the league for expected goals per 90 minutes. That disconnect—dire results against genuinely promising attack—makes Double Gameweek 33 worth a second look.

Chelsea face Manchester United at home before traveling to Brighton. On paper, neither opponent is a gift. United and Brighton rank fifth and sixth respectively for expected goals conceded across the season. Brighton have shipped just three goals in their last six matches, keeping three clean sheets in the process and sitting second in xGC during that run. United, meanwhile, have been solid by most metrics. But a closer look reveals cracks worth exploiting.

---

## Manchester United: More Vulnerable Than The Table Suggests

United's defensive numbers look respectable until you notice the clean sheet count. Only Burnley and Wolverhampton Wanderers have kept fewer in 2025/26. On Monday alone, Leeds United carved them open for four "big chances" and 2.57 expected goals—a performance that Rosenior's side would very much like to replicate.

The timing matters. Harry Maguire (£4.4m), serving a one-match ban after being charged by the FA for improper conduct, may not return in Gameweek 33. Kobbie Mainoo (£4.7m) is also a doubt. Without two likely starters in the back line, Chelsea's attacking players—particularly those in the £7-9m bracket—become considerably more attractive. The data suggests goals are there for the taking.

---

## Brighton: Formidable on Paper, Fragile in Practice

Brighton's xGC ranking is real. Third-most chances conceded in the last six matches, with only Liverpool and Nottingham Forest ahead of them. But the Turf Moor trip on Saturday offered a cautionary illustration. Burnley scored twice only to see both goals chopped off by narrow offside calls. Bart Verbruggen (£4.5m) made four saves of genuine quality to preserve the clean sheet.

Riding luck isn't the same as being solid. Chelsea, for all their current problems, have created enough under Rosenior to profit from a side that has scraped by rather than dominated. The underlying numbers suggest two goals across the double is entirely realistic—one from each match.

---

## What Rosenior's Chelsea Actually Offer

The recent results are ugly. Three straight defeats, no goals scored, and a defensive record that has collapsed badly since Enzo Maresca's final weeks in charge. But the attack has held up. Ninth for xGC under Rosenior, yes—but third for xG per 90, and that includes matches against City, Arsenal, and Liverpool. The goals will come.

Set-piece threat is minimal (19th for set-piece shots and xG), so anyone targeting Chelsea corners or free-kicks will be disappointed. But open-play creation is genuine, and the fixture double gives owners a rare second bite at both matchups.

The wildcard is rotation. With Champions League qualification still mathematically possible, Rosenior has something to play for, but the squad is stretched. Absentees remain a factor, and the balance between pressing for Europe and protecting key players could shift unexpectedly. That's a risk worth monitoring before the deadline.

---

## The Verdict

Chelsea aren't in form. They haven't kept a clean sheet since January, and their last six weeks have been defined by losses rather than near-misses. But Double Gameweeks are about opportunity cost, and Chelsea's attacking assets remain priced cheaply enough to justify inclusion against opponents with exploitable weaknesses. United are missing bodies and leaking chances. Brighton are riding fine margins.

Two goals across the double feels like a floor, not a ceiling. For managers navigating the run-in with limited free transfers and budget locked elsewhere, that floor is worth building around.
