# Markets in a Permanent Mini-Crisis: Why Investors Are Waiting, Not Predicting

A Weekly Market Debrief That Reveals How Economic Uncertainty Has Reshaped Investor Behavior

The world has grown so accustomed to perpetual uncertainty that the absence of crisis feels almost disorienting. In this week's *Money Debrief* podcast, hosts John Sterwick, senior reporter and author of the *Money Distilled* newsletter, and Merrin Sunset Webb, Editor-at-Large for Bloomberg UK Wealth, explore a phenomenon that has quietly transformed how markets function: the shift from predictive pricing to reactive waiting. The episode arrives at a moment when geopolitical instability—particularly the ongoing Middle East conflict—has created what the hosts describe as a "state of permanent hubbub," where neither full-scale war nor genuine peace defines the investment landscape. Rather than pricing in future scenarios as they once might have, markets now seem paralyzed, waiting for concrete data before committing to valuations. This episode unpacks why this shift matters profoundly for anyone with skin in the game, from pension holders to retail investors navigating an increasingly complex economic environment.

What makes this discussion particularly valuable is its refusal to reduce the market's current malaise to a single cause. Instead, Sterwick and Webb methodically trace the connections between geopolitical uncertainty, corporate earnings disappointments, UK economic data, pension policy battles, and private credit risks—threads that, when woven together, reveal an economy operating under fundamentally different rules than those that governed the 2010s. The conversation also offers rare praise for the House of Lords, a surprising tangent into house price psychology, and a veiled confession that even the hosts sometimes struggle to maintain optimism about the UK's trajectory.

---

The Permanent Mini-Crisis: A New Market Paradigm

The episode opens with Webb and Sterwick addressing the elephant in the room: the Middle East conflict that has defined headlines for months. Their diagnosis cuts through the binary framing of "war days" versus "truce days" that has characterized some media coverage. "We're in a constant mini-crisis," Webb observes, "no one knows quite what's going on rolling mode." Sterwick reframes this as a "state of permanent hubbub"—not constant warfare, but not genuine stability either. The critical insight here is that both sides, despite their posturing, genuinely want an exit ramp they cannot agree upon. Iran recognizes the risks of further entanglement with an unpredictable adversary, while Trump allegedly harbors concerns about potential oil supply disruptions. The result is an elongated, messy de-escalation process that defies easy prediction.

This geopolitical stalemate has created a peculiar market environment where investors cannot confidently price anything because the fundamental variables keep shifting. The hosts argue that markets have always possessed some predictive capability—participants are "pretty clever," as Sterwick notes, and they price considerable information in advance—but that predictive horizon has now "shrunk massively." In the 2010s, famously dubbed the "long duration decade," time effectively didn't matter in investing calculations. Near-zero interest rates meant that profits earned far in the future held nearly equivalent value to profits earned tomorrow, making growth companies with distant horizons the obvious winners. That world has evaporated. The predictability horizon has contracted so severely that the hosts suggest nobody knows what will happen next, especially given how Trump or Iranian leadership can issue statements that completely alter the landscape from one day to the next. Markets, they argue, are behaving rationally in shrinking their discounting ability when the future genuinely resists confident projection.

---

When Corporate Reality Meets Market Waiting: The Profit Warning Case Studies

The theoretical discussion gains concrete grounding through two significant corporate profit warnings that Sterwick examines from the UK market. The first involves Chris Nicholson, a housebuilder whose trajectory demonstrates the problem of pricing in an unknowable environment. Just weeks earlier, Nicholson had assured markets that operations were proceeding normally. By then, the war had been ongoing for approximately a month, yet the company's official position remained reassuring. However, their subsequent trading update told a dramatically different story: sales expectations collapsed so severely that the company announced it would not sell any land during the current year, and profit forecasts underwent substantial downward revision. The market's response was swift and brutal—a 35% single-day share price decline. What makes this case study particularly instructive is that Nicholson had access to the same information as everyone else but still misjudged the trajectory, suggesting that the difficulty of prediction is not merely a market participant problem but affects corporate leadership as well.

The second example comes from Reckitt Benckiser, the consumer goods conglomerate whose share price had been declining since February. When the company issued its latest update, the stock fell another 5% after executives acknowledged that Middle East disruption had materially impacted like-for-like sales. The critical point Sterwick draws from both examples is that markets have reached an equilibrium where they cannot assign prices to events until they observe real-world impacts materializing. "We look at the prices and we say, well the market's not reacting so it can't be that bad," he explains, "but the market is waiting to get data from the real world because it's kind of like, well how do we know what's going to happen next? There is no way to put a price on it." The hosts predict this pattern will continue as bad data feeds through the system—markets require proof rather than projections before adjusting valuations. This represents a profound shift from the historical assumption that sophisticated investors anticipate events before they unfold.

---

The UK Economy: Grim Sentiment Meets Surprisingly Resilient Fundamentals

The conversation then pivots to UK economic analysis, where Sterwick and Webb identify a troubling disconnect between hard economic data and the collective mood. Several positive signals have emerged despite the doom-and-gloom atmosphere: February GDP growth exceeded expectations, though the hosts admit limited enthusiasm given that this data preceded the conflict; UK unemployment fell slightly, though Sterwick suspects this partly reflects discouraged workers exiting the labor force rather than genuine hiring strength; and the budget deficit has declined to a three-year low. The hosts even acknowledge that underlying economic strength is "better than most people had thought," a view they have held for some time. PMI data released during the recording period showed services and manufacturing activity running significantly ahead of expectations for April, partly explained by companies stocking inventory ahead of anticipated Middle East disruptions.

Yet the hosts remain "very negative on the UK economy" and identify the primary culprit: inflation has poisoned consumer sentiment in ways that hard data fails to capture. An Ipsos consumer confidence survey released the previous day revealed British consumers feeling gloomier than they did in the lead-up to the 2008 financial crisis or even the dark days of 1979. Webb cuts to the heart of the matter: "I think inflation is probably the big driver of most people's misery because everyone feels poorer and feels that they're being ripped off and doesn't see things getting any better." The combination of general inflation with falling house prices creates an especially difficult environment for British households. Sterwick observes that people have psychologically anchored to house values that no longer exist, and many will discover when they eventually sell that they haven't accumulated any real wealth despite years of ownership. The hosts reference an upcoming interview with Andy Haldane, the former Bank of England economist, where this household balance sheet question takes center stage—despite significant deleveraging over the past decade and genuinely strong household balance sheets, confidence remains absent, preventing these strengths from translating into economic activity.

The underlying economic picture, while not catastrophic, faces serious risks if interest rates remain elevated or climb further due to the energy crisis the UK likely faces. The housing market, particularly sensitive to rate movements, could deteriorate further, taking the wealth effect with it. Sterwick offers his characteristic blunt assessment: "The biggest problem the UK has is the headwind from bad governance... Labour have certainly raised it to [being as bad as the previous lot]." Yet he maintains that beneath the misery, positive structural dynamics exist that could flourish with competent governance—creating an implicit warning that policy mistakes could squander genuine potential.

The Pension Mandation Battle: When Government Ambition Meets Institutional Resistance

A substantial portion of the episode addresses an arcane but consequential political battle over pension policy. The so-called Mansion House Accord, struck between the UK government and seventeen of the nation's largest pension providers, established a voluntary framework requiring these providers to allocate 10% of defined contribution pensions to private assets, with 5% specifically directed toward UK investments. The hosts park their skepticism about this policy's wisdom and focus on what happened next: the government inserted enforcement provisions into the pensions bill that went dramatically beyond the voluntary agreement. The clause would have given authorities power not merely to mandate compliance with the Mansion House terms but to force pension funds to invest in "anything at all" with no caps whatsoever.

The House of Lords, that often-maligned parliamentary chamber, fortunately rejected this overreach. The government retreated somewhat, offering to limit mandated requirements to the 10% private assets threshold established by the Accord, but the Lords rejected this compromise as well. The bill now faces potential collapse or indefinite delay, unlikely to clear Parliament in the current session. Sterwick expresses uncharacteristic gratitude toward the Lords: "It's nice to see that someone's at least attempting to defend our freedom to invest in what we feel we should be investing in rather than having it dictated to." The hosts then clarify their broader institutional philosophy: both identify as "not political at all" and Sterwick confesses genuine support for the House of Lords, which he describes as functioning "very well for many hundreds of years" despite theoretical objections to unelected legislators. This episode illustrates the chamber's practical value when executive ambition overreaches.

The pension discussion then expands to address NEST, the auto-enrollment pension scheme covering approximately thirteen to fourteen million Britons. The Financial Conduct Authority issued a letter during the week warning pension funds to monitor their liquidity carefully while cautioning that selling private assets could trigger "huge costs." The hosts note that this regulatory language encompasses both genuine transaction expenses and realized losses—selling private assets at a discount to stated net asset value is, fundamentally, accepting a loss, not merely paying a fee. The conversation draws on analysis from Sona Asset Management published in FT AlphaVille, which examined private credit's historical development and current difficulties. The key takeaway Sterwick emphasizes is not that private credit will trigger a systemic crisis like 2008, nor that the asset class lacks validity—junk bonds, after all, survived their own early blowups—but that the asset class faces an extended period of poor performance that makes current entry timing particularly poor. The hosts conclude with urgent counsel: pension holders need to take personal responsibility for understanding what lurks inside their retirement accounts because institutional intermediaries cannot be counted upon to protect them from ill-timed allocations.

Key Insights: Five Critical Takeaways from the Market Wrap

Markets Have Shifted from Predictive to Reactive Functioning

The hosts argue that the 2010s "long duration" environment, characterized by predictable future cash flows and low interest rates, trained investors to think markets would anticipate events. That paradigm has broken. Today, with geopolitical uncertainty常态化 and no clear mechanism for pricing future scenarios, markets rationally wait for actual data before moving. This means investors should expect volatility that tracks real-world events rather than anticipating them. The practical implication: portfolio strategies that relied on market efficiency in pricing future developments may need fundamental rethinking.

The Middle East Conflict Has No Easy Exit—And Markets Know It

Both Iran and Western allies face internal pressures limiting escalation, yet acceptable off-ramps remain elusive. This "permanent hubbub" environment creates a risk premium that refuses to resolve, leaving oil prices and related commodities in perpetual flux. Investors cannot assume geopolitical risk will diminish on any predictable timeline. Diversification and hedging strategies that acknowledge this permanent uncertainty deserve preference over assumptions of imminent resolution.

UK Household Psychology, Not Fundamentals, May Be the Real Problem

Despite terrible consumer sentiment and house price stagnation, UK household balance sheets remain structurally healthier than perception suggests. Haldane's forthcoming analysis confirms significant deleveraging over the past decade. This disconnect means the UK economy could potentially respond vigorously to improved sentiment—but only if government policy addresses the inflationary misery that has depressed confidence. Investors watching UK equities should separate structural potential from sentiment-driven mispricing in both directions.

Private Credit Faces an Extended Underperformance Period

The Sona Asset Management analysis suggests private credit, while not broken, will deliver disappointing returns for some time. For pension holders with auto-enrollment exposure to these assets, this matters enormously. The hosts' explicit warning—"now is probably not the time to be getting into this stuff"—applies equally to retirement savers and institutional allocators. Reading regulatory liquidity warnings as implicit recognition of value compression in private asset marks provides a valuable signal.

The House of Lords Still Serves a Purpose

The episode provides unusual but genuine evidence that unelected check-and-balance institutions retain meaningful function in British governance. When executive ambition overreached into forcing pension allocations with no caps or accountability, parliamentary oversight corrected course. This has implications beyond pensions: broader debates about regulatory overreach, government investment mandates, and institutional autonomy all benefit from the existence of review mechanisms that operate outside immediate political cycles.

Who Should Listen to This Episode

This *Money Debrief* episode serves several distinct audiences with particular value propositions. UK-based retail investors and pension holders will find essential context for understanding why their retirement savings face headwinds they may not perceive from mainstream financial news. The private credit warning deserves particular attention for those with defined contribution pensions automatically enrolled into private market strategies. Professional investors and financial advisors gain a framework for explaining to clients why market behavior seems so unmoored from historical precedent, with the predictive-versus-reactive distinction offering useful counseling language. Economics enthusiasts interested in the UK will appreciate the careful distinction between sentiment indicators and hard data, a nuanced reading that most media coverage fails to maintain. Policy observers will find the pension mandate battle instructive for understanding how regulatory ambition sometimes exceeds both evidence and parliamentary授权, and how institutional checks respond.

The episode's greatest value lies in its refusal to offer false comfort or partisan verdicts. Sterwick and Webb describe the UK economy as facing genuine structural challenges driven by bad governance while acknowledging underlying strengths that proper policy could unlock. They identify serious risks in private credit allocation while dismissing catastrophic scenarios. This intellectual honesty—delivered with the podcast's characteristic dark humor and occasional self-deprecation about the hosts' own gloom—makes the episode worth recommending to anyone trying to understand markets operating in an era when the future stubbornly refuses to reveal itself.
