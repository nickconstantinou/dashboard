# Mastering FPL Chip Strategy: Gameweek 33 Review and Gameweek 34 Free Hit Planning

Fantasy Premier League managers face one of their most consequential decisions of the season as we enter the final stretch with just five gameweeks remaining. For those still holding their Free Hit chip, gameweek 34 presents a unique opportunity—but also a challenging puzzle given the fixture landscape and limited wildcard options remaining. FPL Harry, a prominent FPL content creator, recently shared his detailed analysis of his gameweek 33 performance, his strategic thinking for the Free Hit, and the data-driven approach he uses to maximize his remaining chips.

The content comes at a critical juncture in the FPL calendar. With rank preservation and improvement becoming increasingly competitive, every decision carries amplified weight. Harry's video provides both a retrospective on his bench boost strategy in gameweek 33 and a forward-looking preview of his gameweek 34 Free Hit draft, offering viewers insight into the meticulous planning required to climb ranks in the closing weeks of a Premier League season.

Gameweek 33 Performance: Bench Boost Execution

The gameweek 33 period represented the major double-gameweek of the season, and Harry executed what he describes as a carefully planned strategy involving a wildcard in gameweek 32 followed by the bench boost chip in gameweek 33. After employing his wildcard to optimize his squad specifically for the double gameweek, Harry successfully activated the bench boost, though the results told a complicated story.

Harry achieved 132 points from the bench boost—a score he acknowledges felt satisfying given the planning involved, though circumstances prevented an even stronger outcome. The primary issue arose with João Pedro, who failed to play any minutes during the double gameweek. This meant that rather than maximizing the chip with his intended starting eleven plus bench optimization, Harry found himself forced into making substitutions that cost him potential points. Had João Pedro featured, Harry's bench boost would have accumulated 36 points from the bench alone, a figure he describes as "crazy" for the chip. Instead, the bench still delivered respectably with 26 points, featuring contributions from players like Onana (8 points with a clean sheet), Van Dijk (12 points with an assist and clean sheet), and Bruno Fernandes (6 points).

Despite the João Pedro setback, Harry's overall performance demonstrated strong strategic thinking. He managed a "green arrow" improvement from around 40,000 in the overall rankings to approximately 20,400—a jump of roughly 20,000 places. His captain choice of Erling Haaland proved correct, with the Manchester City striker scoring across both matches in the double gameweek, including a penalty in the first encounter. Harry notes that everyone who captained Haaland achieved similar results, but his team composition allowed him to capitalize on the double gameweek more effectively than those who didn't have access to City coverage.

The transfer activity in gameweek 33 involved selling Gabriel Martinelli, who failed to register any points, and bringing in Jaël Bravo of Leeds (who scored 10 points), along with Darwin Núñez, who uncertainty surrounded regarding his availability but ultimately returned 13 points. Harry expressed some disappointment with the Bournemouth defensive assets, particularly given their home fixture against Leeds, where James Hill scored an own goal despite Bournemouth generally performing well in attack.

Data-Driven Fixture Analysis for Gameweek 34

Understanding the fixture landscape requires careful examination of projected goal data, and Harry utilizes data from Rob TfPl (a respected FPL analyst on Twitter) to inform his decision-making. The analysis reveals several important considerations for Free Hit strategy.

Starting with expected goals, Liverpool emerge at the top with 2.17 projected goals for their home fixture against Crystal Palace. However, Harry expresses skepticism about this figure, noting that Crystal Palace's defense has been remarkably stingy, conceding just one goal across their previous three matches. The complication arises from Palace's involvement in the Europa Conference League semifinal, with their attention potentially divided between domestic and European competitions. Harry suggests that despite Palace playing on Monday night before the weekend fixture, they might field a full-strength side, which could make Liverpool's attacking potential more limited than the raw data suggests.

Arsenal's fixture against Newcastle presents an interesting dichotomy. While Newcastle has been inconsistent over recent weeks, Harry notes that manager Eddie Howe has historically set up his team effectively to counter Arsenal's style of play. Nevertheless, Newcastle currently appears vulnerable, making Arsenal attackers theoretically attractive. The difficulty lies in predicting which Arsenal player will deliver returns—whether it's Martin Ødegaard, Bukayo Saka (potentially returning from injury), or others. Harry describes this as "quite difficult to predict where the attacking returns are going to be," suggesting that the uncertainty might make pure data-driven picks less reliable than expected.

Manchester United's home fixture against Brentford represents one of the more favorable attacking matchups according to the data. Similarly, Tottenham's away game at Wolves catches Harry's attention as potentially the most interesting differential opportunity of the gameweek. After analyzing projected goals and clean sheet odds, Harry observes that "once you go past Spurs, you drop off the data quite a lot," with teams like Fulham, Aston Villa, and West Ham all clustering around 1.44 projected goals—a notably lower expectation that complicates Free Hit selection.

The clean sheet probability data reinforces Arsenal's appeal at 37%, followed by Liverpool at 35% and Tottenham at a surprisingly high 34%. Harry acknowledges that the Tottenham figure "feels quite high" given their inconsistent season, but notes that this reflects the weakness of their opponents Wolves rather than Tottenham's own defensive reliability. Other notable clean sheet percentages include Nottingham Forest against Sunderland at 29%, though Harry highlights the paradox that Sunderland have improved significantly at home while Forest have just conceded four goals.

Harry emphasizes that the overall fixture picture makes gameweek 34 "a difficult week to pick players who are reliably going to perform," but this difficulty also creates opportunities for managers willing to take calculated risks on differentials.

The Tottenham Differential Question

Among the various strategic decisions Harry discusses, the Tottenham situation generates perhaps the most debate. The North London club has received relatively little FPL investment throughout the season due to their inconsistent performances, yet they present an intriguing Free Hit opportunity because of their favorable fixture against Wolves.

Harry identifies potential differential picks including players like Destiny Udogie or Dominik Solanke, acknowledging that "there will be huge disparity of opinion" among the FPL community regarding Tottenham assets. He articulates two competing perspectives: one camp argues that Tottenham's need to fight for Champions League qualification and their improved recent performances against Brighton make them attractive, while the other camp points to their alarming form—specifically that they haven't won since 2026—and questions why anyone would invest in players from such a struggling team.

Harry positions himself "somewhere in the middle," admitting he doesn't currently plan to end up with a Tottenham player in his team. However, he expresses genuine consideration for the possibility of including one Tottenham asset in his Free Hit draft as the deadline approaches, recognizing that their fixture represents one of the better attacking opportunities available in the gameweek.

This hedging demonstrates the nuanced decision-making required for Free Hit chips, where the potential for differential ownership to dramatically affect overall rank must be balanced against the fundamental quality of the attacking opportunity.

Free Hit Draft Construction and Budget Constraints

Harry's preview of his Free Hit draft reveals the tight financial constraints many FPL managers face at this stage of the season. His draft currently has just £0.1 million in the bank—an extremely thin margin that required "pretty active transfers" throughout the week to maintain. He acknowledges that some of his spending represents optimization rather than necessity, noting that "there are players that I'm spending the money on which feels almost like I'm spending it for the sake of it."

The goalkeeper position presents a particularly interesting dilemma. David Raya of Arsenal features in Harry's draft, despite Arsenal topping the clean sheet projections for gameweek 34. However, Harry expresses reservations about this selection, recognizing that goalkeepers rarely provide the ceiling-breaking returns that can transform a Free Hit from merely acceptable to exceptional. He explains that Raya "doesn't make very many saves," meaning he's likely to register only two or six points even in the best-case scenario—a far cry from the 10-15 point hauls that premium attacking options might achieve.

This reveals a fundamental tension in Free Hit construction: the desire to maximize clean sheet probability versus the need to capture attacking potential. Harry admits that if he were "chasing rank more than I feel like I am at the moment," he would consider punting on a goalkeeper with higher ceiling potential and simply hoping that Arsenal don't concede. His current approach prioritizes security over ceiling, though he acknowledges this might not be optimal for aggressive rank climbing.

The budget goalkeeper on his bench is Kinsky of Tottenham for the away fixture at Wolves, representing the kind of budget option managers often employ to maximize spending on premium attacking assets elsewhere in the squad.

This forward-thinking approach allowed him to maximize the chip despite the unexpected João Pedro situation, as the underlying squad construction remained sound.

For gameweek 34, Harry continues this planning mindset, even though he acknowledges that his Free Hit draft "probably" won't include Tottenham players initially. This holistic approach allows him to identify situations where the data might overstate or understate actual attacking potential, as demonstrated in his skepticism about Liverpool's 2.17 projected goals against a defensively improving Crystal Palace.

Key Strategic Insights for FPL Managers

Multi-gameweek planning compounds returns. Harry's successful bench boost demonstrates the value of thinking beyond single gameweeks. By using his wildcard in gameweek 32 specifically to optimize for the double gameweek bench boost, he created a scenario where the entire squad contributed to points rather than just eleven players. Managers should consider how chip usage in one gameweek affects opportunities in subsequent weeks, and plan transfers accordingly to maximize the compounding effect of strategic decisions.

Data provides direction, not certainty. While Harry relies heavily on Rob TfPl's projections, he consistently applies qualitative judgment to the numbers. The Liverpool versus Crystal Palace analysis exemplifies this: despite favorable data for Liverpool, Palace's recent defensive improvements and European commitments create legitimate questions about whether the projected goals will materialize. Managers should use data to identify opportunities but validate those opportunities against contextual factors that raw numbers might miss.

Fixture appeal must be balanced against player quality. Tottenham's favorable fixture against Wolves creates theoretical appeal, but Tottenham's individual player quality and consistency issues complicate the decision. Similarly, budget goalkeepers like David Raya might offer clean sheet probability but limited upside. The Free Hit format requires managers to weigh whether they're chasing the best fixture or the best player for that fixture, with the answer often depending on how much rank risk they're willing to accept.

Different gameweeks require different strategies. Harry's approach to the bench boost (maximize bench contribution) differs from his approach to the Free Hit (optimize the starting eleven for one week). Managers should resist applying a single strategic framework across all situations and instead adapt their thinking based on the specific mechanics and constraints of each chip.

Managing budget requires active attention. Harry's decision to "stretch" his team value with just £0.1 million in the bank demonstrates that squad value management continues to matter even in the final gameweeks. Managers who fail to optimize their budget might find themselves unable to afford the players they want, while those who push too hard risk being unable to react to late injury news.
