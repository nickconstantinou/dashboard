Who's Making Money Off Trump's War in Iran?

An Analysis of War Costs, Political Gambles, and Global Headlines

---

On a typical morning, the world's press presents readers with a relatively coherent narrative about the issues shaping our world. But on the morning covered by this FRANCE 24 press review segment, viewers and readers were greeted with something far more jarring: two completely contradictory storylines competing for attention. On one hand, major newspapers including *The Guardian* were heralding an impending oil shock, warning of cost of living rises amid what appeared to be an escalating war in Iran. French publication *L'Humanité* went further, pointing to capitalism and fossil fuels bringing society to a "breakdown point." The world, it seemed, was facing a historic economic crisis born from conflict in the Middle East.

Yet literally alongside these ominous warnings sat an entirely different narrative—one promoted aggressively by former President Donald Trump himself. Trump had claimed, in statements made the previous night, that the war was "practically finished" and that Iran "has nothing left." British publications including *The Times* and *The Daily Telegraph* led with variations of this claim, suggesting the conflict was nearly over. The juxtaposition was jarring: peace apparently breaking out even as economists warned of catastrophe. The confusion was understandable, and it set the stage for a deeper investigation into what was really happening—and more importantly, who was profiting.

---

Following the Money: The True Cost of War

The press review, led by journalist Antonia Carrian, pivoted immediately to what she described as the most important question: not whether the war was ending or escalating, but rather who was making money from it. Carrian pointed to analysis in the American press that had been diligently "following the money," and what they found was striking.

According to reporting in *The Washington Post*, the war in Iran had already cost American coffers approximately $5.6 billion in munitions alone—spent in just the first two days of strikes. This extraordinary figure represents only the ammunition expended in the initial assault phase, not the broader military operations, logistics, or long-term commitments that would inevitably follow. The administration, Carrian noted, was now preparing to send Congress an additional defense budget request specifically to sustain what was becoming an increasingly expensive military campaign.

Perhaps even more telling than the past spending was the shift in military tactics now underway. In the early days of the conflict, the United States had deployed its most advanced—and most expensive—weaponry. These precision systems, costing millions of dollars per strike, had proven devastating but were not sustainable at scale. Now, the military was "moving away from their more advanced expensive weaponry" toward "cheaper weaponry," shifting from millions per strike down to tens or hundreds of thousands per strike. War, in other words, was becoming more economical—but it remained, as Carrian emphasized, "an extremely expensive business."

---

The Trump Brothers and the Drone Enterprise

If the American taxpayer was footing an enormous bill for the war in Iran, there were also those positioned to benefit handsomely from it—perhaps none more prominently than the sons of the man who had initiated the conflict. Carrian directed attention to an exclusive report in *The Wall Street Journal* revealing that Eric Trump and Donald Trump Jr. had backed a new drone company targeting Pentagon sales.

The company, called PowerUs, represents a direct play for the enormous defense spending that conflicts like the one in Iran generate. According to the reporting, the company is working toward producing 10,000 drones per month—a scale that would make them a leader in American drone manufacturing. With the Pentagon having announced plans to spend $1.1 billion on US-made drones by next year, the Trump sons are positioning themselves to capture a significant slice of this rapidly expanding market.

The timing is notable. While the elder Trump has publicly championed ending wars, his family business interests are simultaneously positioning to profit from the ongoing—and potentially expanding—military operations. This dynamic raises questions about the true incentives at play, though Carrian presented the facts without explicit editorial commentary, letting the numbers speak for themselves.

---

Betting on War: The Rise of Crypto Prediction Markets

Beyond traditional defense contractors, another group has found ways to profit from the conflict: anonymous online gamblers operating in the largely unregulated world of cryptocurrency-based prediction markets. Carrian highlighted reporting from *The New Yorker* that documented a remarkable phenomenon: six anonymous gamblers collectively made $1.2 million by betting on the initial US strikes on Iran.

The mechanism is straightforward in concept but troubling in practice. These bets were placed on prediction market platforms such as Poly Market, which operate using cryptocurrency and face far less regulation than traditional stock markets. The crypto analytics firm Bubble Map has suggested that the extraordinary accuracy of these bets—gains totaling over one million dollars in a single round of wagers—points to something more suspicious than mere luck. The firm suspects that "administration or military chain of command insiders" may have been acting with classified information.

This represents a novel form of what would clearly constitute insider trading in traditional financial markets. When someone with knowledge of impending military strikes bets money on those strikes occurring and wins at an improbable rate, the parallel to classic insider trading is obvious. However, because these transactions occur on cryptocurrency-based prediction markets rather than regulated stock exchanges, existing securities laws may not apply. As Carrian noted, this is "something that of course would be insider trading if it were the classic stock market"—but it isn't, leaving a significant gap in regulatory oversight precisely when information about military operations could be worth millions.

---

France Goes to the Polls: Municipal Elections as a Political Barometer

Shifting geographic focus, Carrian turned to France, where voters were preparing to go to the polls on Sunday for the first round of voting in municipal elections. These elections, which will determine the leadership of 35,000 mayors across the country, represent far more than local governance—they serve as what Carrian described as "a touchstone on the road to the presidentials," a critical indicator of shifting political winds ahead of the next presidential race.

The far-right National Rally party enters these elections with ambitions for significant expansion. Currently controlling only around 15 cities, the party is hoping to "conquer dozens" of municipalities in this vote. Their ambitions extend to "big trophies" like Marseille, France's second-largest city. A National Rally victory in Marseille would represent a symbolic and strategic triumph that could reshape the party's image and electoral prospects.

Polling data from *Le London* revealed a significant shift in voter priorities that helps explain the National Rally's momentum. While cost of living remains the top concern—unchanged from previous municipal elections—and healthcare holds steady in second place, the most notable change involves immigration. Immigration has now risen to third place, surpassing what had traditionally held that position: the environment. This reordering of priorities signals a rightward shift in what French voters care about most, and it presents challenges for parties across the political spectrum.

For the left wing, the elections represent a particularly complex test. Carrian noted that "the left wing is no longer united as they were in the 2024 elections," when coalition politics had managed to hold disparate factions together. The France Insoumise (France Unbowed) party, led by Jean-Luc Mélenchon, is calling for an "anti-fascist front" of left-wing groups to cooperate in second-round voting. However, they have stopped short of saying they will "endorse or cooperate on policy" with the Socialists and Greens, parties they are "pitted against in many of these races."

The fundamental question, as Carrian posed it, is whether the left can overcome its internal divisions: "as the France Insoumise increasingly becomes a pariah to other left-wing parties, will these parties succeed without them, or will the different forces of the left defeat one another?"

---

International Women's Day, North Korean Style

The segment concluded on a notably different note, with Carrian marking International Women's Day, which had been celebrated over the weekend—including in one of the world's most repressive regimes. The *Times of London* documented a ceremony in Pyongyang where Kim Jong-un addressed North Korean women, and the content of his speech drew attention for all the wrong reasons.

Kim's remarks, as Carrian characterized them, seemed "somewhat backhanded" in their praise of North Korean women. The dictator stated that despite their "plain faces and wrinkles"—a remarkable thing to say to an audience—North Korean women were "obviously strong willed." He added that while they might be "physically weak," their willpower compensated. Kim also offered unsolicited commentary on their appearance, suggesting that their "plain faces assume courage" and "their wrinkles denote their strenuous exertion," while ultimately concluding that North Korean women's beauty "does not fade."

The speech, laden with qualifications that undercut virtually every compliment, served as a stark reminder of the status of women in North Korea—even as the regime publicly celebrated International Women's Day. Carrian concluded with a wry acknowledgment, wishing viewers and her co-host a "belated international women's day" after this particular dispatch from Pyongyang.

Detailed Takeaways

The True Cost of Modern Warfare Exceeds Initial Estimates: The $5.6 billion figure for munitions alone in just the first two days of strikes demonstrates that public discussions of military intervention routinely underestimate costs by orders of magnitude. When factoring in long-term commitments, veteran care, and infrastructure, the actual price tag for American military operations will be vastly higher. This matters because it shapes debates about whether military action is justifiable on economic grounds.

War Profiteering Remains Systemic in American Defense Economics: The Trump sons' venture into drone manufacturing is not an aberration but rather part of a long tradition of politically connected individuals and families positioning to profit from defense spending. Understanding this dynamic requires looking beyond the stated political positions of public figures to examine their private economic interests—a practice that the existing press review highlighted through investigative reporting.

Cryptocurrency Prediction Markets Create Regulatory Blind Spots: The $1.2 million in winnings by anonymous gamblers raises profound questions about market integrity and information security. When classified military information can be worth millions in prediction markets that operate outside traditional financial regulations, the entire framework for preventing insider trading requires reexamination. This represents a significant challenge for regulators in the coming years.

European Populism Continues to Reshape Voter Priorities: The shift in French voter concerns, with immigration now ranking third ahead of environmental concerns, reflects a broader transformation in European politics. Parties across the spectrum must reckon with this shift, and the municipal elections will test whether the National Rally can translate polling momentum into actual electoral victories.

Authoritarian Regimes and Gender Equality Remain Incompatible: Kim Jong-un's speech, even as it purported to celebrate women, served as a reminder that autocratic regimes instrumentalize gender in ways that reveal deeper contempt for women's autonomy and dignity. The disconnect between the regime's celebration of International Women's Day and its actual treatment of women encapsulates the performative nature of authoritarian gender politics.
