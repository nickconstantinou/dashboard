FPL Gameweek 32 Round-up: The Southampton Earthquake and Strategic Recalculation

The FA Cup gods have spoken, and they delivered what might be the single most consequential result of the entire FPL season. As the Fantasy Premier League world returned from its Easter slumber, managers were greeted with a fixture landscape that had been fundamentally rewritten by Southampton's shock quarter-final victory over Arsenal—a result that simultaneously eliminated one of the most anticipated double gameweeks while opening new doors for those quick enough to pivot. Welcome to what promises to be the most strategically complex finish to an FPL season in recent memory.

The Earthquake: How Southampton Reshaped Everything

The magnitude of Southampton's triumph over Arsenal in the FA Cup quarter-final cannot be overstated for FPL purposes. As the Planet FPL hosts David and James explained on their Tuesday podcast, recorded just hours after the result became final, "Arsenal picked quite an offensive lineup" featuring Martin Ødegaard and Ethan Nwaneri as number eights, which left them "very very vulnerable off the ball." The result wasn't a fluke—Southampton earned their victory through direct, effective football that exposed the space behind Arsenal's midfield, and the Gunners' exit from the FA Cup means their double gameweek is now off the table entirely.

This single result cascades through every manager's strategy. Arsenal had been slated for a double gameweek with matches against Everton and Brentford in what would have been gameweek 33, with those fixtures rearranged to the midweek slots following the FA Cup semi-finals. Instead, those players will now blank in gameweek 34, forcing a complete recalculation for anyone who had loaded up on Gabriel, William Saliba, David Raya, or Bukayo Saka. The Planet FPL hosts placed the probability of the rearrangement proceeding as expected at "97-98%," meaning the six teams confirmed to double in gameweek 33 are now locked in: Bournemouth, Brighton, Burnley, Chelsea, Leeds, and Manchester City—all of whom will subsequently blank in gameweek 34.

Leeds United emerge as unexpected beneficiaries of this chaos. The Yorkshire side overcame West Ham in their quarter-final on penalties, a result that means "Leeds eventually winning on penalties... they are the team that will have the double gameweek in 33 rather than West Ham," as James confirmed. For managers seeking Leeds exposure, this is a significant development—Joe Rodon at £3.9m with form of 7.0 becomes particularly interesting despite his ankle injury carrying a 75% chance of playing. Pascal Struijk at £4.3m with 6.5 form offers another budget route into a suddenly attractive fixture cluster.

Manchester City's situation deserves special attention. The hosts noted that Pep Guardiola's side "will likely rotate heavily in the league fixtures" after their FA Cup semi-final draw against Southampton, meaning their double gameweek becomes more, not less, attractive for FPL purposes. With players like Erling Haaland at £8.8m and Kevin De Bruyne potentially available at reduced ownership, City assets in gameweek 33 could provide significant captains' upside against teams that will field weakened sides.

The Current Fixture Landscape

The confirmed double gameweek in gameweek 33 features six teams playing twice, with all six subsequently blanking in gameweek 34. This creates the classic "swing" scenario where managers must choose their exposure carefully: loading up on Doubles for gameweek 33 means accepting a blank in 34, while targeting the blanks for differentiation means missing the Doubles.

Gameweek 32 presents a more traditional single-gameweek landscape with twenty confirmed fixtures across all clubs, though the exact positioning of that final West Ham fixture could yet create another double in gameweek 36. The Play Those Chips hosts Tom and Sam suggested this was "very likely to go into Gameweek 36," providing some clarity for long-term chip strategy even if the exact placement remains fluid.

The confirmed blanks for gameweek 31—Arsenal, Crystal Palace, Manchester City, and Wolves—have now passed into history, but the memory of those should inform current transfer decisions. Managers who held onto City assets through that blank are now positioned to benefit from their double, while those who sold in a panic may be regretting their haste.

Captain Recommendations: The Gameweek 32 Calculus

The captaincy conversation for gameweek 32 centers on identifying players with the best combination of fixtures, form, and ownership differentiation. Bruno Fernandes remains the standout option at £10.3m with his extraordinary form of 11.5—the highest in the entire game—and 189 season points demonstrate his consistency. At 44.8% owned, he's anything but differential, but his ceiling justifies the captain's armband regardless.

The Planet FPL hosts highlighted Anthony Gordon (£7.4m, form 10.5, 98 points, 7.6% owned) as a premium differential who offers both security and explosive potential. Newcastle's fixture run includes games that make them attractive, and at only 7.6% ownership, Gordon provides meaningful rank differentiation if he delivers a big score.

William Saliba (£6.1m, form 11.0, 107 points, 13.3% owned) represents the premium defensive option, though Arsenal's unexpected FA Cup exit creates uncertainty. The Play Those Chips hosts were notably frustrated with Eddie Howe's decisions regarding rotating Newcastle players during their double, with Tom admitting "I just hate Eddie [Howe]'s guts right now" after Malik Chow was benched despite playing 44 consecutive games.

For those seeking one-week punts, the FPL Harry analysis identified specific targets based on fixture difficulty. West Ham at home represents favorable home fixture for their attackers, making Jarrod Bowen (£7.0m) a viable option for those without him permanently, though he requires a transfer this week to activate. Harry's analysis showed "West Ham at the top" of fixture difficulty rankings, making Jared burn (£5.3m) a good potential buy for those targeting value.

Transfer Strategy: Immediate Moves and Long-term Positioning

The transfer planning discussion across the podcasts reveals a clear split between managers favoring immediate action versus those playing the long game. FPL Harry laid out both strategies with characteristic precision, walking through the trade-offs between wildcarding in gameweek 32 versus gameweek 35.

The wildcard in gameweek 32 strategy assumes you'll bench boost in gameweek 33 and free hit in gameweek 34. Harry was candid about the limited benefit: "You're not getting that much benefit. Maybe you have an extra Leeds player. Maybe you can change something if someone was to get injured. But if things look as they do now, you're basically going to be freehitting the same starting 11 that people who wildcarded in 32 have in 33, but just not play the bench boost on top." This strategy makes sense primarily for managers whose squads are fundamentally misaligned with the immediate fixture run.

The wildcard in gameweek 35 strategy appeals to those who want maximum flexibility to target the season's final doubles. Under this approach, you'd free hit in gameweek 33, wildcard in gameweek 35, and bench boost in either gameweek 36 or 37. Harry demonstrated what a gameweek 35 wildcard draft might look like, targeting a triple up on Arsenal defense with "Fulham at home, O'Reilly, Semeno," combined with "Crystal Palace with their plan double in gameweek 36." This approach requires tolerance for potentially suboptimal teams in gameweeks 32 and 34 but provides more information and flexibility when making the big restructure.

For immediate gameweek 32 transfers, Harry recommended Echikitique (£4.5m) to Jarrod Bowen (£7.0m), noting that "Echikitique's minutes either side of the PSG games or in between the PSG games either side I should say is a slight risk." The alternative target would be Jared burn (£5.3m), who has Wolves at home and Everton in gameweek 34—"the only two weeks worth considering if you're planning to wildcard in gameweek 35."

The Play Those Chips hosts revealed their own recent transfers. Tom took a -4 point hit to bring in Harry Wilson (£6.1m) and Morgan Gibbs-White (£7.4m), moves that brought him six points beyond what he would have otherwise achieved. While the transfer was mathematically positive, Tom acknowledged frustration with the decision, particularly after "James Hill scoring an own goal which took off minus one"—a cruel twist that cost him a greenshirt by just one point.

Players to Avoid: The Injury and Suspension Picture

Several players require careful evaluation before transfer decisions, with injury and suspension news creating significant risk for gameweek 32 activation.

Konstantinos Mavropanos (£4.4m, West Ham) carries a head injury with only a 75% chance of playing, making him a significant risk at a club with limited defensive premium options. Joe Rodon (£3.9m, Leeds) faces similar uncertainty with his ankle injury at 75% availability, though his favorable fixture run makes him more attractive than the risk might suggest.

Iliman Ndiaye (£6.2m, Everton) has a foot injury at 75% chance of playing, while Calvin Bassey (£4.4m, Fulham) carries a back injury at the same availability percentage. For those holding these players, the smart play is to monitor team news closely and prepare alternatives if necessary.

Sven Botman (£4.9m, Newcastle) faces the most significant risk at only 25% chance of playing due to a facial injury—a scenario that makes selling straightforward. The Dutch defender became unavailable after his injury during Newcastle's double, leaving managers who held him during that gameweek disappointed.

Lewis Dunk (£4.5m, Brighton) is suspended until April 26th, meaning he will blank in gameweek 32 and may not return until gameweek 33. His absence removes one of the more reliable budget options at the £4.5m price point, forcing managers to find alternatives.

The Chip Strategy Debate

The Chip strategy conversation across all creators reveals no single correct approach—the right play depends entirely on your current squad position and rank ambitions.

David from Planet FPL revealed he still has "all of them left... seven weeks left for chips to use," placing him in an extremely flexible position where he can essentially play the season out on his terms. This approach of holding all chips allows maximum reactivity to developing situations but requires patience and discipline to resist the urge to deploy them prematurely.

James from Planet FPL has already used several chips, including a bench boost that "a number of players who decided not to turn up" made disappointing. Despite this, he expressed contentment with his position: "I'm also somewhat pleased that I don't have to worry about it on this strategy line at the moment because it's influencing people perhaps in a positive way, but also perhaps in a negative way." His remaining chips are the Wildcard and Free Hit, giving him two powerful tools but considerably less flexibility than David.

The Planet FPL hosts repeatedly emphasized that "play it your way is very real"—there are numerous legitimate strategies, and no single approach is objectively correct. The key is understanding your own position: your rank, your bench strength, your risk tolerance, and the specific players you're trying to carry.

For the Bench Boost specifically, the optimal timing looks like gameweeks 36 or 37 rather than 33, when the concentration of doubles is higher. Harry noted that "I think it looks slightly better" for gameweek 37, citing "Darlow at home to Brighton" and the potential to transfer "Richards to Harry Maguire" as his one transfer. This allows time to assess the evolving fixture situation while preserving the chip for maximum impact.

Where Creators Disagree

The wildcard timing represents the most significant divergence between creators. FPL Harry laid out both strategies with mathematical precision, showing how each path affects your squad across multiple gameweeks, but clearly favored the wildcard 35 approach for managers who can tolerate short-term suboptimality. The Play Those Chips hosts, by contrast, revealed that Tom had "hit the button late yesterday" on his wildcard, suggesting earlier activation for their squads.

The Planet FPL hosts occupy middle ground, acknowledging that "multiple strategies are viable" while emphasizing the importance of waiting for official fixture confirmation before committing to chips like the Bench Boost. David placed his skepticism at "two or three percent" that one fixture might not follow the expected pattern, advising: "wait for the official confirmation" before making irreversible moves.

On captaincy, all creators broadly aligned around Bruno Fernandes as the default choice, though the differential conversation revealed preferences for Anthony Gordon, Harry Wilson, and various Leeds options depending on individual squad construction.

Three Key Actions for Gameweek 32

Action 1: Pivot from Arsenal assets strategically. The FA Cup result means Arsenal no longer offer the double gameweek that many managers had planned around. Rather than panic-selling Gabriel (£7.2m) or William Saliba (£6.1m), consider whether their form—just 11.0 for Saliba and 9.0 for Gabriel—warrants retaining through the blanks anyway. If you need to rebuild for the gameweek 33 doubles, use the wildcard 35 strategy to restructure around the confirmed doublers.

Action 2: Secure Leeds exposure before the double. Joe Rodon (£3.9m) and Pascal Struijk (£4.3m) represent the cheapest routes into a Leeds side that now has a confirmed double in gameweek 33. Despite Rodon's ankle injury concern, his form of 7.0 and extremely low price make him worth the risk for managers seeking value. Karl Darlow at £3.9m with form of 6.0 provides another budget option if Rodon fails to recover.

Action 3: Identify your chip timing now. If you're holding the wildcard and free hit with no urgent restructuring needed, the wildcard 35 approach offers more flexibility and information. If your squad is misaligned with the gameweek 32-34 fixture run, the wildcard 32 approach can provide immediate improvements, though the additional benefit over a free hit is marginal. Make your decision this week and commit to it—the worst strategy is changing course repeatedly.

The FPL season has reached its decisive phase, and the Southampton earthquake has ensured that traditional approaches may no longer apply. Adapt quickly, position for the confirmed doubles, and make your chip decisions with conviction. The final weeks will reward those who planned methodically and reacted decisively to unexpected developments—which, in FPL, is really just another day at the office.
