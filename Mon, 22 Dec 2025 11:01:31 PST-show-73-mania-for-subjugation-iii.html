# FPL Show 73: Mania for Subjugation III — A Comprehensive Analysis of Gameweek 34 and the Free Hit Strategy

The Final Push Begins

With five weeks remaining in the Fantasy Premier League season, the landscape has never been more volatile—or more exhilarating. FPL Harry returns with Episode 73 of his Mania for Subjugation series, diving deep into the critical blank gameweek that has sent the FPL community into collective strategizing mode. After deploying his Bench Boost chip in gameweek 33 to tremendous success, Harry now finds himself with just one remaining weapon in his arsenal: the Free Hit. This video captures his thought process as he constructs a draft designed to maximize returns across a gameweek where many teams have no fixtures, and those that do present a tangled web of opportunities and risks.

The episode arrives at a pivotal moment in the season. Harry has climbed from approximately 40,000 to just outside the top 20,000, sitting around 20,400 in rank after a spectacular 132-point gameweek 33. That score represented 18 points above his safety threshold—a green arrow that kept his title hopes alive. But with only Bruno Fernandes available from his squad heading into gameweek 34, he enters this blank gameweek essentially rebuilding his entire team from scratch. The question isn't just which players to pick; it's which players offer the highest ceiling within a compressed, unpredictable fixture list where the data tells one story and the form tells another.

The Art of Planning: How the Bench Boost Became a 132-Point Week

The foundation for this episode's strategy was laid weeks earlier. Harry had planned his wildcard for gameweek 32 specifically to enable the Bench Boost in gameweek 33—the season's biggest double gameweek. This level of forward-thinking is what separates casual FPL managers from those competing at the highest levels. "It was always part of the plan," Harry explains, describing how he sacrificed some points in gameweek 32 to optimize for the massive fixture congestion that followed.

The execution, however, wasn't without its frustrations. Harry's bench contributors delivered impressively: Van Dijk accumulated 12 points from the bench (an assist plus a clean sheet), Boothroyd contributed 8 points, and Bruno Fernandes added 6. In total, the bench provided approximately 26 points—a solid return for the chip. However, Jaoe Pedro's failure to play any minutes cost Harry significantly. The Portuguese midfielder sat on Harry's bench while Hinchcliffe, the first sub off the bench, entered the pitch. Had Pedro played, Harry's bench boost would have been worth a staggering 36 points. "That would have made my bench boost a 36 point bench boost, which is a crazy score for the chip," Harry reflects. Instead, what should have been a transformative week became merely a very good one—though Harry remains satisfied with the overall planning.

The Haaland captaincy proved shrewd, with the Norwegian striker scoring in both matches including a penalty in the first leg. Hinchcliffe outperformed expectations compared to the average, though Harry acknowledges he should have selected Pascal Gross instead. The Bournemouth defenders disappointed somewhat, particularly James Hill, who scored an own goal against Leeds, while Sessegnon managed only eight points despite creating several opportunities, including a near-assist that would have added three more points and maximum bonus. The Gabriel transfer—selling him for Jaoe Pedro, then Jacob Beal of Leeds, then finally O'Hara—represented a chain of unfortunate substitutions, though O'Hara's 13-point return provided some salvation.

Reading the Data: What the Numbers Actually Tell Us

Harry turns to the projections from Rob Tfpl, whose statistical analysis provides an objective framework for evaluating gameweek 34. The data begins with expected goals, where Liverpool lead at 2.17 projected goals in their home fixture against Crystal Palace. Yet Harry immediately flags a concern: this figure may underrate Palace's defensive capability. "They've only conceded one goal in total over the past three matches," he notes, raising questions about whether Liverpool's attacking prowess will translate as expected.

Crystal Palace presents a fascinating tactical puzzle. Having played on Monday night, the Eagles face a quick turnaround before Thursday's Europa Conference League semi-final. This congestion might suggest squad rotation, but Harry argues the opposite—that Palace could field their strongest lineup against Liverpool precisely because European ambitions will keep key players fresh for the midweek fixture. "We might get a pretty full strength lineup for Crystal Palace," he suggests, noting that their focus isn't on Premier League survival but on European progression.

Arsenal's matchup against Newcastle generates the next tier of projections. Harry observes that Newcastle "feel like a team to target at the moment" but qualifies this by noting Eddie Howe has historically set up effectively to counter Arsenal's style of play. The difficulty lies in pinpointing exactly where Arsenal's attacking returns will originate. Saka's fitness remains uncertain, while players like Joarez, Eze, Havertz, Mudryk, or even the defensive Declan Rice all present different risk profiles. "Picking a good Arsenal attacker feels quite difficult to predict where the attacking returns are going to be," Harry admits.

Manchester United at home to Brentford shows moderate promise, though both Martinez, Kounde, and Bruno face minor injury doubts. Harry expects all three to start, but Brentford's recent improvement—including a clean sheet against Fulham in gameweek 33—complicates the equation. Below these tiers, the data shows a significant drop-off: Fulham, Aston Villa, and West Ham each project 1.44 expected goals, while Everton, Brentford, and Sunderland sit just above one goal—remarkably low figures when constructing a free hit draft.

The Spurs-Wolves Anomaly: Opportunity in Contradiction

Perhaps the most compelling discussion centers on Tottenham Hotspur's fixture away to Wolves. Harry positions Spurs as "the outlier of a team that we don't really talk about and don't really invest because of the way they're playing, but have quite good projections because of the way that Wolves are playing." This contradiction has divided the FPL community into two camps.

The optimists point to Spurs fighting for European qualification and showing improvement against Brighton. The pessimists counter that Spurs haven't won in 2026 and look "horrible," making any investment unjustifiable. Harry occupies a middle ground, acknowledging he doesn't anticipate landing a Spurs player in his final draft but leaving the door open as the deadline approaches. "Do you want to punt on someone like a Javi Simmons or a Dominik Szoboszlai?" he asks, acknowledging the "huge disparity of opinion" the fixture generates.

The clean sheet odds reinforce this anomaly. Spurs sit at 34%—tied for third-highest in the gameweek—despite their inconsistent form. Wolves, meanwhile, present potential picks from the opposition side, though their own attacking output against Tottenham remains difficult to trust. This fixture serves as a microcosm of the broader challenge facing FPL managers this gameweek: the data suggests value exists in places where traditional analysis finds none.

Building the Draft: Strategy Under Maximum Constraint

Harry's free hit draft represents the culmination of aggressive team value management. With exactly 0.1 million in the bank and having made active transfers throughout the week to afford his current configuration, the squad leaves no margin for error. "I'm really pushing and stretching my team value as much as I possibly can," Harry explains, "and there are definitely ways when we talk through it that I might not need to."

The goalkeeper selection reveals the draft's underlying tension. David Raya occupies the starting spot despite Arsenal facing Newcastle. Harry appreciates the Gunners' strong clean sheet probability (37%—highest in the gameweek) and wants Arsenal double-ups, but he recognizes Raya's limitations. "He's not going to be the player who changes my free hit from being an okay or a bad free hit into an incredible free hit." A goalkeeper like Roles, Hermansen, or Wood at Liverpool could deliver a 10 or 15-pointer through spectacular saves; Raya, who rarely makes many stops even in matches Arsenal wins, offers only a 2 or 6-point ceiling. "If I was chasing rank more than I feel like I am at the moment," Harry admits, "I feel like I would punt on someone over David Raya who's maybe got a higher ceiling and just hope that Arsenal concede."

The bench keeper position falls to Kinsky of Wolves, providing cheap exposure to the Wolves-Spurs fixture that Harry continues to contemplate. The draft leaves several questions unresolved, with Harry acknowledging he'll continue evaluating options as the deadline approaches and news about player fitness emerges.

Five Weeks to Glory: What This Gameweek Means

The Spurs-Wolves fixture offers differential potential for managers willing to stomach volatility, while cheaper options throughout the draft require acceptance of lower ceilings.

Harry's philosophy prioritizes rank preservation over high-risk punts at this stage. For managers in similar rank positions, the lesson is clear: the season's final weeks reward solid fundamentals over gambling on form reversals.
