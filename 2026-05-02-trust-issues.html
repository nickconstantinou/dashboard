# Trust Issues

Key Insights

• Trust issues are defined as a persistent lack of confidence in others' reliability or honesty, often rooted in betrayal or trauma that has accumulated over time rather than occurring as a single event.

• The "Babylonian matrix" is described as a loosh farm system—a conceptual framework where controlling institutions (education, finance, government, healthcare, religion) allegedly work together to create widespread mistrust and Stockholm syndrome in populations.

• Unhealed, traumatized individuals are described as becoming susceptible to "energy harvesting, demonic possession, and soul ties"—a spiritual framework suggesting that unresolved trust wounds create vulnerability to negative external influences.

• The episode frames the fundamental choice facing wounded souls as a binary decision: either trust their intuition and perceive reality clearly, or "gaslight themselves" into doubting their own objective observations and gut instincts.

• The proposed solution involves "returning to self, practicing self-mastery and emotional intelligence, and remaining in alignment with Universal Laws"—a path described as returning to one's "divine" nature and walking toward one's "north node."

Introduction

Episode 370 of what appears to be an esoteric or spiritual podcast tackles the pervasive and often crippling phenomenon of trust issues—not as a surface-level self-help topic, but as a deep wound that connects individual psychology to broader systemic forces and cosmic energetic frameworks.

The episode operates from the premise that trust issues are far more than personal hangups or relationship problems to be solved with better communication strategies. Instead, the host argues that what millions of people experience as individual trauma is actually the symptom of something far larger: a deliberate system designed to create distrust, division, and disconnection. By weaving together psychological concepts like betrayal trauma and gaslighting with spiritual terminology such as soul-level wounding, demonic possession, and the "Babylonian matrix," the episode constructs a comprehensive worldview in which trust issues become both a personal affliction and a spiritual battleground.

What makes this episode particularly compelling is its insistence that the solution to trust issues cannot be found in external relationships or circumstances. Rather, the host asserts that healing requires a radical return to self-trust, intuition, and alignment with what they describe as Universal Laws. This perspective offers readers an empowering—though potentially challenging—reframe: rather than victims of their circumstances or past traumas, individuals are positioned as souls who have simply become disconnected from their own divine wisdom and must find their way back.

Key Concepts and Arguments

The Nature of Trust Issues

The episode opens by establishing what trust issues actually are: a persistent lack of confidence in others' reliability, honesty, and goodwill. The host is careful to distinguish this from ordinary caution or healthy skepticism, characterizing trust issues as something that "significantly impacts relationships by causing suspicion, jealousy, and difficulty in forming close connections." This framing suggests that trust issues are not merely inconvenient but actively destructive to human flourishing and relational satisfaction.

Importantly, the episode identifies multiple sources of trust issues rather than reducing them to a single cause. Adverse childhood experiences—trauma, neglect, or chaos in formative years—create the foundational wounds. Adult relationships can compound these wounds through infidelity, where intimate betrayal shatters previously established trust. Perhaps most insidiously, gaslighting—a specific form of psychological manipulation where victims are made to doubt their own reality—directly attacks the faculty of trusting oneself, which the host considers the most critical form of trust. Additionally, narcissistic abuse from loved ones creates a particularly painful form of trust injury, since it occurs within bonds that should theoretically provide the most security.

The Babylon Matrix and Systemic Control

The episode then pivots toward a more controversial thesis: that trust issues do not originate solely from individual traumatic experiences but are actively cultivated by systemic forces operating through what the host calls the "Babylonian matrix" and "loosh farm system."

The host describes a world that "celebrates evil, manipulation, gossip, thievery, deception, lies, illusions, gaslighting, slander"—not as unfortunate byproducts of human nature but as features of a deliberate design. In this framework, the Babylonian matrix represents a controlling system embedded within major societal institutions: education, finance, government, healthcare, and religion. Each of these institutions, the host argues, participates in creating a "breeding ground of mistrust" through their operations and structures.

The term "loosh farm" appears to reference a concept from various esoteric traditions, suggesting that negative emotions—fear, distrust, division—are harvested as a form of energy or "loosh" by malevolent forces or systems. When combined with the concept of Stockholm syndrome (where victims develop sympathy or loyalty toward their captors), the host paints a picture of humanity systematically conditioned to distrust not only others but themselves.

This section of the episode operates from a conspiratorial worldview that will resonate with some listeners and challenge others. However, the core insight—that systemic factors contribute to individual psychological wounds—has parallels in mainstream psychology and sociology, even if the specific terminology differs dramatically from conventional discourse.

Soul-Level Transmission and Spiritual Vulnerability

One of the episode's more metaphysical claims involves what the host describes as the "soul-level transmission" of mistrust. Rather than trust issues remaining confined to the individual who experienced the original trauma, the host suggests that "mistrust is transferred from soul to soul," creating an intergenerational or energetic inheritance of unhealed wounds.

The implications of this framework are significant. When individuals remain "unhealed, traumatized, and with no outlet to remove the misery from their lives," they become susceptible to what the host describes as "energy harvesting, demonic possession, and soul ties." This represents a dark turn in the trajectory of a wounded soul: from victim of trauma to potential victim of spiritual exploitation.

The host elaborates that a soul with divine potential—capable of "rising into its divinity" and walking on "its narrow path which leads to its north node"—can be "rerouted onto a path of animosity, hatred, envy, jealousy, self-loathing, and self-sabotage" when these vulnerabilities are exploited. The north node reference connects to astrological concepts about soul's evolutionary direction, suggesting that unresolved trust issues can derail an individual's spiritual trajectory and keep them locked in cycles of suffering.

This section will likely feel either deeply resonant or unconvincing depending on the listener's existing metaphysical framework. For those already engaged with esoteric spirituality, the concepts of soul ties and demonic possession may be familiar territory. For others, these ideas may represent a leap too far from observable reality. Regardless, the underlying message—that unhealed trauma has cascading consequences beyond the immediate wound—aligns with mainstream therapeutic understanding.

The Intuition Versus Self-Gaslighting Crisis

Perhaps the episode's most psychologically sophisticated section involves what the host describes as the forced decision that wounded souls eventually face: "Once a soul learns these harsh truths about the world, they will be forced to make a decision on whether or not they want to trust their intuition and see things for what they are, or they're going to gaslight themselves into believing that their objective reality mustn't be real or true."

The host describes this as creating a "schism" between conscious and subconscious mind. The individual finds themselves "battling their own gut instincts and intuition" and ultimately choosing "not trusting themselves." In this framework, self-doubt is not merely an uncomfortable feeling but a profound spiritual disorientation—the soul abandoning its own wisdom in favor of externally imposed narratives.

The host characterizes this internal conflict as the ultimate victory of the controlling system: "This is what the system has always wanted: for us to deny ourselves, not trust ourselves, and therefore project these feelings, energies, aspects, etc., onto the outside world." This places external projection—the tendency to see our internal states reflected in others—firmly within the domain of manufactured distrust. When we cannot trust ourselves, we cannot trust others, and when we cannot trust others, we cannot form the connections necessary for authentic human flourishing.

The Return to Self as Healing Path

Having established trust issues as both personal wounds and systemic/spiritual afflictions, the episode arrives at its proposed solution: "We die for lack of knowledge." This phrase, appearing to reference a biblical proverb, introduces the healing path as one of recovering lost knowledge about one's true nature.

The solution involves "returning to self, practicing self-mastery and emotional intelligence, and remaining in alignment with Universal Laws." The host does not provide specific techniques or exercises but rather points toward a philosophy of self-reliance, internal cultivation, and energetic alignment. Emotional intelligence represents the capacity to understand and regulate one's own emotional responses; self-mastery suggests the discipline to act from wisdom rather than wound; Universal Law alignment implies living according to principles that transcend cultural conditioning and system manipulation.

This healing path, described as the road to one's "north node" and "divine" nature, offers both empowerment and responsibility. The individual is not a passive victim of either trauma or systemic manipulation but an agent capable of reclaiming their own sovereignty, trusting their own perceptions, and navigating reality from a place of aligned wisdom rather than defensive fear.

Detailed Takeaways

Trust Issues Have Multiple Origins, Not Just One

Understanding that trust issues can stem from adverse childhood experiences, infidelity in adult relationships, gaslighting, and narcissistic abuse is crucial for healing. Many people spend years assuming their trust issues stem from a single traumatic event when they actually accumulated through multiple experiences across different relationship contexts. Recognizing the full landscape of potential causes allows for more comprehensive healing work rather than focusing on one wound while others remain unaddressed.

External Solutions Cannot Fix Internal Wounds

The episode strongly implies that finding the "right" partner, the "right" job, or the "right" circumstances will not resolve trust issues because the problem originates within. Until individuals learn to trust themselves—their instincts, perceptions, and intuitive judgments—they will continue projecting distrust onto external relationships regardless of how trustworthy those relationships actually are. This insight challenges the common approach of "finding someone safe" and instead points toward internal work.

Systemic Awareness Can Be Both Helpful and Harmful

The episode's framing of the "Babylonian matrix" offers a macro-level explanation for why so many people struggle with trust issues. This framework can provide comfort—it's not just "my fault" but part of a larger design. However, this same framework can also become disempowering if it leads to paranoia, conspiratorial thinking, or the belief that the system is too powerful to overcome. The distinction between awareness and obsession becomes crucial when engaging with systemic critiques.

Intuition Is the Antidote to Manipulation

When the host emphasizes trusting one's gut instincts and intuition, they're pointing toward an internal compass that cannot be gaslit or manipulated by external forces. Developing this trust in one's own perceptions becomes a form of spiritual self-defense—a way to resist the "schism" between conscious and subconscious that distrust creates. However, this also requires discernment: not every emotional impulse qualifies as intuition, and developing the wisdom to distinguish between genuine inner knowing and reactive fear is itself a skill requiring cultivation.

Healing Requires Active Practice, Not Passive Wishing

The path outlined—returning to self, practicing self-mastery, developing emotional intelligence, aligning with Universal Laws—implies ongoing practice rather than a single breakthrough moment. For many listeners, this will require significant lifestyle changes: meditation or contemplation practices, therapeutic support for trauma, conscious attention to emotional triggers, and ongoing examination of how distrust manifests in daily choices. The episode suggests that healing is possible but demands commitment to self-overcoming.

Who This Is For

This episode will resonate most strongly with listeners already engaged with spiritual or esoteric frameworks—those familiar with concepts like soul contracts, demonic possession, energetic vulnerability, and astrological "north nodes." It bridges psychological language (trust issues, trauma, gaslighting) with metaphysical language (loosh farming, soul ties, Babylonian matrix), making it most accessible to audiences comfortable operating in that hybrid space.

Individuals who have experienced repeated betrayals or long-term narcissistic relationships will find the episode's emphasis on self-trust particularly relevant. Anyone who has felt themselves caught in cycles of mistrust—wanting to trust but feeling unable to—may discover both validation and potential paths forward in the host's emphasis on returning to self as the primary healing mechanism.

Those who prefer strictly evidence-based psychological frameworks may find the spiritual elements challenging, though the core insight about self-trust as foundational to healing remains accessible even without the metaphysical framing. Similarly, listeners deeply embedded in conventional worldview may find the conspiratorial elements concerning, though the underlying message about systemic factors affecting individual psychology contains truth that transcends any particular ideological lens.

Ultimately, this episode offers its greatest value to those seeking a framework that validates both their personal wounds and their intuitive sense that something larger is wrong with the world—and who are willing to consider that healing must begin, and ultimately end, with the self.
