Reviewing Everything on my Desk! (2026)

Introduction: The Philosophy Behind Long-Term Gear Choices

In the world of tech content creation, where new products flood the market every few months, there's something almost radical about holding onto equipment for years—or even decades. In this expansive desk tour, creator KHC takes viewers through every piece of equipment on his workstation, offering candid reviews of items he's used extensively over many years. This isn't a typical unboxing or first impressions piece; instead, it's a thoughtful evaluation of tools that have proven their worth through daily, rigorous use.

The premise is straightforward yet valuable: when you've used something for years, you know its strengths and flaws in a way that short-term reviewers simply cannot capture. As KHC himself notes, "We all know that the tools are just tools. The sculpture is not great because of the chisel that the artist uses, but you better believe that if that artist uses the same chisel for 10 years straight, they're going to know it really well." This philosophy underpins the entire review, transforming what could be a simple product showcase into a masterclass in evaluating long-term investment pieces.

The timing of this review is particularly interesting. With the tech industry constantly pushing toward newer, faster, more expensive equipment, there's value in examining what actually matters in a professional setup—and what doesn't. KHC's workspace represents hundreds of thousands of dollars in equipment, yet his ratings and candid assessments reveal that price doesn't always correlate with satisfaction.

The Foundation: Desk and Chair

The Next Desk Air Pro: A Decade of Reliability

The desk itself is perhaps the most underappreciated piece of equipment in any setup, yet it's the surface upon which everything else rests—literally and figuratively. KHC's desk is the Next Desk Air Pro, now sold under the name Xesk. What makes this desk particularly remarkable is its construction: the surface is a massive 96 inches across by 38 inches deep—essentially an 8-foot-by-3-foot workspace—and it's made from a single piece of aluminum that's just a quarter inch thick.

The engineering feat here is that despite its thin profile and enormous size, the desk doesn't bend, rock, or shake. It's described as "rock solid," supporting all the weight of monitors, computers, and accessories without any flex. KHC acquired this desk nearly a decade ago, when the company was still called Next Desk rather than Xesk—a detail visible on the controller, which still bears the original branding as proof of its OG status.

The only real complaint is minor: unlike almost every other standing desk, this one requires holding the button continuously while raising or lowering the height, rather than simply pressing a button to memorize and move to preset positions. For KHC, this is a small inconvenience that doesn't diminish the overall excellence of the desk. He rates it a 9 out of 10 and suggests this might be his "forever desk."

The Herman Miller Embody: The Perfect Chair

If the desk is the foundation, the chair is the vessel that carries you through long work sessions. The Herman Miller Embody is described as "one of the best investments I've ever made." Having owned one during college and now owning another in his professional setup, KHC speaks from experience when he says the chair has molded around his back after years of use.

The Embody offers tremendous adjustability, though at this point, KHC has found his ideal configuration and doesn't need to adjust it anymore—the chair has essentially become custom-fitted to his body. He emphasizes that investing in comfort and back support pays dividends over the long haul, particularly for anyone spending hours at a desk daily. The chair receives a perfect 10 out of 10 rating, with KHC expressing confidence he'll use one "for the foreseeable future."

The Computing Core: Mac Pro and Storage

The M2 Ultra Mac Pro: An Aging Workhorse

The computer at the center of this setup is the M2 Ultra Tower Mac Pro, which KHC purchased approximately six years ago for around $13,000. At the time of purchase, this was Apple's most powerful computer, featuring 24 CPU cores, a 76-core GPU, 192 gigabytes of RAM, and 8 terabytes of storage. It's a configuration that remains impressive by most standards.

However, the passage of time has revealed some uncomfortable truths. Apple has since released the M3 generation of chips, including an M3 Ultra in the Mac Studio, and has moved into the M4 generation—yet the Mac Pro has not been updated for any of these newer chip generations. "I'm not giving up on it yet, but I'm pretty sure Apple already has," KHC admits, capturing the awkward position of owning a premium professional machine that Apple appears to have abandoned.

The primary reason KHC chose the Mac Pro was its expandability—he has a PCI card inside, specifically an OWC 64TB SSD that serves as a live backup drive for everything the studio creates. This kind of expansion is why the Mac Pro existed in the first place, built around the premise of professional customization.

He keeps the tower on the desk rather than underneath it, explaining that with a sit-stand desk, having the computer underneath would require cables to move up and down during height adjustments. Keeping it on the desk allows cables to remain hidden and static.

The rating is surprisingly modest: 4 out of 10. KHC anticipates replacing it "pretty soon," hoping for an M5 Ultra Mac Studio that he could swap out, potentially paired with a Thunderbolt OWC SSD for backup instead of the current internal PCI solution. For now, it continues to function adequately.

The Display Setup: Pro Display XDR

Two Pro Display XDRs: Professional Grade, Professional Price

KHC's monitor setup consists of two Apple Pro Display XDR monitors—one of the most specialized and expensive consumer displays ever made. These are 32-inch, 60Hz LED-backlit LCD panels with a 16:9 aspect ratio, featuring no built-in webcam or speakers. The price is staggering: $5,000 for the display itself, $1,000 for the stand, and another $1,000 for the nano-texture coating that reduces glare—so each monitor costs approximately $7,000.

For context, KHC notes that "for that price, you could get an entire setup that looks actually a lot like this," referring to a new studio video he made comparing alternatives. The Pro Display XDR is, by his own admission, "a ridiculously niche product."

In his brightly lit studio with an entire wall of windows, the nano-texture coating proves "extremely effective" at managing reflections, but it comes with a significant drawback: it's nearly impossible to clean. Once touched or marked, smudges and marks become permanent fixtures. This forces a careful maintenance routine that most users wouldn't tolerate in a regular office environment.

For professional color work, the XDRs are invaluable—the entire studio team uses them, ensuring everyone is matching and comparing reference color on identical displays. However, for daily non-video-editing tasks, KHC finds himself wishing for a higher refresh rate and additional features that the display doesn't offer. He rates them 8 out of 10, acknowledging both their excellence in their intended use case and their limitations as general-purpose displays.

The webcam is an Opal C1, plugged into one of the monitor's ports on the back—a clean solution that keeps the desk uncluttered. However, since KHC invested in the company, he declines to review it, noting he'd obviously think it's great.

Storage, Audio, and Accessories

The Groveade Shelf: Premium Storage

Above the main desk area sits a Groveade shelf that provides extra storage for smaller items: cables, SIM card removal tools, pens, flash drives, and a Sharpie. It's a simple solution for keeping miscellaneous items organized and within reach.

KHC acknowledges these are pricey but justifies the cost by noting they're designed to support significant weight—potentially dual monitors, though his setup doesn't use them that way. He admits you could "easily replicate this exact same effect with something way cheaper," but appreciates that the shelf does exactly what it's supposed to do and looks good doing it. The rating is a perfect 10 out of 10 for aesthetic and functional satisfaction.

The Audio Interface: A Buggy Necessity

The audio setup includes an external digital-to-analog converter (DAC) connected via Thunderbolt. This serves as both a DAC and what KHC describes as "a giant glorified volume knob"—a satisfying tactile control for adjusting audio levels. The interface connects to both the speakers and headphones.

The device includes various software features and plugins, though KHC admits he uses "almost none of them." More problematically, the software is "really buggy"—he frequently has to reset it, and updates sometimes cause it to "forget what it is," requiring new firmware downloads. This creates more hassle than such a device should, though it ultimately gets the job done. After many years of use, he's awarding it just 4 out of 10.

The Yamaha HS8 Speakers: Studio Reference Classics

The speakers are a pair of Yamaha HS8 active near-field studio monitors—devices so iconic they've become synonymous with professional desktop audio setups. These 8-inch speakers with 1-inch tweeters cover the full frequency range down to 40 Hz, eliminating the need for a subwoofer in most setups.

KHC loves these speakers for their versatility: they're equally capable for reference listening, video editing, audio editing, and casual music listening. They excel at delivering flat, accurate sound that professionals need for evaluating their own work.

However, there's one quirk: the shielding isn't perfect. When a phone sits next to them long enough, RF interference can cause a faint buzzing sound—and occasionally, right before receiving a call or text message, the speakers will buzz as they detect the incoming signal. It's a minor issue that KHC acknowledges with a chuckle, noting it's "crazy" but not a dealbreaker for an otherwise excellent speaker system.

Key Takeaways

Investment in Ergonomics Pays Dividends: The Herman Miller Embody receives a perfect 10/10 rating because comfort and back support matter more than any other factor in a workspace you'll use for years. The cost is justified by the daily benefit to health and productivity. Anyone building a long-term setup should prioritize the chair before considering any other upgrade.

Professional Tools Have Trade-offs, Not Flaws: The Pro Display XDR demonstrates that specialized tools excel in their intended use but may frustrate as daily drivers. The nano-texture coating is essential in bright environments but nearly impossible to clean. Understanding the specific use case prevents buyer's remorse.

Long-Term Support Matters More Than Initial Power: The Mac Pro illustrates how even a $13,000 machine becomes problematic when the manufacturer stops updating it. When investing in professional equipment, consider not just current specifications but the company's track record of long-term support.

Software Quality Directly Impacts Hardware Value: The audio interface receives a poor rating not because the hardware fails but because buggy software makes daily use frustrating. This serves as a reminder that modern devices are as much about their software ecosystem as their physical capabilities.

Speaker Placement Affects Functionality: The RF interference issue with the Yamaha HS8s highlights how environmental factors—like phone placement—can create unexpected problems. Planning workspace layout includes considering electronic interference beyond simple cable management.

Who This Is For

This desk tour and review will appeal most to creative professionals, video editors, and content creators who are building or upgrading their own workstations. The value here isn't in copying KHC's specific choices—few people need dual $7,000 displays or a $13,000 computer—but in understanding the evaluation framework he uses.

Anyone wondering whether premium equipment is worth the investment will find honest, long-term perspective that short-term reviews can't provide. The candid discussion of flaws in expensive equipment (the standing desk's height buttons, the audio interface's buggy software, the monitor's cleaning challenges) provides realistic expectations that marketing materials never offer.

Finally, those interested in the tech industry will appreciate the behind-the-scenes look at how professional content creation actually works—the Mac Pro's storage strategy, the color-accurate display requirements, and the audio monitoring setup reveal the infrastructure required for high-quality video production.
