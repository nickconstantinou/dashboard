The Greatest Video Games of All Time: Jeff Kaplan's Ultimate List

In the world of video game development, few names carry as much weight as Jeff Kaplan. As the former game director of World of Warcraft and Overwatch at Blizzard Entertainment, Kaplan has shaped the experiences of hundreds of millions of players worldwide. Now working independently on a new project, Kaplan recently sat down with podcast host Lex Fridman for a wide-ranging conversation that touched on his personal philosophy of game design, the titles that have defined his career, and what he considers the greatest achievements in interactive entertainment. The conversation offers a rare glimpse into the mind of one of the industry's most influential designers, revealing not just what makes games great, but why certain titles achieve the rarefied status of masterpiece.

The Crown Jewel: Why Breath of the Wild Reigns Supreme

When pressed to name the greatest video game ever made, Kaplan's answer comes without hesitation: The Legend of Zelda: Breath of the Wild. For Kaplan, this isn't merely a matter of personal preference—it represents what he considers an almost perfect synthesis of game design principles. Every element of the game, he argues, demonstrates a level of thoughtful consideration that most game studios can only dream of achieving.

What sets Breath of the Wild apart, according to Kaplan, is how seamlessly its components work together. The art direction doesn't simply look beautiful—it actively reinforces the gameplay mechanics and technological capabilities of the Nintendo Switch. The world itself feels like a toy, inviting experimentation in ways that other open-world games rarely achieve. You can chop down a tree and watch it float down a river. You can climb almost any surface. You can combine items and abilities in ways that the developers themselves may not have explicitly designed, yet somehow everything functions exactly as intuition would suggest it should.

Kaplan draws a striking comparison to Ocarina of Time, widely considered one of the greatest games ever made when it released in 1998. The remarkable thing, he notes, is that Nintendo managed to follow up that masterpiece with something even better—a feat he considers nearly impossible given the pressure of having already created multiple legendary entries in the series. Most game studios would be fortunate to get even one element of game design right: compelling combat, engaging narrative, satisfying itemization, or meaningful exploration. Breath of the Wild, he argues, gets all of them right simultaneously, and executes each better than anything that came before.

Nintendo: The Untouchable Mecca of Game Design

Throughout the conversation, Kaplan's reverence for Nintendo becomes increasingly apparent. He describes the company simply as "the mecca"—the ultimate reference point for what great game design looks like. This isn't blind fandom, but rather the acknowledgment of a consistent track record spanning decades. "They're the best," Kaplan states flatly. "That's all there is to it."

What fascinates Kaplan is how Nintendo continues to deliver exceptional experiences despite having been in the industry for so long. He admits he doesn't understand how the company operates internally—describing his understanding as "none at all"—yet he maintains an almost irrational trust in their output. His phrasing is telling: "I kind of rationally or irrationally just worship. It's just sort of if it's from Nintendo, it's going to be great."

This trust has been earned through repeated demonstration. Kaplan recounts his initial skepticism about various Nintendo design decisions, particularly controller innovations, only to be won over completely once he actually played the games. The reaction he describes is almost childlike in its wonder—he references a moment playing Breath of the Wild with his son, who also makes games, where the younger Kaplan expressed genuine sadness at finishing the experience, declaring that he'd never make anything as good. That a professional game developer would feel such profound inadequacy in comparison speaks volumes about the standard Nintendo has set.

Red Dead Redemption 2: The Shrine-Worthy Achievement

While Nintendo dominates Kaplan's conception of excellence, he reserves special praise for Rockstar's Red Dead Redemption 2. This is a game he explicitly states he "put[s] on a shrine"—not just for its quality as a player, but for what it represents as a game maker. The question that haunts him is both simple and impossible to answer: "How the hell do you make that?"

Kaplan admires RDR2 on multiple levels. The game's writing is exceptional, featuring character development and dialogue that he compares to Quentin Tarantino's work in terms of quality. He pushes back against the tendency among gamers and developers to praise story-driven games simply for having good writing, noting that "very few games have great story." Red Dead Redemption 2, in his view, is one of the rare exceptions that actually earns its narrative acclaim.

Perhaps more significantly, Kaplan marvels at the craft required to build such an ambitious project. He attributes the achievement specifically to Rockstar's decades of experience making open-world games, suggesting that no newcomer or less experienced studio could possibly compete with what Rockstar delivered. The game represents a pinnacle of the single-player narrative-driven experience—a category that Kaplan clearly respects deeply, even as his own work has primarily focused on multiplayer environments.

The Defining Games: EverQuest and Rust

Beyond the canonical masterpieces, Kaplan identifies two games that have been more personally formative than any others: EverQuest and Rust. These titles have shaped not just his gaming preferences but his entire approach to game design, and both continue to influence the project he's currently working on.

EverQuest, the landmark MMORPG that launched in 1999, represents one of Kaplan's most significant time investments—he's logged approximately 6,000 hours in the game. More than just a time sink, EverQuest fundamentally changed how Kaplan thought about persistent worlds and online communities. The game created experiences that couldn't exist anywhere else, forging connections between players that transcended the digital realm.

Rust, the brutal survival game developed by Facepunch Studios, occupies a similar position of importance despite being a very different experience. Kaplan has logged around 5,000 hours in Rust, and he speaks about it with the reverent intensity of someone who has been profoundly affected by a piece of art. He describes it as "a cult"—100%, he insists—and advises against anyone playing it unless they're truly called to experience it. "Rust will come calling to you if you are up to play it," he says cryptically.

The Genius of the Resetting World

What fascinates Kaplan most about Rust is its approach to world design: the world resets. This mechanic, which might seem counterintuitive to players accustomed to permanent progression, represents for Kaplan one of the most innovative ideas in survival game design. He wants to evolve and build upon this concept in his own upcoming project.

In Rust, the resetting world creates a peculiar dynamic. When a new cycle begins, everyone starts from the same position—naked, vulnerable, and equal. Kaplan describes the excitement this generates: players actively look forward to the reset because it means a new adventure begins. This stands in stark contrast to traditional MMOs, where veteran players often become effectively inaccessible to newcomers. In World of Warcraft, for instance, if Kaplan is level 80 and a friend is level one, there's no meaningful shared experience they can have together. In Rust, they simply wait for a reset and both spawn on the beach at minute one, ready to begin together.

The key insight Kaplan draws from this is understanding player psychology around resets. The challenge isn't preventing players from being upset that their progress disappears—it's figuring out why they'd actually be excited about the reset. The most dedicated Rust players, those with thousands of hours in the game, don't mourn the loss of their accumulated items and structures. Instead, they eagerly anticipate the fresh start, treating each reset as a new chapter in an ongoing story.

Kaplan's New Project: Evolution of the Concept

While not revealing specific details about his new game, Kaplan offers some hints about its design philosophy. The world will reset, probably on a monthly basis—fast enough that players won't become too emotionally attached to their accumulated possessions, but slow enough to make the experience rewarding. He's clearly thinking carefully about the psychological dynamics involved, acknowledging that players can get "very angry about resetting worlds" while believing that the right implementation can transform that anger into anticipation.

The game won't be purely PvP-focused like Rust, but it will include PvP elements. This suggests a hybrid approach, drawing on Kaplan's experience with both the massively multiplayer world of World of Warcraft and the more intense, survival-oriented dynamics of Rust. Given his demonstrated ability to understand player psychology—evidenced throughout his Blizzard career—it's worth taking seriously his assertion that the resetting world mechanic represents something genuinely innovative that he wants to explore further.

Legacy Games: Zork and the Origins of Interactive Fiction

The conversation also touches on Kaplan's formative gaming experiences, including classic titles like Zork and Ultima. Speaking about Zork, the seminal text adventure from the late 1970s, Kaplan becomes almost nostalgic. He recalls sitting with his mother and brother on an old IBM PC, desperately trying to figure out how to keep the lights on in the game's famous house—because if they failed, the grue would eat them. These early experiences with interactive fiction helped establish Kaplan's lifelong appreciation for games that create genuine emotional responses, whether fear, wonder, or satisfaction.

Key Takeaways

First, excellence in game design requires every element to work in harmony. Kaplan's praise of Breath of the Wild isn't about any single feature—it's about how the art, design, technology, and narrative all reinforce each other. This holistic approach to game creation represents the standard against which all other games should be measured.

Second, player psychology is the foundation of great game design. Whether discussing Rust's resetting world or his own upcoming project, Kaplan consistently returns to the question of how players actually feel about game mechanics. The best designers don't just implement features—they understand why those features create the experiences they do.

Third, certain games transcend entertainment to become meaningful life experiences. Kaplan describes finishing Breath of the Wild and Red Dead Redemption 2 with something approaching grief—feelings of loss and inadequacy that suggest these games touched something deeper than typical entertainment. Understanding this emotional dimension separates good games from great ones.

Fourth, there may be no substitute for accumulated expertise. Kaplan's amazement at Red Dead Redemption 2 stems partly from his recognition that only Rockstar, with decades of open-world development experience, could have created it. This suggests that ambitious game projects require not just talent but institutional knowledge that can only be built over time.

Finally, games that create community through shared experience endure. Both EverQuest and Rust, despite their age and intensity, remain influential because they create bonds between players that can't be replicated elsewhere. Kaplan's desire to build a game where friends can start together at any time reflects a deep understanding of what makes multiplayer experiences meaningful.

Who Should Listen to This Conversation

This conversation will resonate most strongly with game developers and designers seeking to understand what separates good games from great ones. Kaplan's perspective, shaped by decades of shipped titles at the highest level, offers practical insights into design philosophy that would be difficult to find elsewhere. Additionally, serious gamers who want to understand why certain titles achieve canonical status will find Kaplan's analysis illuminating. Finally, anyone interested in the psychology of player experience—what makes games feel meaningful, why players become emotionally invested, how game mechanics create emotional responses—will find this conversation packed with relevant observations. Kaplan speaks with the authority of someone who has not only studied these questions extensively but has also had to answer them in practice, shipping products that hundreds of millions of people have experienced.
