# Why VLC Never Sold Out, Even When It Almost Died: Jean-Baptiste Kempf on Sacrificing Millions for Open Source Integrity

## Key Insights

- **2005 was the crisis year**: VLC's VideoLAN project nearly collapsed, with only **two active developers** remaining before Kempf chose to dedicate himself fully to the project and rebuild from hundreds of thousands to **billions of users worldwide**.
- **Ethical rejection mechanism**: Kempf explicitly rejected bundling toolbars (spyware), changing default search engines, and inserting advertisements—not because he opposes monetization, but because "there is a right way of doing that" that doesn't compromise user trust or project integrity.
- **Netflix as the "what if" scenario**: Had Netflix approached VideoLAN to embed their service inside VLC, Kempf acknowledges "the story would have been different"—but the only companies that approached him were "shady ads companies" with extractive, data-stealing proposals.

- **"Selfish" self-interest in integrity**: Kempf describes his ethical stance as "very selfish"—not from idealism, but because he "needs to go to bed at night and be happy about what I've done," framing long-term reputation preservation as self-interest.

## Introduction

In an era when the internet was still learning to reckon with questions of privacy, trust, and the true cost of "free" software, one developer made a decision that would define not just his career, but the trajectory of one of the most widely-used pieces of software ever created. Jean-Baptiste Kempf, the founder of VideoLAN and the creator of VLC media player, sat down with Lex Fridman to recount the untold story of how the beloved media player survived, thrived, and refused to compromise—even when doing so meant turning away tens of millions of dollars.

The conversation reveals a rare window into the tension between open source idealism and the brutal economics of software development. VLC is now installed on what Kempf estimates are **billions of devices worldwide**, playing video in countries from Syria to rural India, on every platform imaginable. Yet this ubiquity almost never happened. In 2005, the project had collapsed to just two active developers. The technology that would eventually become the default answer to "how do I play this video file?" was on the verge of death.

Kempf's story is not simply a tale of technical perseverance. It is a meditation on what it means to build something lasting in an industry that constantly tempts creators with shortcuts—shortcuts that feel harmless in isolation but accumulate into betrayals of user trust. The offers came repeatedly: bundle our toolbar with your downloads (spyware in everything but name), change your users' default search engine, insert advertisements into the media player interface. Each time, Kempf said no. Not because he didn't need money—his startup background proves otherwise—but because he recognized that selling out the spirit of the project would kill the very thing that made it valuable.

This conversation matters now more than ever. As debates about data privacy, platform integrity, and the ethics of technology persist, the VLC story offers a counter-narrative to Silicon Valley's growth-at-all-costs mentality. It demonstrates that the long game—playing for decades of user trust rather than quarters of revenue—can actually produce more sustainable and ultimately more valuable outcomes.

## The Origins and Near-Death of VideoLAN

The VideoLAN project predates Kempf's involvement, emerging originally as a student project at a French university, which itself was a continuation of an earlier initiative called "network 2000." When Kempf arrived in 2003, he inherited not just code but a philosophy—a belief that video technology should be free, open, and accessible to anyone. His first major act was extracting the project from the university environment and formalizing it as a non-profit organization called VideoLAN, creating a sustainable structure that could outlast any individual academic institution or corporate sponsor.

What followed was a period of survival against overwhelming odds. By 2005, the project had reached a crisis point that nearly ended VLC before it could become the household name it is today. The ranks of active developers had dwindled to just two people. The technology worked—it was useful, innovative, and solved real problems for users dealing with the chaos of incompatible video formats—but the human infrastructure needed to maintain and grow it was collapsing. Many projects in this position would have simply faded into obscurity, their code archived on forgotten servers, their potential unrealized.

Kempf chose a different path. Rather than abandoning a project he believed had genuine value, he made it his life's work. This decision transformed him from a contributor into the project's primary steward, dedicating the time and energy necessary to carry it through the wilderness years. He understood that the technology itself was only part of what made VideoLAN valuable; the other part was the promise it represented—a promise that software could serve users without extracting tribute in the form of compromised privacy or hijacked browsing habits.

The growth from those desperate early days to the present was not merely organic. It required active cultivation of a global community of contributors, drawing in developers and enthusiasts from countries as diverse as Syria and India. These volunteers, working across time zones and cultural contexts, poured countless hours into improving the code, expanding format support, and keeping the project alive through periods when corporate interests were actively trying to acquire or co-opt it.

## The Temptations: Money, Spyware, and the Open Source Dilemma

The narrative of VLC's survival becomes particularly compelling when examining the specific offers Kempf turned down. Throughout the mid-to-late 2000s, as the project gained users and visibility, it attracted attention from actors with very different visions for its future. The dominant mode of monetization for free software at that time was bundled toolbars—those infamous strips of functionality that claimed to enhance your browsing experience while silently monitoring your activity and redirecting your searches to affiliated partners.

Kempf describes these offers with the weariness of someone who heard the same pitch dozens of times. Companies approached VideoLAN with proposals that would have inserted their toolbars into the VLC installation process, hidden in licensing agreements that users would scroll past without reading. The technical implementation was straightforward—bundle a checkbox, add an installation step, and watch the money flow in. What made these offers tempting was their profitability. Individual installations generated small but reliable revenue, and at VLC's scale, even fractions of a cent per installation would have accumulated into life-changing sums.

Yet Kempf recognized what many developers at the time did not: the offers weren't really about bundling software. They were about stealing data, redirecting user behavior, and ultimately destroying the trust that would take decades to rebuild. He drew a sharp distinction between earning money ethically and extracting it through deception. "There is a right way of doing that," he told Fridman, making clear that his objection was never to commerce itself but to the specific mechanism being proposed. His subsequent success founding multiple startups demonstrates that he understood how to build sustainable businesses—the difference was that those ventures operated transparently rather than through surreptitious data harvesting.

The hypothetical that haunted these negotiations, as Kempf frames it, was Netflix. What if the streaming giant had approached VideoLAN with an offer to embed their service directly into the media player? Would he have accepted then, as others might have? The answer is probably yes, because a legitimate partnership with a transparent business model would have aligned with his stated values. But Netflix never came calling. The only parties interested were advertising networks and toolbar bundlers—the shadiest actors in an ecosystem full of shadow practices.

## The Long Game: Why Integrity Was Also Good Business

What makes Kempf's account particularly valuable is his refusal to frame his decisions purely in idealistic terms. He is unapologetically pragmatic about money, openly discussing his startup ventures and his belief that wealth creation is a legitimate pursuit. This context makes his ethical stances more credible, not less—because he was not operating from a position of naivety about financial pressures. He understood exactly what he was turning down and did it anyway, which suggests something deeper than simple principle.

The business case for saying no was, in its own way, almost as compelling as the moral one. Kempf explained to Fridman that accepting the toolbar and advertising offers would have created a predictable three-year cycle: revenue would flow in, but user experience would deteriorate, trust would erode, and eventually the community would fork the project—that is, copy the code and create an alternative version without the compromises. Once that happened, the original project would be abandoned by the community that built it, leaving only the tainted, compromised version to struggle against forks that offered a cleaner experience.

This cycle plays out repeatedly in open source history. Projects that sell out to corporate interests often find themselves hollowed out from within, as contributors with better options leave and users migrate to purer alternatives. By refusing the tainted money, Kempf avoided triggering this exodus. The developers who stayed—the global network of volunteers in Syria, India, and everywhere else—did so because they trusted that the project would remain true to its mission. That trust, once broken, cannot be rebuilt on demand.

The VLC that exists today—ubiquitous, trusted, running on smartphones, computers, smart TVs, and embedded systems worldwide—stands as evidence that the long game works. Kempf estimates billions of active installations. The software has become a utility, as fundamental to computing as a text editor or web browser. This success would have been impossible if the project had been salted with spyware and advertisements, because the users who matter most—power users, IT departments, organizations that evaluate software for security and privacy compliance—would have migrated away at the first sign of compromise.

## Personal Philosophy: The Selfishness of Integrity

One of the most revealing moments in the conversation comes when Kempf reflects on his personal motivations. He explicitly calls his ethical stance "very selfish," not as a dismissal but as an honest accounting of his psychology. He needs to be able to look at himself, to go to sleep at night knowing that the work he has done represents genuine value rather than extracted exploitation. "I believe there is right and wrong," he told Fridman, and this belief is not abstract—it's load-bearing in his decision-making framework.

Kempf traces this orientation, tentatively, to his upbringing. He mentions his parents in a way that suggests they modeled ethical behavior even when it was costly, leaving an imprint that he cannot fully explain but recognizes as formative. This framing is important because it acknowledges that ethical decision-making is not purely rational—there is a visceral, emotional component to doing the right thing that operates below the level of explicit calculation. He wasn't running a cost-benefit analysis when he turned down the offers. He was protecting something ineffable that he couldn't bear to lose.

This self-interest in personal integrity served the project, but it also protected the broader ecosystem of contributors who trusted Kempf to steward their work. "If I had sold out," he said, "I would have betrayed so many other people's work." The thousands of volunteer hours that built VLC were not commodities to be traded for toolbar revenue; they were expressions of faith that the project would remain true to its purpose. Selling out would not have been merely a personal failure but a collective betrayal.

Fridman's response captures the significance of this moment: "It's inspiring for others... that it's okay to do these kinds of huge sacrifices if you believe it's right." The conversation becomes a meditation on what it means to build something lasting—not just a software project, but a reputation, a community, a legacy. The sacrifices were real. The tens of millions of dollars that were on the table would have transformed Kempf's life in concrete ways. But the decision to prioritize integrity over immediate gain created something that money could not have purchased: the unshakeable foundation of genuine trust.

## The Broader Significance for Open Source Culture

The VLC story has implications that extend far beyond one media player's development history. The early 2000s, when these decisions were being made, represented a transitional period in how the internet thought about software distribution. As Fridman notes, "it was really normal to download a program and it secretly install some spyware." The practice was so common that many users had simply accepted it as the price of free software. Installation wizards were designed to obscure the real terms of agreement, burying consent in text boxes that nobody read, relying on the assumption that users would click through without scrutiny.

In this environment, saying no required what Fridman calls "guts and vision." The individual developer or small team facing a take-it-or-leave-it offer had no institutional support, no legal recourse, and no guarantee that their integrity would be rewarded. The counterfactual was always personal financial ruin while the companies that made unethical deals continued to thrive. The fact that many developers succumbed to these pressures is understandable, if regrettable. The fact that some didn't—that they held the line even when it was costly and lonely—created the ethical reference points that the open source movement still points to today.

Kempf's refusal to compromise VLC helped establish what open source could mean beyond merely "free of licensing fees." It demonstrated that open source could also mean "free of ulterior motives"—that the code you downloaded would not betray you, would not harvest your data, would not manipulate your behavior for someone else's benefit. This distinction matters because the alternative was and remains ubiquitous. Most free software in the 2000s came with strings attached; VLC became notable for coming without them.

The global community that rallied around this principle—from individual contributors in countries facing their own political and economic challenges to the broad base of users who simply wanted software that respected them—suggests that there is real demand for ethical technology. Not demand that manifests in purchasing decisions or market movements, but demand that shows up in the willingness to contribute time, code, and advocacy to projects that align with one's values. VLC became a symbol precisely because it embodied the possibility of technology that serves rather than exploits.

## Conclusion

The story of VLC's refusal to sell out is, at its core, a story about the compounding value of integrity. Each ethical decision did not merely prevent a discrete harm; it built a reservoir of trust that enabled subsequent growth. The developers who stayed, the users who recommended the software to friends and colleagues, the organizations that standardized on it for enterprise deployment—all of them were responding to something that cannot be manufactured through marketing or purchased through advertising: the genuine sense that this tool was made for them, not at their expense.

Jean-Baptiste Kempf's account of these decisions does not present them as heroic sacrifices. He is matter-of-fact about the trade-offs, acknowledging the real costs while explaining why he found them acceptable. This restraint makes the story more compelling, not less. We are not asked to admire a saint but to learn from a pragmatist who discovered that doing the right thing was also, when viewed from sufficient distance, the smart thing.

In an industry that often treats user attention as a resource to be extracted and user data as a liability to be minimized, the VLC story remains a counterpoint worth remembering. It demonstrates that technology can be built differently—that software can respect the people who use it, that developers can say no to easy money, and that communities can sustain themselves across decades without compromising the values that gave them meaning in the first place. The millions of dollars that Jean-Baptiste Kempf left on the table became, in the long run, an investment in something more valuable than money: the trust of billions of users who never had to wonder whether their media player was working against them.
