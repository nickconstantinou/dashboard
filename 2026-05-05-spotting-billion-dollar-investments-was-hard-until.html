# Key Insights

  • The 1-in-10 Rule: One in 10 Americans influence the other nine, making influencer marketing fundamentally about finding and activating that minority rather than attempting mass reach—a principle Oza has applied from radio DJs in the 90s to YouTube creators like Alex Earls today.
  • Poppy's $2B Exit Origin Story: Oza acquired 25% of "Mother" on Shark Tank for approximately $250,000-$400,000 in annual revenue, despite terrible packaging, a non-trademarkable brand name, and sharks literally spitting out the product. After shutting down the original brand and rebranding as Poppy, the company sold for over $2 billion.
  • Strategic Silence as Competitive Advantage: Oza deliberately remained mute during the Shark Tank pitch even after tasting the product and recognizing its potential, specifically to avoid signaling excitement to other sharks and to avoid splitting the deal—ultimately securing 25% for himself alone.
  • Brand = Emotional Desire: Oza defines brand not as logos or taste, but as creating an emotional connection that makes consumers "reach for a Poppy," echoing Coca-Cola legend Robert Woodruff's vision of placing products "within arm's reach of desire."

    Spotting Billion Dollar Brands: The Rohan Oza Playbook

    Every investor claims they can spot winners. Very few have the track record to prove it. Rohan Oza isn't making that claim—he's simply built a portfolio that includes Vitamin Water, Smartwater, Vita Coco, Popchips, Vital Proteins, Farmer's Dog, and eventually, Poppy. Combined, these brands represent billions in enterprise value. On the "My First Million" podcast, Oza sat down with host Sam Parr to decode whether brand-building is "just art or is there some science behind it." His answer cuts through the mythology: "Nobody wins the lottery seven times. You got sharks spitting out the product. You've got a terrible brand name, and you pick up 25% of that company." There is a system, he argues—and it can be learned.

    ---

    Oza's first and most foundational principle is what he calls "Influence the Influencer." The premise is simple but the implications are profound: given any marketing budget—$100, a million, or $100 million—you cannot reach everyone. The goal isn't to shout at the masses but to find the one in 10 people who influence the other nine and get them to carry your message.

    When Oza first started in this industry, those influencers were radio DJs. He describes flying in the top two DJs from 25 major cities—50 people total—to a Vegas event that became so valuable that artists themselves would seek out his brand activations because they understood the reach concentrated in that room. With a single event, they could hit 20 cities in two hours. "It was almost like the cities came to you versus you traveling," Oza explains.

    The medium has changed; the principle hasn't. Today, those same cultural nodes are occupied by YouTube creators and social media influencers. Oza mentions figures like Alex Earls as contemporary equivalents to those radio DJs of decades past. The skill is recognizing who holds real influence versus who just has large follower counts—and understanding that influence often clusters in unexpected places.

    What separates Oza's approach is his operationalization of this insight. He didn't just identify influencers; he built relationships that felt authentic. At Sprite and later Vitamin Water, he hired team members who "lived the brand" rather than simply marketing it. "They didn't market the brand," he says. "They lived Weidemood. That was their vibe." This authenticity mattered because, as Oza notes, "If you're a dork, it's not gonna work" when trying to connect with someone who genuinely loves music, culture, or wellness.

    The Poppy Origin: From Shark Tank Disaster to $2 Billion Exit

    The most concrete illustration of Oza's philosophy in action is Poppy, and the story is more instructive for being messy.

    The founders, Steven and Allison, walked into Shark Tank with a product originally called "Mother"—a prebiotic drink built around apple cider vinegar. The brand name was untrademarkable ("everyone has a mother"), the packaging looked like sparkling wine for elderly women, and the product itself was, as Oza recalls, "horrible." Sharks literally spat it out on camera. Kevin O'Leary and the others passed.

    Oza, however, saw something different. Several things were happening simultaneously in his mind.

    First, he recognized that beverage was his "real house." While Cuban had a bigger name and Lori Greiner dominated QVC, "beverage, they look at me." He knew the category deeply and approached every pitch with that specific lens.

    Second, despite what the founders were pitching—a health-focused ACV drink—Oza was secretly searching for what he called "modern soda." Growing up in Zambia with only three beverage options (Coke, Sprite, Fanta), Oza had moved away from soda for health reasons, but he always kept an eye out for something he could feel good about. "In the back of my head, I've always been looking for soda I can feel good about."

    When he tasted the orange flavor, the realization hit. "This is Fanta meets Orangina. If you guys ever—and I'm like, this liquid is in my head takes me back. This is my modern soda."

    Third, he saw hustle. The founders were chiseled, good-looking, and Allison was visibly pregnant—"like eight months pregnant, she could deliver any second." Oza's immediate read: "They've got hustle."

    Crucially, Oza executed what he calls "strategic patience." Despite tasting something he recognized as special, "I don't get excited because I know it's the minute I get excited, these guys get excited." He let the other sharks pass. He let the bidding war happen with no bidders. He remained mute even when Cuban and Bethany Frankel turned to him and asked if they should split the deal. His response: "No, because if this works, which it did, why would I wanna split it? And if it doesn't work, I'm fine to lose it, but in the end, I'm betting on myself."

    The result: 25% of the company for what was effectively a $250,000-$400,000 investment when the company had barely any revenue.

    ---

    The deal closed, but the real work began immediately. Oza's first decision was brutal but necessary: shut down Mother. The founders had poured their hearts into the original brand, and telling them "we're now cofounding Poppy together" required, as Oza acknowledges, "a lot of faith on their part."

    The rebranding process revealed Oza's strategic creativity operating alongside what he calls "tactivity creatives"—team members who execute rather than just strategize. The process was a triangle offense: the founders brought the product formulation, Oza brought the strategic direction, and Stevie from his team brought the design and naming execution.

    Oza's strategic insight was crucial: he wasn't building a health drink. He was building the modern soda category. The ACV base and prebiotic benefits were secondary to the primary value proposition—which was a beverage that tasted like the sodas people craved but without the guilt.

    For naming, they brought in David Plastick, the branding expert who named BlackBerry and Swiffer. The eventual choice of "Poppy" was intentional on multiple levels: it phonetically evokes "pop" (soda in many regions, and especially in the UK), it's fun and bubbly-sounding, it's easy to say, and it's highly trademarkable. Oza admits he initially liked "Mom and Pop" as a nod to small businesses, but his team correctly shot that down.

    What Brand Actually Means

    Oza's definition cuts through the noise: "A brand is something that allows a consumer to have an emotional connection with a product." It's not about taste or packaging or even quality—those are table stakes. The brand is the emotional bridge that makes someone reach for your product without consciously deliberating.

    He illustrates with the Robert Woodruff quote, one of the godfathers of Coca-Cola's success: Woodruff wanted Coke "within arm's reach of desire." Oza breaks this down: "Brand is creating that desire. And then the distribution is putting it in arm's reach." You can have the best product with perfect distribution, but without brand, you're just another SKU on a shelf.

    This emotional connection is why people request Poppy specifically, why they post about it on social media, why it has a community rather than just customers. It's also why Oza's investment thesis focuses so heavily on founders who understand this emotional dimension, not just those with impressive formulations or supply chain logistics.

    ---

    One overlooked element in Oza's approach is what he calls the "ecosystem" he's built across his portfolio brands. At an LP conference, cofounder Gorez of Gymkhana Fine Foods pointed out that when you look at the full sheet of brands under his fund CarVoo, "it's insane, this ecosystem you've created." Oza admits he hadn't fully appreciated how his portfolio brands could cross-promote, share learnings, and create network effects.

    This ecosystem thinking—treating brand investments not as isolated bets but as interconnected assets with shared customers, distributors, and cultural cachet—is increasingly relevant as CPG markets consolidate and shelf space becomes more competitive.

    Detailed Takeaways

    The 1-in-10 Targeting Principle Is Non-Negotiable

    Oza's most transferable insight is his targeting philosophy. Rather than trying to reach everyone—which only works if you have Amazon's budget—you should identify the one in 10 people who influence your target market and focus all energy there. This applies whether you're spending $500 on micro-influencer partnerships or $5 million on a national campaign. The mechanism is the same: find where influence actually concentrates and activate those nodes.

    Strategic Patience Compounds Returns

    The Poppy deal happened because Oza waited. He had already tasted the product, already recognized its potential, and still said nothing until the other sharks passed. This patience served multiple functions: it avoided signaling interest (which might have attracted competition), it avoided inflating the valuation through visible enthusiasm, and it secured full ownership of his 25% rather than splitting with other investors. In high-stakes negotiations, especially for early-stage deals, visible excitement is expensive.

    Reframe, Don't Just Build

    Oza didn't save Mother; he destroyed it and built something entirely different. The founders had built a health drink. Oza saw a modern soda and made that his strategic frame. This reframing—seeing what the product could become rather than accepting what it was—is a critical startup and investing skill. It requires separating the product's actual attributes from the story it's currently telling and asking: if we told a different story, who would want this?

    Founder Chemistry Determines Exit Potential

    Oza's process involves identifying not just good products but founders he can work with long-term. When he and the Poppy founders went to dinner the day after the deal closed, Oza's first thought was, "Oh, God, they're horrible." But he immediately pivoted to working together closely—the four of them (Oza, Allison, Steven, and Stevie) essentially co-founded the new company. The ability to navigate that dynamic, to give founders "full credit" while redirecting their creation toward a new vision, is what separates billion-dollar outcomes from million-dollar ones.

    Brand Building Requires a Triangle Offense

    No single person builds a billion-dollar brand alone. Oza's framework identifies three essential roles: product creators (the founders who invented the formulation), strategic directors (those who see the market opportunity and positioning), and tactical creatives (those who execute naming, packaging, design). The misalignment happens when any single role overreaches into another's domain—founders who think they can name their own products, investors who think they can design packaging, or creatives who try to set strategy without market insight.

    ---

    This content is essential for anyone building or investing in consumer brands—from first-time founders pitching to angels, to operators at growth-stage CPG companies, to investors evaluating consumer goods deals. It's particularly valuable for those in the food, beverage, or wellness spaces, where brand differentiation is the primary competitive moat.

    The principles translate beyond CPG, however. The "influence the influencer" framework applies to any market where word-of-mouth and cultural cachet drive adoption. Software companies, professional services firms, and even B2B brands have their own "one in 10" dynamics—they just look different than soda purchases.

    What makes Oza's framework particularly valuable is its specificity. He doesn't offer vague inspiration about "building a great brand." He provides concrete mechanisms: the DJ event strategy, the triangle offense model, the strategic silence technique, the reframing playbook. Anyone can extract actionable frameworks from this conversation regardless of whether they've built a brand before.

    The final word belongs to Oza himself, who frames his success with characteristic humility: "There's always luck, guys. It's not like this. You can't—you gotta the dice has gotta roll your way a little bit. But if you have a game plan, which I've sort of honed over time, the odds flow in your favor." The lottery metaphor is apt, but as Oza proves, skill determines who keeps playing when luck doesn't break their way—and who spots the ticket when it finally lands.
