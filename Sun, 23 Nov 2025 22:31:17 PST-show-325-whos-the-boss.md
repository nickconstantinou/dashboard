<p># Common Sense Show 325: Who's the Boss?

## A Constitutional Crisis Hidden in Plain Sight

In this episode of *Common Sense*, Dan Carlin returns to the airwaves with a sense of urgency that belies his typical measured delivery. The veteran journalist and historian has been preoccupied lately with events unfolding 2,300 to 2,400 years ago—his ongoing series on the Peloponnesian War—but explains that something has dragged him back to the present. What he found, he says, was "wheat not chaff": a genuinely important issue obscured by the constant noise and distraction that characterizes modern American political discourse.

Carlin opens by citing a recent news item that would seem at first glance to belong to some distant country: former Bangladesh Prime Minister Sheikh Hasina was sentenced to death for her role in ordering the military to crack down on protesters. Over three weeks, the military killed 1,400 people. The lesson, Carlin suggests, is not just that such violence occurs elsewhere, but that it occurs *because* safeguards fail—and that America has built-in protections designed to make such scenarios "extremely unlikely" in the United States. When those protections are challenged, even slightly, it becomes reason enough for him to abandon his preferred historical studies and address the present moment.

The real substance of the episode centers on a recent controversy involving six Democratic lawmakers who, many of them with military or intelligence backgrounds, produced a video reminding active duty service members of their legal and ethical obligations. Specifically, the video emphasized that service members can refuse to follow illegal orders—and are expected to do so. If ordered to commit war crimes or shoot peaceful protesters, they not only have the right but a duty to say no. The reaction from President Trump was swift and fiery: he called the lawmakers' statements "really bad and dangerous to our country," labeled their words "seditious behavior from traitors," and demanded they be "arrested and put on trial." He later added posts suggesting "seditious behavior punishable by death" and endorsed a supporter's call to "hang them." While Trump eventually walked back the most extreme language, Carlin argues that the initial posts reveal something worth taking seriously.

## The Anatomy of Military Disobedience

Carlin devotes the bulk of the episode to explaining why the six Democratic lawmakers were doing nothing more than restating bedrock American military doctrine—and why the reaction to their video was so revealing.

The principle at stake is often called the "superior orders defense," and it has been systematically rejected in American military law for generations. The most famous establishment of this principle came after World War II, when Hitler's generals and cronies were hauled before the victorious Allied powers at Nuremberg and attempted to deflect responsibility by claiming they were "just following orders." The defense failed then, and it has failed repeatedly since. As Carlin notes, this principle was reinforced in court case after court case, drilled into U.S. service personnel since 1947, and actually extends back even further—to military justice cases from as early as the War of 1812.

The lawmakers' video, therefore, was not revolutionary or seditious. It was a reminder of what every soldier, sailor, Marine, and Airman already knows: they take an oath to the Constitution, they receive extensive education on the laws of war, and they are personally responsible for their actions even when acting under orders from superiors. If someone orders you to torture civilians, commit atrocities, or violate the laws of armed conflict, the order does not protect you from prosecution. This is not ambiguous or controversial within military law—it is foundational.

Carlin draws a direct parallel between this American tradition and what occurred in Bangladesh, where soldiers opened fire on their own people because they were told to. Those Bangladeshi soldiers, he notes, would have been in trouble for following such orders in the United States. "That is a feature not a bug," Carlin says. "That is our last line of defense in this country against somebody who might want to use our military against us."

## The Historical Precedent Nobody Can Ignore

To illustrate the real-world consequences of following illegal orders, Carlin invokes two case studies from American military history, both of which challenge the simplistic notion that soldiers should simply obey.

The first is the My Lai Massacre of 1968, in which U.S. Army soldiers under Lieutenant William Calley killed between 350 and 500 Vietnamese civilians—women, children, and elderly men—during the Vietnam War. The defense offered by Calley and others was that they were following orders from their superior, Calley's direct commander. But what makes My Lai particularly shocking, Carlin emphasizes, is the context: there was no combat threat. "There's more than a hundred troops there, there's helicopters, they're facing a bunch of civilians—old people, infants, women," Carlin describes. "And they certainly weren't ordered to do the gang rapes, which they also did." The soldiers, Carlin points out, had time to stop and think. "They took a lunch break." General William Westmoreland, the overall commander, acknowledged this in his testimony—indicating that there was "plenty of time to consider what you're doing."

Carlin acknowledges that My Lai is such an extreme case that one might accuse him of "cherry-picking," so he offers a second, less famous example: *United States v. Kenan*, a court-martial from 1966. This case involved Marine PFC Charles W. Kenan and a small combat patrol of ten Marines led by a 22-year-old corporal operating in Viet Cong territory. The situation was, as Carlin describes it, "about as difficult a situation as you can put people into"—ten young men in enemy country, with limited information and high stress. When ordered to commit abuses against civilians, Kenan followed those orders and was subsequently held accountable. The key legal principle established: even in the most challenging combat conditions, even under intense pressure from a superior, individual service members are expected to exercise judgment and refuse illegal orders.

This is precisely what the six Democratic lawmakers were reminding troops of in their video. Their critics, Carlin argues, were acting as though American soldiers are "robots" who should follow any order blindly. But this mischaracterizes both the law and the men and women who serve. "If they simply do that—they're exquisitely educated, what they swear oath to, that they take so seriously—if they simply do that, they're somehow seditious and treasonous," Carlin observes, noting the absurdity. "Or if somebody reminds them that they're supposed to do that, they're seditious and treasonous."

## The Firewall That Matters Most

At the heart of Carlin's argument is a framework for understanding what truly protects American democracy from sliding into authoritarianism or military dictatorship. He identifies multiple layers of protection, but argues that the most important—our "last fail safe"—lies with the individual soldier on the ground.

The United States is "somewhat insulated" from the dynamic that plays out in countries like Bangladesh, Carlin explains, because of "such a long and powerful tradition of the military being subordinate to civilian authorities." But that is not the whole story. The military is composed of Americans: volunteers who joined to serve their country and protect their people, not conscripts pressed into service for a king. These men and women have taken an oath to the Constitution, not to any individual president. "The military is ours, not his," Carlin says explicitly. "And any attempts to blur that line is to try [to] chip away and make a crack in the most important firewall we had that separates us from a place like Bangladesh."

Carlin applies this reasoning to both sides of the political aisle, a characteristic move for a commentator who has spent decades trying to rise above partisan tribalism. "Just imagine this power and worry on the side you are most concerned about," he advises. "If you're not worried about the current president having the power to do something nasty, then just imagine in the hands of the people you are most afraid of—that's the proper way to view these things, right? Powers change hands." The safeguard he is describing is not about protecting one political faction or the other; it is about ensuring that no future president, Republican or Democrat, can deploy American military power against American citizens with impunity.

This brings Carlin back to the question of "wheat and chaff"—a distinction borrowed from Steve Bannon's vocabulary, though Carlin uses it in his own way. "Flooding the zone with crap" is a deliberate tactic, Carlin explains, designed to obscure genuinely important issues by throwing up "all kinds of unimportant things to distract you." By the time you notice what you should have been focused on, "it's too far along for you to do anything about it." Issues like transgender rights, immigration policy, and even the Epstein scandal—which Carlin mentions as a possible distraction—can wait. "Those issues pale in comparison and importance though to breaking down the firewalls that keep us safe."

## The Weight of Presidential Words

Carlin also addresses the broader question of presidential speech and its consequences, drawing on both legal principles and practical considerations.

Free speech, Carlin notes, is not merely a legal right but what the Founders considered a natural right inherent in free people—"not something they gave you, but something they enunciated because you're born with it," as he puts it. Political speech—criticizing leadership, questioning lawmakers, holding the government accountable—is the most important category of speech. "Criticizing the president is as American as apple pie," Carlin says. "It's the national sport, and always has been. In fact, it was the national sport before the Constitution of the United States was even drafted in 1787."

But beyond the First Amendment questions, Carlin reflects on what it means for the most powerful person in the world—someone with control over 5,000 nuclear weapons—to use that platform to call for the arrest, trial, and execution of political opponents for simply exercising their own right to speak. He notes that previous presidents have been "so careful about what they said and how they said it because they don't want to destroy the equilibrium." The instability such rhetoric creates affects not only domestic politics but "the stability of the planet."

Carlin also pushes back on the desensitization that has occurred over time. He asks listeners to "put [themselves] back in the moment"—to imagine what a series of social media posts calling political opponents "traitors" and suggesting they should be executed would have done "if Trump had done it on his very first day in office in his first term." The "astonishment, shock, and then almost immediate unity to do something about that would have been palpable back then," he suggests. "But you know, we get there by degrees, and you just sort of shrug your shoulders now." This gradual normalization, Carlin warns, is itself part of the danger.

## The Systemic Wobble

Throughout the episode, Carlin returns to a metaphor he has used before: the American system of government as a "wobbling top." The co-equal branches of government—executive, legislative, judicial—are designed to check and balance each other. "We've been talking about for years how unbalanced that system has become," Carlin says. "And we're like a wobbling top now, and everybody sees it." The current president, he suggests, has made a practice of identifying and exploiting the creases that appear in the constitutional edifice—"pulls them apart wider for all sorts of reasons"—whether to deflect from other controversies or simply as a reflexive response to criticism.

Carlin invokes the so-called "torture hearings" of the George W. Bush administration, when Congress attempted to establish what the government could and could not order people to do. He references a memorable exchange in which John Yoo—author of the so-called "torture memos"—was asked whether the president could order the torture of a child to extract information from a parent. Yoo's answer, that it "would depend on why the president thought he had to do that," horrified many listeners precisely because it suggested there were no clear limits. Carlin uses this example to underscore the need for multiple layers of protection: co-equal branches that check each other, military leaders who refuse illegal orders, and a body politic that remains vigilant.

## Key Insight: The Military Belongs to the People

The most fundamental point Carlin makes is one that many Americans may never have considered explicitly: the U.S. military is not the president's personal instrument. It is not the property of whoever occupies the Oval Office. It belongs to the American people—serving the Constitution, not any individual leader. When the president attacks lawmakers for reminding troops of their constitutional obligations, or suggests that following the law makes someone a "traitor," this is an attempt to blur a line that has protected Americans for generations. "What kind of country does this guy want?" Carlin asks, referring to the apparent desire for a presidency unencumbered by criticism.

## Key Insight: The Soldier's Judgment Is the Final Firewall

Carlin argues that formal constitutional protections and institutional checks, while important, are ultimately insufficient if the men and women carrying weapons on the ground are unwilling to act morally. "If somebody ever tries to rest those military folk from us," he says, referring to efforts to politicize the military or undermine its professional culture, "this is something that should grab your attention." The individual soldier's refusal to obey an illegal order—made possible by education, ethics training, and the oath to the Constitution rather than any individual—is the ultimate safeguard against atrocities and abuses of power.

## Key Insight: Power Rotates—Build Safeguards That Protect Everyone

Carlin repeatedly stresses that his concern is not about any single president but about the office itself. "Powers change hands," he says. The safeguards discussed in this episode must work for whoever holds power, in either party. If you support these protections when your opponents hold office, you must support them when your allies do too. Conversely, if you are not concerned about a president you favor having such powers, "just imagine in the hands of the people you are most afraid of." The proper frame for evaluating presidential power is not "does this help my team" but "would I want this power in the hands of my worst political enemy?"

## Key Insight: Distraction Is a Tactic, Not an Accident

Carlin's discussion of "flooding the zone with crap" reflects a sophisticated understanding of information warfare in the digital age. The constant churn of scandals, outrages, and controversies is not random; it serves a purpose. It keeps the public exhausted and unable to focus on the structural issues that determine whether democracy functions or fails. "There's a lot of chaff out there in our society right now that is obscuring the really important things," he observes. The skill required is not just consuming information but cultivating the judgment to distinguish "wheat from chaff"—the genuinely consequential from the merely entertaining or enraging.

## Who This Episode Is For

This episode of *Common Sense* will be of particular value to several audiences. Americans concerned about the current state of democratic institutions—regardless of their political affiliation—will find here a framework for thinking about presidential power, military subordination, and the structural protections built into American governance. History and political science students will appreciate Carlin's use of specific case studies (Nuremberg, My Lai, *United States v. Kenan*) to ground abstract constitutional principles in concrete examples. Veterans and active-duty service members may find Carlin's defense of their ethical autonomy particularly resonant, as he explicitly argues that being asked to exercise judgment is not a burden but a safeguard. And anyone struggling to maintain perspective amid the relentless churn of daily political news will find in Carlin's "wheat and chaff" framework a useful tool for prioritizing what truly matters.

For readers who want to understand not just what is happening in American politics but why the structural safeguards matter—why Bangladesh is not an abstract foreign story but a cautionary tale—this episode provides essential context. It is a reminder that the things that make countries like the United States different from countries where soldiers kill 1,400 of their own citizens are not mysterious or accidental. They are built into law, training, oath, and culture. And they require constant vigilance to maintain.</p>
