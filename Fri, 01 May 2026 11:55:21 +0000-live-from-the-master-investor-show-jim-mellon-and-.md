# Live From The Master Investor Show: Jim Mellon and Stephen Yiu

## Introduction

At the UK's largest gathering for private investors—the Master Investor Show held on April 25th—Meryn Somerset Webb, host of the Meryn Talks Money podcast, brought together three seasoned investors for a wide-ranging discussion that touched on the structural problems plaguing both the British and American economies, the extraordinary valuations in US equity markets, and one of the most consequential investment themes of our era: artificial intelligence and its infrastructure requirements.

The panel featured John Stepak, an investor and writer; Jim Mellon, chairman of the Burn Bay Group and manager of the Blue Whale fund; and Stephen Yiu, also manager of the Blue Whale fund. What emerged was a candid conversation that cut through the optimism often peddled on financial television, with both Mellon and Stepak offering particularly sobering assessments of the fiscal traps their respective countries have set for themselves—while Stephen Yiu, representing the practical concerns of a fund manager actually deploying capital, offered a more nuanced and selective view of where opportunity still exists.

The timing of this conversation feels particularly significant. With geopolitical tensions escalating, central banks navigating an uncertain path on interest rates, and AI spending reaching what some consider unsustainable levels, the perspectives offered by these three investors offer a valuable framework for thinking about where the real risks—and genuine opportunities—lie in the current environment.

## The UK Economy: Debt as the Root of All Problems

The discussion began with an honest assessment of the UK's economic situation, which John Stepak opened by acknowledging that "things don't look great" across multiple dimensions. The conversation quickly centered on the fundamental constraint that shapes all other policy choices: the UK's debt position.

The UK currently carries debt-to-GDP of approximately 95%, a figure that Stepak described as creating a situation where "there's no appetite for cutting public spending, but there's also no acknowledgment that we're on the far side of the Laffer curve on very many areas of the tax take." The implication is stark: the UK has limited tools available to stimulate growth, cut taxes meaningfully, or increase spending without either exacerbating inflation or pushing debt to unsustainable levels.

Stepak traced the recent trajectory of the UK economy, noting that the country entered 2026 with expectations that interest rates would fall faster than previously anticipated—a hope driven by inflation declining more quickly than forecast, partly because of Rachel Reeves' handling of the October budget. "That's really bad that we should be so dependent on the direction of interest rates because that just highlights how the lack of resilience in the overall economy," Stepak observed.

Then came the war, which Stepak characterized as having "turned everything upside down." The geopolitical disruption has complicated the inflation picture, meaning the rate cuts markets had been pricing are now uncertain. This matters enormously for UK households, as Stepak explained: "Mortgage rates have already shot up by about a percentage point, which means that the housing market—which has been wobbly for a long time now—it seems to be a bit of a tipping point."

On housing specifically, Stepak noted that UK house prices have effectively gone nowhere for approximately a decade in nominal terms, with London prices actually having fallen in real terms—a phenomenon he and Jim Mellon have long predicted. "The forecast that John and I have been making about the great house price crashes coming through always in real terms rather than nominal," Mellon confirmed, acknowledging that while the crash hasn't shown up in headline prices the way some expected, the real-terms collapse has been substantial.

Beyond debt and housing, Stepak identified energy vulnerability as another structural weakness. "We are extremely vulnerable to energy problems because we have a somewhat irrational energy policy," he stated, though the conversation didn't dive deeply into specific policy mechanisms.

The critical insight Stepak offered was that debt isn't just one problem among many—it's the root cause underlying political instability and most other economic dysfunctions. "The real problem is the debt, and the only way out of that at this point is to grow your way out, grow away, or inflate your way out," he concluded.

## Jim Mellon's Counterintuitive View: The UK Looks Relatively Good

What made the UK portion of the discussion particularly interesting was Jim Mellon's divergence from the prevailing pessimism. While Stepak was painting a picture of structural constraint and vulnerability, Mellon offered a counterintuitive reassessment: "I am actually much more optimistic about the UK, I think, than John."

The key distinction Mellon drew was between government debt—which is indeed problematic—and the financial health of British households and corporations. "The consumer in the UK is in remarkably robust shape, and corporations are generally in robust shape as well," he stated. This matters because economic growth ultimately depends on private sector activity, not government expenditure. If the UK were to experience some unexpected political alignment that produced sensible economic policies, Mellon argued, the underlying fundamentals suggest the country is well-positioned to respond.

This point bears emphasis: despite years of hand-wringing about the UK's economic performance, the household sector has undergone what Mellon called "extraordinary" deleveraging since the 2008 financial crisis. British consumers entered the current period of uncertainty with their balance sheets in far better shape than their American counterparts—a distinction that could prove crucial if conditions improve.

## The US Economy: Where the Real Crisis May Be Brewing

If the UK situation represents managed stagnation, Jim Mellon's analysis of the United States suggested something more alarming. His central thesis was that while the UK has problems, the US situation is "really out of control"—a distinction he believes most investors are failing to appreciate.

The numbers Mellon cited were striking. US federal government debt stands at $39 trillion, an all-time high both in absolute terms and as a percentage of GDP. "It's higher now than it was at the end of the Second World War," Mellon noted, drawing a historical parallel that should give investors pause. When you add state debt (approximately 15-20% of GDP on top), and consumer debt—which is at an all-time high of approximately $18 trillion—the total debt picture becomes roughly 130% of GDP, compared to the UK's 95%.

But raw debt numbers understate the vulnerability. Mellon pointed to the extraordinarily weak financial position of American consumers: "The average—the median, as they call it—savings in the US is between 5 and $8,000. And a lot of people don't have any money in the United States, and they have a lot of debt. So the average American has $105,000 of debt, of which $21,000 is non-mortgage debt. And that's much, much more than we have here."

The savings rate in America, Mellon emphasized, is roughly half the level of the UK—despite the UK being perceived as fiscally challenged. This creates a dangerous combination: a government that cannot control its spending, combined with a consumer base that has minimal financial cushion to weather economic shocks.

The fiscal situation compounds this vulnerability. "The fiscal deficit is running at about 6% of GDP, and it's getting bigger," Mellon explained. "And what's more, there's no way in which they can cut government expenditures. They're basically nondiscretionary." With the war in Iran adding approximately $1 billion per day in additional expenses, the US is, in Mellon's colorful phrase, "racking up debt like a drunken sailor."

The implications extend far beyond American borders. Mellon noted that the US dollar's share of global central bank reserves has declined from approximately 70% in 2008 to around 57% today, with the trend accelerating. Meanwhile, central banks globally have been accumulating gold—"as predicted by the three of us here," Mellon noted, referencing previous podcast discussions—as they diversify away from dollar-denominated assets.

Mellon's view is that the "pinch point" for the global economy will be US government debt, which he believes will trigger "a major devaluation of the US dollar, which is already falling." This de-dollarization trend, combined with the stock market's stretched valuations, creates conditions he considers highly dangerous.

## Stock Market Valuations: The US at Historic Extremes

Stephen Yiu, representing the practical concerns of a fund manager who actually buys and sells securities, was asked specifically whether US equity valuations worry him. His response was measured but revealing: while he acknowledged the interconnection between government fiscal health and market performance, he noted that his investment approach is bottom-up rather than macro-driven.

"Even though in today's context, we still have about maybe 70% invested into American businesses," Yiu explained, "when you look at the balance sheet strength, the business model and everything, pricing, or even the margin, they are highly profitable. And most of our holdings are net cash on the balance sheet."

In other words, Yiu's confidence in his US holdings stems not from macro optimism but from the specific quality of the businesses he's selected. Even if the broader US economy faces headwinds, the companies in his portfolio have fortress balance sheets that can weather disruption.

Jim Mellon, however, was less sanguine about the broader market. He highlighted several warning signs: the CAPE Shiller ratio sits at just under 40, a level that "every time it's ever got to 40, there's been a subsequent major correction." Record levels of margin debt suggest investors are highly leveraged and vulnerable to margin calls. And the "gamification" of stock market investing in the US—with young people trading on their phones through Robinhood and similar platforms—"has become a sort of Nash national obsession and akin to the speculation in possibly 1929."

The comparison to 1929 wasn't casual. Mellon was drawing a parallel to an era when stock market speculation had become a cultural phenomenon disconnected from underlying economic fundamentals—a pattern he sees repeating today.

## AI Infrastructure: The Debate at the Heart of Modern Investing

Perhaps the most substantive investment debate in the conversation centered on artificial intelligence and the extraordinary capital expenditure being directed toward AI infrastructure—particularly data centers. This is where the panel's views diverged most interestingly, with implications for how investors should position themselves.

Mellon and Stepak's skepticism clashed with Yiu's more optimistic assessment of the AI buildout's durability. Mellon noted that approximately half of American GDP growth is currently being generated by AI-related investment, particularly data center construction. "Half of all US growth is basically and the growth is being generated by monetary expansion largely and debt, is the building of data centers," he stated.

Mellon's concern was that this concentration creates vulnerability. "The seven major spending companies and they're all effectively like the railroads in the eighteen sixties and eighteen seventies," he explained. "They're laying rail tracks in exactly the same direction adjacent to each other. Competing one's ahead of one and the other one's not ahead of the other, etcetera, etcetera. And they're absorbing massive amounts of money as Steven just said."

The implication is that there cannot possibly be sufficient demand to justify all this spending. "There is no way there is sufficient money to keep the seven going," Mellon stated. He also noted that all the Mag 7 companies, with the exception of one, have moved from being "hugely cash flow positive" to "cash flow neutrality or negative and they're in debt." This shift from profit generation to cash consumption could be "the trigger point for a problem with the market."

Stephen Yiu, however, pushed back with a more nuanced perspective. His fund has deliberately avoided the "AI spenders"—the companies like Microsoft, Amazon, and Google that are pouring billions into AI infrastructure—and instead positioned itself with "AI infrastructure companies which are the company that are receiving the investment."

Yiu identified three specific holdings that illustrate his approach. First, **Vertiv**, which manufactures liquid cooling systems for AI data centers, addresses the energy intensity problem that makes AI computing so expensive. Second, **Hynex**, a South Korean memory company, benefits from the explosion in data generation. Yiu explained: "We think we're going to see more machine generated data than the entire human population has got on record. Just think about the interaction that we would have with AI. AI is generating a lot of images, videos, a lot of things that are coming out from AI. Everything needs to be archived."

Third, **Lumentum**, an $80 billion market cap American company that makes fiber optics for data transmission. Yiu sees this as particularly compelling because "within the AI data center, they are going to replace copper wires with fiber optics to transmit data because it's more energy efficient and it's going to be better for data transmission in terms of efficiency as well."

Yiu's philosophical approach to the AI buildout question was revealing. Rather than trying to predict whether AI infrastructure spending will collapse, he focuses on who benefits regardless of the outcome: "The money that we are actually going to receive as shareholders rather than you take a speculative view about all we're investing the AI which I agree with Jim that a lot of money is going to be wasted. But I want the money to be wasted with my companies as a shareholder."

## The China Factor: DeepSeek and AI Competition

Mellon introduced an element that complicated any simple narrative about AI infrastructure: the competition from China, and specifically the emergence of DeepSeek. "What we've not talked about is the competition from China," he stated. "DeepSeek, as Stephen well knows, can produce a million tokens for about a tenth the price of Anthropic."

This cost advantage stems from fundamental structural differences. "The energy cost in China and the availability of energy—it's got three times the amount of electricity as the United States as an example—is much lower," Mellon explained. Even if American AI models maintain a quality edge, their higher costs could erode competitiveness over time. "Although they might not be right at the cutting edge, they're good enough to be highly competitive with the US models, and ultimately they'll overtake the US models."

Yiu acknowledged the DeepSeek phenomenon and its implications but offered a more balanced view. "The way that you see... we have two big scenarios. One was DeepSeek that came out from China last year. And then recently we have Alphabet coming out with a way of optimizing data storage." These developments suggest that AI capabilities are advancing rapidly on multiple fronts, potentially increasing demand even as costs per unit of computation fall.

Yiu's framework was that efficiency gains lower the barriers to adoption: "All these developments or advancement is going to lower the cost of training, lower the energy intensity, and it's going to increase the penetration and adoption and also expansion of the AI tools that we can embrace with." In other words, cheaper AI could paradoxically drive more infrastructure investment by expanding the addressable market.

He also pointed to new applications as evidence that the AI buildout hasn't peaked. "We have seen, Infrastructure providers come out with Mythos, which is a cybersecurity tool. I think that's going to be quite game-changing in a way that suddenly a lot of governments across the world, at the same time enterprises will be embracing this." If new applications continue emerging at a rapid pace, the data center infrastructure will need to expand accordingly.

The panel seemed to agree that the AI story isn't over—Yiu explicitly stated "we are probably at the early innings of this AI supercycle"—but they diverged on where the value will accrue and whether the current pace of investment is sustainable.

## Detailed Takeaways

### The Debt Trap Is Real, But Location Matters

Both the UK and US face serious fiscal challenges, but the distribution of pain will be uneven. The UK has a debt problem at the government level, but households have repaired their balance sheets remarkably since 2008. The US has a debt problem at every level</p>
