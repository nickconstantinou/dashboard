# Melt-Up or Mirage? Markets Ignore Everything but Earnings

## Introduction

The financial commentary world has rarely felt more schizophrenic. On one hand, geopolitical flashpoints—the escalating tensions between Iran and Israel, the wider Middle East instability—are generating headlines that would traditionally send markets into避险 mode. On the other, equity indices from London to New York to Tokyo are printing fresh highs with an almost brazen disregard for world affairs. According to the hosts of Bloomberg's *Market Wrap*, the explanation is simple: earnings season has arrived, and nothing else seems to matter.

This episode of *Merrin Talks Money Market Wrap* brings together Merrin Somzat-Webb, Editor-at-Large for Bloomberg UK Wealth, and John Stevick, Senior Reporter and author of the *Money Distilled Newsletter*, for a wide-ranging conversation that refuses to take the surface narrative at face value. Rather than simply celebrate the melt-up, the hosts interrogate the structural vulnerabilities that bull market euphoria tends to obscure. Their discussion weaves through contrarian indicators, historical parallels, IPO dynamics, private market stress, and the peculiar inflation-prone characteristics of the British economy.

What emerges is a nuanced portrait of markets at an inflection point—where the official narrative of resilient growth and AI-fueled productivity gains coexists uneasily with warnings about equity supply floods, historical rhyme patterns, and the hidden fragility of private asset portfolios.

## The Earnings Obsession: Markets in a Vacuum

The episode opens with a striking observation that sets the tone for everything that follows. Despite the Iran conflict—described by Somzat-Webb as having "kicked off"—the Nasdaq has climbed 11%. When Stevick interjects that "the market literally doesn't care," the hosts are not simply making small talk. They are diagnosing a market regime.

According to the discussion, the current earnings season has been exceptionally strong, with companies across the board delivering surprises rather than disappointments. One particularly eye-catching data point circulates among the financial community: **S&P 500 earnings growth is "about to break out on the upside of a 90-year channel."** If accurate, this would represent a statistical anomaly—the kind of earnings supercycle that justifies lofty valuations and bullish positioning.

The hosts reference a metric that backtests as roughly fair value historically: the **PEG ratio**, calculated by dividing the forward price-to-earnings multiple by long-term earnings growth. With this ratio now hovering "just over one," the bulls have a convenient quantitative rationalization for current prices. As Stevick deadpans, "Everything is fine. Unless earnings growth expectations are wrong."

This qualification is not rhetorical. The discussion pivots to historical precedent that suggests the bullish reading may be dangerously incomplete.

---

## The Magazine Cover Indicator: Contrarian Wisdom or Statistical Fluke?

Stevick raises what he calls one of his "favorite bits of trivia"—the magazine cover indicator—and the hosts explore how this contrarian signal has performed in recent cycles. The Economist receives particular attention, and Stevick is careful to explain why the publication works especially well for this purpose: it represents "the closest thing you get to the crossover between mainstream and business news," meaning its covers reach the broadest possible audience of market participants. When an investment thesis appears on The Economist's cover, it has, by definition, entered the collective consciousness and should theoretically be "in the price."

The oil market provides the clearest evidence. In the late 1990s, The Economist ran a cover featuring the headline "Drowning in Oil"—a declaration that crude prices would never recover and had hit their permanent floor. That call coincided with the lowest oil price in decades (until the negative pricing event of April 2020). Most recently, The Economist ran a cover suggesting oil prices were "far too low." Since that cover appeared, Brent crude has declined approximately 10%. The hosts acknowledge that The Economist "may yet be right" given potential supply constraints masked by inventory levels, but the contrarian signal has beenremarkably prescient on oil specifically.

Business Week enters the discussion with perhaps the most celebrated cover in financial media history: **"Death of Equities,"** published in 1979, just before a generational bull market took hold. The timing was remarkable—by essentially declaring equities dead as an asset class at the moment of maximum pessimism, the magazine captured the ultimate contrarian opportunity.

Somzat-Webb adds a meta-layer to this analysis: **Business Week launched in 1929**, the very year the Roaring Twenties collapsed into the Great Depression, precisely when "people were prepared to put up the money for Business Week." Money Week, where Somzat-Webb served as launch editor, arrived in 1999-2000—"very, very bearish on the dot-com boom"—right as the speculative excess peaked. The pattern suggests that financial magazine launches cluster near market extremes, when public appetite for market coverage reaches saturation.

## The 1960s-70s Parallel: When Earnings Tripled and Markets Went Nowhere

Perhaps the most intellectually challenging section of the episode involves the hosts' examination of the 1960s-70s period—a historical analogy they describe as the market "rhyming" with past conditions. Stevick poses the question directly: "Is it possible that earnings growth was really great just before everything went wrong in the 1970s?"

The answer, confirmed by historical data, is yes—emphatically. Earnings entering 1969 had achieved record highs. Yet by 1970, earnings fell sharply. More striking still: **earnings tripled during the 1970s**, yet the US equity market as a whole "went absolutely nowhere" for the entire decade. Investors lost enormous sums in real terms despite rising corporate profits. The mechanism was simple but devastating: **P/E multiples collapsed** as inflation spiraled and sentiment deteriorated. Strong earnings were simply insufficient to offset the derating of the market's valuation.

This historical precedent directly challenges the comfortable assumption that current elevated earnings growth justifies current equity valuations. If inflation resurfaces—if energy prices climb amid Middle East disruption, if protectionist trade policies add cost pressures—then even a world where S&P 500 earnings break out of their 90-year channel may deliver disappointing investor returns. Earnings can rise while multiples compress, leaving stock prices stuck or declining.

The hosts do not claim certainty about this outcome, but the comparison is offered as a legitimate risk factor that bulls are currently ignoring.

## The IPO Flood: Supply Meets Demand at the Worst Moment

Somzat-Webb turns the conversation toward a structural concern Stevick has been developing: the equity supply dynamics即将 coming in the summer months. The discussion references analysis by Bloomberg colleague Simon White, whose column *Macroscope* addresses supply-side mechanics in equity markets.

White's research highlights **share buybacks as "the biggest source in the US market of equity coming out of the market of de-equitization."** On an annual basis going back to 2000, only three years produced positive net equity supply—meanwhile, new equity issuance exceeded buybacks, increasing the supply of shares available to investors. Those three years were **2000, 2009, and 2020**—each a major market bottom or crash year. This counter-intuitive finding suggests that when equity supply surges into the market, it often coincides with or precedes significant drawdowns, not rallies.

Beyond buybacks, the hosts flag an impending wave of **mega-cap IPOs** that could dramatically increase equity supply at a delicate moment. SpaceX is the most prominent candidate, but multiple AI-related ventures and private equity exit vehicles are reportedly queued for listing. The assumption in markets currently is that these offerings will be digested easily—demand for new equity is robust, and any IPO tied to artificial intelligence or space exploration commands premium attention.

Stevick draws a direct analogy to **the dot-com bubble's bursting in 2000**: the market commonly searches for a specific "trigger" for that crash, but one overlooked mechanism was the mass expiration of **IPO lockup periods**. Founders and employees who received shares at IPO were legally required to hold those shares for six months. When that lockup expired across dozens of companies simultaneously, insiders flooded the market with selling pressure. **Sir John Templeton** reportedly identified this dynamic and executed "probably the best short trade in history," netting approximately **$1 billion** by shorting a basket of dot-com IPOs ahead of their lockup expirations. "Most of us are not him," Stevick wryly acknowledges, but the structural lesson is clear.

The fear is that summer 2024-2025 will replay this pattern in slow motion: equity supply surges into a market that is currently hungry but may develop "indigestion" within six months. Add to this the **private market valuation reset** that could accompany floating prices for formerly illiquid assets, and the supply-demand calculus shifts unfavorably.

## Private Market Stress: The End of Extend and Pretend

Beyond IPOs, the hosts raise the quieter but potentially significant stress building in private markets. For years, private equity and venture capital funds have held onto assets, extending holding periods while praying for valuation recovery or favorable exit windows. The underlying investor base—pension funds, endowments, sovereign wealth vehicles—has largely tolerated the "extend and pretend" strategy.

Stevick's concern is that this patience is wearing thin: **"You've had like five or six years of stuff going through the pipeline and it's not getting any better. We want our money back."** When major IPOs succeed, they may provide a valve for some private market pressure. But if early offerings falter—if SpaceX underperforms expectations or AI valuations face skepticism—then the entire ecosystem of private company markups and stale valuations faces sudden scrutiny. Funds will be forced to acknowledge that assets held at 2021 peak valuations are worth substantially less. Their clients, having waited years for distributions, may simply walk away from the asset class.

This dynamic could cascade: forced selling by private funds to meet redemption requests depresses private market valuations further, triggering more investor withdrawals, in a repeat of patterns seen during prior cycles but at much larger scale given the expansion of private equity and private credit over the past decade.

## UK Inflation and Governance: The Structural Vulnerability

The final substantive topic shifts to the UK economy and bond market. The hosts note that they are recording on the day of UK council elections and Scottish and Welsh elections, constraining their political commentary but not eliminating it entirely. Somzat-Webb raises what he considers a "more fundamental issue": the UK's persistent **inflation-prone structural characteristics**.

The discussion attributes this susceptibility to "poor governance over a very long period of time and bad policy making," citing former Bank of England rate-setter Andy Haldane's recent podcast appearance. Haldane argued that "there's an awful lot of potential in the economy that can be unlocked just by improving the policy making"—implying that UK economic underperformance is partly self-inflicted rather than structurally inevitable.

The troubling implication is that regardless of electoral outcomes—whether Keir Starmer continues as Labour leader or is succeeded by someone else—the fundamental policy approach is unlikely to shift dramatically. The hosts characterize potential leadership outcomes as ranging from "slightly better" to "significantly worse," but none suggesting transformative improvement. The characteristic response to economic challenges remains "slapping a price control or a new law on it," rather than addressing underlying supply constraints. Specific examples mentioned include restricting North Sea energy development and maintaining energy pricing structures that exacerbate inflationary pressures.

For UK gilts, this suggests that investors should demand an inflation risk premium that may not be fully reflected in current yields.

## Detailed Takeaways

**1. The PEG ratio near 1 is not a buy signal—it is a conditional green light.** The hosts explicitly treat the metric's return to historical fair value as meaningful only if earnings growth expectations are accurate. In an environment where analyst consensus has already been revised upward multiple times, there is limited further upside to earnings beats. If even modest earnings disappointment materializes, the PEG ratio would deteriorate sharply, and historical fair value would become historical overvaluation. Investors using this metric as a valuation anchor should stress-test it against downside earnings scenarios before increasing positions.

**2. The magazine cover indicator deserves tracking as a sentiment extreme gauge, particularly for commodities.** The Economist's track record on oil calls demonstrates that when mainstream business publications run contrarian covers on commodity markets, the odds favor the opposite direction in the near term. This does not mean oil prices will fall indefinitely—the hosts acknowledge potential supply-side catalysts for reversal—but it does suggest that consensus positioning in oil may be crowded. Similar logic applies to equity covers, though the timing is less precise. Investors should monitor the frequency and tone of financial magazine covers as a sanity check on consensus positioning rather than an actionable trading signal.

**3. Earnings growth in isolation is insufficient protection against negative real returns.** The 1970s experience remains the most important historical lesson from this episode. Investors who mechanically bought US equities because earnings were strong would have waited a decade for prices to recover in nominal terms and much longer in real terms. The critical variables were P/E multiple compression driven by inflation and the deterioration of investment sentiment. Today's environment contains parallels: if inflation resurfaces due to energy disruption, fiscal deficits, or tariff-driven price increases, the earnings narrative may prove insufficient to sustain valuations. Position sizing in equity markets should account for the scenario where earnings grow but multiples compress simultaneously.

**4. IPO supply dynamics represent an underappreciated risk for summer 2024-2025.** The combination of planned mega-listings (SpaceX, AI companies, private equity exits), potential disappointment on share buyback execution, and the historical precedent of 2000 lockup expirations suggests that equity supply could surge precisely when demand for new shares is most stretched. Investors planning to participate in IPO markets or hold newly public companies should consider hedging strategies or maintaining dry powder for opportunistic buying if post-IPO weakness materializes. The Templeton precedent suggests that shorting a diversified basket of IPO candidates heading into their lockup expiration dates has historically been profitable at cycle turning points.

**5. Private market valuations face an inevitable reset that may arrive faster than anticipated.** The five-to-six-year holding period for private equity and venture capital assets is extending well beyond typical fund life cycles. When major IPOs succeed, they provide a partial exit valve for stale private holdings. But when they underperform—as some high-profile recent listings have—confidence in private market valuations erodes rapidly. Limited partners should be preparing for potential forced distributions at depressed prices. Fund managers should stress-test portfolio companies against scenarios where exit multiples are 30-40% below current implied valuations before any mark-to-market adjustments.

**6. UK-specific risks in fixed income deserve separate consideration from global rate dynamics.** The structural inflation propensity of the UK economy, driven by governance failures and policy choices over decades, means that UK gilts may require a larger inflation risk premium than comparable sovereign debt from countries with better policy track records. For international investors deciding where to allocate fixed income exposure, the hosts' discussion suggests that UK bonds deserve a discount relative to fundamentals implied by headline yield spreads.
