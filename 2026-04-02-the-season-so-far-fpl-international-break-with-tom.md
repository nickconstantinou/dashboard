# The Season So Far: An International Break Deep Dive into Fantasy Premier League

## Introduction: A Rare Moment to Reflect

The international break has arrived, offering Fantasy Premier League managers a precious three-week reprieve from the relentless Thursday-Sunday deadline cycle that dominates their lives. Yet rather than completely stepping away from the FPL world—as the hosts initially suggest they should—Sam and Tom Campbell of the *Who Got the Assist* podcast settle in for their traditional international break episode: a more relaxed, zoomed-out conversation that examines the season from 30,000 feet rather than obsessing over individual gameweek transfers.

This particular episode marks a milestone in the FPL podcast ecosystem, as Tom Campbell—known on social media as Tom Campbell on X—finally makes his debut appearance on *Who Got the Assist*. The achievement is particularly noteworthy given that Campbell has already appeared as a guest host on both *Always Cheating* and *Planet FPL*, completing what the hosts playfully describe as "the hattrick of OG FPL podcasts." The episode captures the unique camaraderie that exists within the FPL content creation community, where hosts from competing shows maintain genuine friendships and mutual respect.

Recorded on Tuesday, March 24th, the conversation comes at a pivotal moment in the season. With the Premier League campaign approaching its decisive final stretch and Mo Salah having just announced his departure from Liverpool at season's end, the episode weaves together personal reflection, statistical analysis, and forward-looking strategic discussion. The timing allows for both celebration of what has been and contemplation of what lies ahead.

## The Hosts and Their Dynamic

Sam, the principal host, brings his characteristic wit and genuine enthusiasm for the game to every episode, though he admits during this recording that even he cannot fully escape the FPL world during the international break. Rather than resting, Sam confesses he has immediately "dived into playing FPL Traitors instead," a game variant that has apparently "already consumed my entire life." The admission underscores the addictive nature of the FPL community and content ecosystem—no matter how much the rational mind knows it should step away, the pull back into this world of differentials, chips, and differentials remains irresistible.

Tom Campbell, making his *Who Got the Assist* debut, introduces himself as both a longtime listener of the show and a content creator who has been deeply embedded in the FPL community for years. The genuine warmth between the two hosts becomes immediately apparent as they exchange pleasantries, with Sam expressing genuine surprise that Campbell has never before appeared on the show despite the extensive cross-pollination between FPL podcasts. "It feels like you've done it multiple times," Sam tells his guest, noting how frequently he has heard Campbell's voice on other shows and seen him active in the community.

The chemistry between the hosts establishes the tone for the entire episode—professional yet relaxed, analytical yet entertaining, serious about FPL strategy while maintaining appropriate perspective on a game that ultimately exists for enjoyment. This balance between competitive intensity and recreational fun becomes a recurring theme throughout their conversation.

## Tom Campbell's Chaotic Season: A Case Study in FPL Philosophy

When Sam invites Campbell to walk through his season so far, the response is immediate and characteristically self-deprecating: "relatively catastrophic." The admission comes with an important asterisk, however. Campbell reveals that his guest appearances on *Planet FPL*—appearing alongside James and SG—coincided precisely with his lowest rank of the season, reaching gameweek 10 at approximately 5.6 million overall. The correlation has left him wondering whether his hosting duties somehow "cursed" his fantasy performance, though he notes hopefully that his subsequent appearances may have "turned my fortunes around" as he has climbed back toward "relative respectability in the top 500k."

What makes Campbell's season particularly fascinating is the deliberate philosophy he adopted from the very beginning. Having experienced significant FPL fatigue during the previous season—characterized by what he describes as "the feeling of ambivalence that sort of I found pretty pervasive"—Campbell seriously considered not playing at all this campaign. When friends eventually convinced him to participate, he faced a critical question: how could he avoid the burnout and disinterest that had plagued his previous season?

His answer was radical. Rather than playing safe, Campbell committed to "taking huge swings" and "going for the fences" throughout the season. The most striking example came from gameweek one itself, when he publicly declared he would not include Erling Haaland in his team. While Haaland "started like an absolute train," Campbell embraced the contrarian approach, enduring significant pain as his differential strategy initially seemed disastrous. The correlation eventually worked in his favor—Haaland's form dropped significantly, coinciding with Campbell's improving fortunes—but the psychological fortitude required to stick with such a strategy through weeks of underperformance deserves recognition.

Similarly, Campbell also excluded Mo Salah from his initial setup, though he acknowledges this decision was less impactful given Salah's comparatively modest season by his extraordinary standards. The pattern reveals a manager willing to accept short-term pain for long-term philosophical consistency, betting that the herd's favorite assets would eventually disappoint while his differentials would shine.

The approach extends to chip usage as well. By the time of this recording, Campbell reveals he has "burnt them all point"—meaning he has used all his chips, including a free hit during gameweek 31. That particular free hit manifested in what he candidly describes as "not something I would advocate your listeners would have done or anyone in their right mind would do": a triple Fulham defense stack, including captaining a Fulham defender. He also deliberately selected two Tottenham players for his free hit squad.

Did it work? By Campbell's own admission: "it didn't work out at all." But the spirit behind the decision matters more than the outcome. "The spirit like that underpinned it was a sense of fun and a sense of it could have been something spectacular that would have felt like, you know, my point and that's how I've been trying to play the game this season."

This philosophy—prioritizing entertainment and potential spectacular outcomes over optimal expected value—represents a legitimate approach to FPL that often goes unacknowledged in the community's discourse. For players like Campbell who derive more satisfaction from bold, memorable decisions than from safe accumulation of points, the season's results become almost secondary to the journey itself.

## The 200 Club: Elite Performers Approaching a Milestone

The conversation shifts toward examining which players might achieve the prestigious 200-point threshold this season. While no one has reached this milestone yet, the hosts discuss the "runners and riders"—the candidates who could potentially breach this ceiling before the campaign concludes. This segment demonstrates the backward-looking yet simultaneously forward-looking nature of FPL analysis: understanding who has accumulated points helps managers project who might continue producing.

The 200 club represents an extraordinary achievement in FPL circles, requiring consistent excellence across the vast majority of a 38-gameweek season. Reaching such a milestone demands not merely prolific point-per-game averages but also strategic chip deployment, effective captain selection, and significant luck with fixtures and form. The hosts' discussion of candidates illuminates both the mathematical requirements for reaching 200 points and the practical factors that might prevent or enable various players from achieving this distinction.

## Mo Salah's Impending Departure: The End of an FPL Era

Perhaps the most emotionally significant topic of the episode emerges when the hosts address the breaking news that Mohamed Salah has announced his departure from Liverpool at the end of the season. The announcement came literally minutes before recording began, with Sam sharing the news as a "special mention to a certain someone who tends to live in the 200 club year after year."

Salah's importance to the FPL community extends far beyond his considerable statistical output. For multiple consecutive seasons, Salah has represented the cornerstone around which most successful teams were built—whether as an auto-include starter or as the player managers could not afford to be without. His consistency, premium pricing, and ability to deliver returns through goals, assists, and bonus points made him the defining asset of an era.

The hosts acknowledge they will "be having a word on him and what he's done for FPL over the last few years," signaling that this episode represents merely the opening of a larger conversation about Salah's legacy in the fantasy community. For managers who have relied on him season after season, the news represents not just the end of an era but the beginning of a significant strategic recalibration. With Salah's departure, an enormous chunk of expected points evaporates from the premium forward bracket, fundamentally reshaping how managers will approach team construction next season.

The conversation reflects the perpetual planning that characterizes serious FPL managers—even when official deadlines are weeks away, the mental work of team optimization continues.

## Key Takeaways for FPL Managers

**Embrace philosophy over perfection.** Tom Campbell's season demonstrates that FPL can be legitimately approached as entertainment rather than pure optimization. Managers who have lost the joy in chasing expected points might consider embracing bold strategies that prioritize memorable experiences over safe accumulation. The psychological cost of playing "correctly" but joylessly often exceeds the value of marginal point gains.

**Fatigue is real and valid.** Campbell's honest discussion of FPL fatigue provides important validation for managers who have experienced similar feelings of ambivalence and burnout. Recognizing these feelings and taking action—whether that means strategic experimentation, reduced engagement, or complete breaks—represents mature management of one's relationship with the game.

**Contrarianism has costs and benefits.** Campbell's decision to exclude Haaland and Salah cost him significant points during their peak periods but eventually coincided with their relative decline. The lesson is not that contrarianism is always correct but that it requires psychological fortitude and philosophical consistency to execute successfully.

**Chip strategy reveals priorities.** Campbell's complete chip usage—free hit, wildcard, and bench boost—reflects a manager prioritizing potential spectacular moments over strategic flexibility. Understanding one's own chip philosophy helps avoid the common trap of using chips reactively rather than according to a coherent plan.

**Data illuminates truth.** Campbell's statistical analysis represents the growing sophistication of FPL discourse. Managers who incorporate data-driven insights into their decision-making—while maintaining appropriate skepticism about small samples and cherry-picked statistics—will develop more nuanced understandings of the game.

**The Salah era is ending.** While the specifics of next season remain uncertain, managers must begin mentally preparing for an FPL landscape without Mo Salah as a reliable premium option. This represents both a challenge—redistributing the enormous expected points Salah provides—and an opportunity, as the freed salary allows different team construction strategies.

The episode ultimately embodies the spirit of what makes FPL content compelling: genuine expertise combined with authentic enthusiasm, strategic analysis balanced with appropriate humor, and competitive intensity moderated by recognition that this game exists to be enjoyed. Whether you are a top-500 manager or a casual participant, the conversation offers something of value while reminding us all that sometimes the most important move is knowing when to step back—and when to load up the FPL Traitors variant instead.
