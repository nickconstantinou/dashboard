Wildcard 32 vs Wildcard 35: FPL Transfer Strategy for the Run-In

Introduction

The Fantasy Premier League season is hurtling toward its climax, and managers face one of the most consequential decisions of the campaign: when to deploy their precious wildcard. For those holding onto this valuable chip, the timing could mean the difference between a mediocre finish and a genuine title push—or at least a significant leap up the rankings.

In this detailed analysis, FPL Harry walks through the two dominant strategies competing for managers' attention this season: the Wildcard 32, Bench Boost 33, Free Hit 34 approach versus the Free Hit 33, Wildcard 35, Bench Boost 36 (or potentially 37) pathway. With the season entering its critical final stretch, Harry examines his own team's current state, explores potential transfer moves, and uses planning tools like fpl.team to visualize how each strategy plays out over the remaining weeks.

The timing of this decision matters enormously because the fixture schedule creates different blank and double gameweeks that dramatically reshape which players offer the best value. Harry approaches this as someone with five free transfers already banked and just £0.1million in the bank—a relatively comfortable position that actually makes the wildcard decision more complex, not less.

Understanding Your Current Team State

Before diving into wildcard timing, Harry emphasizes that managers must honestly assess whether their current squad actually needs a wildcard. This isn't just about maximizing the bench boost chip; it's about whether the team has fundamental problems that require a complete overhaul.

Looking at his own squad, Harry notes that while there are "luxury changes" he could make—questioning whether he wants Juju Bhour and Anderson starting every week, whether he could improve on Taroski—he doesn't look at this team and feel like he "definitely needs a wild card." This is a crucial point of self-assessment that many managers overlook. The grass always looks greener, but if your team is fundamentally sound and has good fixtures coming, forcing a wildcard can be a mistake.

Harry's current team has five free transfers to use immediately, which is significant because he must make at least one transfer this gameweek. The fixture difficulty analysis on his screen shows West Ham ranked at the top of the "easy fixtures" list when combining gameweeks 32 and 34, making players like Jared Burn particularly attractive for the coming period.

The Free Hit 33 Strategy: What a Draft Looks Like

For managers considering the Free Hit in gameweek 33, Harry walks through what an early draft would look like. The free hit allows managers to completely reshape their squad for a single gameweek, typically used when a team's players are blanking (not playing).

Harry's draft includes Darlow, Stra, and Cal Leuen in goal, with particular attention to Leeds as his primary team investment. He explains that Leeds' double—with a home game against Newcastle and an away game at Bournemouth—makes them attractive: "I think their double is slightly better than that of Bournemouth with Leeds at home, Newcastle away versus Bournemouth away, Wolves at home."

The triple up for Manchester City features O'Reilly, Seno, and Harland, while Cole Palmer provides Chelsea coverage. Erling Harland is slated for the captaincy, with Smeno, Calbert Lewen, and Pascal Gross completing the squad. Harry notes the final midfielder spot is "quite up in the air" and could also feature Ryan or Antoine Stack if Stack recovers from his FA Cup injury.

A critical insight Harry provides is that the free hit in gameweek 33 "looks pretty similar to all the teams that people who wildcarded in 32 will have." This means the advantage of wildcarding earlier isn't as substantial as it might seem—you're essentially fielding the same starting eleven regardless, just without the bench boost option on top. The real benefits come from having flexibility if injuries occur or if you want long-term exposure to certain players rather than others.

Transitioning Through Gameweek 34

The transition between chips becomes crucial when looking at gameweek 34. Harry projects his team will look something like Van Dijk starting over either Anderson or Taroski, with "Taroski drops to the bench again." This represents the challenge of managing multiple positions across difficult fixture runs.

With one transfer likely available in gameweek 34, Harry identifies his weakest point as the forward line playing away at Manchester United. He discusses a potential downgrade of Erling Harland to free up funds: "If I wanted to buy MoSalah that is something I could consider. So downgrading Erling Harland to a forward, let's go and put in Gimenez for example and upgrade Anderson all the way to MoSalah if I wanted to do that as an example."

This flexibility is precisely why carrying transfers forward can be valuable—it allows managers to be more aggressive when opportunities arise rather than forcing changes early.

The Wildcard 35 Strategy: A Complete Team Rebuild

When examining the wildcard 35 strategy, Harry walks through what a complete team rebuild would look like at that point. His draft features a triple up on Arsenal defense with Fulham at home, recognizing Arsenal's favorable fixture run. Calbert Lewen, Jar Pedro Gordon, and Seno form the core of his midfield, with Bruno Fernandes completing the setup.

The triple up on Crystal Palace is particularly interesting because they have a double in gameweek 36, making them valuable for that specific round. Harry notes he'd have "a triple up on Manchester City if they double" in gameweek 36, with Ryan, Gabrielle, Bruno, Juice Hall, and Anthony Gordon completing his squad.

One strategic observation Harry makes is that he "actually thinks the bench boost looks slightly better in 37" than gameweek 36. This is worth considering because the bench boost works best when you have maximum confidence in your starting eleven and want to leverage your substitutes. The fixture run includes Darlow at home to Brighton, and Harry suggests potentially using a transfer to move from Richards to Harry Maguire, with Jbury Hall and Daniels completing the defensive options.

Specific Transfer Options Under Consideration

Harry details several specific transfer options he's weighing for gameweek 32, starting with the most straightforward: Ekitique out to Jared Burn. His reasoning is telling—"I think Ekit's minutes either side of the PSG games in between the PSG games either side I should say is a slight risk. And I like Burn. He's got Wolves at home and he also has Everton at home in gameweek 34."

The fixture combination matters enormously here. Burn faces Wolves at home in gameweek 32 and Everton at home in gameweek 34—the only two gameweeks Harry needs to consider if he's planning to wildcard in gameweek 35. This creates a neat package deal where Burn provides value across the exact fixtures managers using the wildcard 35 strategy need.

Other transfers under consideration include Richards to Muki, as "Muki's got Spurs at home and Forest at home in the two fixtures that I'd be looking to buy him for." Harry also considers Brian or Burmo to Kuna, explaining: "I do think that Brian and Burmo will start for Manchester United at home to Leeds, but he could once again be hooked early, get reduced minutes. So Mattz Kuna is probably slightly safer."

The Harry Wilson versus Juh Hall debate at forward represents another close call, with Harry noting: "Maybe I go with double Everton. Maybe Harry Wilson's goal scoring form is better than Juh Hall. It's really close."

Comparing the Two Strategies

Harry directly compares the two wildcard strategies, concluding that the wildcard 35 approach is "definitely better if you don't have lots of transfers available. It allows you to sort of cut the remainder of the season."

However, for managers like Harry who have accumulated five free transfers and potentially more available, the calculation changes. He explains: "I have a lot of spare transfers. So I can be more aggressive with things like buying Solarin in 34 if I wanted to. If I wanted to make bigger upgrades on the likes of Tarovski, Ekitik this week, I have lots of spare transfers further down the line if I'm going to wildcard 35."

This is perhaps the most nuanced point in the entire video: the value of carrying transfers forward is that it provides optionality. Managers with banked transfers can react to injuries, form changes, and fixture swings without using their wildcard prematurely.

Detailed Takeaways

Takeaway 1: Assess Whether You Actually Need a Wildcard

The most important decision point isn't about the chips themselves but about your team's fundamental quality. Harry emphasizes asking: "Do you have a team in gameweek 32 that you think needs a wild card? Are there enough potential changes that you would look to make to your team?" If your squad is solid with good upcoming fixtures, forcing a wildcard because it seems like the "thing to do" can leave you stranded later in the season.

Takeaway 2: Fixture Combinations Drive Transfer Decisions

When evaluating transfers like Jared Burn, Harry looks at the combined fixture difficulty across gameweeks 32 and 34. Players who provide value across both the blank and the surrounding gameweeks offer better value than those who only blank in one specific round. West Ham's fixtures make them attractive, which explains Burn's prominence in Harry's thinking.

Takeaway 3: Carrying Transfers Provides Valuable Optionality

With five free transfers banked, Harry can be more conservative with his wildcard timing. He explains he can "be more aggressive with things like buying Solarin in 34" or make bigger upgrades "further down the line if I'm going to wildcard 35." This optionality has real value that shouldn't be dismissed.

Takeaway 4: The Free Hit and Wildcard Often Produce Similar Teams

A surprising insight is that gameweek 33 free hit teams tend to look very similar to teams that wildcarded in gameweek 32. The difference is marginal unless injuries force changes or managers want long-term exposure to different players. This suggests the timing advantage of wildcarding earlier is smaller than many managers assume.

Takeaway 5: Consider Bench Boost Timing Carefully

Harry personally thinks the bench boost "looks slightly better in 37" than gameweek 36, though he acknowledges this isn't something that must be decided immediately. The bench boost works best when you have strong confidence in your starting lineup and can comfortably field four or five starting players you fully trust.
