I Need A Cuddle | Planet FPL S. 9 Ep. 46 | GW31 Review | Fantasy Premier League

Introduction

Welcome to Planet FPL, the long-running podcast dedicated to the intricacies of Fantasy Premier League strategy. In this episode, recorded after Gameweek 31, hosts Serge and James find themselves in a particularly reflective mood. The title "I Need A Cuddle" sets the tone perfectly—it has been a bruising weekend for both hosts, who sit at 17th and 18th in their respective mini-leagues, and the conversation frequently drifts toward the emotional toll that FPL can take on dedicated managers.

The episode arrives at a pivotal moment in the season. With only eight fixtures in Gameweek 31 due to FA Cup commitments, many managers faced a "blank" week where their captain and key assets couldn't deliver. But beyond the immediate disappointment lies a more significant structural shift: the podcast confirms a three-week break from Premier League action, with FA Cup quarterfixtures creating a unique gap in the schedule. This intermission forces managers to confront difficult decisions about team structure, wildcard usage, and which assets to trust when the league resumes.

The Personal Toll of a Brutal Gameweek

Both hosts open up about their disappointing GW31 performances, and their honesty provides valuable insight into how even experienced managers can struggle with the randomness of FPL. James accumulated only 51 points—a red arrow that extends his losing streak to five consecutive weeks. "It's not the biggest red arrow, but it's not green," he admits, capturing the peculiar frustration of FPL where even moderate disappointment feels like a significant setback after a streak of bad luck.

Serge faired slightly better with 58 points, though he describes his week as "a bit of a roller coaster." His team featured Harry Wilson (captain), who delivered a respectable nine points, but the overall experience was marred by defensive clean sheet points that came too late to save the gameweek. Both hosts reflect on the psychological weight of these results, with James noting that his "joy comes from other people's defeats" in the current circumstances—specifically that Arsenal lost the Carabao Cup, providing some small comfort amid his own struggles.

The conversation turns mordantly humorous when James admits he "sold Freddy Pototts for Harry" (transferring from one to the other), only for Toney to get injured in the warm-up, forcing Pototts into the starting lineup where he predictably scored. "He's guaranteed to score just to make my transfer from Pots to Wilson worthless," James sighs. These self-deprecating reflections underscore the randomness that defines FPL management, even for experienced podcasters who understand the mathematics behind their decisions.

Understanding the Defcon System and Its Impact

A significant portion of the discussion revolves around the "defcon" system—an FPL mechanic where players risk losing points based on their team's goal difference or match situation. Serge explains his frustration with Virgil van Dijk: "He never seems to get actually get a defcon because I feel like every time I've had a defcon from him, he's either conceded more than two or himself booked. And so, you never feel like you're actually getting the points off him."

The hosts discuss how defcons particularly affected certain decisions in GW31. James mentions that Tom Hadley posted late on the update that Alderete would have hit defcon, but it was "too late" to capitalize. This highlights the ever-increasing speed of FPL information flow, where managers must check updates even during matches to catch last-minute defcon opportunities. Serge also notes that "Eigor Thiago Defcon" was the "most entertaining aspect" of the Everton match, as he watched the game knowing Thiago needed to stay out of trouble to secure defcon points.

The defcon system creates unique strategic considerations. Players who consistently concede goals or pick up bookings become unreliable sources of defcon points, making them less attractive even when their underlying statistics look good. This invisible aspect of FPL rewards managers who watch matches closely and understand which players are likely to deliver clean sheet points versus those who might concede at any moment.

The Double and Blank Discussion

With the three-week break approaching, the conversation naturally turns to the complex fixture landscape ahead. Serge explains that "dead end 31 might for some people become dead end 32," meaning that teams managers thought would double in GW32 might not actually play twice. The uncertainty around FA Cup quarterfinal results creates a moving target—Southampton and Port Vale winning would create different scenarios than expected.

James highlights the Chelsea situation as particularly problematic: "Chelsea obviously if Chelsea win and double and you want three of them but they don't they don't look in great form right now." Even a struggling Chelsea team can provide returns, but the combination of poor form and uncertainty around whether they'll even double makes them a risky investment.

The discussion reveals the tension between logical fixture planning and trusting what managers see with their own eyes. Serge notes that Liverpool "could beat City in a single game but they're not in like great form, are they at the moment?" The same applies to Chelsea and Brighton—on paper, their doubles look attractive, but watching them play raises questions about whether form justifies the investment.

This leads to Serge's key advice: "Wait." The biggest reason not to wildcard immediately is that managers might end up with a team "that looks worse than what they have in 32" after seeing how the fixtures actually fall. Waiting allows the situation to clarify before committing to major transfers.

Team Analysis and Transfer Priorities

Both hosts walk through their squads to identify problem areas and potential transfer targets. James's team includes Robert Sanchez, Enzo Fernandez, and Bryan Brobbey—players he admits he "don't even know why you're there." These Chelsea assets particularly frustrate him: "I think there's a lot of Chelsea fans that are looking at their real life football team thinking why are you there?"

Looking at his fixtures for GW32, James identifies several playable matchups: Tottenham at home for Alderete, Fulham at home for Van Dijk, Manchester United's home game for Brobbey and Fernandez. Harry Wilson faces Liverpool away, which is "not great," though Wilson is "any game kind of player." The Chelsea boys face Manchester City at home, which James acknowledges "doesn't look great."

Serge's team has a triple Arsenal reserve, which gives him flexibility depending on how fixtures fall. He notes that his team "isn't terrible actually to be honest with you," but he still plans to wildcard after the FA Cup weekend. The key question is whether his current squad can survive without taking hits, or whether he should make smaller adjustments now and save the wildcard for after the break.

The discussion reveals different approaches to the break. James is "logging out" and leaving the country in three days, so he'll wait to wildcard until after seeing what happens with the FA Cup. Serge is more inclined to see if he can fix his team without pulling the wildcard trigger immediately.

Key Strategic Insights

The Value of Patience Over Reaction: The most consistent message from this episode is that managers should wait before making major moves. With three weeks of no Premier League action, the fixture landscape will become clearer. The FA Cup results will determine which teams double in GW32 and beyond. Reacting immediately to GW31 disappointment could lead to worse outcomes than waiting.

Form vs. Fixtures Trade-off: The hosts extensively discuss the tension between attractive fixtures and current form. Teams like Chelsea and Liverpool might have doubles on paper, but their recent performances suggest caution. Conversely, teams in better form (like Brentford with their clean sheet) might be worth considering even without obvious doubles.

Defcon Awareness Matters: The defcon system creates hidden value that casual managers might miss. Understanding which players consistently deliver defcon points versus those who concede or get booked provides an edge. This requires watching matches and checking updates during games—a time investment that separates dedicated managers from casual players.

The Wildcard Trap: Serge warns that "some people are going to wildcard into a team that looks worse than what they have in 32." This happens when managers react emotionally to bad gameweeks without considering how fixtures will actually fall. The wildcard is a powerful tool, but only when used at the right time with complete information.

Small Improvements vs. Wildcard: Both hosts acknowledge that some problems can be fixed without using the wildcard. James mentions that even with "two threes," if he "didn't want to take hits, I could fix it up without pulling the wild card." This suggests that managers should assess whether their squad has enough flexibility to improve through normal transfers before burning the wildcard.

Looking Ahead to Gameweek 32 and Beyond

The hosts confirm that content will continue during the break, though there will be a reduced schedule. They urge listeners to "switch off, log out, and take a break" from FPL, acknowledging that "there's a lot that's going to happen in the next couple of weeks" and the intensity of the season has been draining.

For managers still engaged, the key tasks during the break include: monitoring FA Cup results to understand which teams will double, reviewing current form to identify which assets are worth keeping, and preparing wildcard strategies for when the league resumes. The managers who use this time effectively will be better positioned when GW32 arrives.

The episode concludes with the hosts acknowledging their mutual struggles while maintaining the dark humor that defines the Planet FPL approach. "I need a cuddle" becomes a metaphor for the collective pain of FPL managers everywhere—the feeling that despite our best efforts, sometimes the game just beats us down. But as always, there's always next week, and with a three-week break ahead, there's time to recover, regroup, and come back stronger.

Who Should Listen

This episode is particularly valuable for FPL managers who are struggling with the current blank gameweek, feeling frustrated by their results, and contemplating whether to use their wildcard. The discussion provides both emotional validation (you're not alone in this struggle) and practical strategic advice (wait for more information before major moves). Intermediate to advanced managers will benefit most from the detailed discussion of defcons, fixture planning, and the psychological aspects of FPL management. Beginners might find the discussion of specific teams and transfers overwhelming, but the underlying message about patience and careful planning applies to all experience levels.
