# How TikTok Hijacked The Future Of Music

Key Insights

  • TikTok rewards "clip moments" — breakdowns, vocal gymnastics, and technical flourishes — allowing metal to bypass traditional album listening by delivering instant payoffs, creating what the speaker describes as "watching a horror movie and only getting hit with the jump scares"
  • Bands are increasingly writing songs "backward" — starting with the climactic breakdown or TikTok-worthy moment, then building the rest of the track around it
  • The 2008 era of deathcore featured deliberate "bass drops" and "reverse snares" as inside jokes between producers and musicians, yet this culture naturally produced the exact moments that now go viral on short-form platforms
  • Metal has NOT experienced the same old-song revival as other genres (hip hop, pop, ABBA), despite 2004–2010 producing formative classics — a stark contrast to how older tracks resurface through viral dance videos or meme culture elsewhere
  • Knock Loose succeeds on short-form despite "not caring about any of that" precisely because their music is genuinely intense and "paced so well" — demonstrating that authenticity outperforms manufactured virality

    Introduction

    Nik Nocturnal sits down with co-host Jared to dissect one of the most consequential shifts in how modern metal gets made, discovered, and consumed: the TikTok era's reshaping of the genre's creative DNA. Nik, known for his extensive YouTube presence covering metal and examining musical technique, brings both the perspective of a longtime listener who discovered his foundational bands through YouTube's early days and someone attuned to the industry's evolving mechanics.

    The conversation traces a fascinating through-line from YouTube's disruption of radio and physical media discovery (Myspace, Facebook) to TikTok's emergence as the dominant discovery platform for an entirely new generation. What makes this discussion particularly compelling is not just the acknowledgment that platforms change how music spreads, but the granular examination of how they alter the actual composition process itself — from how songs are structured to which creative decisions get made in the first place.

    The timing matters significantly. Metal has experienced a remarkable cultural renaissance in the 2020s, with bands like Sleep Token and Bad Omens breaking into mainstream consciousness while established acts like Bring Me The Horizon and Architects continue achieving career-best chart positions after decades of work. Understanding whether TikTok's influence is a net positive for the genre's longevity — or a corrosive force incentivizing shallow, moment-dependent garbage over lasting art — requires examining both the mechanics and the moral stakes.

    ---

    The Clip Economy: Why Metal Found Its Natural TikTok Habitat

    The conversation opens with Nik establishing TikTok's discovery mechanics, but quickly pivots to why metal specifically thrives in this environment. The platform's vertical video format and algorithmic promotion of high-engagement moments creates a selection pressure that favors what Nik terms "clip moments" — the sonic peak experiences that metal has always concentrated into breakdowns, vocal transitions, and technical passages.

    This isn't incidental. Nik draws the comparison that experiencing metal exclusively through TikTok clips is "like watching a horror movie and only getting hit with the jump scares." The listener gets the visceral payoff without the sustained tension-building that makes those moments land with full impact. Yet this same quality — the ability to deliver immediate sonic intensity — makes metal clips uniquely shareable in a scroll-dominated attention economy.

    The specific elements that work: guitar passages with unusual techniques, vocal "goblin noises" and extreme range demonstrations, drum moments of technical precision, and of course the breakdown — that metronomic, syncopated rhythmic assault that has defined heavy music since the metalcore explosion of the mid-2000s. Nik notes that most traditional songs require a minute-and-a-half of listening before reaching the climactic moment, whereas TikTok delivers that payoff instantly.

    ---

    "Can You Feel My Heart" and the Anatomy of Viral Metal

    The episode pivots to concrete case study territory when Jared requests to examine Bring Me The Horizon's "Can You Feel My Heart" — a track from the post-human album that Nik describes as one of his favorites from that record, yet argues it "shouldn't be 10 times better known than the next best known Bring Me track."

    The mechanism behind its disproportionate popularity: the track is exceptionally well-suited to video overlay content. When users can lay their personal videos over a song's hook or breakdown, that track accumulates the visibility that algorithmically promotes it further. The song itself is excellent — Nik is emphatic that the track justified its popularity through quality — but the specific sonic characteristics that made it adaptable to visual content propelled it beyond objectively comparable material.

    This leads Nik to pose the uncomfortable question he's certain the industry is already wrestling with: "How many bands are thinking almost short form first?" The question carries genuine concern about creative direction. If bands are reverse-engineering songs to produce multiple TikTok-worthy segments while consciously planning "meme seeding" strategies, the structural integrity of heavy music could suffer.

    ---

    The Backward Songwriting Revolution

    One of the episode's most revealing segments involves Nik's examination of how the creative process itself has transformed. He describes a scenario that would have been unthinkable in garage-band culture: writing a song "at the breakdown" first, beginning with "the climactic moment" and "working back" from there — essentially writing "how the movie ends" before establishing the narrative that leads there.

    The contextual factors driving this shift are multiple. The democratization of bedroom production means more musicians work alone or with a single producer rather than in live band settings. AI tools now exist within this workflow (Nik mentions this with characteristic understatement: "which is its own fun thing"). The social nature of collaborative songwriting — that organic moment of discovery with bandmates, the "mom kicks us out of the house" energy of teenage rehearsal sessions — has become less central to how music gets made.

    Nik is careful to acknowledge that some bands maintain the traditional approach ("let's just write our bangers"), but observes that many young artists have internalized the logic of the clip economy. If an artist watches a band "blow up by literally just having one moment that was crazier than the other moments," the incentive to structure a song around delivering multiple extreme moments becomes almost irresistible.

    ---

    Knock Loose and the Authenticity Paradox

    The conversation introduces Knock Loose as a counterpoint case — a band that has achieved significant traction on short-form platforms "by being kind of the most extreme sound that most people have ever heard," yet who Nik believes "don't care about any of that."

    The distinction matters: Knock Loose's success isn't a result of chasing algorithms or reverse-engineering viral moments. Their music is "so intense and so well momented" and "paced so well" that it "naturally works in that way." This reveals a paradox that Nik articulates clearly: "Social media is supposed to be something that happens that wasn't meant to fugazi everybody into loving it. Being loved on social media because it's a window into something outside of it."

    The critique of manufactured virality is sharp: "The problem is if you try and make it for social media, you also have to mask over the fact that this isn't supposed to look like it was made for social media because that then completely undermines it." When a song is transparently constructed around its potential to be clipped, stripped, and memed, something essential — the authenticity that made the extreme moment resonate in the first place — gets lost.

    Nik's conclusion is blunt: "A lot of time, unfortunately, the song just sucks. In the end, you get a shitty song, and that's the worst part." The tradeoff is immediate engagement for long-term staying power. These manufactured moment-songs lack "replayability" and "timelessness," becoming "the meme song" that users recognize without genuinely valuing.

    ---

    The 2008 Underground and Its Unlikely Revival

    A substantial portion of the conversation examines the 2008-era of extreme metal — particularly the deathcore explosion associated with bands like Job for a Cowboy — as a lens for understanding contemporary dynamics. Nik and Jared share the specific memory of hearing that iconic moment: "I remember we were listening to that in my friend's car. I must have been 17, driving through the mean streets of the Northeast of the UK. And he had a proper subwoofer in the boot of his car, you know, like, the fancy head unit up front. And I remember that shook my fucking teeth when that thing dropped."

    What characterized that era, beyond the sonics, was a specific creative culture. Nik describes "the do the thing" phenomenon: producers literally pressing buttons for bass drops or reverse snares while everyone in the session laughed, treating these as inside jokes. They were "having fun" with their producer, creating tracks that were "a fun thing that they know no one would have cared about outside of them." The bass drops were absurd in the best way — deliberate, over-the-top, almost comedic in their excess.

    This proves significant because many of those same sonic characteristics — the exaggerated dynamic shifts, the compressed breakdowns, the intentionally ridiculous production flourishes — are exactly what now plays as compelling content on short-form platforms. Nik observes that "there's so many of those moments in that music that naturally would be the TikTok moment."

    The evidence of this revival is embodied in Bring Me The Horizon's recent work, specifically their decision to "do Count Your Blessings again because why not?" — referencing their foundational 2007 debut and its brutal, over-produced excess. The cycle has come around: music that was made as underground novelty for scene kids with subwoofers now provides the template for viral content consumption.

    ---

    Why Metal Hasn't Had Its Own Viral Revival

    Nik raises a striking observation: while hip hop, pop, and even legacy acts like ABBA have experienced resurgences through viral discovery (he references the iconic Ocean Spray cranberry juice video that propelled a decades-old track back into circulation), he hasn't witnessed the same pattern in metal.

    The 2004–2010 era produced formative classics that "would have kind of been very formative" for listeners of their age bracket. Tracks like Bullet for My Valentine's "Tears Don't Fall" represent material that should have accumulated cultural staying power equivalent to its quality. Yet unlike other genres, there's been no wave of rediscovered metal deep cuts going viral and pulling new listeners into that era's catalogue.

    Nik's theory for why: the metal listening experience is too integrated with personal identity and community context to fragment cleanly into clip-sized viral moments. You can't separate "the thing" from the full album experience the way you can with a pop hook or a dance routine. The very qualities that make metal work on TikTok — its intensity, its technical demands, its emotional weight — require the sustained context of album listening to fully appreciate.

    This creates a fascinating double-bind: metal adapts well to short-form platforms in terms of shareable content, but doesn't leverage those platforms for the "catalog discovery" that has revived older material in other genres.

    ---

    The Consistent Artists: Architects, Bring Me, and the New Guard

    The episode examines specific bands that have maintained creative momentum across extended careers, using them as models for what sustained artistic integrity looks like in the streaming era.

    Architects receive particular attention for their career trajectory — currently on album nine or eleven, with each successive release achieving larger chart positions than the last. Nik recalls a conversation with Sam Carter where the guitarist acknowledged awareness of how many bands had "ripped off" the "Doomsday" riff, responding with equanimity: "It is what it is." This willingness to accept influence while continuing to evolve defines their approach.

    The rarity of this linear growth is emphasized: "How many bands that after nine albums or 11 albums, have that be their biggest one? That's the first number one album or the first time that they chart or the first time that they whatever after doing it for two decades. But really rare. It's very tricky for bands to be that consistent."

    Bring Me The Horizon serves as the primary example of genre evolution without compromise. Nik notes Jordan Berkeley's "orthogonal approach" — adding "more complexity, mastering, the sound's gonna become wider, everything's gonna become bigger, it's more electronic" while maintaining the band's core identity. The association with Mick Gordon (legendary composer of the Doom soundtrack, which Nik notes "is ten years later now" and remains "one of the biggest influences on modern metal") suggests a band perpetually seeking influence from unconventional sources.

    Loathe represents the COVID-era revelation — a band that released what Nik describes as a genre-fluid record blending Deftones, Meshuggah, and shoegaze sensibilities, then watched their audience grow organically over subsequent months without releasing new material. The question people kept asking: "Is this metal? Is it shoegaze?" The genre ambiguity itself became part of the appeal.

    Sleep Token, Bad Omens, and The Sweetest Boxes are mentioned as contemporary acts demonstrating similar consistency, while Nik asks about Loathe specifically, highlighting that band's trajectory as particularly instructive for understanding how genuinely excellent, genre-fluid material builds lasting audiences without explicit viral strategy.

    ---

    Genre Fluidity: From Nu Metal to the Modern Era

    The conversation contextualizes contemporary genre blending within a longer history, tracing a lineage from nu metal's deliberate mixing of hip-hop accessibility with heavy riffs through the hybrid sounds of bands like A Day to Remember.

    Nik contrasts early A Day to Remember — with their "very poppy vocals" and Jeremy McKinnon "working real hard on that" approach — against their later material. The earlier work featured "the cleanest vocals possible and the heaviest breakdown that they could get using the same tone" and was effective but "did feel quite fragmented." Their more recent output is "very flowy," demonstrating the evolution toward genre integration rather than genre collision.

    The distinction matters for understanding modern metal's TikTok moment: contemporary genre-fluid acts like Loathe or Sleep Token achieve seamless transitions between aggressive and melodic passages that feel organic rather than manufactured. Nik notes that while nu metal established the template for this approach, doing it in a way "that doesn't feel too partitioned" — maintaining coherence across stylistic shifts — represents a more sophisticated achievement enabled by modern production sensibilities.

    Who This Conversation Is For

    This episode serves listeners with several distinct but overlapping interests. Musicians and songwriters considering how to navigate short-form platforms without compromising creative integrity will find Nik's framework for the authenticity paradox particularly valuable — the insight that transparent viral-mongering undermines the very quality that makes content shareable.

    Music industry observers and journalists gain substantial analytical material for understanding how algorithmic platforms reshape creative output across a specific genre. The concrete examples (Bring Me's "Can You Feel My Heart," Knock Loose's organic traction, Architects' linear career growth) provide evidence for broader claims about sustainable artist development versus manufactured virality.

    Longtime metal fans who experienced the 2008–2010 era will appreciate the nostalgia-threaded examination of that period's sonic characteristics and creative culture, while the discussion of why metal hasn't experienced the same viral revival as other genres raises genuinely interesting questions about community, identity, and the limits of platform-driven discovery.

    Metal newcomers seeking to understand the genre's current moment will benefit from the historical grounding and the explicit articulation of what makes certain bands succeed across extended careers versus fading after momentary viral spikes.

    The conversation's ultimate argument is neither pro-TikTok nor anti-TikTok, but rather a nuanced examination of how platforms shape creative output, and a defense of sustained artistic integrity as both an aesthetic and commercial strategy. The artists who endure — Architects, Bring Me, Knock Loose, Loathe — are those who make music genuine enough to function as "a window into something outside of" the algorithm, rather than music manufactured specifically for it.
