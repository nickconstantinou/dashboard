# The Ultimate FPL End-of-Season Strategy: How FP Harry Plans His Championship Push

Understanding the High-Stakes FPL Landscape

Fantasy Premier League managers are now entering the most critical phase of the season, where chip usage and transfer timing can mean the difference between a mediocre finish and a genuine title challenge. In this detailed video, FP Harry—a prominent FPL content creator—breaks down his strategic approach to the final stretch of the season, revealing the thought process behind his wildcard timing, bench boost plans, and specific player transfer decisions. With gameweek 33 presenting a valuable double gameweek opportunity, Harry explains why he reversed his initial hesitation about playing the wildcard chip in gameweek 32, despite potentially losing points in the short term.

The video focuses extensively on Harry's Manchester City assets, particularly the uncertainty surrounding Matheus Nunes O'Reilly's fitness, and whether he should maintain or offload his City defenders during this crucial period. Harry also analyzes Leeds United's defensive options as potential transfer targets, weighing the merits of Pascal Struijk against Kristoffer Olsson for the Leeds double gameweek. His approach demonstrates how experienced FPL managers think several gameweeks ahead, using their limited transfers strategically rather than reactively.

The Wildcard Reversal: Strategic Timing Over Immediate Gains

Harry opens by explaining why he ultimately decided to play his wildcard in gameweek 32, despite previously hesitating about the move. The key factor driving his decision was simple: he possessed five free transfers heading into the gameweek, which he recognized as an ideal foundation for wildcarding. Rather than spreading those transfers across multiple gameweeks to gradually improve his team, Harry chose to use them all at once through the wildcard, effectively sacrificing his accumulated transfers to completely restructure his squad for the upcoming double gameweeks in 33 and 34.

The logic behind this approach becomes clear when Harry explains his long-term planning. By wildcarding in gameweek 32, he positions himself to bench boost in gameweek 33 and free hit in gameweek 34, then preserve his remaining transfers for gameweek 35 onwards. This creates a strategic advantage over managers who wildcarded earlier and now find themselves with few or no transfers for the season's final crucial weeks. Harry acknowledges the inherent risk in this strategy, admitting that he fully expected to lose points in gameweek 32 compared to what his pre-wildcard team might have achieved.

The Friday night before gameweek 32's deadline seemed to validate those fears, as Jarrod Bowen—who would have been Harry's single transfer—scored an impressive 14-pointer. On Saturday, additional missed opportunities from players like Thiago (13 points) and Virgil van Dijk (9 points) appeared to confirm that Harry had made a catastrophic mistake, potentially costing himself 20 to 30 points over the weekend. The situation looked particularly grim before the Manchester City versus Chelsea match on Monday night.

However, the final twist proved far less damaging than anticipated. O'Reilly delivered a 14-pointer, while other players Harry had transferred out—particularly Brennan Johnson and Eberechi Eze—either didn't play or came off the bench without returns. The final tally revealed that Harry's wildcard team lost just six points compared to what his original squad would have achieved. Harry now sits at approximately 40,000 overall rank, down from 36,000, but he considers this acceptable given the chip strategy he now has in place for the remaining gameweeks.

The Bench Boost Blueprint: Maximizing the Double Gameweek

With the wildcard executed successfully despite the minor point loss, Harry's focus shifts entirely to maximizing his bench boost opportunity in gameweek 33. He explains that this chip should allow him to not only recover those six lost points but potentially gain an additional 15 to 20 points through having a fully loaded bench of premium assets. This strategy transforms what initially appeared to be a costly mistake into a potentially profitable long-term move, assuming the bench boost performs as expected.

The bench boost strategy involves identifying which teams have double gameweeks in gameweek 33 and ensuring Harry has maximum exposure to those fixtures. By spreading quality players across his bench, he can effectively field 15 players instead of the usual 11, theoretically doubling his points potential from those bench assets. Harry's 55 points in gameweek 32—saved primarily by a 9-pointer from an unnamed player and O'Reilly's 14-pointer—demonstrates how individual performances can either justify or undermine strategic chip usage.

Transfer Strategy: Gabriel's Exit and Leeds' Opportunity

One of Harry's planned transfers for gameweek 33 involves selling Arsenal defender Gabriel to acquire a Leeds United defender instead. Harry is forthright about the fact that this transfer wouldn't make sense if he only had one, two, or three transfers available, as it essentially costs two transfers—he'd need to sell Gabriel now and buy him back later. The reasoning is straightforward: Arsenal faces Manchester City away, while Leeds has a double gameweek with fixtures against Bournemouth and what appears to be Wolves or another opponent that Harry identifies as favorable.

By swapping Gabriel for a Leeds defender, Harry can gain Leeds coverage during their double gameweek while accepting that he'll need to re-purchase Gabriel after the bench boost, likely in gameweek 35 following the double gameweeks and before the blank gameweek where he'll use his free hit chip. This represents a cost of two minus eight points (the standard -4 penalty for two transfers), but Harry calculates that the Leeds defender needs to outscore Gabriel by at least eight points across the double to justify the move.

The risk assessment here is nuanced. Harry notes that if Gabriel's Arsenal concedes goals away at Manchester City while his Leeds defender returns clean sheets or attacking points, the transfer makes mathematical sense. However, he acknowledges that this strategy only works because he has five transfers available—he doesn't need to preserve them for other urgent needs, so he can afford to be slightly more aggressive with his chip timing.

Leeds Defender Deep Dive: Struijk vs. Olsson

The Leeds United defensive situation requires careful analysis, and Harry dedicates significant attention to comparing Pascal Struijk with Kristoffer Olsson (transcribed as "Bej" in the captions). Both players present different advantages, and Harry outlines the specific conditions under which each would be the superior choice for his squad.

The first consideration involves their assist potential relative to their clean sheet contributions. Harry calculates that a defender needs at least two assists across the double gameweek to outweigh the defensive clean sheet points that Struijk might accumulate through solid defensive displays. If Leeds keep two clean sheets and Struijk earns two defensive bonus points, while Olsson records only one assist, the math doesn't favor the more attacking option. This demonstrates how FPL managers must think beyond simple attacking returns and consider the total points formula.

The second factor centers on rotation risk. Harry raises the possibility that if Leeds win their home fixture against Wolves and other results go their way, they might be nine points clear of the relegation zone with an FA Cup semi-final against Chelsea approaching on the following Sunday. In that scenario, Leeds manager might rotate key players, particularly for their away fixture at Bournemouth. Harry doesn't consider center-back rotation particularly likely, but the possibility exists and affects which defender offers the most security.

The Struijk versus Olsson comparison also involves long-term planning considerations. If Harry wants to keep his Leeds defender option through gameweek 35 when Burnley visit Elland Road, he needs to consider whether Kristoffer Olsson might face competition from the returning Ryan Rodon. Harry ultimately concludes that Pascal Struijk represents the slightly safer option because he's more likely to maintain his starting position even when Rodon returns to full fitness. Struijk's slightly higher price point also makes him marginally better for defensive clean sheet points, though the attacking threat between the two defenders is roughly comparable.

An interesting footnote Harry mentions involves the possibility of Struijk taking penalties for Leeds. While Harry believes the primary penalty taker will remain either Kalvin Phillips or another designated player, he acknowledges a small window where Struijk could end up on penalties if Phillips doesn't play the full 90 minutes every week. This kind of detailed observation exemplifies how premium FPL managers research not just expected starts but also set-piece responsibility when evaluating assets.

The O'Reilly Dilemma: Manchester City's Crown Jewel

The most complex decision Harry discusses involves Matheus Nunes O'Reilly, who remains a doubt for Manchester City's upcoming fixtures. Harry describes O'Reilly as "by far probably the second-best Manchester City player to own at the moment" and suggests he might even be a better pick than Erling Haaland himself. This assessment reflects O'Reilly's combination of minutes consistency, attacking involvement, and Manchester City's remaining fixture schedule as they chase the Premier League title.

Harry's primary recommendation is to keep O'Reilly if at all possible, even if there's uncertainty about his availability for the immediate fixture against Arsenal. He plans to wait for Pep Guardiola's press conference scheduled for Friday at 12:30 PM, which will provide approximately 24 hours before the gameweek deadline to assess the situation. If positive updates emerge or if O'Reilly appears in training, Harry intends to keep him rather than risk losing the City's best defender asset.

The reasoning behind this conservative approach involves historical precedent. Harry recalls a similar situation a couple of years ago where FPL managers debated between Ederson and another Manchester City defender, ultimately choosing the safer option. That defender then scored three goals in the final five matches, creating a massive swing in points potential for managers who had made the opposite choice. Harry sees a parallel with O'Reilly—if he sells O'Reilly for a seemingly safer option like Mateo Kovacic or another City player, he risks missing out on crucial points during the run-in.

Harry acknowledges that O'Reilly's potential absence would require him to sell the player as part of the bench boost strategy, as you cannot bench boost with injured players who won't feature. However, he's careful to note that O'Reilly's injury doesn't appear to be season-ending, so someone like Norgod is not on his radar. The comparison between Matheus Nunes and the other City options remains close, with Harry noting that while Nunes offers strong underlying baseline bonus points, other players like Bernardo Silva offer significant set-piece threat from free kicks and corners that Nunes doesn't provide.

Long-Term Planning: The Run-In and Championship Mindset

Throughout the video, Harry consistently emphasizes the importance of thinking several gameweeks ahead rather than optimizing for any single gameweek. His chip strategy—wildcard in 32, bench boost in 33, free hit in 34, preserve transfers for 35 onwards—demonstrates how experienced managers create a roadmap for the entire season's final phase rather than making reactive decisions based on immediate circumstances.

This long-term perspective also influences his player transfer decisions. Harry explicitly states that he doesn't want to be forced into making more defensive transfers in the run-in to the season, which is why he's willing to accept slightly lower point returns from Pascal Struijk compared to Kristoffer Olsson. By choosing the safer option, he preserves his transfer flexibility for later gameweeks when more critical decisions might arise.

Harry also discusses potential future transfers, particularly the plan to buy Gabriel back after the double gameweeks and possibly acquire Anthony Gordon from Newcastle. However, he notes that Newcastle's gameweek 32 performance has given him pause about that particular target. These examples illustrate how even planned transfers require constant reevaluation based on new information, and how the best FPL managers maintain multiple contingency plans rather than rigidly following an initial strategy.

Key Takeaways for FPL Managers

Chip timing can create multiplicative advantages. By understanding which gameweeks contain double fixtures and planning your wildcard, bench boost, and free hit around those opportunities, you can effectively field 15+ players during crucial double gameweeks. This isn't just about the individual chip performance but about creating cascading advantages across multiple gameweeks.

Short-term point losses can be worth long-term strategic gains. Harry accepted losing six points in gameweek 32 because it positioned him for the optimal chip sequence across gameweeks 33-35. The key question isn't whether you lose points this week, but whether your decision improves your expected points across the entire remaining schedule.

Injury management requires balancing risk and reward. The O'Reilly situation demonstrates how selling a potentially injured premium asset carries hidden costs beyond the immediate points lost. If O'Reilly misses only one fixture and then returns to full fitness during City's double gameweeks, managers who sold him miss significant value and face additional transfer costs to reacquire him.

Team context matters as much as individual fixtures. Harry's analysis of Leeds' potential rotation based on their FA Cup commitments and league position shows how broader team dynamics affect player availability and point potential. Understanding managerial incentives and fixture congestion helps predict which assets face rotation risk.

Limited transfers require strategic patience. Having five free transfers allowed Harry to execute a more aggressive transfer strategy than he would have attempted with fewer. Managers with limited transfers should prioritize preserving flexibility over maximizing immediate returns, while those with abundant transfers can afford calculated two-transfer moves that have longer-term payoffs.
