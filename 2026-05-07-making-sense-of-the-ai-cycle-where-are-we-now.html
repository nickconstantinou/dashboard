    Where the AI Money Flows: Making Sense of the Investment Cycle

    Introduction

    If you had invested in SanDisk stock one year ago, you would have watched your money multiply by 40 times. Micron delivered 8x returns. Intel and AMD both surged nearly four-fold. These aren't penny stocks or speculative bets—they're large-cap semiconductor and infrastructure companies now at the center of what may be the most significant capital reallocation in technology history.

    The conventional wisdom held that AI was a bubble—that the hundreds of billions being spent by the world's largest companies would never translate into sustainable revenue. The Q1 2026 earnings reports from four of the largest hyperscalers—Amazon, Meta, Microsoft, and Google—have obliterated that narrative. These companies proved not just that their AI spending generates returns, but that they were understating their commitments. The original 2026 CapEx projection of $630 billion has already been revised upward to $800 billion, with analysts projecting $1.1 trillion in spending the following year.

    This represents a fundamental shift in how investors should think about the AI investment cycle. The money isn't disappearing into speculation—it's flowing like water through a defined stack, from the applications users interact with, down through cloud infrastructure, into semiconductors, and further still into the components that make semiconductors function. Understanding where you are in that flow matters enormously for where you put your capital to work.

    Brad Gerstner, founder of Altimeter Capital and one of the technology industry's most prominent thought leaders, recently appeared on CNBC to make the case that "this time is different." He wasn't speaking abstractly. He was pointing to the concrete reality of $800 billion being deployed this year and $1 trillion-plus next year, money that's generating genuine revenue streams at companies like Anthropic and OpenAI, where ARR is growing by billions of dollars per week.

    The Architecture of the AI Stack

    Layer Zero: Where It All Starts

    The AI economy begins with the foundation that most public market investors cannot access directly: OpenAI and Anthropic. These two private companies—housing the ChatGPT and Claude models that billions of people use daily—funnel virtually all retail AI usage through their interfaces. They have paying enterprise customers, they have paying consumers, and they are the fountainhead from which the entire waterfall of capital expenditure begins its descent.

    This is a critical structural point: the companies generating the most visible AI revenue are almost entirely private. OpenAI and Anthropic aren't trading on any public exchange. They sit above everything, collecting the money that eventually filters down through every other layer of the stack.

    Layer One: The Hyperscalers

    From those foundation models, money flows into what the analysis calls the "layer one" of the AI stack—the hyperscalers and cloud platforms that provide the compute infrastructure and distribution layer. This includes Google, which operates both as a model lab (with its TPUs) and as a cloud provider (Google Cloud), Amazon with AWS, Microsoft with Azure, and Meta with its social platform infrastructure.

    These companies are the critical intermediaries. They take the revenue generated from AI usage and convert it into the massive capital expenditure programs that define this era. Google's recent earnings demonstrated this perfectly: the company is now the most valuable in the world, having passed Nvidia, and its cloud infrastructure business is the primary vehicle through which AI models are distributed to enterprise customers, governments, and individual users.

    The profit margins at these hyperscalers are expanding as AI adoption accelerates. Every dollar that flows into Google Cloud or AWS or Azure represents a margin-preserving operation for the parent company, and the hyperscalers are in an exceptional position because they control both the infrastructure and, in some cases, the models running on it.

    Layer Two: The Semiconductor Inflection

    The next layer down is where the story becomes particularly interesting for investors who missed the initial run-up in foundation model companies. Layer two encompasses the GPU and semiconductor companies—and here is where the traditional "picks and shovels" narrative begins to break down in instructive ways.

    Nvidia sits at the top of this layer as the world's second-most-valuable company, with a market capitalization approaching $4 trillion. For the first phase of the AI buildout—from zero to one—Nvidia was indispensable. Companies needed GPUs to spin up AI capabilities from scratch. Jensen Huang's company provided the critical infrastructure.

    But the analysis suggests Nvidia's position is now largely "baked in." The extraordinary run-up in the stock reflects its essential role in the early AI buildout, and there's a recognition that money is now cycling further out on the risk curve—beyond the most obvious picks and shovels into the layers beneath them.

    The CPU Revolution: The Unexpected Winner

    Why the Paradigm Shifted

    This is where the episode delivers its most counterintuitive insight. The conversation centers on the rise of agentic AI—AI systems that don't require human prompting to operate. Think of an AI that can autonomously browse the web, execute code, interact with other AI systems, and complete complex multi-step tasks without being told what to do at each step. This is the paradigm that OpenAI and Anthropic are pushing toward with products like OpenClaw and Claude Code.

    Here is the critical insight: agentic AI systems require something that pure LLM-based chatbots didn't—CPUs. Not just as supporting infrastructure, but as the orchestrating brain that manages GPU-based inference workloads.

    The historical context makes this striking. When GPT-4.0 was trained and deployed, the CPU-to-GPU ratio was "almost zero"—CPUs barely factored into the equation at all. You typed a query into an interface, and GPUs handled the computation. Fast forward to today, and that ratio has flipped to nearly one-to-one. Within the next six months, according to the analysis, CPUs will *outnumber* GPUs in AI infrastructure deployments.

    Jensen Huang himself addressed this at a recent conference, noting that GPU consumption is "going through the roof" due to the rise of agentic AI. But the nuance is that the actual limiting factor—and therefore the area of greatest demand—is the CPU infrastructure needed to orchestrate the increasingly complex AI agent workflows.

    Why CPUs Are Now Critical

    The mechanism is this: when AI agents operate autonomously, they need to coordinate with other AI agents, access tools, and execute actions faster than humans could perform them manually. The orchestration layer that manages these interactions requires sophisticated CPU-based computing. When you use a tool like Claude Code and notice its ability to rapidly interact with browsers and run compilers, that capability comes from access to robust CPU infrastructure.

    This is why AMD stock surged 17% in a single day following its earnings report, why it has climbed 320% over the past year, and why Intel—which operates heavily in the CPU space—has nearly quintupled in value. Intel, in particular, has found itself positioned at the center of an industry it spent decades building the foundations for.

    The Memory Layer: 50% of the Cost

    The analysis also highlights memory as a critical component at this layer of the stack. Memory comprises approximately 50% of the bill of materials cost for a GPU. This means that for every dollar spent on GPU infrastructure, a proportionally large sum flows into memory companies like Micron and SanDisk—hence their extraordinary recent performance.

    The Round Hill Memory ETF, which launched just over a month ago, offers investors a way to access this memory theme as a basket rather than picking individual stocks. It has returned 72% in that short timeframe, reflecting the enormous demand for memory that the AI buildout is generating.

    The Government Play: Strategic Positioning

    One of the episode's most striking examples involves the US government's investment in Intel. Approximately 18 months ago, the federal government acquired a 10% stake in Intel at $20.47 per share. The strategic reasoning was straightforward: the US had become too dependent on TSMC, a company based in Taiwan that China has expressed interest in controlling. Bringing semiconductor manufacturing capabilities on shore—specifically through Intel, which has been working on both CPUs and frontier GPUs—represented a matter of national security.

    Today, Intel trades above $111. The government is sitting on approximately a 5X return on that investment. Whatever your views on industrial policy, the trade worked precisely because the structural demand for domestic semiconductor capability aligned with the AI infrastructure buildout.

    The Flywheel and Why "This Time Is Different"

    Brad Gerstner's argument that "this time is different" rests on a specific mechanism: the flywheel of AI utility. So long as AI continues to be useful—and so long as new, more valuable use cases continue to emerge—the amount enterprises and consumers will pay for AI capabilities will continue to increase. That increased spending flows back into capital expenditure, which funds more infrastructure, which enables more capable AI, which unlocks more use cases.

    The evidence for this flywheel is visible in the earnings reports. The hyperscalers spent hundreds of billions of dollars, and it resulted in expanding profit margins and growing revenue—not because they were burning cash on speculation, but because the AI services they're selling are generating genuine value for customers.

    The paradigm shifts the analysis identifies reinforce this trajectory. We moved from simple LLMs (chatbots, one-query responses) to reasoning and chain-of-thought systems (more tokens required per query) to agentic AI (autonomous multi-step operations). Each paradigm shift has increased token consumption dramatically. And token consumption is the fundamental driver of compute demand across every layer of the stack.

    Detailed Takeaways

    1. Follow the CapEx waterfall, not the headline companies. The most important thing isn't whether Nvidia goes up or down—it's understanding where the $800B-to-$1T in annual hyperscaler spending is flowing at any given moment. Right now, it's flowing beyond the most obvious GPU plays into the CPU and memory layers that support the agentic AI paradigm shift. The money is cycling outward on the risk curve, and smart investors position for where it will flow, not where it has already flowed.

    2. The CPU narrative is in its early innings despite extraordinary recent gains. Intel and AMD have already delivered 4X and 3.2X returns respectively, yet the underlying structural shift toward CPU-dependent agentic AI is just beginning. The ratio of CPUs to GPUs in AI infrastructure is still transitioning, and the expectation that CPUs will soon *outnumber* GPUs represents a multi-year demand tailwind for companies in this space. This isn't a trade—it's a structural reorientation of what AI infrastructure requires.

    3. Memory is the most underappreciated component layer. With memory comprising 50% of GPU bill of materials, any analysis of AI infrastructure spending must account for the memory companies that disproportionately benefit from every GPU purchase. SanDisk's 40X return and Micron's 7.25X return reflect this reality, and the existence of a dedicated memory ETF (Round Hill, up 72% in a month) confirms this as a recognized theme—even if retail investors are still catching on.

    4. Private company exposure requires creative approaches. OpenAI and Anthropic sit at layer zero and generate enormous revenue, but they're inaccessible to public market investors. The entire point of the AI investment stack is that money flows downward from these private companies into accessible public equities at the hyperscaler, semiconductor, and component layers. Understanding which public companies benefit most from which layers of the stack is the practical work of AI investing.

    5. Paradigm shifts create the asymmetric opportunities. The analysis explicitly traces three paradigm shifts (LLMs → reasoning → agentic AI), noting that each one changed the infrastructure requirements. The investors who made the most money in AI didn't just buy "AI" broadly—they understood which specific paradigm was emerging and positioned accordingly. The agentic AI turn means the opportunity now lies in the orchestration and CPU layers, not in the foundational GPU layer where the previous paradigm concentrated value.

    Who This Is For

    This analysis is most valuable for investors—whether institutional or individual—who are trying to understand where they stand in the AI investment cycle and what the next phase of value creation looks like. If you've been watching the extraordinary returns in AI stocks and wondering whether you've missed the boat, the specific answer the episode provides is: you've missed the first phase, but the structural drivers of the second phase are even larger and less understood by the public.

    It's also valuable for technology professionals and business leaders who want to understand the infrastructure dynamics driving AI capabilities. Understanding that agentic AI fundamentally changes CPU requirements, or that memory represents a 50% cost component of the most important AI hardware, provides concrete insights into supply chain vulnerabilities, investment opportunities, and strategic decisions.

    Finally, for anyone who heard "AI bubble" and accepted that framing uncritically, the data here provides a complete rebuttal. The Q1 2026 earnings from the hyperscalers demonstrate that hundreds of billions in CapEx translate into measurable profit expansion. That's not a bubble—that's an industry building out infrastructure at a pace that hasn't been seen since the early internet era, with correspondingly large opportunities for those who understand the architecture.
