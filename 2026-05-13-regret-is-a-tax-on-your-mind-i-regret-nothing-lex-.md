## Introduction

This exchange emerges from a Lex Fridman Podcast conversation with contributors to open-source projects, particularly those associated with FFmpeg and VLC media players. The discussion takes a philosophical turn when the topic shifts to regret and public expression, with the speaker articulating a clear stance: regret is not merely unhelpful—it is actively harmful to one's mental state.

The context matters here. This is someone who has operated in the public eye of the open-source community, where code, decisions, and statements are permanently archived and subject to retrospective judgment. Their "no regrets" philosophy isn't naive denialism; it's a pragmatic response to the reality that past actions cannot be undone, only built upon or corrected. The French phrase "Je ne regrette rien"—I regret nothing—serves as a philosophical anchor, grounding the discussion in a cultural reference that emphasizes intentionality over perfection.

What makes this segment notable is its brevity paired with depth. In just a few minutes of conversation, a coherent framework emerges for processing mistakes without dwelling on them, one that connects to broader discussions about emotional economics and cognitive burden.

---

## The Philosophy of Not Regretting

The speaker's core argument rests on a simple but powerful distinction: learning from mistakes and regretting them are not the same cognitive process. One is forward-looking and constructive; the other is backward-looking and draining. "Regrets are attacks on your mind," they state directly, framing the emotional toll not as a natural consequence of past errors but as a self-inflicted wound. This framing matters because it places agency squarely with the individual—if regret is an attack, then it's something you can choose to stop, not a predetermined consequence of making mistakes.

The reasoning is straightforward but worth examining: since no one possesses a time machine, regretting past actions serves no functional purpose. The past cannot be rewritten; the only variables in your control are present and future behavior. Regret, therefore, represents wasted cognitive resources—mental energy spent on something that cannot change, detracting from energy that could be directed toward correction, growth, or simply living well.

---

## The Economic Perspective on Emotion

The conversation connects to a broader framework when Johnny Depp's observation is introduced: "hate is a very expensive emotion." This parallel illuminates the speaker's philosophy more fully. The idea is that negative emotions—regret, resentment, hatred—carry hidden costs. These aren't just metaphorical costs; they're neurological and physiological ones. Cortisol production, sleep disruption, impaired decision-making, strained relationships—all the downstream effects of dwelling on what cannot be changed.

By this logic, choosing not to regret isn't just an abstract philosophical position; it's an optimization strategy. It conserves mental resources for what actually matters: making better decisions going forward, maintaining healthier relationships, and preserving cognitive bandwidth for creation rather than rumination. The "tax on your mind" metaphor captures this precisely—regret extracts a continuous cost that compounds over time unless actively resisted.

## Applied in Context: Open-Source Community and Public Expression

The philosophy gains concrete meaning when applied to the speaker's specific context. Working on open-source projects like FFmpeg and VLC means your work, your words, and your decisions are public and permanent. Code commits don't disappear; tweets remain; past positions can be scrutinized. Someone operating in this environment who carried regret would be systematically accumulating weight with every commit, every decision, every public statement.

The mention of "spicy tweets" as something to be grateful for suggests the speaker has made controversial or provocative statements in public forums and explicitly rejects the urge to regret them afterward. This isn't about being reckless or dismissive of consequences—it's about recognizing that the alternative, dwelling on past public expressions, provides no benefit while consuming mental energy. The "sexy hat" moment, where the speaker feels like "a wizard" and "special," suggests this conversation occurs in a context of genuine appreciation and celebration, reinforcing that operating without regret correlates with enjoying one's work and community.

## Key Takeaways

**Regret and learning are distinct processes.** The critical insight here is that dwelling on past mistakes and extracting lessons from them occupy different mental modes. One is constructive and forward-directed; the other is destructive and backward-directed. You can honor the lesson without honoring the regret—indeed, honoring the regret actively undermines the lesson by draining the energy you need to implement it.

**The time machine test is clarifying.** If you could go back and change the past, regret might serve a purpose—it could motivate the journey to change it. Since you cannot, regret becomes pure liability. This test isn't about denying responsibility; it's about accepting reality so completely that you stop wasting energy on illusions.

**Emotions have costs, not just meanings.** The parallel to hate being "expensive" suggests treating negative emotions as resource drains rather than inevitable burdens. This reframing invites active management of emotional states, not through suppression but through conscious allocation—deciding what your attention is worth and refusing to let unproductive emotions claim it.

**Public-facing work requires psychological resilience.** The open-source context illustrates a broader principle: anyone whose work is visible and lasting will face retrospective judgment. The ability to "not regret" becomes a survival skill, a way of staying productive and present rather than paralyzed by past decisions that cannot be undone.

## Who This Is For

This discussion resonates most for people who find themselves repeatedly reviewing past decisions—whether in creative work, business, personal relationships, or public-facing roles—and feeling the weight of those reflections. It's particularly valuable for those in communities where contributions are permanently archived and subject to ongoing scrutiny, such as open-source developers, public intellectuals, content creators, or anyone who has published ideas that have later been reconsidered or criticized.

The practical value is clear: it offers a conceptual framework for handling the inevitable moments when you look backward and wish you'd done differently. Rather than treating regret as a natural, inevitable response to past mistakes, it frames regret as a choice—and one with a cost that often outweighs its benefits. For anyone seeking to maintain creative momentum, psychological wellbeing, or simply a clearer mind, this philosophy provides both permission and method to let go of what cannot be changed while remaining committed to learning and growth.
