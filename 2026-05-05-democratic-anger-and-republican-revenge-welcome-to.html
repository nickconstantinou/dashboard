# Democratic Anger and Republican Revenge: Welcome to the Primaries

Key Insights

  • Trump's Indiana vengeance campaign targets 7 Republican state senators who refused to support his redistricting effort to carve up two Democratic seats — he's spending millions on these down-ballot races (which normally cost $150,000-$200,000) to send a message to any future Republican who might resist his demands.
  • Thomas Massie votes with Trump 91% of the time but faces a Trump-backed primary challenger (former Navy SEAL Ed Gowerine) for opposing him on the "big beautiful bill," Epstein files, and Iran war — revealing that loyalty to Trump now requires near-total compliance even on major policy disputes.
  • The Senate has historically been the "last holdout" on unconditional Trump loyalty, which is why taking out Bill Cassidy (who voted to convict Trump in the 2021 impeachment trial) would signal the complete rightward consolidation of the institution.
  • Historical context: 18 out of the past 20 midterm elections saw the incumbent president's party lose seats, and current polling shows voters feel worse about housing, groceries, and gas than when they elected the Trump administration — suggesting Republicans face "levels of badness" in November.
  • The redistricting test case: After the Supreme Court weakened the Voting Rights Act, Trump's purge of dissenting Indiana Republicans effectively signals to other state legislatures that resistance means political extinction — likely making this year's midterm elections structurally harder for Democrats.

    Introduction

    On May 5, 2026, The Daily podcast from The New York Times launched the first midterm election roundtable of the season, bringing together reporters Shane Goldmacher, Lisa Lerer, and Reid Epstein to analyze what promises to be one of the most consequential primary seasons in recent memory. The conversation arrived at a critical inflection point: over the next several weeks, both Democratic and Republican voters would begin selecting nominees in races up and down the ballot, and those contests would reveal much about where each party stands heading into the second half of what the hosts described as "the second Trump presidency."

    The timing matters enormously. With less than six months until the midterm elections, Republican strategists privately acknowledge that the question for their party is not whether these elections will go badly, but how badly. President Trump's approval ratings remain deeply underwater, his signature policy initiatives face public resistance, and economic indicators—particularly on the cost of living, housing, and everyday goods—show voters feeling worse, not better, about their financial circumstances. Yet rather than focusing on reversing these trends, the reporters explained, Trump has chosen to launch what amounts to a "revenge tour," using his political operation to target Republicans who have crossed him, recruit loyalists, and enforce a new standard of fealty from state legislative chambers all the way up to the United States Senate.

    The conversation traced this dynamic through three key primary battles in Indiana, Kentucky, and Louisiana, revealing how Trump's approach represents both a test of his continued dominance over the Republican Party and a potential distraction from the more pressing political challenges facing the GOP as November approaches.

    ---

    The Indiana Revenge: Targeting State Senators Who Said No

    The episode began with what Shane Goldmacher described as an "unusual level of campaign for a president to be involved in." The focus was not on congressional primaries or U.S. Senate races but on more than a half-dozen Indiana state senators whom Donald Trump is actively trying to unseat. The reason for Trump's opposition: these seven Republican state senators refused to go along with his effort to redraw the state's legislative maps last fall in ways that would have carved up two Democratic seats, effectively deleting them from the electoral map.

    Under normal circumstances, Republican-controlled state legislatures have enthusiastically performed this redistricting function for Trump—Texas, Missouri, and North Carolina all complied without significant resistance. Indiana, however, represented "a rare moment where Republicans out in the world said no to President Trump," as Lisa Lerer explained. The Republican leaders in the Indiana state senate objected on both political and moral grounds, determining that the redistricting scheme was neither good policy nor good politics, and they refused to be seen as taking orders from Donald Trump in Washington about how to draw Indiana's district lines.

    Trump responded with characteristic swiftness and hostility. He declared publicly that "everyone who opposed this needs to be voted out" and set about recruiting, funding, and running advertisements against all seven dissident Republicans. His political operation and allied groups have dumped millions of dollars into these races—extraordinary sums for state legislative contests that normally cost between $150,000 and $200,000. In one particularly notable example, one of the challengers was endorsed by Trump before even announcing his campaign, then withdrew after a few weeks of second thoughts, flew to Washington for a meeting with Trump, took a photograph with him behind the Resolute desk in the Oval Office, and then jumped back into the race.

    The reporters explored one targeted incumbent, state Senator Travis Holdman, who articulated the paradox at the heart of Trump's Indiana campaign: while millions are being spent to punish Republicans who resisted the president, not nearly enough resources are being directed toward defeating the one genuinely vulnerable Democrat in Indiana who is, as Holdman put it, "on his way to skating toward reelection." This represents a stark misallocation of Republican political resources—spending to settle scores rather than to expand the party's majority.

    What makes Indiana truly significant, the reporters argued, extends far beyond those seven state Senate seats. The implications reach directly into the battle for control of Congress itself. The Supreme Court had recently issued a decision weakening the Voting Rights Act, unleashing a new wave of redistricting battles across the country. Trump's purge of dissenting Indiana Republicans sends an unmistakable signal to future state lawmakers in any state where the president wants to redraw maps: if you resist, you will be destroyed politically. Republican legislators in every state observing what happened in Indiana are now far more likely to comply with Trump's redistricting demands, creating a structural advantage for Republicans in upcoming elections that did not exist before.

    The Kentucky Test: Thomas Massie and the Cost of Dissent

    The conversation then shifted to Kentucky, where a primary race in just a few weeks pits incumbent Republican Congressman Thomas Massie against a Trump-backed challenger named Ed Gowerine, a former Navy SEAL whom the president personally recruited to run against the congressman.

    Massie represents what the reporters identified as a genuinely unusual figure in today's Republican Party: "a very conservative Republican" who nonetheless "has opposed Trump on things like the big beautiful bill, on the Epstein files, now in the Iran war." He votes with his party approximately 91 percent of the time, but that near-unanimity is not sufficient for a president who demands total loyalty.

    Trump's public campaign against Massie has been "very unsparing," as Reid Epstein described it. The president traveled to Kentucky specifically to campaign against the incumbent, calling him "a disaster for our party," mocking his Ivy League education ("He went to a good college, but I know a lot of stupid people that went to a good college"), and labeling him a "loser" and a "disaster" repeatedly on social media. This represents a remarkable expenditure of presidential attention and political capital against a member of his own party who agrees with him on the vast majority of issues.

    When the reporters spoke with Massie, he explained his philosophy clearly: the deviation from party loyalty comes when Republicans are "blowing our budget," "protecting pedophiles," or "starting wars overseas." He drew particular attention to the Epstein files controversy and his positions on Iran. Massie has made clear that he views his race as more than a personal contest—it is, in his view, an opportunity to demonstrate that space exists within the Republican Party for elected officials to criticize the president when he errs. He wants to be "the guy holding that umbrella up in the air," maintaining the legitimacy of dissent within the GOP.

    Yet the historical record offers Massie little encouragement. As the reporters noted, the predecessors who have made the most hay from criticizing Trump within Congress—Liz Cheney, Adam Kinzinger, and others—have consistently found that "those folks tend not to have long careers in Congress." Marjorie Taylor Greene, by contrast, generates enormous cable television attention but ultimately faces electoral consequences. The pattern is clear: dissent from Trump, even from a conservative who votes with the party 91 percent of the time, carries severe political risks.

    The Kentucky race thus becomes something more than a test of Massie's personal political survival. It represents a referendum on whether the Republican Party will continue to side with Trump even when its members quietly disagree with him, and whether any space remains for principled conservative opposition within the caucus.

    The Louisiana Riddle: Can Trump Punish a Senator Who Tried to Atone?

    The final Republican case study involved Louisiana's Senate race, where incumbent Senator Bill Cassidy faces a primary challenge from Congresswoman Julia Letlow, backed by the president whom Cassidy once crossed.

    Cassidy's "original sin," as Shane Goldmacher described it, was voting to convict Donald Trump of impeachment following the January 6th Capitol riot in 2021. Since that moment, Cassidy has undertaken extensive efforts to atone for his apostasy, drawing closer to Trump in advance of his reelection bid. He is a physician by training and has at times opposed figures like Robert F. Kennedy Jr. and his vaccine skepticism, but ultimately voted to confirm Kennedy as Secretary of Health and Human Services. Despite these gestures toward reconciliation, the original sin of voting to convict the president has not been forgotten or forgiven.

    Trump's response has been to endorse Letlow, a congresswoman, as a primary challenger to Cassidy. Yet notably, Cassidy has not run as an anti-Trump Republican. He continues to tout his credentials and highlight what he has done to advance the president's agenda in the Senate. Louisiana thus presents a somewhat different test than Indiana or Kentucky: it is not a case of a Republican openly opposing Trump, but rather one of a senator who once dissented and now seeks to make amends.

    The reporters identified this race as significant for what it reveals about institutional dynamics. The Senate has historically been "the last holdout when it comes to unconditional loyalty to Trump as an institution." While the Senate has backed much of Trump's agenda, it has also at times counseled him to drop nominees and put up occasional resistance—behavior that stands in sharp contrast to the House, which has moved very far to the right. If Trump successfully takes out Cassidy, it would signal a decisive step toward the complete rightward consolidation of the Senate, matching what has already occurred in the House of Representatives.

    The reporters traced the evolution of the Senate's relationship with Trump from his first election, when Mitch McConnell was "outspokenly critical" of the new president, to the present day, describing the shift as "pretty big." The Cassidy race may determine whether the Senate maintains any independent identity or becomes simply another instrument of Trumpian loyalty enforcement.

    The Strategic Calculations and Risks

    Throughout the conversation, the reporters grappled with the apparent absence of direct political risk for Trump in these interventions. His picks in Republican primaries generally perform well, and these are red-state contests where the winner of the primary almost certainly goes on to win the general election without meaningful Democratic opposition. What, then, is the downside?

    Reid Epstein articulated the core risk: with less than six months until a midterm widely expected to be "very competitive" for Republicans, attention and resources are being directed toward extracting "pounds of flesh" from intra-party disputes rather than toward the issues "top of mind for voters"—cost of living concerns, the trajectory of the Iran war, and other matters that will determine whether swing voters reward or punish the GOP in November.

    Lisa Lerer added the specific observation that Trump's involvement in these races is also a distraction from competitive general election contests. The president is not spending his political capital talking about defeating vulnerable Democratic incumbents like Georgia Senator John Ossoff, who faces his own reelection fight. Instead, he is expending his influence on races that are mostly safe Republican seats, consolidating control over the party apparatus rather than expanding the party's reach.

    Yet the reporters acknowledged that Trump's decade-long record in Republican primaries has been "pretty darn strong," making his interventions genuinely dangerous for any Republican who might face them. The question is not whether Trump can win these individual battles, but whether winning these battles comes at the cost of losing the larger war against an electorate that, by almost all indications, is increasingly unhappy with the direction of the country under his administration.

    The Democratic Side: Parallel Dynamics

    While the bulk of the conversation focused on the Republican revenge tour, the hosts noted that the Democratic primaries represent a mirror image of sorts. Democratic voters are being asked to signal their appetite for change versus continuity, while Republican voters are being asked to demonstrate their appetite for loyalty versus dissent. The parallel story of Democratic primaries will unfold across the same period, testing whether the party's base seeks more radical change or prefers a more cautious approach to the Trump era.

    Detailed Takeaways

    Takeaway 1: Trump's Loyalty Test Has No Safe Havens, Even Among 90%+ Supporters

    The Thomas Massie example reveals that Trump's definition of acceptable Republican behavior requires near-total compliance. Massie agrees with Trump on the vast majority of policy questions, regularly votes with the administration, and has not led any anti-Trump rebellions—yet he faces a primary challenger backed by the full weight of presidential endorsement and personal campaigning. This suggests that Trump is not merely testing for policy agreement but demanding a specific posture of deference and public support that extends to issues even friendly Republicans might question. Any Republican who has publicly criticized Trump on any significant matter should understand that the president's political operation will eventually seek their removal.

    Takeaway 2: Redistricting Resistance Has Become Politically Unsustainable

    The Indiana case demonstrates that Trump's willingness to spend millions of dollars and deploy his political operation against state legislators who resist his redistricting demands effectively ends the possibility of bipartisan or anti-Trump Republicans in state legislatures serving as a check on aggressive gerrymandering. Every Republican observing what happened to the seven who said no understands that resistance means political extinction. This creates a ratchet effect where future redistricting battles will proceed with far less internal Republican opposition, making midterm elections structurally more difficult for Democrats in any state where Trump wants to redraw maps.

    Takeaway 3: The Senate's Institutional Independence Is on the Ballot in Louisiana

    Bill Cassidy's race matters not because he is an outspoken Trump critic but because he represents the last category of Republican senators who retain any independent standing: those who once dissented and now seek to rehabilitate themselves within the party. If Trump can successfully topple a senator who has spent years making amends and voting with the administration on key issues, it signals that no past deviation will ever be forgiven and that the Senate's institutional resistance to Trumpian demands will eventually be eliminated entirely through electoral removal. This would complete the transformation of both chambers into purely Trump-loyal institutions.

    Takeaway 4: The Revenge Tour Represents a Strategic Distraction with Electoral Consequences

    With six months until November, Republican strategists are observing that the president is spending his political capital on personal grievances rather than on the economic and foreign policy issues that will determine competitive races. Voters who care about the cost of groceries, housing affordability, and the Iran conflict are not being addressed by a president focused on taking out seven state senators in Indiana or campaigning against a congressman who votes with him 91 percent of the time. The strategic risk is that Trump's primary victory discipline—whipping up the base through loyalty enforcement—may be poorly suited to a midterm environment where the party needs to expand its coalition beyond the most devoted Trump supporters.

    Takeaway 5: Primary Season Is a Diagnostic Tool for Party Health

    The hosts framed these primary contests as "telling us a bigger story about where the two parties are" as they seek to control the country's direction. The Republican story is one of consolidation and punishment—the test of whether Trump can enforce complete loyalty and eliminate even minor dissent. The Democratic story will be revealed in the coming weeks as well. These races do not merely select nominees; they reveal what each party believes it means to be a member in good standing, what behaviors will be rewarded, and what values will guide the party's direction in the second half of the Trump presidency. Watching how these loyalty tests resolve will provide crucial intelligence about which party is more unified, more disciplined, and better positioned for the general election fight ahead.
