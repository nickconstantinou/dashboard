System Design: The Five Essential Questions Every Developer Must Ask About Performance

Introduction

In the world of software engineering, system design remains one of the most challenging and consequential disciplines to master. Whether you're building a small application or architecting a massive distributed system that serves millions of users, the decisions you make about performance can make or break your product. Performance isn't merely a technical concern—it's a business imperative that directly impacts user experience, operational costs, and ultimately, the success of your application.

In this comprehensive guide, content creator and software engineer Atesh breaks down the complex topic of system design performance into five actionable questions that every developer should ask when designing or optimizing a system. Drawing from his experience setting up database monitoring for production applications, Atesh provides a framework that balances theoretical knowledge with practical, real-world considerations. This video serves as a logical continuation of his previous content on scalability, now diving deeper into the nuances of performance optimization.

The core philosophy underlying this discussion is that system design is fundamentally about trade-offs. There's no perfect solution—every choice comes with advantages and disadvantages, and understanding these compromises is what separates junior developers from senior engineers. Atesh emphasizes that performance optimization isn't about making everything fast; it's about making the right things fast while accepting calculated trade-offs elsewhere.

Understanding the Five Critical Questions

Before diving into specific technical concepts, Atesh establishes a clear framework of five questions that should guide every performance-related decision in system design. These questions serve as a mental checklist that developers can apply to any system, whether they're building from scratch or optimizing an existing application.

The first question asks whether the system is latency sensitive. Latency refers to the time it takes for a single request to be processed—from the moment a user makes a request until they receive a response. Some applications require extremely low latency; consider a real-time gaming platform where every millisecond matters, or a stock trading application where delays could result in financial losses. However, as Atesh notes, not all systems are equally latency-sensitive, and over-optimizing for latency in scenarios where it doesn't matter can lead to unnecessary complexity and resource expenditure.

The second question concerns throughput sensitivity. Throughput measures how many requests or operations a system can handle per unit of time—typically expressed as requests per second (RPS) or queries per second (QPS). While related to latency, throughput is a distinct concept that often involves trade-offs with latency. A system might be optimized for high throughput but operate with higher per-request latency, or vice versa.

The third question—can I cache this?—addresses one of the most powerful performance optimization techniques available. Caching involves storing frequently accessed data in a location that allows for faster retrieval than the original source. As Atesh emphasizes, approximately 80% of performance improvements in typical applications come from effective caching strategies.

The fourth question asks about indexing, particularly in the context of database performance. Proper indexing can dramatically speed up query execution, but as Atesh cautions, over-indexing can also introduce performance problems. Finding the right balance requires understanding the specific access patterns of your data.

The fifth and final question addresses whether users need to wait for a particular operation to complete. This question helps determine whether operations can be handled asynchronously, which can significantly improve perceived performance and system responsiveness. Operations that don't require immediate user feedback—such as sending confirmation emails or processing analytics—can be queued and handled in the background.

Latency vs. Throughput: A Crucial Distinction

One of the most valuable contributions of this video is its clear explanation of the difference between latency and throughput—two concepts that are often confused but have fundamentally different implications for system design. Atesh uses a memorable highway analogy that makes these abstract concepts concrete and easy to understand.

Imagine an empty highway where you're driving at 100 km/h with no other cars in sight. This scenario represents low latency—the road is clear, and you can reach your destination quickly. The highway represents your system's infrastructure, and the speed at which a single car (request) travels represents latency. In this analogy, the empty highway with fast-moving traffic is akin to a system optimized for minimal delay on individual requests.

Now imagine a different highway where the speed limit is only 40 km/h, but this highway can accommodate 10,000 cars per hour. This represents high throughput—many more requests can pass through the system per unit of time, even though individual requests take longer. The cars move more slowly, but the sheer volume of traffic is much higher.

This distinction becomes critical when making architectural decisions. Atesh illustrates this with a compelling example using Zomato, a popular food delivery platform. Consider two options for handling food order requests: Option one achieves 50 milliseconds latency but can only handle 1,000 queries per second. Option two provides 200 milliseconds latency but can handle 50,000 queries per second. At first glance, the first option seems superior—faster response times are always better, aren't they?

Not necessarily. Atesh points out that the first option would crash during peak lunchtimes when demand spikes. The second option, while slightly slower, would remain stable under heavy load. This is the essence of system design thinking: you must understand your bottleneck and choose the trade-off that best suits your specific requirements. For an e-commerce platform like Zomato, system stability during high-traffic periods is often more valuable than marginally faster response times.

This framework applies across many domains. Real-time gaming applications might prioritize extremely low latency even if it means handling fewer concurrent players. High-volume data processing systems might accept higher per-operation latency to maximize throughput. Social media platforms often need to balance both while optimizing for the most common user actions.

The Power of Caching

Caching represents perhaps the most impactful performance optimization technique available to developers, and Atesh dedicates significant attention to explaining its importance and application. The fundamental principle is simple: instead of regenerating the same data or computing the same result repeatedly, store the result once and serve it quickly on subsequent requests.

Atesh uses a McDonald's restaurant as an analogy that makes this concept immediately relatable. When you walk into McDonald's, they don't start making your burger from scratch after you order. Instead, they prepare burgers continuously throughout the day, keeping them warm and ready. When a customer arrives, the burger is handed over immediately. This is caching in action—pre-computing and storing results (prepared burgers) so they're available instantly when needed (when customers order).

The effectiveness of caching is backed by a well-known principle in IT: the 80/20 rule. In most applications, approximately 80% of requests are served by approximately 20% of the data. This means that a relatively small set of data or computations is responsible for the majority of your system's workload. By identifying and caching this frequently accessed data, you can dramatically reduce the load on your backend systems while improving response times for the majority of your users.

However, caching isn't without its challenges. Cache invalidation—ensuring that cached data remains consistent with the source of truth—remains one of the hardest problems in computer science. Stale cached data can lead to incorrect results, while aggressive cache invalidation can negate the performance benefits. Additionally, caching consumes memory resources, so you must balance cache size against the memory available in your system.

Despite these challenges, Atesh emphasizes that caching should always be considered as a first step in performance optimization. The performance gains are substantial, and modern tools and frameworks make implementing caching strategies increasingly straightforward.

Database Indexing: The Double-Edged Sword

Indexing represents another critical performance optimization, particularly for database-driven applications. An index is a data structure that improves the speed of data retrieval operations at the cost of additional storage space and slightly slower write operations. When properly applied, indexes can transform a query that takes seconds or minutes into one that completes in milliseconds.

Atesh emphasizes that asking "can I index this?" should become a reflex for developers working with databases. Most slow query problems can be traced to missing indexes on columns used in WHERE clauses, JOIN conditions, or ORDER BY expressions. By analyzing query patterns and adding appropriate indexes, you can often achieve orders-of-magnitude performance improvements.

However, this optimization technique requires careful judgment. Each index consumes additional storage space, and every time you insert, update, or delete data in a table, all indexes on that table must also be updated. This means that over-indexing can actually degrade overall system performance, particularly for write-heavy workloads. A table with dozens of indexes will perform poorly on INSERT, UPDATE, and DELETE operations, even if SELECT queries become faster.

The key is to understand your access patterns. Tables that are primarily read-heavy can benefit from extensive indexing, while tables that serve as write buffers or handle frequent updates require more conservative indexing strategies. Regular analysis of query performance using tools like database monitoring—exactly the kind of monitoring that inspired Atesh to create this video—can help identify which indexes are actually being used and which might be candidates for removal.

Atesh mentions that he was inspired to create this video while setting up database monitoring for an application and examining the "slow queries" section. This real-world experience underscores the importance of actively monitoring database performance rather than simply assuming indexes are working as expected. Understanding how your database measures "slow queries"—what parameters it uses and how it categorizes query performance—is essential for effective optimization.

Asynchronous Operations and User Wait Time

The final question in the framework—does the user need to wait?—addresses one of the most nuanced aspects of performance optimization: the distinction between synchronous and asynchronous processing. Understanding when to use each approach can dramatically improve both actual and perceived system performance.

Synchronous operations are those where the user initiates a request and must wait for completion before continuing. The system blocks, and the user sees a loading spinner or frozen interface until the operation finishes. While sometimes necessary, synchronous processing can create poor user experiences, especially for operations that take significant time to complete.

Asynchronous processing, on the other hand, allows the system to acknowledge a request immediately while the actual processing happens in the background. The user can continue interacting with the application, and they're notified when the operation completes. This approach is particularly valuable for operations that don't require immediate user feedback.

Atesh provides an excellent example: confirmation emails. When a user places an order on an e-commerce site, should they wait for the confirmation email to be sent before seeing their order confirmation? Obviously not—the email is a separate service that operates on a different timescale. By making email sending asynchronous—perhaps using a message queue system—the core transaction completes quickly while the email is delivered in the background.

Message queue systems like BullMQ or Apache Kafka are commonly used to implement asynchronous processing in production systems. These tools allow you to decouple time-consuming tasks from the main request processing flow, improving both performance and reliability. If the email service experiences issues, the message queue can retry delivery without affecting the user's experience.

The key insight here is that not all operations are equally urgent from the user's perspective. Operations that provide immediate value to the user should be synchronous and fast. Operations that happen "behind the scenes" can almost always be asynchronous. The art of performance optimization lies in correctly categorizing operations and choosing the appropriate processing model for each.

The Role of CDNs in Performance Optimization

While not one of the five core questions, Atesh briefly mentions Content Delivery Networks (CDNs) as an additional tool in the performance optimization toolkit. CDNs are distributed networks of servers that store copies of your content in multiple geographic locations, allowing users to access data from a server physically closer to them.

CDNs can dramatically improve performance for static content like images, videos, stylesheets, and JavaScript files. By serving this content from edge locations near users, CDNs reduce latency and improve load times. For global applications, CDN usage is often one of the quickest and most impactful performance improvements available.

Atesh notes that CDNs can fundamentally change the performance characteristics of a system, making them worth considering in almost any production deployment. However, like all optimizations, CDN usage comes with trade-offs, including cost considerations and the complexity of cache invalidation for dynamic content.

Key Takeaways

Understanding the latency-throughput trade-off is fundamental to system design. These aren't just abstract concepts—they directly impact architectural decisions. Applications requiring real-time responses (gaming, financial trading) prioritize low latency, while high-volume systems (e-commerce during sales, data processing) often benefit more from high throughput. The Zomato example demonstrates that faster isn't always better; system stability under load often matters more than marginal latency improvements.

Caching is responsible for approximately 80% of performance gains in typical applications. This is perhaps the most important single insight in the video. Before diving into complex optimizations, ensure you're effectively caching frequently accessed data. The McDonald's analogy illustrates that successful businesses have intuitively understood caching for decades—preparing items in advance rather than waiting for each individual request.

Database indexing requires balance and ongoing maintenance. While proper indexing can dramatically improve query performance, over-indexing degrades write performance and consumes storage. Regular monitoring and analysis of actual query patterns is essential to maintaining the right indexes for your workload.

Asynchronous processing dramatically improves user experience for non-critical operations. The question "does the user need to wait?" should guide decisions about synchronous versus asynchronous processing. Operations like sending emails, processing analytics, or generating reports can be queued and handled in the background without impacting the user's perception of system responsiveness.

Performance optimization is about trade-offs, not absolutes. There's no single "best" approach—every decision involves sacrificing something to gain something else. The goal is to understand your specific requirements and make informed choices that optimize for what matters most in your particular context.
