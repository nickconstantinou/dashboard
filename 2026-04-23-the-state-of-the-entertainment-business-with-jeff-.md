## The Schedule Plot Twist Nobody's Talking About

Buried beneath the gameweek recap is what Ross considers a genuinely important observation about the upcoming schedule—one he admits he "haven't really seen talked about" despite strong evidence it could unfold. The potential scenario involves Bournemouth and Manchester City potentially doubling in Gameweek 36 instead of the currently projected Gameweek 37, which would create a cascading effect: Bournemouth blanking in 37 while Palace takes their place as the only team doubling that week.

Ross walks through the proposed structure with careful precision. Under this alternative schedule, Bournemouth would face Fulham away and then Manchester City at home, securing their double in gameweek 36. Man City, in turn, would have Brentford home and Bournemouth away for their own 36 double. The implications ripple outward: Bournemouth would receive a blank in 37, with Palace the sole doublers that week (Brentford and Manchester City away). Ross created a graphic to visualize this possibility and emphasizes there's "actually a strong chance that it plays out this way."

The critical dependency, however, is Manchester City defeating Southampton next weekend. "We'll have to see maybe the red herrings," Ross offers cautiously, but acknowledges the breadcrumbs exist. The alternative—should City progress—sees them and Palace doubling in 36 with no other teams doubling and nobody blanking in 37. "So keep that one at the back of your head," Ross advises. "We're likely to know after City have played against Southampton on the weekend anyway." The potential schedule swap adds another layer of complexity to free hit decisions, particularly for managers considering assets from these affected clubs.

## Template vs. Differentials: The Clone Feature Revelation

This philosophical pivot represents perhaps Ross's most candid observation of the session. "I think the biggest misconception in FPL is you need differentials to gain rank," he begins. "And also, I think a big misconception is having a template team means you can't gain rank." He offers his wildcard as evidence: "My wild card was very template minus a couple of alterations like keeping Thiago who bagged the brace in 32." Despite the supposedly conservative approach, "the rank climb has been quite significant so far."

The more striking statistic involves the clone feature on Live FPL, which tracks how many teams in the top nine million match your exact squad. Ross reveals that "my wild card only had about 1,300 identical teams in the whole world"—an extraordinarily low number for a supposedly template side. "And that's on a wild card week, let alone you know the following weeks where the teams start to move different directions with free transfers." Ross has deliberately modded his team throughout the season, partly because he recognizes viewers might copy his selections, "but there's been loads and loads of game weeks over the entire season where my team doesn't have a single clone—not a single team the same in the whole world."

He challenges viewers to verify this using the clone feature themselves: "You might think that can't be true, but if you look up Live FPL's clone feature, it tells you how many other teams in the top n million have the same team as yours." The implication is profound—template ownership and differential-chasing are false binaries. Consistent decision-making around fundamentals can produce genuinely unique teams even while following sound foundational logic.

Ross reflects on his own evolution: "I did used to play really aggressive and go with big differentials too much, and it is really hard to"—the thought trails off, but the lesson is clear. The aggressive differential approach that feels exciting often undermines the mathematical discipline required to climb ranks over a full season. His current approach, built around clean sheet odds, goal-scoring projections, fixture difficulty, and squad depth for doubles, has delivered results without requiring contrarianism for its own sake.

## Key Insights for FPL Managers

The first critical takeaway involves the schedule uncertainty around Bournemouth and Palace. Managers holding assets from these clubs should monitor Manchester City's result against Southampton closely. If City win, the entire Gameweek 36-37 double structure could shift, changing which teams blank and which enjoy doubled fixtures. Free hit decisions made this week could be invalidated by information arriving next weekend.

The second insight concerns clean sheet probability as the foundation for defensive selections. Ross explicitly states his methodology: "I'm very clearly looking at the clean sheet and goal-scoring odds and moving backwards from there." Liverpool at 40%, Arsenal at 38%, and Tottenham at 34% aren't just abstract percentages—they're the statistical backbone justifying every defensive transfer. Managers should access similar clean sheet probability tools when constructing free hit squads.

Third, the Newcastle defensive collapse presents a specific opportunity. Expected goals conceded data showing 12.98 over six matches—second worst in the league—translates directly into Arsenal attacking asset appeal. Rice represents the cleanest route to Newcastle exposure given Saka's fitness uncertainty, but the underlying fixture quality justifies even captain selections on Arsenal forwards.

Fourth, the free hit's greatest value lies in maximizing high-quality template assets rather than chasing differentials. Ross's team-of-the-season alignment approach suggests that free hit works best as a tool to deploy your best possible squad during favorable fixture congestion, not as a lottery ticket for rank-altering differential hits.

Finally, the FPL Vert disaster reminds managers that chip activation requires the same mechanical diligence as squad selection. In the pressure of deadline timing, the simple act of pressing "activate" can be overlooked—and the cost can be catastrophic. Ross's -72 penalty for 19 transfers should prompt every manager to verify chip status twice before confirming their final squad.

For managers navigating the season's final stretch, Ross offers a model that balances data-driven analysis with personal constraint awareness. His team's clone count reveals that disciplined, fundamentals-first decision-making naturally produces unique squads over time. The free hit, deployed thoughtfully against favorable fixture combinations, represents the season's last opportunity to reshape your rank before the finish line—and Ross intends to use it accordingly.</p>
