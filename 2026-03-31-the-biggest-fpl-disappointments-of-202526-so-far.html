Fantasy Premier League's Biggest Disappointments of 2025/26: A Season of Underperforming Stars

The 2025/26 Fantasy Premier League season has delivered unexpected heartbreak for managers who invested heavily in premium-priced forwards, with several high-profile strikers failing to justify their lofty price tags through the first 30 gameweeks. According to an analysis by Fantasy Football Scout published on March 30, 2026, three of the most anticipated FPL assets—Arsenal's Viktor Gyokeres, Aston Villa's Ollie Watkins, and Liverpool's Mohamed Salah alongside Newcastle's Alexander Isak—have all fallen significantly short of expectations, leaving thousands of FPL managers scrambling for alternatives during the international break.

Viktor Gyokeres has perhaps been the most costly disappointment for managers who splashed out on his £9.0m price tag. The Swedish international arrived at Arsenal last summer following a remarkable two-season spell at Sporting CP, where he plundered 97 goals across all competitions. Arsenal manager Mikel Arteta clearly identified Gyokeres as the final piece of his title-challenging puzzle—a physical centre-forward capable of leading the line in North London. However, the transition from Portuguese to English football has proven far more difficult than anticipated. Through 29 Premier League appearances (23 starts, 6 substitute appearances), Gyokeres has managed just 11 goals with zero assists, blanking in 17 of his 23 starts. His points-per-match average of 3.5 places him behind nine other FPL forwards despite his premium pricing, with his total tally of 101 points failing to justify the substantial investment. Questions have emerged about whether Gyokeres can adapt to Arteta's specific tactical requirements, though with Arsenal still in the title race, there remains time for redemption.

Ollie Watkins presents another case of catastrophic value erosion for Villa fans and FPL managers alike. Priced at £9.0m at the season's start, Watkins was expected to thrive in a World Cup year with his club's attacking burden no longer shared by departed stars Jhon Duran and Marcus Rashford. Instead, the England international has looked a shadow of his former self, managing just nine goals and two assists across 30 Premier League appearances. His 3.6 points per match average has been beaten by three separate Villa teammates, while his profligacy in front of goal has been staggering—Watkins has missed 15 of his 23 Opta-defined "big chances," placing him among the worst finishers in the league alongside strikers like Erling Haaland (24 missed) and Igor Thiago (17 missed). The concerning decline has been reflected in his England prospects; manager Thomas Tuchel recently dropped Watkins from the national team squad, raising serious questions about whether the 29-year-old has lost his edge permanently.

Mohamed Salah and Alexander Isak round out the list of major disappointments, though their struggles carry different narratives. Salah, the most expensive player in FPL at £14.5m, was coming off a record-breaking 2024/25 campaign where he shattered the all-time FPL points record. Yet through 22 appearances (19 starts, 3 sub appearances), the Egyptian has managed only five goals and six assists, with his price already dropping to £14.0m. His points-per-match average of 4.4, while respectable for most players, represents a dramatic decline from his usual output and leaves managers who captained him week after week nursing significant point deficits. Isak, priced at £10.5m, has been even more disappointing with just two goals and one assist from 10 appearances (6 starts, 4 sub appearances), averaging a paltry 2.9 points per match—his price has dropped to £10.3m, suggesting the market agrees his returns have been well below expectations.

Why This Matters

The underperformance of these premium assets has fundamentally disrupted FPL strategies across millions of managers worldwide. FPL boasts over 11 million active managers globally, and those who invested heavily in this expensive striker quartet have found themselves playing catch-up against competitors who identified better value elsewhere. The Gyokeres situation is particularly significant given Arsenal's title ambitions—his struggles may represent not just an individual failure but potentially a tactical mismatch that could cost Arsenal crucial points in the closing weeks of the season. Similarly, Watkins' decline has broader implications for England's World Cup preparations, with Tuchel's decision to drop him signaling a potential shift in the national team's attacking hierarchy. For the FPL community, these disappointments underscore the eternal challenge of the game: even the most logical investments can fail to deliver, and price alone never guarantees performance.

    Context & Background

    This season's striker crisis represents a broader trend in FPL where premium-priced forwards have struggled to deliver value. The game has evolved significantly, with managers increasingly finding value in midfielders and budget enablers rather than expensive striker options. Gyokeres' struggles highlight the significant jump from the Portuguese Primeira Liga to the Premier League—a transition that has proven difficult for numerous players in recent years, despite their gaudy statistics abroad. Watkins' decline is perhaps the most alarming given his consistent output over several seasons; whether this represents a genuine technical or physical decline, or merely a temporary blip in form, remains to be seen. The article notes that compared to his 16-goal, 8-assist 2024/25 season, this campaign has yet to see "Watkins at his top level."

    What's Next

    With the international break providing time for reflection, FPL managers face critical decisions ahead. For Gyokeres, Arsenal remains in the title race with sufficient fixtures to turn his season around, though managers must weigh whether to hold or cut losses. Watkins and England face a crucial period as the World Cup approaches—Tuchel's decision to bench him could either spark a revival or confirm a terminal decline. Salah managers will hope the veteran can recapture his best form as Liverpool pushes for Champions League qualification. The article invited reader feedback on notable exclusions or unfair inclusions, suggesting the debate over this season's biggest disappointments is far from settled. As the business end of the season approaches, those who invested in these underperforming assets will be watching closely to see whether redemption is possible—or whether their FPL seasons have been defined by these costly misfires.
