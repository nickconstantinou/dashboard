# FPL Strategy: Gameweek 34 Free Hit Planning, Fixture Analysis, and Differential Picks for the Final Sprint

Introduction

For Fantasy Premier League managers, the season's final stretch is where championships are won and lost. With just five weeks remaining, every transfer decision carries heightened weight, and the strategic deployment of precious bench boost and free hit chips can mean the difference between a memorable finish and a forgettable end to the campaign. In this comprehensive analysis, popular FPL content creator FPL Harry breaks down his gameweek 33 performance, walks through his strategy for the crucial gameweek 34 fixture against Brentford, and provides data-driven insights into which players and teams offer the best value for managers looking to maximize their free hit chips.

Harry, known for his meticulous planning and calculated risk-taking, opens by addressing his absence from Wednesday's transfer targets video, explaining he was out of London for most of the day and wanted to assess gameweek 33 results before diving into planning. He mentions that a complete guide will be released on Friday morning for managers still deciding which players to target with their final chips of the season. The context here is critical: this is the big double gameweek of the season, and Harry had wildcarded in gameweek 32 specifically to optimize his bench boost deployment in gameweek 33.

Gameweek 33 Results and Chip Strategy Analysis

Harry reveals he scored an impressive 132 points in gameweek 33 after deploying the bench boost chip, a strategic decision that required careful squad construction in the preceding wildcard. He notes that while he's pleased with the result—achieving 18 points above his safety score and earning a green arrow from around 40,000 to just outside the top 20,000—there's a sense of what might have been. His bench boost would have reached a remarkable 36 points had João Pedro not failed to play any minutes, forcing him to substitute in Ethan Hainsworth from his bench rather than enjoying the full benefit of his carefully constructed squad depth.

The frustration centers on the fact that Harry should have picked Pascal Gross instead of Hainsworth, as Gross would have provided better value. However, Hainsworth still contributed positively, with Harry describing him as "my star of the gameweek," though he acknowledges this doesn't feel quite right given the alternative selection. Despite João Pedro's non-appearance, the bench boost remained productive: Van Dijk contributed an assist and clean sheet for 12 points, Onana managed a clean sheet for eight points, and Bruno Fernandes delivered six points from the bench. Harry emphasizes that the planning that went into wildcarding and bench boosting was sound, even if execution wasn't perfect.

Harland emerged as the captaincy solution, scoring goals in both matches including a penalty in the first leg of Liverpool's fixture against West Ham. Harry expresses satisfaction with this selection and notes that losing a couple of bonus points from both Van Dijk and Harland doesn't significantly impact his overall standing since most managers captained the same players. The transfer activity during the week involved selling Gabriel (who scored zero points) for Jaël Bèlo of Leeds (who delivered a 10-pointer) and then moving that player toward Onana, whose availability was uncertain but ultimately proved sufficient for 13 points.

The Bournemouth defenders, particularly James Hill and Sessegnon, performed somewhat disappointingly, though Harry notes Sessegnon was unlucky not to secure another assist in their home match against Leeds, which would have earned him maximum bonus points. Instead, he finished with eight points, which Harry describes as "quite unlucky" given his overall contribution. This highlights the variance in FPL defending returns, where individual errors or near-misses can dramatically shift point outcomes.

Data-Driven Fixture Analysis

Harry presents data from Rob TfPl's projections to guide free hit decisions, beginning with expected goals scored. Liverpool leads the pack at 2.17 projected goals in a home fixture against Crystal Palace, though Harry expresses skepticism about this figure, suggesting it may underrate Palace's recent defensive improvements. Crystal Palace have conceded just one goal across their past three matches, and while their European ambitions mean their focus isn't entirely on the Premier League, Harry believes the data underestimates their current form. Importantly, Palace face a Thursday Europa Conference League semifinal following their Monday fixture, which might prompt manager Oliver Glasner to field a full-strength lineup against Liverpool—potentially improving Palace's attacking output even if their defensive record suffers.

Arsenal rank second for projected goals in their away match against Newcastle, and Harry observes that Newcastle have been inconsistent over recent weeks. Historically, Eddie Howe has set up his teams effectively to counter Arsenal's style of play, but current Newcastle feel like a team to target for opposing attackers. The challenge lies in predicting which Arsenal attacker will deliver returns—whether Joãor, Eze, Havertz, Saka (if back from injury), Mudryk, or even the more conservative Declan Rice option. Harry admits this prediction difficulty makes selecting a single Arsenal attacker somewhat of a gamble, though the fixture ceiling remains attractive.

Two teams stand out as having marginally better fixtures: Manchester United at home against Brentford and Tottenham away at Wolves. Harry delays detailed Tottenham discussion, noting they could offer interesting differential opportunities that many managers are overlooking due to Tottenham's inconsistent form. Regarding Manchester United, he flags slight doubts over Matthijs de Ligt and Luke Shaw for Brentford, though he expects both to start. Brentford have improved defensively, keeping a clean sheet against Fulham in gameweek 33, which complicates United's attacking prospects.

Below these top fixtures, the projected returns drop significantly. Fulham and Aston Villa sit at 1.44 projected goals, with West Ham also at that level. From there, the numbers decline further to around one goal for Everton, Brentford, and Sunderland, which Harry notes is "pretty low when you're trying to pick someone on a free hit."

Tottenham Hotspur: The Contrarian Differential Opportunity

This is where Harry's analysis becomes particularly interesting. Tottenham represents what he calls "the outlier of a team that we don't really talk about and don't really invest in because of the way they're playing, but have quite good projections because of the way that Wolves are playing." He acknowledges the obvious concerns: Tottenham haven't won in 2026 and "look horrible," making it difficult to justify selecting any of their players based on recent form. However, the fixture at Wolves presents a mathematical opportunity that shouldn't be dismissed entirely.

Harry describes a clear split in FPL community thinking: one camp points to Tottenham fighting for something and showing improvement against Brighton, suggesting attacking players like James Maddison or Dominic Solanke could return. The other camp emphasizes the negative recent form and argues against any Tottenham investment. Harry positions himself "somewhere in the middle," acknowledging he doesn't expect to end up with a Tottenham player in his final draft, but he expresses genuine uncertainty. As the deadline approaches and managers finalize their free hit teams, he wonders whether having one Tottenham asset might be worthwhile despite the obvious risks.

The underlying logic is straightforward: when a team faces a weak opponent in poor form, even struggling teams can exceed expectations. Wolves have been conceding goals freely, which elevates Tottenham's attacking potential regardless of their own inconsistent performances. This represents the classic FPL tension between form and fixture, with Harry suggesting fixture advantage may deserve more weight than many managers are currently giving it.

Clean Sheet Probabilities and Goalkeeper Analysis

Harry transitions to clean sheet odds, revealing Arsenal at 37% for their match against Newcastle, Liverpool at 35%, and Tottenham surprisingly at 34%. Harry admits Tottenham's 34% clean sheet probability "feels quite high" given their defensive struggles, but the fixture against Wolves explains this projection. Forest face Sunderland at 29%, which Harry notes feels elevated because Forest are "very good at home." The paradox emerges when considering that Sunderland have "just shipped four goals whereas Forest have just scored four goals," making a Sunderland defender an unappealing choice despite the reasonable clean sheet percentage.

The Manchester United home fixture against Brentford presents a similar puzzle: many managers might target United defenders given the fixture, but Brentford's recent defensive improvement complicates the clean sheet equation. West Ham face Everton at home, but Everton are "playing quite well" recently, which Harry suggests makes this fixture less attractive than it initially appears. The broader takeaway is that gameweek 34 presents a difficult week for reliably predicting which defenders will deliver returns, a theme Harry emphasizes throughout his analysis.

Harry's Updated Draft and Goalkeeper Selection

Harry reveals his updated draft has "exactly 0.1 million in the bank," representing aggressive stretching of team value. He acknowledges he's "definitely ways" where he might not need to be this stretched, suggesting some spending appears justified "for the sake of it." This careful self-assessment demonstrates the fine line between necessary investment and wasteful spending that characterizes late-season squad management.

His goalkeeper selection is David Raya of Arsenal, which represents both opportunity and compromise. Raya benefits from Arsenal's position at the top of clean sheet projections, but Harry identifies significant limitations: "He's not going to be the player who changes my free hit from being an okay or a bad free hit into an incredible free hit." Raya simply doesn't make enough saves to post the 10 or 15-point hauls that premium goalkeepers like Alisson, Meslier, or even Woodman at Liverpool might achieve. His ceiling appears limited to 2-6 points most weeks, which Harry considers underwhelming for a free hit investment.

The logic for selecting Raya centers on Arsenal double-up coverage alongside his defenders, but Harry recognizes that alternative options might offer higher ceilings with acceptable risk. If he were "chasing rank more than I feel like I am at the moment," he would punt on a goalkeeper with greater point-scoring potential and simply hope Arsenal don't keep a clean sheet. This represents a calculated trade-off between safe floor and exciting ceiling that every FPL manager faces when deploying limited chips.

Harry's budget bench goalkeeper is Kinsky of Tottenham, positioned for the away fixture at Wolves. This selection follows the Tottenham differential logic discussed earlier: despite Tottenham's poor form, the fixture offers enough attacking promise that their goalkeeper could benefit from saves if the match becomes open.

The Free Hit Dilemma: Predictability in an Unpredictable Gameweek

Harry's central conclusion is that gameweek 34 presents unusual difficulty in identifying reliably performing players. The data suggests expected goals and clean sheet probabilities across the league are relatively low, with no fixture standing out as obviously premium. This creates a strategic dilemma: managers on free hit need to take risks to differentiate their squads, but the risks themselves carry less certainty than usual.

He frames this as a fundamental challenge in FPL strategy: when the field's choices all carry significant variance, the difference between success and failure becomes less about skill and more about luck. His own approach involves maintaining the free hit for this gameweek while accepting that the optimal draft might look quite different from what the data suggests. The acknowledgment that "picking a good Arsenal attacker feels quite difficult to predict where the attacking returns are going to be" applies equally to most other options available.

For managers still deciding on their free hit approach, Harry's analysis suggests focusing on fixtures that offer both attacking potential and defensive vulnerability from opponents, while accepting that premium players in tough fixtures may underperform regardless of their quality. Tottenham against Wolves represents the most contrarian interpretation of this principle, but the data supporting their expected returns cannot be entirely dismissed.

Key Strategic Takeaways

Plan chip deployments many gameweeks in advance. Harry's 132-point bench boost didn't happen by accident—it resulted from wildcarding in gameweek 32 specifically to optimize squad depth for gameweek 33. Managers who wait until the last moment to deploy chips sacrifice the planning advantages that separate elite finishes from average ones.

Accept variance and focus on process over results. João Pedro's failure to play minutes cost Harry significant points, yet he remains satisfied with his decisions because the planning was sound. Similarly, Sessegnon's unlucky near-assist demonstrates that individual moments of bad fortune shouldn't override systematic approaches to player selection.

Fixture-based differentials deserve weight even for underperforming teams. Tottenham represent a textbook case: their form has been poor, but Wolves' defensive struggles elevate their expected returns. The FPL community's collective avoidance of a team creates genuine differentiation opportunity for managers willing to zig when others zag.

Cheap goalkeepers with difficult fixtures may outperform expensive options with easier ones. David Raya offers a floor but limited ceiling, while alternative options might post 10-15 points if conditions align. Free hit chips, by definition, are one-week gambles—swinging for higher ceilings often makes more sense than settling for safe floors.

Five weeks leaves room for significant rank movement. Harry pushed from 40,000 to nearly 20,000 in a single gameweek, demonstrating that late-season chip deployment and smart transfer strategy can still yield dramatic improvements. Managers shouldn't assume their positions are locked, regardless of current standing.
