# Owning the Future: USVC and the Democratization of Venture Capital

The venture capital industry has long operated as one of finance's most exclusive clubs. For decades, accessing the most transformative technology companies before they went public was a privilege reserved for those with million-dollar net worths and connections deep within Silicon Valley's inner circles. SpaceX, OpenAI, Anthropic—these names represent not just the cutting edge of artificial intelligence and aerospace, but the kind of wealth creation that historically bypassed everyday Americans entirely. A new investment vehicle called USVC is attempting to fundamentally change that equation, and its founder Ankur Nagpal recently sat down to explain exactly how it works, why it exists, and what investors should actually expect.

The timing, according to Nagpal, is not coincidental. These companies have become so valuable—OpenAI alone valued in the hundreds of billions—that they've essentially priced out ordinary participation in their growth stories. "If you look at where most of the money is being made in private technology companies now, it has been happening before these companies go public," Nagpal explains. "Businesses are taking so long to get out there. And the result of that is the average person misses out on that wealth creation." USVC emerges as a response to that structural gap, a registered fund approved by the SEC that allows investors to participate with as little as $500.

But this isn't simply about giving retail investors access to yesterday's winners. Nagpal frames USVC as a vehicle for owning the future of American technology broadly—not just the household names of today, but the companies that will dominate conversation three, five, and seven years from now. The fund is structured to invest across multiple stages, from late-stage private tech to the earliest pre-seed rounds, with a strategy designed to capture both the established leaders and the emerging challengers that will define the next decade of innovation.

## The Structural Difference That Matters

Perhaps the most critical distinction Nagpal draws throughout the conversation is the difference between USVC and the various ETFs that have attempted to offer similar exposure. Products from Fundrise (ticker VCX) and Robinhood have attracted attention for trading on public markets, but Nagpal argues these vehicles have a fundamental flaw that investors may not immediately recognize: their prices become "divorced from the underlying value of assets."

He illustrates this with a striking example. The Fundrise VCX ETF currently trades at roughly four times the price of the companies it actually holds. "Even right now, it's trading at roughly four times the price of the underlying companies. And if you're an early investor, that's great. You're up in the money and it can work really well. But in down markets, this can also sort of trade, not in lockstep with the underlying companies. So you could, as an example, buy this ETF. The companies could do well, but you could still lose money because the pricing is sort of divorced from the underlying value."

USVC takes a fundamentally different approach. It is not structured as an ETF, meaning it doesn't trade on public markets with the kind of price discovery mechanisms (or dislocations) that creates. Instead, the fund operates with NAV-based pricing—the $19.95 price shown to investors only changes when and if one of the underlying companies receives an updated valuation from a new funding round, an acquisition, or other market activity. "The NAV that you see only changes if and when one of the underlying companies has an updated valuation," Nagpal explains. "So it's a slower sort of moving NAV."

This distinction is crucial for understanding what USVC actually is. Investors aren't trading sentiment on private tech companies—they're owning a direct stake in the underlying assets, with valuations that move when the companies themselves move, not when market情绪 shifts. The tradeoff, which Nagpal is forthright about, is liquidity. Unlike ETFs that can be bought and sold throughout the trading day, USVC requires a longer-term commitment that aligns more closely with traditional venture fund structures.

## The Liquidity Question and Quarterly Tenders

The natural question that follows is straightforward: if this isn't easily tradable, how do investors actually get their money out? Nagpal walks through the redemption mechanism in detail, explaining that the fund aims to offer quarterly tender offers of up to 5% of the total fund NAV at actual NAV price.

To use his concrete example: if the fund manages $100 million in assets, it would make $5 million available for redemptions every quarter, distributed to investors on a pro rata basis based on their shareholdings. "It doesn't mean each investor is limited to 5%," Nagpal clarifies. "It means 5% of the entire fund will be made available for redemptions every single quarter."

The board reserves the right to skip tender offers, though Nagpal emphasizes that "it is in our best interest, our interests are aligned. If we are a fund that is skipping tender offers, it becomes a less attractive investment product and it ultimately hurts us quite a bit." Still, he wants investors to approach this with appropriate expectations. "Again, this is venture. It's a different asset class. Think about a venture fund. It is a highly illiquid investment. This does have tender offers, which is typically better than a venture fund. And a venture fund, your money is locked up for 10 years. You have nothing you can do about it."

This structure serves a dual purpose. Beyond providing some redemption mechanism, it also protects the fund from the kind of panic-selling that can occur when price decouples from value—as happened with the fund Nagpal references that launched and saw a 10x spike followed by a crash. "There's a lot more stability than something that we saw with BCX," he notes.

The fund also intends to maintain a cash buffer precisely to honor these quarterly tenders. Currently sitting at 56% cash, the target allocation is closer to 10-20% in cash and cash equivalents. "If all your dollars are invested in venture, that's very illiquid and quarterly tenders become a little bit difficult," Nagpal explains. This buffer ensures the fund can meet its redemption obligations without being forced to liquidate positions at unfavorable times.

## Understanding the Fee Structure

One of the areas that drew the most criticism and confusion online involved fees. Nagpal directly addresses a calculation error that had circulated widely after he responded to a clarifying tweet. The original framing suggested a "double layer" structure totaling around 3% in management fees—3% at the USVC level plus additional fees from underlying investments. Nagpal reframes this more precisely.

"What investors care about is their expense ratio, but that's different from a management fee," he explains. The current expense ratio—the actual net percentage investors pay—is 2.5%, despite the underlying mechanics running closer to 3%+ before Ankur subsidizes the difference out of pocket. The reason for the elevated cost, he argues, is structural rather than greedy: "When you start funds like these, they're pretty expensive to start. So all these funds will have very high expense ratios up front because there are fixed costs. And when you have like 10 or 20 or 30 million in assets, those fixed costs are really high."

Nagpal positions this 2.5% expense ratio as competitive within the venture ecosystem, even if it looks high compared to Vanguard index funds. "Venture funds typically are 2% and some are 2.5%. However, if you're a venture fund of funds, which we partially are, those are typically 3% if you include the underlying. So this 2.5% cap makes us very competitive, especially when you factor in we don't charge carry."

The carry point is particularly important for direct investments on the cap table. "If it's direct on the cap table, it means there's no management fee. But also because this type of registered fund doesn't charge carry, there's no carry as well, which makes it very, very competitive from a pricing and fee perspective for any of those direct investments." Some underlying fund investments will carry their own fees, but investors in direct positions avoid these entirely.

## Portfolio Composition and the XAI Position

The current portfolio raises obvious questions that Nagpal addresses directly. Notably, 56% of holdings as of the conversation are cash—a fact that drew criticism from some potential investors who didn't want to buy into a fund that was largely sitting on the sidelines. Nagpal explains this is partly by design for the tender offer structure, but also reflects the early stage of the fund's deployment.

The largest single position is XAI at 20.25% of holdings. This position became particularly interesting when SpaceX announced its acquisition of XAI on February 3rd at a $1.25 trillion valuation. Nagpal clarifies what this means for fund investors: "The most recent mark on this was a few weeks ago based on some internal documentation. That's not the IPO price, right? That's speculative. That hasn't happened. We don't know when that is."

The distinction matters enormously. Investors in USVC are not buying at SpaceX's IPO price—they're buying at a mark derived from an acquisition that hasn't occurred yet and may never occur on those terms. "My general caveat with all of this is, look, like some of this may look super opportunistic," Nagpal says. "You can get in before certain marks and all of that. I don't encourage that type of thinking in general. I think you should think about this as a long-term investment."

The other major holdings—Anthropic, OpenAI, and others—similarly represent positions taken at various valuations in an environment where AI companies have been marked up maximally. Nagpal is candid about the risks this creates. "These are great companies. We wish we owned more of them. But right now, we've added a lot in cash. These positions have effectively gotten diluted. And we think the future is not just these companies, but the companies that everyone will talk about three years from now and five years from now."

## The AngelList Platform and Deal Flow

USVC operates on AngelList, and Nagpal explains why this platform is particularly suited for this product. "AngelList has been around for 15 years. They're sort of at this point they've had multiple sort of generations of leaders and employees come. And it's been a fixture since I've now built and sold two companies and they're how I raised money for my first company over 10 years ago. It's been around for a very, very long time."

The platform's relevance goes beyond track record. AngelList serves as the infrastructure for the majority of early-stage investing in the United States. "They are where the majority of early stage investing happens. They are the fund manager of choice for a lot of early stage investors. They are the SPV tool for most companies. And as a result, their platform has really good exposure to all of these early stage assets, which is a very good vantage point for us if we're like, hey, we want to go acquire a stake in Anthropic. Let's see, there's a lot of Anthropic investors on AngelList. We know who to reach out to."

This institutional relationship gives USVC access to deal flow that would be extremely difficult to replicate for a new fund attempting to access the same companies directly. The platform also enables the fund to announce investments in ways that can activate company communities—a feature Nagpal seems genuinely excited about for future portfolio companies.

## The Investment Thesis and Long Game

Nagpal is careful to frame USVC not as a trading vehicle or a way to speculate on the next funding round for OpenAI, but as a long-term investment in venture capital as an asset class. "The best outcome for us is if you look at the data of venture capital as an asset class has the potential to outperform public markets while also being somewhat uncorrelated," he explains. "If we can effectively outperform while not being fully correlated, that's why endowments invest in venture, right? Like it's two things. It's the alpha and the lack of beta."

This framing is intentional. Nagpal explicitly warns against investing based on excitement about any specific position. "Don't do it because you really like, you know, the SpaceX position or the Anthropic position. These are great companies. We wish we owned more of them. But right now, you've added a lot in cash. These positions have effectively gotten diluted. And we think the future is not just these companies, but the companies that everyone will talk about three years from now and five years from now."

The question for potential investors becomes whether they believe in venture capital as an asset class over a three to five year or longer timeframe—and whether they can afford the illiquidity that comes with it. Nagpal's guidance is direct: "We always say with VC, only invest what you can afford to lose. It is an illiquid investment. Like, do not think of it comparable to investing in the stock market. However, if you're someone that otherwise has a balanced portfolio, it could make sense to allocate, you know, three, five, seven percent, something like that to this basket of high growth technology companies."

## Strategic Architecture: How Capital Gets Deployed

The fund's investment strategy operates on multiple fronts. For early-stage companies—pre-seed and seed rounds—USVC's approach is to identify the most promising fund managers and serve as their capital source. "It's very hard for us to keep track of what are the most promising companies," Nagpal admits. "So there our goal is to find the most promising GPs that run these specialized pre-seed and seed funds and be their source of capital. The benefit of doing this is when they want to write a follow on check, like a series A or a series B check, we can be their source of capital."

This fund-of-funds approach provides diversification across many early bets managed by specialists, with USVC gaining access to future rounds as those managers deploy capital. For later-stage positions, the fund will pursue direct cap table entries where possible—eliminating the fee layers that come with intermediate structures—and will also opportunistically buy secondary positions at discounts when fund managers or early investors look to exit.

The fund plans to be transparent about its moves, with Nagpal committing to public announcements of new positions. "We'll announce newer positions," he says. "And so people can kind of see the portfolio evolve in real time." This communication strategy is designed to build trust through transparency as the fund grows and deploys its substantial cash position.

## Detailed Takeaways

**1. Structure determines behavior—know what you're actually buying.** The difference between NAV-based pricing (USVC) and market-based pricing (ETFs like VCX) is not academic. ETFs that trade at multiples divorced from underlying asset values can crash even when their holdings perform well, and can spike in ways that have nothing to do with actual company value. If you want direct exposure to private tech company growth rather than sentiment trading, the fund structure matters enormously.

**2. Quarterly tenders are a feature, not a guarantee.** Investors should understand that 5% quarterly redemptions of total fund NAV is a target, not a contractual obligation. The board can skip tenders, though incentives are aligned to prevent this. The more important takeaway is that this structure still represents a significant improvement over traditional venture funds with 10-year lockups, and the cash buffer strategy makes it more robust than it might appear.

**3. The fee comparison requires the right frame of reference.** 2.5% expense ratio looks alarming next to index funds charging 0.1%, but that's the wrong comparison. The relevant benchmark is venture fund-of-funds at 3% with carry on top. USVC's no-carry structure on direct investments combined with the 2.5% cap makes it competitive within its actual peer group. As the fund grows and fixed costs spread across more assets, the expense ratio should decline naturally.

**4. Current portfolio concentration in cash reflects deployment constraints, not a permanent state.** The 56% cash position exists partly by design (for tender obligations) but also because the fund is early in its deployment cycle. Investors buying today are buying access to future investments more than existing holdings. The XAI position is marked to SpaceX's acquisition price, but that remains speculative until an actual IPO or public trading occurs.

**5. Treat this as venture capital, not public market equity.** The three-to-five-year minimum horizon is not marketing spin—it's the actual operational reality of how private company valuations get updated.</p>
