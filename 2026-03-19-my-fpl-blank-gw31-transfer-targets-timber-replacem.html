FPL Gameweek 31 Transfer Targets: Blank Gameweek Strategy and Best Player Recommendations

Introduction

Fantasy Premier League managers up and down the country are facing a familiar dilemma as gameweek 31 approaches: how to navigate a blank gameweek while keeping one eye on the remainder of the season. With four teams not playing this weekend, the transfer market becomes a complex puzzle of short-term needs versus long-term planning. FPL Harry, a prominent FPL content creator, has released an in-depth video breaking down the best transfer targets for gameweek 31, while emphasising that your chip strategy—whether you've already played your wildcard, are saving it for gameweek 32, or planning a free hit in gameweek 33—will fundamentally alter which players make sense for your squad.

The beauty of FPL at this stage of the season lies in the diverging strategies managers are employing. According to polling data from FPL Harry's Twitter, approximately 70% of managers are committed to playing their wildcard in gameweek 32, leaving a significant minority pursuing alternative approaches. This division means there is no one-size-fits-all answer when discussing transfer targets, and Harry makes this explicitly clear from the outset. The video serves both those looking for immediate gameweek 31 solutions and those planning for the weeks ahead, though Harry specifically directs one-week punt players toward his separate gameweek preview video for more targeted advice.

Understanding the Blank Gameweek Context

Gameweek 31 presents a blank for four Premier League teams, meaning those clubs will not be in action this weekend. This immediately complicates transfer planning because managers must ensure they have eleven starting players while also considering whether their new signings will provide value beyond a single gameweek. However, FPL Harry emphasises that the uncertainty surrounding future fixture announcements adds another layer of complexity to the decision-making process.

The key uncertainty revolves around which teams will receive double gameweeks in subsequent weeks. At the time of recording, three fixtures appeared most likely to be rearranged and create double gameweeks: Manchester City versus Crystal Palace, Brighton versus Chelsea (contingent on Chelsea beating Portsmouth in the FA Cup), and Arsenal versus Newcastle (contingent on Arsenal progressing against Southampton in the cup). If Manchester City were to lose to Liverpool in the FA Cup, this would further alter the fixture landscape. This explains why FPL Harry advises against using all your transfers this week—you're essentially buying somewhat blind until the gameweek 32 deadline reveals which teams have favourable double fixtures.

The projected goal data from Rob TfPL reveals an interesting tactical insight: no team is predicted to score two goals or more in gameweek 31. This suggests that investing heavily in attackers for this specific gameweek may not be the most efficient strategy. The teams with the best projected scoring outputs are Fulham, Newcastle, and Manchester United, followed closely by Aston Villa, Chelsea, Liverpool, and Bournemouth—all clustering around similar expected goal numbers.

Defensive Options: The Two Viable Paths

When it comes to defending options, FPL Harry identifies only two genuinely appealing pathways for gameweek 31: Fulham at home to Burnley and Newcastle at home to Sunderland. These represent the two matches where clean sheet probabilities are highest and goal-scoring opportunities for the attacking players should be most plentiful.

For those seeking one-week punts, particularly differential goalkeeper options, Harry recommends three choices: Ramsdale (presumably Arsenal's goalkeeper), Leno (Fulham's goalkeeper), and Vicario (Tottenham's goalkeeper). The Vicario recommendation is particularly interesting because Tottenham face Nottingham Forest at home—a fixture that might appear challenging but Forest have been struggling to score goals recently, making this a potential swing game and six-pointer at the bottom of the table.

Among defenders, Lewis Hall receives a mention as a one-week punt, though Harry explicitly states his preference for Malick Chalobah of Newcastle for longer-term considerations. The analysis here is detailed: Chalobah's Defcon 90 metric (essentially clean sheet probability per 90 minutes) is superior to Hall's, he creates more big chances, his expected goal involvement per 90 is better, he plays more minutes consistently, and he's less injury-prone. While Hall offers slightly more explosive attacking threat in open play, the data favours Chalobah as the more reliable option, particularly given that Newcastle are competing for European qualification across multiple competitions.

Fulham's Anderson emerges as Harry's favourite Fulham defender, particularly for those needing a one-week solution at home to Burnley. However, he acknowledges that Fulham's long-term fixtures aren't as favourable as some alternatives. Other options include Ate (right back) or Calvin Bassie (centre back) if doubling up on Fulham defence, though Harry cautions against trying to predict whether Robinson or Sessegnon will start at left back.

For Brentford, Vandenberg represents the cheapest and most nailed-on option, with decent minutes per 90 and acceptable clean sheet credentials alongside promising long-term fixtures. Cheap alternatives like Rodon and Harry Maguire are flagged as serviceable for both short-term and longer-term needs, while Matty Cash of Aston Villa offers an interesting profile despite carrying a yellow flag—his attacking threat and favourable upcoming fixtures (including West Ham at home this week) make him a viable entry point.

Midfield and Attack Recommendations

Moving into midfield, FPL Harry opens with an emphatic recommendation for Bruno Fernandes. For managers who don't already own the Manchester United playmaker, now remains an excellent time to buy. The fixture away at Bournemouth in gameweek 31 makes him the most captained player in the game, with approximately 75% of managers indicating they will hand him the armband. Beyond this week, Manchester United's run of fixtures remains appealing with Chelsea and Brentford following, creating a nice long-term trajectory.

Other midfield options discussed include Harry Wilson, Obi, and Anthony Gordon. Gordon is particularly noteworthy because he's currently playing as a forward for Newcastle and has been awarded penalty duties. While Bruno Guimarães' potential return could theoretically impact this, Harry expects Gordon to retain penalty responsibility, making him an attractive option at home to Sunderland.

In attack, Calvert-Lewin at home to Brentford receives the nod, as does Raül Jiménez despite a slight risk of minutes with Mun being available again. Cesco is another option mentioned, though Harry acknowledges that obvious choices like Darwin and Saka are less attractive as one-week punts precisely because they're already heavily owned.

The Strategic Framework: Why Your Chip Strategy Matters

Perhaps the most valuable element of FPL Harry's analysis is his framework for thinking about how chip strategies should influence transfer decisions. He explicitly breaks down three distinct approaches and how they should shape your buying behaviour this week.

If you've already played your wildcard and are looking for long-term investments, your decisions should be guided by whether you need players who will double in gameweek 33 or those who will play in the blank in gameweek 34 (given Manchester City and Crystal Palace's rearranged fixture). The uncertainty here is significant because the cup results over the coming week will dramatically reshape the fixture list.

If you're committed to wildcard 32, you don't really need to worry about long-term fits—you can simply buy for gameweek 31 and then completely reshape your team the following week. This is why wildcard 32 is expected to be the most popular chip play.

For those pursuing a free hit 33 or wildcard 35 strategy, more flexibility is required. You need players who won't become dead weight when you eventually play your chip, meaning longer-term fixture consideration becomes more important even when buying for gameweek 31.

FPL Harry explicitly states that using all your transfers this week isn't necessarily the best strategy because the fixture information you need—particularly around which teams will double in gameweek 33—won't be confirmed until just a couple of days before the gameweek 32 deadline. The message is clear: maintain some flexibility in your squad construction rather than committing fully to a short-term gamble.

Detailed Takeaways

Takeaway One: Defenders are the primary target for gameweek 31. With no team projected to score two or more goals, the data strongly favours investing in clean sheet probability rather than attacking returns. Newcastle and Fulham represent the two best defensive fixtures, with Malick Chalobah emerging as Harry's preferred long-term pick over Lewis Hall despite Hall's slightly higher ceiling.

Takeaway Two: Your wildcard strategy fundamentally changes which players you should consider. Managers on wildcard 32 can buy more freely for immediate gratification, while those saving chips need to think two or three gameweeks ahead. This isn't abstract theory—it directly impacts whether players like Bruno Fernandes (excellent short-term, good long-term) or defenders with better future fixtures become better buys.

Takeaway Three: Anthony Gordon's penalty duty makes him a standout differential. Newcastle's current formation sees Gordon playing as a forward, and he's been entrusted with penalties. This combination makes him an attractive option even in a week where Newcastle's fixture, while good, isn't exceptional.

Takeaway Four: The fixture announcement for gameweek 32 will be pivotal. With cup results determining which teams get double gameweeks in gameweek 33 (and subsequently gameweek 34), managers should hold off on completely committing their transfer strategy until the picture becomes clearer. The advice to "use transfers wisely" isn't just about saving money—it's about preserving optionality.

Takeaway Five: Premium forwards may be overkill for blank gameweeks. Given the low expected goals across all matches, investing in budget options for gameweek 31 while saving your money for double gameweeks later may prove more efficient than loading up on expensive attackers for a single uncertain return.
