# Lessons from the Vikings and the Roman Empire: What History Tells Us About Our Future

In a wide-ranging conversation that spans over a thousand years of human history, historian Lars Brownworth and host Lex Fridman explore what the Vikings, the Byzantine Empire, and ancient civilizations can teach us about human nature, governance, and our uncertain future. Brownworth, known for his podcasts "12 Byzantine Rulers" and "The Viking Age," brings a scholar's depth and a storyteller's warmth to examining the lessons embedded in history that remain remarkably relevant today.

## Challenging the Viking Myth

The conversation begins with a surprising revelation that challenges one of the most enduring popular images: the hygiene and grooming habits of the Vikings. "Everyone has this very clear picture of what a Viking looked like and also has no idea what a Viking looked like somehow at the same time," Brownworth observes. "Almost everything about them is wrong that we think of."

Far from the unwashed barbarians of stereotype, Vikings maintained meticulous personal grooming routines that shocked their contemporaries in unexpected ways. They washed themselves regularly and took baths more frequently than their reputation suggests—a fact that drew mockery from the English, who called them "soft" for their bathing habits. "You're going to mock the Vikings for being soft because they took too many baths," Brownworth notes with dry humor.

The Vikings' distinctive blonde hair came from using lye to dye it—a practical solution that also killed lice. Both men and women devoted significant attention to their appearance, leading to what Brownworth describes as almost absurd historical irony: English chroniclers worried that Viking men were too attractive to local women. "In the Danelaw, you're invaded by these people, kicking your rear end militarily, and now they're stealing your women just to insult you as well."

Yet Muslim travelers offered a completely different perspective, writing that the Vikings were "God's filthiest creatures" because of their shared bathing practices—sharing water and even passing cups to the next person after each person spit into them. The lesson, Brownworth suggests with characteristic wit, is simple: shower daily. But beneath this humorous takeaway lies a deeper point about how our assumptions blind us to reality.

## The Spirit of Exploration

Moving from hygiene to the broader lessons of the Viking Age, Brownworth addresses the statue controversies that erupted several years ago—debates about whether monuments to historical figures like Christopher Columbus should be removed because of their problematic actions. He found these debates somewhat beside the point.

"When you have a statue of Christopher Columbus, you're not glorifying every single thing the man ever did and all the bad stuff that comes from this or that," Brownworth explains. "You're honoring something about him—the spirit it takes to cross an ocean not knowing what's on the other side and that spirit of exploration."

The same principle applies to the Vikings. Beyond the practical lessons about personal hygiene lies something more profound: "There's this way you approach the world, this fearless pragmatic approach." As an American, Brownworth sees the Vikings as embodying "the ultimate rags to riches" myth that Americans tell themselves—the idea of starting with nothing and ending as a respected sea king, sung about by poets. The danger, he suggests, is when a society stops cultivating this spirit of exploration and initiative.

## The Byzantine Model: A Thousand Years of Lessons

When Fridman asks about the Byzantine Empire, Brownworth's enthusiasm becomes even more evident. "This is a much bigger one," he begins. "Thousand year history." The Byzantine Empire, he argues, represents perhaps the most valuable case study in how to run a civilization—particularly relevant to understanding modern challenges.

In many ways, the Byzantine Empire resembles the modern United States. Brownworth points to "the polyglot nature of their inhabitants," their Greco-Roman and Judeo-Christian roots, the diversity of their population. Yet in other ways, they were "incredibly alien"—a hierarchical system that "abhorred democracy," men sitting on top of pillars as ascetics, an autocratic structure more extreme than almost anything in the modern world except perhaps the papacy.

This makes the Byzantines both instructive and cautionary. "I think it's a route we could have taken," Brownworth reflects. Their thousand-year history offers a laboratory for examining "how they handled things immigration, inflation, war, peace, diplomacy." The lessons are concrete, practical, and directly applicable to contemporary governance challenges.

## The Rarity of Durable Institutions

Only a handful of states have lasted a thousand years, Brownworth notes. "You can count on the fingers of one hand states that have lasted a thousand years, right? Byzantium and Venice, I think." Venice was itself an offshoot of the Byzantines, making this essentially a single tradition of institutional durability. "That's a good for a government to last a thousand years is a rare thing. Like we should be taking a look at this."

The system wasn't merely Augustus's creation. "He built the system, yeah, but there was a lot of people along the way from Constantine to Justinian to the Basils—there's so many emperors along the way that revolutionized and then restabilized the empire after it was almost falling apart." This suggests that durable institutions require constant vigilance and adaptation, not a single moment of perfect founding.

## The Corruption of Absolute Power

One of the most sobering lessons from Byzantine history concerns the nature of power itself. "What happens when you give someone that level of power?" Brownworth asks. "Like I love Justinian, but I wouldn't have liked to know him. You know, I wouldn't like to be one of his subjects. I love Basil the first, but man was a bloodthirsty tyrant."

The Byzantine experience validates Lord Acton's famous maxim: absolute power corrupts absolutely. "Like that's quite clear throughout Byzantine history. And as technologies become more powerful, absolute power becomes potentially more destructive."

This observation leads to what Brownworth identifies as the defining challenge of the 21st century. "I mean, this is the project for the 21st century," he says. "The 20th and the 21st centuries post-industrial revolution, post the computer, post-nuclear weapons discovery. How do we construct societies that last like the Byzantine Empire did a thousand years?"

Nuclear weapons, he suggests, represent a kind of "Greek fire that you can apply to the entirety of human civilization." Just as Greek fire revolutionized warfare in the ancient world, nuclear technology has fundamentally altered what unchecked power can accomplish. History books will be written about our era—and Brownworth hopes those books will show that we learned lessons from empires like Byzantium rather than repeating their mistakes.

## The Unchanging Nature of Humanity

What ultimately gives Brownworth hope, even amid these weighty considerations, is the profound continuity of human nature across time. During graduate school, he read Frederick Douglass's autobiography and encountered a passage that moved him deeply. Douglass wrote that he could "sit with Plato and Cicero and they would not flinch"—meaning that the great conversation was available to everyone, regardless of skin color, income level, or intelligence.

"I think that's actually why history comes alive for me," Brownworth explains. "Because these are not alien people." When asked how similar ancient people are to us psychologically, his answer is striking: "They were identical to us, which is why we can understand them."

This is why we should read Marcus Aurelius's *Meditations*. "This is not just some dry whatever talking to himself in a culture that you cannot understand and can never recreate. It's a human talking about being human."

## The Diagnosis That Matters

Brownworth's central insight concerns human nature itself. "So we are flawed and broken. That's the human condition. We're going to be flawed and broken." This isn't pessimism but realism—the foundation for understanding history and building functional societies.

He traces different political ideologies to their underlying assumptions about human nature. "If you want to understand history, you have to know about human nature. What is our human nature? If you think it's a blank slate and we can kind of educate ourselves to a utopia or you know, like the Marxists said, then okay. Hasn't really worked out, but okay. If you believe we're basically bad, there's a whole set of things that come with that. If you believe we're basically good, there's a whole set of right."

The most accurate diagnosis, Brownworth suggests, is that we are flawed—a mix of good and evil, capable of both. This explains why teaching kindness to children is essential while teaching unkindness would be redundant. "One of those is natural and one is not."

In perhaps the most quotable exchange of the conversation, Brownworth invokes Oscar Wilde's observation: "All of us are in the gutter, but some of us are looking up at the stars." The task, then, is teaching as many people as possible to look up.

## Key Takeaways

**The Viking hygiene revelation** demonstrates how thoroughly our stereotypes obscure reality. Vikings weren't the unwashed barbarians of popular imagination—they were fastidious about grooming, which contributed to their attractiveness to local populations and drew contradictory criticism from both Christian and Muslim observers. This reminds us that reputation often bears little relationship to reality.

**Statues and historical figures** represent honoring certain qualities rather than endorsing everything about a person. The spirit of exploration that drove Columbus and the Vikings alike deserves recognition even when their actions included atrocities. This framework helps navigate current controversies without pretending historical figures were perfect.

**The Byzantine Empire** offers the most extensive case study in successful governance, having survived for a thousand years while handling challenges remarkably similar to modern ones: immigration, inflation, war, peace, and diplomacy. Their story suggests that institutional durability requires constant adaptation by capable leaders rather than a single perfect founding moment.

**Absolute power's corruption** isn't merely metaphorical—it's documented throughout Byzantine history. As technology increases what power can accomplish, the dangers of concentrated authority grow correspondingly. The 21st century's central project may be constructing institutions that last while preventing destructive concentrations of power.

**Human nature's continuity** means that ancient wisdom remains relevant. Reading Marcus Aurelius or Frederick Douglass offers genuine insight because their authors faced the same fundamental challenges we face. Understanding this continuity should make us humble about quick fixes and utopian promises.
