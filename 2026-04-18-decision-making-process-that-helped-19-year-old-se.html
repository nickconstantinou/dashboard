The Decision-Making Framework That Helped a 19-Year-Old Build a $100 Million Company

An inside look at how Samyr Williams leveraged influencer marketing, strategic sponsorships, and analytical thinking to achieve an early exit

---

In the world of entrepreneurship, stories of young founders striking it big often get dismissed as lucky outliers—flukes that can't be replicated or learned from. But on the My First Million podcast, a nuanced conversation with 19-year-old founder Samyr Williams reveals something more instructive: a methodical approach to decision-making that consistently put him in positions to succeed. Williams, who built and sold his fitness app for nine figures, offers a masterclass in strategic thinking that goes far beyond the typical startup success story. His approach to growth, mentorship, and major life decisions demonstrates how calculated risk-taking—backed by frameworks rather than gut feelings—can dramatically improve outcomes for young entrepreneurs.

The conversation digs into what actually moved the needle for Williams's business: not just the viral TikTok strategies that got initial traction, but the sophisticated marketing evolution that followed, the surprising economics of high-profile sponsorships, and most importantly, the mental models he uses to evaluate opportunities. For anyone building a business or trying to make better decisions in general, this episode offers actionable frameworks that even seasoned executives might overlook.

---

The Two-Phase Growth Strategy That Scaled to $2 Million Monthly Revenue

When Williams describes the growth of his fitness app, he doesn't credit a single viral moment or stroke of luck. Instead, he breaks it down into two distinct phases, each with its own strategy and set of challenges. The first phase, which brought the company to roughly $2 million in monthly revenue, was powered almost entirely by influencer marketing. Williams and his team developed a systematic approach: they would message large numbers of influencers, getting them to create organic, native content about the app that would then generate awareness and downloads among their followers.

This strategy worked extraordinarily well, but like most growth tactics, it had a ceiling. Eventually, the team noticed they were plateauing—continuing to invest in influencer marketing wasn't delivering the same returns, and it wasn't going to be enough to take the business to the next level. The plateau forced a strategic pivot that Williams identifies as crucial to everything that followed.

"We got to about $2 million a month just from influencer marketing," Williams explains. "The thing is after that, we kind of plateaued a little bit and what really got us over the hump, the next step, was running ads, performance ads, on Instagram and TikTok and Facebook." This shift required a significant investment of time and resources to learn the intricacies of paid acquisition, but it became the engine driving most of the company's growth at the time of the conversation. The lesson here isn't just about paid versus organic—it's about recognizing when a strategy has run its course and having the discipline to shift resources toward a new approach before you're forced to by declining metrics.

---

The Mr. Beast Sponsorship: When Short-Term Losses Create Long-Term Gains

One of the most illuminating parts of the conversation involves a major sponsorship deal that, on paper, looked like a failure. Williams and his team paid Mr. Beast $500,000 to be featured in one of his videos—a 30-second segment where a contestant who had been locked in a gym for an extended period (documented in a previous Mr. Beast video) was filmed, and Williams was given an opportunity to come in and talk about the app directly.

The immediate economics were disappointing. Looking at promo code usage and time overlap between the video release and app downloads, Williams estimates they made approximately $350,000 to $400,000 from the sponsorship—making it a clear short-term loss on the $500,000 investment. "I'd say that the results of that right away definitely was a little bit unprofitable," Williams admits. "It's always hard attribution because there's no way like most people are going to the app store directly and downloading. Not many people are clicking a link so you don't know exactly how many people went and downloaded."

But here's where the decision gets interesting: Williams argues the deal was still worthwhile because of the long-term brand equity it created. "You having the brand name Mr. Beast saying we were in a video, we were able to do way more deals afterwards. We had way more credibility with our brand and so the profit from those deals that's enabled for us has exceeded the total amount." This is a crucial distinction that many founders miss when evaluating sponsorships and marketing spend—immediate ROI isn't always the right metric, especially when building a brand that will open doors to partnerships and deals down the line.

Williams also notes that the sponsorship gave him an experience money couldn't buy: flying out to Mr. Beast's studio to watch the filming happen in real-time, an experience that deepened his understanding of how large-scale content creation works. For a young entrepreneur, this exposure to world-class production has intangible value that compounds over time.

The Decision-Making Framework That Changed Everything

When the host asks Williams what makes him different from other young entrepreneurs, he doesn't point to his technical skills or work ethic. Instead, he highlights his decision-making ability—a consistently good track record of choices that compound into success. "I feel like so many of your decisions have been good. In fact, probably that's the most impressive part about you, right?" the host responds. "You're a well-spoken guy, you've obviously hustled, you built a cool app. Like all of that's great, but yeah, the decision-making is actually what stands out to me in a way that like is not common amongst people in general, but definitely amongst younger people, right?"

Williams attributes much of this to his mentorship from Blake Anderson, his co-founder at Cal-I, who he describes as helping him with "so many things, not just business life, but personal life, too." Anderson taught Williams frameworks for analyzing decisions, and Williams specifically highlights Expected Value (EV) as the tool that transformed how he evaluates opportunities.

The EV framework, borrowed from gambling and probability theory, works by multiplying the probability of an outcome by its value. "It's like the percent chance of winning something and then the prize and you multiply them together," Williams explains. "Whatever you have a 100% chance of getting versus the chance of getting versus the chance of losing it all versus the prize money you could have. Whichever is greater, like that's which one you should often pick."

Williams applies this directly to the decision of whether to sell the company. Rather than making the choice based on emotion, fear of missing out, or greed, he evaluates it by asking: What do we think the EV is of keeping the business? What's the chance we actually achieve our growth targets? What's the chance everything falls apart? By running these calculations, he gets to a more objective answer that removes the emotional noise from a high-stakes decision.

The host walks through a practical example: "Let's just pretend somebody offers you $100 for your business. So you have a 100% chance of the $100 or maybe not even 100, maybe 90 cuz it might not close. But okay. So you have you basically the expected value of that, let's call it, is $100, 100% times $100. And then you say, but how? If we keep going, we could get to 500? Okay, great. You what if? Sure. Then you ask the question. Well, what do you think are the chances you could get there? There's a 10% chance. Okay, so 10% times 500 gives you 50. So it's a better decision to take the 100 expected value over the 50, right? Um or let's say you think it's a 20% chance. So now you're even. Okay, but what's the percent chance it all falls apart, goes to zero, right? There's going to be if you keep running it, you have some risk on going risk of it falling apart."

This systematic approach—breaking down decisions into probabilities and values rather than relying on gut feelings or market noise—allows Williams to make decisions with confidence, knowing he's evaluated them objectively rather than being swayed by how he feels on a particular day or what fear and greed might be whispering in his ear.

The College Strategy That Follows Steve Jobs' Playbook

Even Williams's approach to education reveals his strategic thinking. Rather than declaring a business major or following the obvious path that his success might suggest, he went "undecided"—a choice that puzzled some but reflects a deeper understanding of how creativity and business innovation actually work.

"I'm trying to be a computer science major? I first joined the business school or I applied into it, but I switched out and went undecided because I wanted to do more of kind of what Steve Jobs always talked about, how he took like the calligraphy class that really enabled him to build the Macintosh operating system," Williams explains. Instead of grinding through traditional business curriculum, he's taking a VR class focused on the metaverse, a design class, and a business class with a professor he describes as "a really awesome guy."

The parallel to Steve Jobs is instructive. Jobs famously took a calligraphy class at Reed College purely out of interest in beautiful typography, and that aesthetic sensibility later influenced the revolutionary design of the Macintosh computer. Williams is betting that the cross-pollination of ideas from diverse, interesting classes will make him a better founder and decision-maker than strictly practical coursework would. "Long-term that's actually going to help me maybe," he says, acknowledging the uncertainty but believing the optionality is worth more than a traditional path.

The host draws an apt comparison: "Do you know how like China like raises like super athletes? Like they get like two 7-ft people and they like make them breed and like they like set them up to where at the age of 12 the kid has everything they need. This is like you for business—you already have an exit, running a company, you're downloading Steve Job ideas and like going through." It's a striking observation: just as elite athletic programs identify talent early and invest heavily in development, Williams is treating his early success as a launching pad for continuous learning and growth.

Key Insights for Founders and Decision-Makers

Expected Value Calculations Transform High-Stakes Choices

Williams's use of the EV framework demonstrates how probability-based thinking can cut through the noise of emotional decision-making. Rather than asking "Do I want to sell?" or "What will I regret?", he asks "What is the probability-weighted value of each option?" This applies to any major decision: career changes, investments, partnerships, or product pivots. The key is honestly estimating probabilities rather than being overly optimistic or pessimistic about outcomes.

Immediate ROI Isn't Always the Right Metric for Marketing

The Mr. Beast deal teaches that sometimes spending money at a loss makes strategic sense if it builds brand equity that compounds over time. Every founder faces this tension: do you optimize for this quarter's metrics or this decade's positioning? Williams chose the latter, and while he can't prove the exact ROI of the credibility he gained, he clearly believes it exceeded what a simple accounting of promo codes would show.

Recognize When Strategies Have Plateaued

Williams's pivot from influencer marketing to paid ads wasn't because influencer marketing stopped working entirely—it stopped being the highest-leverage use of his team's time and resources. Founders often cling to strategies that once worked, even after they've diminishing returns. The discipline to recognize a plateau and reallocate resources toward new opportunities is a competitive advantage most people underestimate.

Invest in Mentorship and Decision-Making Frameworks

Williams credits much of his success to the frameworks he learned from Blake Anderson. This suggests that the highest-leverage investment for young entrepreneurs isn't necessarily more technical skills or hustle—it's finding experienced mentors who can share mental models for evaluating decisions. Frameworks like EV don't guarantee good outcomes, but they dramatically improve the quality of choices over time.

Curiosity and Optionality Have Long-Term Value

Choosing to be undecided in college, taking VR and design classes purely out of interest, flying to watch a Mr. Beast filming—these choices aren't obviously "productive" in the traditional sense, but Williams treats them as investments in his future creativity and decision-making ability. The best founders often have wide-ranging interests that eventually connect in unexpected ways.

Who Should Listen to This Episode

This conversation is essential listening for early-stage founders who are trying to figure out not just how to build a product, but how to make strategic decisions that compound over time. Williams's willingness to share the specific numbers behind his marketing spend and the exact calculations behind his biggest decisions makes this particularly valuable—too often, entrepreneurship content stays at the level of vague principles without showing the actual math and reasoning underneath.

Aspiring entrepreneurs still in school or early in their careers will find Williams's approach to education particularly thought-provoking. His willingness to optimize for curiosity and optionality rather than traditional credentials challenges the conventional wisdom about what makes a successful founder.

Finally, anyone who struggles with high-stakes decisions—whether to accept an acquisition offer, make a major investment, or pivot their career—will benefit from the EV framework Williams describes. It's a practical tool that can be applied immediately to any decision where the outcomes are uncertain, helping readers move from gut-feeling choices to more objective, defensible reasoning.
