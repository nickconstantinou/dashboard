FPL GW31: Inside the Mind of the World's Number One Manager

*Eric, the current world number one FPL manager, reveals his strategy for navigating the blank gameweek and maintaining his 15-point lead with 13 million competitors behind him*

---

For Fantasy Premier League managers climbing the ranks, the dream of reaching the summit—becoming the best among 13 million players worldwide—seems almost unattainable. Yet for Eric, a medical student balancing his studies with the cutthroat world of FPL, that dream became reality in Gameweek 29 when he claimed the world number one spot for the first time. More remarkably, he held onto it through Gameweek 30, extending his lead to 15 points over the nearest challenger. In a game where a single point can shift rankings by thousands of positions, such a margin feels insurmountable—yet Eric remains characteristically nervous about maintaining his position through what promises to be the most chaotic period of the season: the blank gameweeks.

This exclusive interview captures Eric at the height of his powers, dissecting the decisions that got him there while revealing his strategic approach to one of FPL's most challenging puzzles: the blank gameweek. With a flawless record of zero hits taken all season—a feat virtually unheard of among top managers—Eric's philosophy offers a masterclass in patience, mathematical thinking, and the subtle art of ownership management. His insights reveal not just what moves to make, but why he makes them, and how his medical training influences his analytical approach to FPL decision-making.

Gameweek 30 Recap: How 71 Points Extended the Lead

Coming off a Gameweek 30 performance that yielded 71 points, Eric admitted to feeling both nervous and genuinely satisfied with his output. The highlight of his week came from a somewhat contrarian choice: Dominik Szoboszlai, the Liverpool midfielder who many managers had overlooked in favor of the premium options of Mohamed Salah or Luis Díaz. While the FPL community collectively pivoted toward Liverpool's attacking assets, Eric saw no value in the premium price tags and instead opted for Szoboszlai as what he describes as a "safe" option—one that also delivered through his set-piece responsibilities, particularly on free kicks.

"I just did not see the value in Salah or Díaz," Eric explained. "So I just went with Szoboszlai because he's just been a safe option but also just been scoring on those free kicks. So he really ended up paying off."

Bruno Fernandes captained his team to a huge haul, with Eric confessing he spent the gameweek celebrating every one of the Portuguese midfielder's assists. Meanwhile, Eric's decision to transfer out Erling Haaland to Kai Havertz—a move that required selling his premium striker—proved unfortunate when Haaland blanked, though Eric noted the decision was more about the future blank gameweek strategy than chasing short-term points. The Arsenal defensive assets he held, particularly in the backline, delivered clean sheet points as the Gunners kept another shutout.

Despite the overall success, Eric acknowledged one regret: his chip usage with Havertz, which returned only a single point. "I did post about it a bit like analysis behind it, but statistically it just looked like the better play, especially with the budget now that I have. I can really pick anyone I want. But it didn't pay off—with one point, I was fine with it."

The Ownership Equation: Effective Ownership and Differential Strategy

Among the top 100 or top 1,000 managers in FPL, effective ownership has become a crucial factor that can swing rankings dramatically. Eric confirmed that he actively monitors ownership percentages when making transfer decisions, though he balances this against pure value considerations.

"I definitely look at ownership now," he said. "It's definitely a bigger play, especially now when effective ownership is such a big deal in the top 100 or top thousand."

The clearest example of this in his team is Bruno Fernandes, who has become virtually untouchable in his selection. "Selling Bruno is out of the question because it has such a high effective ownership that I can't even consider it."

However, Eric has maintained some differential picks to keep his team unique at the highest levels. Szoboszlai and Ben Hill have served as his main differential options, with Jao also in the mix—though he notes that Jao has become "very high in ownership" himself. This blend of essential high-ownership players with selective differentials reflects the tightrope walk that elite FPL managers must perform, balancing the safety of popular picks against the need to outpace the field.

The Biggest Regrets: Lessons from a Season at the Top

When asked about his biggest regrets from the season, Eric pointed immediately to his handling of Son Heung-min during December and January. "That was just so many points where I tried to go for a different differential play and then Son just ended up being the right play, and he could have harvested so many points."

Beyond his transfers, Eric cited bench management as another area where he could have improved. "I've also made such bench mistakes and should have trusted Thiago earlier. Plenty of mistakes, but I'm number one so I don't know if it's been that bad."

That final comment carries the confidence of someone sitting exactly where he wants to be—but it's also a reminder that perfection isn't required to reach the summit. Eric's ability to acknowledge mistakes while maintaining forward momentum has clearly served him well, even if the theoretical points lost to suboptimal decisions were never actually subtracted from his total.

The Anti-Hit Philosophy: Why Eric Has Never Taken a Point Deduction

Perhaps the most striking aspect of Eric's season is his pristine record: zero hits taken across all 30 gameweeks. Among the top managers, this is virtually unheard of. The fifth-ranked manager in the overall standings has taken minus 20 points in hits this season alone. Eric's approach couldn't be more different.

"Every single time I've tried to mathematically break it down, a hit has never—I've never seen the value in a hit," Eric explained. "You know, I'd rather bench a player and play someone else on my team."

This philosophy has shaped how he constructs his squad, with significant attention paid to building a strong bench that provides flexibility when players blank. Rather than taking a minus 4 penalty to bring in a player for a single week, Eric prefers to have bench options that can slot in seamlessly when needed. The result is a squad constructed not just for its starting eleven, but for its depth.

The only scenario that might change Eric's stance? A genuine crisis in his starting eleven combined with the absence of any wildcard or other chip. "If I really were in a crisis with a starting 11 and didn't have any wildcard or anything, that might just be the tipping point then."

For most managers, that tipping point comes far more frequently—a testament to how Eric's transfer strategy inherently builds in protection against the need for hasty, costly decisions.

Gameweek 31 Team Analysis: Position by Position

Goalkeeper: Jordan Pickford

Eric's goalkeeper pick comes with an obvious bias: he's a devoted Everton fan, which explains his loyalty to Pickford despite a home fixture against Chelsea that carries some clean sheet risk. However, Eric's reasoning extends beyond sentimentality.

"Even if Everton are at risk of losing the clean sheet, I'd rather that he maybe plays for the save points than swap him out for someone else and take using a transfer, which could be so valuable later to maybe get a clean sheet with someone else. I wouldn't change that."

This decision exemplifies Eric's long-term thinking: a transfer in goalkeeping is rarely worth the cost when fixtures and save potential can fluctuate wildly from week to week.

Defenders: The Trossard Uncertainty

Eric's back three of Destiny Udogie, Virgil van Dijk, and Ben Hill faces a potential complication with Pedro Porro's injury. West Ham manager David Moyes was characteristically cryptic when asked about the defender's status, offering no concrete information about the severity of the problem. With only 10 players if Trossard is unavailable and no defensive transfer made, Eric has a contingency plan.

"My plan would be to sell Gaya to have an extra player on the bench instead of just taking out Trossard directly, because if I had blanking players on the bench anyway, rather have the extra guy on the bench to give flexibility if anything would happen."

For the replacement spot, Eric is considering several options, with Chiao emerging as a favorite for future double gameweeks, while Antonee Robinson against Fulham or a Manchester United defender against a misfiring Bournemouth attack represent one-week punts worth considering. His shortlist reflects careful analysis of both fixtures and future scheduling.

Midfielders: Semeno's Exit and Gordon's Appeal

Eric's midfield currently features Bruno Fernandes as captain, with Callum Wilson as vice-captain, alongside Semeno and Zaboski. With Semeno likely departing in the upcoming transfer, Eric is weighing his options for a one-week replacement, with Newcastle's Anthony Gordon emerging as a particularly attractive option.

"Gordon is playing at the 10 as a midfielder and on penalties—that is exactly what you want as a Premier League manager or like an FPL manager. So that looks ideal, especially against Sunderland, where I can really imagine it being a bloodbath with yellow cards. I would pick him just on the bet of a penalty, just the hope that he maybe gets a penalty."

This comment highlights one of FPL's most underappreciated factors: the penalty upside. Gordon has demonstrated the ability to win spot kicks even in poor individual performances, providing a safety net that pure goal-threat players lack. Eric noted that managers often undervalue penalties, but the mathematics are compelling—a penalty can transform a one-point blank into a five-point haul with bonus points on top.

Palmer was also considered but deemed a difficult pick against Everton, while Odsonne Édouard against Leeds presented another appealing option. The lesson here is clear: matchup analysis matters, but so does identifying which players have multiple paths to points beyond just goals.

Forwards: Havertz, Thiago, and Pedro

The front three of Havertz, Thiago, and Pedro represents what Eric considers an ideal setup. Notably, Havertz was benched in Gameweek 30, which Eric views as actually increasing the probability of a start in Gameweek 31—a case of using rotation patterns to one's advantage rather than being caught off-guard by them.

Key Takeaways for FPL Managers

1. The Mathematics of Hits Don't Work in Your Favor—Except in Crises

Eric's zero-hit season isn't just a fun fact; it reflects a fundamental truth about FPL mathematics. The minus 4 cost of a transfer requires significant overperformance to justify, and more often than not, the player you're bringing in won't deliver enough extra points to offset the penalty. By building squad depth and accepting occasional bench points, Eric has avoided the costly spiral that affects many managers. Only in genuine emergencies—when multiple starters blank and no bench options exist—should hits be considered.

2. Ownership Is a Strategic Tool at the Elite Level

While casual FPL players might focus purely on fixtures and form, top managers must account for effective ownership, particularly in mini-leagues and overall rankings. Bruno Fernandes' high ownership makes selling him a risky proposition regardless of his underlying stats, because falling behind the pack in a critical category compounds the damage. Eric's approach combines essential high-ownership players with selective differentials—a balance that maintains rank security while preserving unique advantages.

3. Squad Construction Must Account for Blank Gameweeks

The blank gameweek strategy isn't just about which players to buy; it's about how to structure your bench and squad depth to survive without fielding a complete XI. Eric's plan to sell Gaya for an extra bench option rather than directly replacing Trossard demonstrates this forward thinking. Managers should always have at least 15 players and consider how blank gameweeks will affect their ability to field eleven starters.

4. Penalty Upside Is an Undervalued Factor

Eric's enthusiasm for Gordon specifically highlights the value of players who can win penalties. A forward who draws fouls in the box provides a floor that pure goal scorers lack—their points don't depend solely on finishing. When evaluating budget and mid-price options, managers should prioritize players with demonstrated penalty involvement over those with favorable fixtures alone.

5. Long-Term Transfer Value Beats Short-Term Gains

Selling Haaland to Havertz didn't pay off immediately, but it positioned Eric for future success by freeing up budget and aligning his squad with blank gameweek demands. This patient approach—valuing the next five gameweeks over the next one—is what separates consistent managers from those chasing points that never seem to arrive. Eric's transfer philosophy prioritizes fixtures, form, and future planning over chasing the hot hand.
