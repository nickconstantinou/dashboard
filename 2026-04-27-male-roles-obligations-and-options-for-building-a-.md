# Introduction

The episode opens with an unusual moment of vulnerability. Andrew Huberman confesses he was nervous driving to the studio—not from lack of preparation, but from genuine respect for his guest and a desire to do the conversation justice. It's a humanizing opening that sets the tone for a discussion intended to grapple with questions about masculinity that neither host fully has answers to, especially regarding what it means to be a young man in 2026.

This tension—between wisdom from elders and the irreducible difference of lived experience at different ages—becomes a through-line. Huberman poses the question directly: How can someone his age (he shares he's 50) genuinely offer guidance to 16, 25, or 30-year-old men when the world those young men inhabit looks so different from the one he navigated decades ago? The question isn't rhetorical. It's the kind of honest reckoning that sets this conversation apart from typical self-help fare.

Galloway's response is both humble and practical. He acknowledges he can't fully know what it's like to be a teenager today—one who has "a casino in your pocket" and porn accessible in ways previous generations didn't. Instead, he commits to three practices: staying open to criticism, grounding his thinking in data rather than pure intuition, and staying close to younger people (he has 15 and 18-year-old sons he observes and asks questions of). He offers a frame that will carry through the entire conversation: "If it really hurts and it's upsetting, it's usually because they're right."

The result is a discussion that doesn't pretend to offer timeless truths divorced from context, but rather a framework rooted in enduring principles—provider, protector, procreator, service—that can flex and adapt to different life circumstances. It's a conversation about what men should strive for, but also about the gap between aspiration and reality, and about the specific landscape young men face today.

# Key Concepts & Arguments

## The Code: Why Men Need an Operating System

Galloway begins with a diagnosis: "I wonder if there's so many lost men right now." The remedy, he argues, is what he calls a "code"—an internal operating system that helps you make hundreds of daily decisions in a generally positive direction. Some people get this code from religion, from military service, from strong family connections, or from institutions like competitive sports that demand discipline and teamwork.

Galloway himself traces his code to two sources: his first job at Morgan Stanley, which instilled professionalism, and his time at UCLA playing sports, which taught him resilience and teamwork. But he recognizes that these traditional institutions are no longer as available or appealing to many young men, which raises the question of what alternatives might serve as a code today.

The question is particularly urgent because Galloway sees a crisis of masculine identity. Young men aren't inheriting clear blueprints for what it means to be a good man, and in their absence, they often default to whatever images are most available—which tend to be either hypermasculine stereotypes or the androgynous, passive figures criticized in progressive circles. Neither serves as a real foundation for a fulfilling life.

## Provider, Protector, Procreator

Galloway breaks down his framework for positive masculinity into three qualities, acknowledging upfront that this is "very reductive" but arguing that frameworks serve as useful scaffolding even when they're not perfectly complete.

**Provider** isn't about the world as it is but "the way the world should be." Every young man, Galloway argues, should have a plan to become economically relevant—though this plan might take many forms. He uses himself as an example: when his partner was working at Goldman Sachs and making substantially more than his academic salary at NYU, he didn't retreat into wounded pride. Instead, he "tried to pick up slack and provide more labor at home and take charge of our finances." The essence of the provider role is being willing to do whatever form of work—whether economic, domestic, or emotional—that the partnership needs at any given time. The key is having a plan and moving toward something, even if that plan evolves.

Galloway is careful to note that economic viability isn't just about money—it's about self-esteem, "sexual currency," and "the esteem of the tribe." This isn't a moral claim about what should matter; it's a realistic assessment of how men are evaluated. Young men who ignore this reality often pay a price in their own confidence and in their attractiveness to potential partners.

**Protector** is about developing skills and strengths such that you can shelter others. The most satisfying masculine moments, Galloway says, come when his family feels safe: "The kids are asleep. My partner feels loved and supported. And I've been able to, hopefully through partnership, through economic viability, been able to provide a warm, comfortable life for them so they can focus on the things that are important to them."

He notes that many men who should be seen as role models—politicians, the wealthiest people in the world—"seem to have skipped the protection part." The money and influence are means, not ends. The ends are creating conditions where others can thrive.

**Procreator** involves a reframe that challenges progressive narratives about male sexuality. Rather than treating young men's sexual desires as pathological or problematic, Galloway argues that desire can be "a tremendous motivator to be a better man." He uses a metaphor: sexual desire is like fire. It can be destructive, but "if you put it in a steel casing with spark plugs, it can create tremendous progress."

His own origin story makes the point. At the Raleigh Hotel in Miami, he saw an attractive woman and, without alcohol to boost his courage, lacked the confidence to approach. But he promised himself he would. He ran back after getting his car, approached her, and introduced himself. "Eighteen months later, our son's middle name is Raleigh." He jokes: "I didn't look at her, Andrew, and think I'd really like lower rates on auto insurance. I looked at her and thought I'd really like to have sex with her." The point isn't that sex is the goal—it's that the desire, channeled into action, became the spark for something meaningful.

Sexual desire, in Galloway's framework, when properly directed, makes you want to "be a better man, have a kindness practice, demonstrate excellence, shower for God's sakes, work out, have a plan, have resilience, have perseverance, demonstrate excellence."

## The Missing Component: Service

Galloway acknowledges what he "missed in the book"—a fourth element that completes the framework: **service**. The question becomes: are you optimizing for attention or for service?

This distinction matters enormously. Someone optimizing for attention wants to be seen, validated, praised. They consume more than they contribute. They take offense easily because criticism threatens their need for validation. Someone optimizing for service wants to add value. They're not devastated by criticism because their worth isn't contingent on constant affirmation. They're too busy producing to worry much about being appreciated.

The litmus test comes from Richard Reeves, whom Galloway calls "sort of my Yoda on this stuff": **surplus value**. Some men are born males, but "never die having become men." The difference isn't a religious ceremony or an age milestone. It's a moment of honest self-reflection where you can ask: Do I add surplus value? Do I create more than I consume? Do I pay more in taxes than I receive in services? Do I listen more than I complain? Do I love more than I'm loved?

Galloway confesses he didn't "get" this for a long time. His approach to relationships was "capitalist"—he wanted a girlfriend better to him than he was to her. He wanted a job where he got paid more than he contributed. It took until his forties to realize "the whole shooting match" was about creating surplus value for others.

He articulates this most powerfully in what he says to his children: "You go to these amazing schools, all these talented people, negative value. Me and your mom, we are constantly investing in you. This would be impossible for you to pass back." This isn't a complaint—it's a recognition that parenthood is fundamentally asymmetric. You give more than you receive, and that's the whole point.

## The Role of Rejection: Learning to Embrace "No"

At the episode's opening, before the main conversation begins, Huberman introduces a pre-interview segment where Galloway describes "the goal" of his mentorship approach: "I'm gonna call you after you've made the approach. You're gonna text me. I did an approach. Did you get a no? Yeah. I got a no. That's exactly the point."

The logic is counterintuitive but powerful. Everyone you admire—every person who has "killed it"—got there through a track record of rejection. The only guarantee is that the path to meaningful achievement runs through a field of "no" responses. "The only thing that got them there was the willingness and the endurance to anticipate no."

This connects to Galloway's own origin story. The decision to approach the woman who would become his wife required pushing through fear of rejection. He sat in his car and thought, "God, you're such a fucking wimp." The self-talk propelled him back into the hotel to make the approach he knew he should make.

The framework for young men, then, isn't just about having a plan—it's about building the resilience to act despite likely rejection. Every "no" is data, not failure. It's confirmation that you're playing the game, taking shots, and putting yourself in position to eventually get a "yes."

## The Comparison Point Problem

Huberman pushes back on one aspect of Galloway's framework: if the goal is to "make better decisions than the average around you," who exactly is the comparison point? How do you avoid the trap of measuring yourself against the worst to feel good about your mediocre performance?

Galloway responds by identifying a common failure mode: the myth that success equals exploitation. There's a narrative, particularly from progressive circles, that anyone who's achieved real success must be cheating, leveraging privilege, or stepping on others. This narrative is comforting because it lets you dismiss successful people as morally compromised, which lets you off the hook.

The corrective, Galloway argues, is choosing role models deliberately and honestly. For him, coming from a background without economic security, his role models were people who'd "figured out economic success." He doesn't pretend his motivations were purely noble—he wanted money in part because it felt like validation. But acknowledging that motivation made it actionable.

On roads and highways, Galloway used to be the driver who felt disrespected by minor slights, who would retaliate against someone who cut him off by cutting them off in return. He was "that asshole." The goal isn't to deny these impulses but to recognize them, feel the feelings, and choose differently—realizing that someone cutting you off in traffic almost certainly isn't a personal attack.

## Mentoring Young Men: Tactical Advice

The conversation shifts from the meta-level to the practical when Galloway describes his approach to mentoring "two or three young men at any given time." These aren't Ivy League students heading to Goldman Sachs. They're young men "struggling," often still living at home, "not these people who went to Brown."

He shares what he calls "the first hack"—though the transcript cuts off before fully revealing the details. In the pre-interview, he had been discussing "gambling" on options markets ("I sell calls and puts") and consuming porn, both framed as vulnerabilities he's willing to confess. The pattern seems to be: before offering guidance, demonstrate that you yourself are a work in progress, that perfection isn't the prerequisite for trying to help others.

The mentorship approach isn't about having all the answers. It's about proximity and relationship—being present in a young man's life, sharing what has (and hasn't) worked, and modeling that growth is possible at any age.

# Detailed Takeaways

## 1. Develop a Personal Code—Even If It's Not Traditional

Galloway argues that men without an internal operating system tend to drift into poor decisions under pressure. Traditional sources of code (religion, military, strong family) aren't available to everyone, so the task becomes creating a personal code through deliberate practice.

**Why it matters**: Without a code, you default to whatever feels good in the moment. With a code, you have a reference point for navigating ambiguity and difficulty. When you know what you stand for, decisions become easier to evaluate.

**How to apply it**: Identify the institutions, relationships, and practices that have shaped your best moments. Athletics taught discipline? A mentor modeled generosity? A job demanded professionalism? Formally articulate the principles these experiences taught you. Revisit and revise as you grow.

## 2. Channel Desire into Growth, Don't Suppress It

Galloway's reframing of sexual desire as a motivator is controversial but useful. The claim isn't that sexual access should be the goal—it's that the energy that drives desire can be harnessed for self-improvement when it's not immediately gratified.

**Why it matters**: Many young men feel ashamed of their sexual desires, especially under contemporary progressive frameworks that can frame male sexuality as inherently problematic. That shame doesn't eliminate the desire—it just removes its constructive potential. If desire becomes a source of shame rather than fuel, young men lose a powerful motivator for self-improvement.

**How to apply it**: Rather than suppressing or indulging desire immediately, treat it as information. The desire to be found attractive is a desire to grow. Let it push you toward the gym, toward better grooming, toward developing interesting skills and perspectives. The goal isn't to deny desire—it's to direct it.

## 3. Aim for Surplus Value in Every Relationship and Endeavor

Galloway's most demanding principle: stop keeping score in relationships. The goal isn't to get back what you put in—it's to consistently give more than you receive. This applies to friendships, marriages, parenting, professional relationships, and community involvement.

**Why it matters**: Most relationship conflict emerges from perceived imbalances—someone feels they're giving more than they're getting. If you shift your goal from fairness to surplus, you eliminate most of this conflict. You're not being taken advantage of—you're creating surplus value that is its own reward.

**How to apply it**: In your most important relationships, make a conscious decision to give more than you receive—for at least six months to a year. Don't keep score. Don't expect reciprocity. At the end of that period, assess honestly: do you feel depleted or energized? Most people find that surplus-giving is more sustainable than transactional relationship management.

## 4. Expect Rejection—Make It Your Performance Metric

Galloway flips the script on success: instead of avoiding rejection, track it. If you're not getting "no" responses regularly, you're not putting yourself in enough positions to succeed.

**Why it matters**: Fear of rejection is one of the primary barriers to taking risks. When you reframe rejection as evidence that you're trying, it loses some of its sting. Every "no" is proof that you're in the game.

**How to apply it**: Set a goal for approaches, not outcomes. Ask someone on a date. Pitch an idea at work. Submit a proposal. Request a meeting. Track how many "no" responses you get. Celebrate the "nos" as confirmation that you're playing the game.

## 5. Distinguish Between Service and Attention-Seeking

Galloway identifies a diagnostic test for evaluating your daily behavior: are you doing this for attention or for service? The distinction isn't always obvious—sometimes service gets attention, and sometimes attention-seeking looks like service.

**Why it matters**: Attention-seeking behavior is ultimately hollow. You never get enough validation, and the quest for it depletes you. Service-oriented behavior generates its own momentum—you're creating something that has value beyond whether anyone acknowledges it.

**How to apply it**: At the end of each day, review your actions through this lens. Did you post that social media update to inform people or to get likes? Did you help that colleague because it added value or because you wanted to be seen as helpful? The goal isn't to eliminate attention-seeking entirely—it's to notice when it's controlling your behavior and to choose service when you can.

# Who This Is For

This conversation will be most valuable for **young men between 16 and 35 who feel adrift**—who sense that the traditional scripts for masculinity don't fit, but who haven't found a replacement framework that feels authentic and actionable. If you're someone who suspects you might be a "lost man" but who has enough self-awareness to question rather than default to resentment, Galloway's Provider-Protector-Procreator framework offers a starting point for thinking about masculine purpose.

It's also valuable for **men in their 30s and 40s who have achieved external success but feel unfulfilled**. Galloway's discussion of surplus value and the shift from attention-seeking to service speaks directly to men who have checked the conventional boxes—career, relationships, family—and found that the boxes don't contain what they expected. His confession that he didn't understand surplus value until his forties is permission to stop expecting early enlightenment.

For **women who want to understand men better**—whether in relationships, friendships, or professional contexts—this conversation offers a rare window into how thoughtful men actually think about masculine identity, desire, and purpose. It's not a guide to manipulating men</p>
