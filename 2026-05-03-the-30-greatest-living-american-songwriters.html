# The 30 Greatest Living American Songwriters: How The New York Times Built the Definitive List

Key Insights:

  • The balloting process generated over 700 individual names from a diverse pool of music experts including working musicians, producers, label executives, critics, scholars, and DJs
  • Billy Joel was notably excluded despite universal agreement he belongs on such a list—the decision hinged on his placement relative to peers like Springsteen and Dylan, and the project's goal of representing the broader "piano man schmaltz tradition" without redundancy
  • Taylor Swift revealed a specific phonetic rule: she refuses to let one word end on the same letter the next word starts with—changing "talk real low" to "talk real slow" in "Our Song" for this reason

  • Taylor explicitly credited criticism as "a huge jumping off point" and "creative writing prompt"—directly naming Blank Space and Anti-Hero as songs that wouldn't exist without negative feedback about her personality and romantic history

    Introduction

    In May 2020, The New York Times Magazine undertook an ambitious project: identifying the 30 greatest living American songwriters across all genres. The result wasn't just a list—it was a comprehensive exploration of American musical creativity, complete with extended interviews with the artists themselves. In this episode of The Daily, hosted by Michael Barbaro, Times journalists pull back the curtain on how such a contentious, subjective list gets made, and offer unprecedented access to the songwriting minds behind some of America's most beloved music.

    The episode features Sasha Weisz, deputy editor of the New York Times Magazine and one of the project's central architects, along with Joe Coscarelli, the Times' pop music reporter who conducted one of the project's most anticipated interviews—with Taylor Swift, who hadn't sat for a substantive media conversation about her creative process in years. Together, they illuminate not just the list itself, but the living art of songwriting in America today.

    What emerges is a meditation on craft, legacy, and the evolving definition of what it means to be a songwriter in the 21st century—from the confessional folk tradition inaugurated by Bob Dylan in the 1960s to the production-heavy, collaborative sounds of contemporary pop and R&B.

    ---

    The Making of the List: How 700 Names Became 30

    Drawing on the Wisdom of the Crowd

    Sasha Weisz explains that the project began not with a committee's opinions, but with a deliberate effort to "canvas a lot of different music experts from all walks of life." The magazine sent ballots to an eclectic mix of professionals: working musicians and songwriters, producers, label executives, multiple generations of music critics, editors of music magazines, scholars, authors, DJs, and music supervisors. "We really tried to go wide," Weisz notes.

    The result was a preliminary list containing over 700 individual names—an overwhelming starting point that reflected the sheer diversity of American songwriting talent. This raw abundance would later require brutal winnowing.

    The Debate Room: Passion, Contentment, and Occasional Crushing Defeat

    What followed the balloting was a months-long process of in-person deliberation, with Times critics and affiliated journalists gathering at the magazine for extended sessions of debate. Weisz describes the atmosphere as "passionate, contentious, sometimes heated." Music played constantly in the room. Different factions championed their favorites. And when consensus couldn't be reached, painful decisions had to be made.

    "Some people felt crushed at various points," Weisz admits. "It was crushing."

    The obvious candidates—Stevie Wonder, Dolly Parton, Bob Dylan, Carole King—"balloted high above the rest" and secured their spots early. But the real battles emerged in the middle and lower tiers, where the committee had to balance representation across genres, eras, and songwriting traditions.

    Complicating the Bard: Why the List Needed to Be More Than Dylan and His Disciples

    One of the project's core ambitions was to challenge the popular image of the songwriter as solitary bard with acoustic guitar. Weisz explains that this archetype, while important, is "relatively new"—really only dating to the 1960s when Dylan and others inaugurated that mode. "We wanted to represent these other traditions," she says.

    This meant difficult questions about how to handle Nashville songwriting, how to incorporate producers who layer in production and sound that threads through R&B, soul, and hip-hop, and how to honor the "schmaltz" tradition—the emotionalism, opera-like bigness of storytelling represented by artists like Billy Joel, who ultimately didn't make the final 30 despite being considered a great songwriter by nearly everyone in the room.

    The final list, Weisz argues, "reflects the world that we move through"—from bar mitzvah speakers to TikTok, from karaoke standards to avant-garde indie weirdness. "Songwriting is not just one kind of songwriting," she says, and the list tells that story.

    The Billy Joel Question: A Case Study in Impossible Choices

    Michael Barbaro, ever the advocate, raises the conspicuous absence of Billy Joel—"the poet laureate of New York, the man who turned dinner at an Italian restaurant into an opera." Weisz doesn't disagree that Joel belongs among America's great songwriters, but explains the cruel arithmetic of limiting a list to 30.

    "When you're making a list of 30 and you're rooting it in a balloting process, you have to make hard choices. And there were people who were higher than he was." Additionally, the project needed to represent the "piano man schmaltz tradition" without redundancy. Some critics in the room argued that compared to peers like Springsteen and Dylan, Joel's lyricism fell just short. "He's not quite there," Weisz acknowledges. "And there are others who are better."

    Billy Joel represents what was cut, not what was deemed unworthy.

    Inside the Taylor Swift Interview: Craft, Criticism, and the Divine Spark

    Joe Coscarelli's Approach: Starting at the Beginning

    Joe Coscarelli, who has covered Taylor Swift as a journalist for twelve of his fifteen years at The Times, was assigned the project's most coveted interview. He explains his strategy: starting with the first line of the first song of her first album—"Tim McGraw" from 2006—to trace the thread forward through her entire career.

    "We used that to get into all of these little songwriting tricks that she still does today that started with her initial schooling in what it means to make a record."

    The Plot Twist: A Signature Technique Since Day One

    One of Swift's earliest and most persistent techniques is what Coscarelli calls the "plot twist" or "perspective shift at the end of a song"—a very country music device where the narrative pivots unexpectedly. In "Tim McGraw," she's singing about lost love, a letter left on a doorstep. Then the bridge reveals: "I wrote this song, and I hope he hears it."

    The same technique appears in "Our Song": the romance builds, and then in the final moments, "I grabbed a pen and an old napkin, and I wrote down Our Song."

    Decades later, this self-aware reveal still appears in her work. In "The Last Great American Dynasty" from the album *folklore*, she tells the story of a woman who lived in a beach house, sold it, and it was later bought by... Swift herself. "You're like, every time I get to that part when I would sing it on tour," she told Coscarelli, "I just like wanted my grin to go from here to here, but that looks crazy."

    The emo Revelation: Where Her Clever Wordplay Really Comes From

    During the interview, Swift revealed an unexpected influence: emo music. "I was intensely impacted by emo music," she said, citing Dashboard Confessional, Chris Carrabba, Fall Out Boy, and Pete Wentz's lyrics. This was a revelation for Coscarelli, who grew up alongside Swift during the MySpace era.

    Emo bands were known for taking common phrases and "twisting the knife of it"—turning a cliché into something surprising. "I'm just a notch in your bedpost, but you're just a line in a song. Drop a heart, break a name." Swift told Coscarelli she'd read those lyrics and immediately think, "Oh my god."

    This connects directly to a songwriting principle she articulated: "I really gravitate towards juxtaposition and polarity in a line." She offers examples from her own work: "What could you possibly get for the girl who has everything and nothing all at once?" or "Our coming of age has come and gone."

    A Phonetic Rule: The Letter That Ends and Starts

    Swift shared a specific personal rule that even longtime students of her work might not know: she doesn't like one word ending on the same letter that the next word begins with. Coscarelli pressed her on this, and she confirmed it with a concrete example from "Our Song."

    The original line was "when you're on the phone and you talk real low." But she caught the phonetic overlap: the "l" at the end of "real" preceded the "l" at the start of "low." So she changed it to "when you talk real slow."

    This attention to sonic texture—even in early, seemingly naive country-pop—suggests a musicality embedded in Swift's songwriting from the very beginning.

    The Bridge as Canvas: Taylor's Most Celebrated Technique

    Swift is famous for her bridges—those standalone sections near the end of songs that often deliver the most emotionally devastating moments. She explained her philosophy to Coscarelli using an extended artistic metaphor:

    "I think the importance for me of a bridge... it just feels like we're painting a picture, we're setting a scene. You can start painting the picture in the verse. You can get to the heart of it at the chorus, but then the bridge can be where you zoom back—you walk 20 feet back—and you see what this entire painting was supposed to be. Like, you've seen brushstrokes, you've seen the color tones, but the bridge can be when you step back and you feel everything that that piece of art was supposed to make you feel."

    She further identified a specific variant: the "rant bridge," where stream-of-consciousness pours out with intrusive thoughts, metaphor, discussion, and almost-shouting. "You want this ramp bridge to feel the most intense of what that feeling is that you're trying to establish over the course of the song."

    How Songs Actually Come to Her: The Divine Spark

    Perhaps the most magical moment in the interview comes when Swift describes the spontaneous way an entire song can arrive. She recounts a recent example: while on vacation with boyfriend Travis Kelce, she spent the car ride explaining Elizabeth Taylor's legacy—her fight for artist rights, her exploitation by the media, the debate over whether her eyes were blue or violet.

    "I go on and on explaining to Travis, like, why I love Elizabeth Taylor so much," she said. She could imagine his expression: "just sort of like, okay. Interesting."

    Then, after arriving home, it hit her: an intrusive melody in her head, the lyric forming itself—"I'd cry my eyes violet, Elizabeth Taylor." She scrambled to record it.

    "For somebody who is writing songs constantly, I think a conversation easily becomes art. But that's like one of those spontaneous places where it floats down like a cloud in front of you, and all you have to do is grab it."

    Responding to Critics: Turning Hostility into Fuel

    One of the episode's most revealing threads involves Swift's relationship with criticism. Coscarelli notes that she's "exceedingly sensitive" to allegations that other people write her songs—a charge that emerged early in her career. Her third album, *Speak Now* (2010), was written entirely alone: "I was coming from this big massive moment with *Fearless*—first country album to win Album of the Year at the Grammys—and there were all these people saying, 'Look at this little girl who's writing her songs.' I was like, these discussions can lead to a really bad place if I don't do something to counteract them."

    The result was proof of her authorship. But Swift is also savvy about how criticism can serve her art. She explained this philosophy directly:

    "Criticism has been a huge fuel for me. It's been a huge jumping off point, like a creative writing prompt or something."

    She named specific songs: "Blank Space would not exist if I hadn't had people be like, 'Here's a slideshow of all her boyfriends.'" And "Anti-Hero is a song that I'm so proud of still. That song doesn't exist if I don't get criticized for every aspect of my personality that people have a problem with."

    Her advice to young artists is pointed: "Don't respond to trolls in your comments. That's not what we want from you. We want your art. Make art about this. Don't post it—write about it."

    Detailed Takeaways

    1. The Best Lists Are Built on Crowdsourced Wisdom, Then Refined Through Rigorous Debate

    The Times' methodology offers a template for any high-stakes subjective ranking: first, collect as many perspectives as possible to avoid institutional blind spots; second, subject that data to extended human deliberation. The 700-name ballot ensured the committee couldn't simply default to conventional wisdom. The debate sessions ensured the final 30 reflected actual conviction, not just name recognition. Neither step alone would have sufficed.

    2. Genre Diversity Is a Feature, Not a Compromise

    The project's most interesting debates centered on how to represent Nashville songwriting, production-heavy R&B, hip-hop production, and "schmaltz" alongside the confessional folk tradition. Weisz's point—that the songwriter-as-bard is historically novel and shouldn't crowd out other traditions—suggests that lists claiming to rank "the best" often reveal more about their creators' biases than about actual quality. The most useful lists map a landscape, not a hierarchy.

    3. Songwriting Technique Can Be Traced Across an Entire Career

    Coscarelli's interview with Swift demonstrates how a journalist can use a songwriter's earliest work as a lens for understanding their latest. The meta-fictional reveal at the end of "Tim McGraw" (2006) and the similar self-aware twist at the end of "The Last Great American Dynasty" (2020) are separated by 14 years but share a fundamental DNA. This suggests that authentic artistic voices often exhibit identifiable signatures that persist across decades and reinventions—a finding with implications for critics, historians, and the songwriters themselves.

    4. Criticism Is Raw Material, Not the Enemy

    Swift's explicit framing of criticism as "fuel" and a "creative writing prompt" offers a counter-intuitive lesson for artists. The instinct to defend oneself or silence critics is natural but, she argues, counterproductive. Blank Space and Anti-Hero exist because Swift chose to engage criticism on its own terms—as material for art rather than ammunition for argument. This requires psychological distance but transforms the most hostile feedback into professional assets.

    5. The Bridge Is Where the Real Work Happens

    Swift's bridge-as-canvas metaphor is one of the episode's most practically useful insights for aspiring songwriters. The verse establishes, the chorus declares, but the bridge pulls back to reveal what the entire song has been about. Her "rant bridge" concept—stream-of-consciousness at maximum intensity—represents an advanced technique for songs that need to release accumulated pressure in a single sustained burst. Not every song needs a bridge, but when it does, it must earn its position.

    Who This Is For

    This episode and the broader project behind it will appeal most directly to several audiences. Songwriters and aspiring musicians will find Taylor Swift's technical insights—her phonetic rules, her bridge philosophy, her approach to juxtaposition—immediately applicable to their own work. Music critics and journalists will benefit from the Times' transparent methodology, which offers a replicable model for subjective rankings in any creative field. Dedicated fans of contemporary pop will enjoy the rare depth of Swift's interview, which goes far beyond typical promotional conversations to examine craft, legacy, and artistic philosophy. And anyone interested in American cultural history will find the project a useful map of songwriting traditions—from the formal innovation of Dylan to the production complexity of modern R&B to the emotional directness of Nashville and beyond.

    The core value of this episode is its demonstration that songwriting is both more mysterious and more learnable than it appears. The divine spark Swift describes—"floating down like a cloud"—arrives unpredictably, but it's arrival is enabled by decades of craft development, self-education, and willingness to absorb influence (emo, country, pop) into a distinctive voice. Great songwriting, this episode suggests, is built from both inspiration and technique, both reputation and craft.
