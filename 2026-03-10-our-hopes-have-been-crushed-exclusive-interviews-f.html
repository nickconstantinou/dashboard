'Our Hopes Have Been Crushed': Exclusive Interviews from Inside Iran

A FRANCE 24 Report on Civilian Perspectives Amid Regional Conflict

---

Introduction

In a rare window into life inside Iran during an escalating regional conflict, FRANCE 24 has obtained exclusive interviews with several Iranian citizens who describe a nation caught between fear, uncertainty, and a deepening crisis of legitimacy for the Islamic Republic. Despite stringent internet restrictions, the news organization's team managed to reach individuals who shared their experiences anonymously for their safety.

The report comes at a moment of profound upheaval in the region. According to the interviews, Iranians have been subjected to approximately ten consecutive days of what one interviewee describes as "incessant bombing throughout the country." The strikes have targeted critical energy infrastructure, including an oil depot in the capital Tehran, creating secondary environmental hazards and triggering fuel supply concerns across the nation.

Perhaps most significantly, the civilian voices captured in this report reveal a complex political landscape. Many Iranians express despair about recent political developments—specifically the nomination of what appears to be a successor to the current supreme leader—while simultaneously questioning whether the ongoing conflict might finally bring about systemic change. Yet the prevailing sentiment among those interviewed is one of profound uncertainty about both the immediate future and the ultimate direction of the war.

---

Key Concepts and Arguments

The Political Crisis: Leadership Succession and Public Despair

The report opens with reference to what the interviewees describe as devastating political news: the nomination of a new figure to a position of supreme leadership, replacing a predecessor who was killed. One interviewee, identified only as Moshtaba, referred to this development as "the worst news we could have had during this war." The emotional weight of this statement is significant—these words come from someone who already described their circumstances as "living in difficult circumstances" prior to this announcement.

Another voice, Darish, a resident who communicated with the FRANCE 24 team via encrypted message, articulated what appears to be a widely held sentiment: "Since his nomination, our hopes have been crushed." This phrasing suggests that many Iranians had invested political hope in the possibility of leadership change, perhaps viewing the current conflict as an opportunity for broader transformation. The crushing of these hopes, according to Darish, has created a belief that "they might announce an end to the war without any concrete change" and that "the objectives of the people won't be achieved." The fear is that Iran will simply return to the status quo ante—back to "step one or even" worse.

This perspective reveals a nuanced political understanding among ordinary Iranians. Despite the apparent divisions among Iran's leadership and various factions within the regime, Darish suggests that these competing groups share at least one thing in common: "They want to get rid of the Islamic Republic." The implication is that while internal power struggles may exist, the fundamental structure of the regime remains the primary target of popular opposition.

Civilian Life Under Bombardment

The civilian experience described in these interviews paints a picture of daily life disrupted and increasingly dangerous. For approximately ten days, according to one resident, the country has faced continuous aerial bombardment. The strike on an oil depot in Tehran created additional suffering beyond the immediate damage—the resulting fire has filled the air with pollution, forcing at least some residents to flee the capital temporarily.

One interviewee described venturing outside "once or twice to go to the shops," suggesting that most people have been sheltering in their homes. The primary motivation for leaving, according to this person's account, was twofold: first, the polluted air from the uncontained fire created health concerns, and second, "for the last few days I have been shut in at home" and "needed to get some fresh air." This description suggests a population under significant psychological strain, confined to their residences not only by fear of attack but by the secondary consequences of infrastructure damage.

The targeting of energy infrastructure emerges as a particular point of contention among the civilian population. One interviewee expressed a sentiment that reveals both a desire for self-preservation and a strange form of patriotism: "I'd prefer if the bombs they are dropping hit military targets." This person went on to enumerate what they viewed as more legitimate military alternatives: "There are so many bases, so many checkpoints and barracks rather than targeting our oil depots."

Economic Hardship and Infrastructure Collapse

The strikes on Iranian energy infrastructure have created immediate economic consequences that affect ordinary citizens directly. According to the report, petrol stations across the country have implemented severe rationing, limiting customers to just 20 liters per day. One interviewee captured the widespread anxiety this has created: "It's very worrying. A lot of people want to be able to travel."

The disconnect between official statements and reality has only deepened public distrust. These interviewees note that "the authorities say there is no shortage, but this rationing shows the truth." This represents a familiar pattern in authoritarian states where official narratives diverge so dramatically from lived experience that citizens become attuned to reading the regime's true situation through practical constraints rather than public announcements.

The broader implications of this infrastructure damage extend far beyond immediate convenience. Oil depots and energy facilities represent not only economic assets but also strategic resources whose destruction can have cascading effects on a nation's ability to function. For a country already under heavy international sanctions, the loss of domestic refining capacity creates compounding pressures on an economy already struggling with multiple crises.

Regime Resilience and Repressive Tactics

Despite the severity of the bombing campaign and the apparent targeting of leadership figures, the interviewees suggest that the Islamic Republic retains significant repressive capacity. One person provided a striking observation: "Supporters of the regime take to the streets every night between 8 and 10:00 p.m. It's as if they have a fixed meeting."

This description of organized pro-regime demonstrations raises questions about the authenticity and spontaneity of such gatherings. The precise timing—"every night" within a specific two-hour window—suggests organized, possibly mandatory participation rather than organic popular support. Regardless of their authenticity, these demonstrations serve a political purpose: projecting an image of regime stability and popular backing in the face of military pressure.

The physical presence of security forces remains overwhelming. One interviewee estimated that "about 70% of the regime is still there," and described the streets as filled with "military vehicles like you see in Hollywood films." The description of these armored vehicles is particularly evocative: "These vehicles are so frightening, we don't dare to approach them." This fear is significant—it suggests that the regime's capacity for violence remains intact and is being actively deployed to maintain control over the civilian population.

Uncertainty About the Conflict's Future

Perhaps the most prominent theme across all these interviews is profound uncertainty about how the conflict will develop and end. One interviewee articulated this directly: "I don't understand what their plan is. What's their endgame? They speak so confusingly—whether it's Trump, Netanyahu, or"—the statement appears to be cut off, but the implication is clear.

This confusion reflects the broader challenge facing ordinary citizens caught in a conflict driven by geopolitical calculations made by leaders in Washington, Tehran, and Jerusalem. The average Iranian civilian appears to have little clarity about what victory would look like, what concessions might be acceptable, or how the current military escalation might transition to either negotiation or deeper conflict.

The lack of any "letup since it started" creates psychological pressure that compounds with each passing day. War that becomes normalized—sustained over weeks and months rather than consisting of discrete campaigns—creates its own form of exhaustion and trauma, distinct from the acute stress of initial attacks.

---

Detailed Takeaways

The nomination of a new supreme leader has crushed hopes for political change: Among the most significant findings from this report is the apparent despair among many Iranians about recent leadership developments. Whether this refers to an actual succession plan or to other political appointments, the emotional response indicates that significant portions of the population had invested hope in the possibility that the current conflict might catalyze fundamental political change. The feeling that this hope has been extinguished represents a crucial data point for understanding the political psychology of the Iranian population during wartime.

Infrastructure targeting is creating civilian suffering and economic crisis: The deliberate targeting of oil facilities has had consequences far beyond military objectives. Petrol rationing at 20 liters per day disrupts everything from commuting to work to transporting goods to basic economic activity. The environmental damage from burning oil facilities adds a public health dimension to an already complex situation. This suggests that even if the military logic of such strikes is sound, the humanitarian costs are substantial and may be counterproductive to any goal of winning "hearts and minds."

The regime retains substantial coercive capacity: Despite leadership losses and infrastructure damage, the 70% figure cited by one interviewee suggests the regime remains functionally intact. The visible presence of military vehicles, the organized pro-regime demonstrations, and the continued ability to restrict movement all indicate that the Islamic Republic has not been militarily crippled to the point of collapse. Any expectation that bombing campaigns would trigger internal revolution appears, at least so far, to be unmet.

Civilians bear the costs of conflict without understanding its purposes: The repeated expressions of confusion about the conflict's goals, the endgame, and the intentions of various actors reveal a population caught in a conflict they did not choose and cannot influence. This creates conditions for alienation from all parties to the conflict—neither the domestic regime nor foreign adversaries appear to be acting in the interests of ordinary Iranians as expressed in these interviews.

Propaganda and information warfare shape domestic narratives: The disconnect between official claims of "no shortage" and the visible rationing at petrol stations illustrates how the regime maintains control through information management. Simultaneously, the existence of these interviews—obtained despite internet restrictions—demonstrates the persistence of channels for information to flow outward, suggesting the regime's control over the information environment is incomplete.

---

Who This Is For

This report holds particular value for several distinct audiences. Policy analysts and academics studying Iran, regional security dynamics, or the relationship between military conflict and political change will find the firsthand civilian perspectives invaluable for understanding the human dimensions of the current escalation. The interviews provide ground-level data that complicates simplistic narratives about either regime vulnerability or popular uprising.

Journalists and media professionals can learn from the methodology used to obtain these interviews—encrypted messaging, anonymization, and careful security practices—that provides a template for reporting from restricted environments. The report also demonstrates the continuing importance of international journalism in providing windows into places where domestic media cannot operate freely.

Humanitarian organizations and advocacy groups will find the descriptions of civilian suffering, infrastructure damage, and economic disruption essential for understanding the scope of humanitarian needs. The petrol rationing alone has implications for food distribution, healthcare access, and general economic activity that extend far beyond the immediate conflict zone.

General readers seeking to understand the lived experience of people in the region will benefit from these direct voices. Too often, coverage of military and diplomatic developments abstracts away the human reality of those who cannot leave, who must continue living under bombardment while powerful actors debate their futures in distant capitals.

Ultimately, this report serves as a reminder that behind every military statistic and diplomatic maneuver are real people trying to survive, understand what is happening to them, and maintain hope for a future different from their present anguish. The voices captured by FRANCE 24 have provided a rare and invaluable service: ensuring that the experiences of ordinary Iranians are not lost in the noise of conflict.
