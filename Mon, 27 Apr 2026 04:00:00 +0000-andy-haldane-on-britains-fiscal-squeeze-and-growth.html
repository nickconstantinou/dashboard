# Key Insights

  • UK GDP per capita ranking: The UK ranks 6th globally by total GDP but falls to 26th-27th by GDP per capita—a distinction Haldane describes as "something of a shock for most Brits" and directly ties to stagnant living standards.
  • Fiscal straitjacket quantified: The UK has run no fiscal surplus this century, pays over £100 billion annually in debt interest, and welfare/pensions spending (~£330+ billion) now matches or exceeds total income tax revenue—meaning "every penny" collected in income tax goes toward benefits.
  • Defense funding arithmetic: Closing the defense spending gap (from ~2.5% to ~3.5% of GDP) requires approximately £30 billion in additional annual spending—Haldane states this "cannot be achieved from any budget other than NHS or welfare/pensions."
  • Private sector is "rooted for lift-off": Despite fiscal deterioration, UK households and companies maintain strong balance sheets with modest debt levels by historical standards—Haldane calls this "plenty of fuel in the tank" but emphasizes that something must shift to incentivize spending over saving.
  • UK innovation paradox: The UK ranks 3rd globally (behind the US and China) in the number of VC-backed scale-up businesses ("kilties and thoroughbreds"), yet remains 27th in GDP per capita—highlighting a critical failure to nurture and retain these companies domestically.
  • Pension funds' missing home bias: Haldane identifies that UK pension funds are uniquely the only ones globally without a home bias toward their own companies, creating a "plumbing problem" where trillions of pounds sit in cash or overseas equities rather than financing UK growth.

    Introduction

    The United Kingdom faces a paradox that has confounded economists and politicians alike: a nation once considered an economic powerhouse now finds itself ranked among the lower tier of developed economies when measuring individual prosperity. While the UK maintains its position as the world's sixth-largest economy by total output, its citizens are, on average, poorer than their counterparts in countries like Slovenia, Estonia, and Lithuania—a jarring reality that speaks to decades of underperformance.

    In a candid conversation on *Merryn Talks Money*, Andy Haldane—former chief economist at the Bank of England and now chief executive of the Royal Society of Arts—confronts these uncomfortable truths head-on. Speaking with host Merryn Somerset-Webb, Haldane dismantles any pretense that Britain's economic difficulties admit easy solutions while simultaneously identifying genuine reasons for optimism. His analysis cuts to the heart of the nation's twin predicament: a public finances sector strangled by debt service costs and an aging population, alongside a private sector possessing remarkable financial strength but lacking the conditions to deploy it productively.

    The timing of this discussion carries particular resonance. As of late January 2025, the UK stock market has shown signs of extraordinary fragility—a profit warning from Cranswick, down 39% in minutes, serves as but one data point illustrating what Haldane describes as a "high beta leveraged bet on the global economy." Yet within this pessimism, Haldane finds threads of genuine hope, rooted not in government intervention but in the latent power of British enterprise itself.

    ---

    The Fiscal Straitjacket: Why the UK Has No Room to Maneuver

    Haldane opens by establishing the gravity of Britain's fiscal position with a clarity rarely heard from former central bankers. The UK has not run a fiscal surplus in the entire 21st century—a remarkable admission that underscores how successive governments have borrowed against future generations to fund current consumption. The debt stock relative to national income has traveled sharply upward, and the country now commits over £100 billion annually simply to service interest payments on existing debt. "I would say there is very little, if any, fiscal space left unless some hard choices are made about cutting spending," Haldane states flatly.

    The tax situation compounds this constraint. Historically, the UK has settled around a ceiling of 36-37% of GDP in total taxation. The country now approaches 40%, with the Office for Budget Responsibility having indicated—albeit in carefully calibrated language—that further increases risk shrinking revenues by disincentivizing work and entrepreneurship. The UK may already be at or near the peak of the Laffer curve, the theoretical point beyond which higher rates produce lower absolute revenue. "We are taxed out as a nation," Haldane observes. "We are maxed out when it comes to borrowing. So the knife must fall on public spending if we are to chisel out some more room."

    This fiscal reality creates the context for one of the most contentious policy debates of the current parliamentary session: defense spending. Haldane acknowledges Britain's genuine security needs in an increasingly volatile world while recognizing that the treasury simply lacks the funds to meet them conventionally. The arithmetic is stark: reaching the NATO-commended 3.5% of GDP target from the current ~2.5% requires an additional 1% of GDP—roughly £30 billion per year. "You're not getting that out of any budget other than NHS or welfare and pensions," Haldane declares.

    The Triple Lock Dilemma

    Haldane turns to the welfare and pensions budget as the logical source of fiscal relief, though he does so with evident awareness of the political sensitivity. Total spending on welfare and pensions now exceeds £330 billion annually—roughly equivalent to the entire income tax take. "If you said to a young person today, by the way, every penny that you pay in income tax is going on welfare, going on some kind of benefits plan—that hurts," Haldane acknowledges, speaking to the generational resentment such figures can provoke.

    The key nuance Haldane introduces is that the growth in this spending has come predominantly from pensions rather than traditional welfare benefits, driven by demographic aging and the retention of the pensions triple lock (which guarantees pension increases by whichever is highest: inflation, wage growth, or 2.5%). While Haldane stops short of calling for outright abolition of the triple lock, he is unambiguous that "looking seriously at the triple lock, looking seriously at the allocation of welfare spending, is a sine qua non of us getting to a more defensible position on defense."

    The Case for Defense Bonds

    One novel proposal Haldane engages with is the concept of "patriot bonds" or "war bonds"—government securities specifically marketed for defense spending, potentially offering tax advantages to attract buyers. Haldane expresses cautious support for this approach but insists it cannot substitute for spending reforms. "The market's tolerance for this, the credibility of the UK's fiscal position, firstly—buttresses means this government demonstrating it has the ability to make difficult politically difficult choices around spending," he argues. "Without that, any further borrowing risks a raspberry from the markets."

    The innovative element Haldane endorses is making these bonds free of inheritance tax, creating a unique selling proposition that could tap into Britain's extraordinary personal savings culture. He points to research showing over £2 trillion sitting in cash earning negative real returns—"parked up in cash earning negative real returns." A patriotic bond offering tax advantages could redirect some of this hoard toward productive public investment. "I think that could be quite good marketing, quite good fiscal arithmetic," Haldane concludes. "Yes, I think that ought to be an option on the table, but as a complement to rather than substitute for doing something on spending."

    Growth as the Great Redeemer

    Haldane pivots from fiscal constraints to what he clearly considers the fundamental challenge: Britain's failure to generate sustainable economic growth. "None of this would matter at all if the economy was growing properly," he tells Somerset-Webb. "If GDP per capita was growing at any particular speed, we wouldn't particularly have to worry about cutting spending because these problems take care of themselves. But it's just not."

    Here, Haldane delivers what the host has been anticipating—the "but" that represents genuine optimism. "If you were a Martian or any other planet, actually an alien, looking at not the public balance sheet but the private balance sheet of the UK, you would say, in aggregate, it is in rude health." Haldane elaborates that UK households and companies are both running financial surpluses, and their debt levels—both corporate and household—remain at modest levels by historical comparison. "In terms of the balance sheet fundamentals of UK plc, we are poised for lift-off. There is plenty of fuel in the tank, plenty of ammunition there to be fired. Balance sheets are poised to be put to work—investment, spending, and the like."

    This framing is crucial: Britain does not face a crisis of resources but a crisis of incentives and environment. The nation's private sector has prepared itself for growth; the problem lies in whether conditions will permit that growth to materialize.

    The Friction Problem: Why Private Capital Stays on the Sidelines

    Having established that private balance sheets are healthy, Haldane turns to the critical question: why aren't households and companies deploying this capital? "Something has to change to incentivize them to do so," he observes. "And a very high tax, very high regulation, very politically uncertain environment makes that difficult."

    Haldane identifies two categories of explanation. The first is external: global uncertainty and genuine reasons to save for a "rainy day." "The world is a noisy, uncertain place," Haldane acknowledges, "and I totally get the desire to squirrel away some money for a rainy day." A year that has included crises in the Middle East and associated market volatility reinforces this behavior rationally.

    The second category is domestic and self-inflicted. "A chunk of it is domestically induced self-harm," Haldane states, "which is uncertainty around policy, which has been very acute over the past few years." He specifically cites Budget 2024 and Budget 2025 as examples where either fear of tax rises or actual tax increases "took the legs from beneath the animal spirits of companies and households." The solution, Haldane argues, lies in what he terms the "Hippocratic Oath" for economic policy: first, do no harm.

    Tax and Regulatory Complexity: The Invisible Drag

    Beyond tax rates themselves, Haldane turns to what he considers an even greater obstacle: administrative complexity. The UK tax system runs to 21,000 pages—"among, if not the longest, in the world." This sheer volume creates an environment where compliance itself becomes a barrier to activity. "Taking a chainsaw or a pickaxe to that, rather than the pruning shears, is absolutely what was needed," Haldane argues, and what has not happened.

    The regulatory landscape compounds this problem. Haldane—who himself worked as a regulator at the Bank of England—acknowledges the genuine necessity of rules governing financial markets, workplace safety, product standards, and countless other domains. Yet he observes that "the individually well-intended actions of now more than 100 regulators in the UK nonetheless can collectively add up to a quagmire, and that is where we find ourselves." Each regulator pursues its own rational objective; the aggregate effect creates friction that dampens economic activity.

    Somerset-Webb echoes this point from her own experience. "In many cases, it's often less the rate of tax, particularly when it comes to corporate activity and starting a small business... What you do think about is the admin hurdles, the admin and regulatory hurdles, and these are so difficult." She notes that much of the "tax gap"—the difference between what HMRC should collect and what it actually receives—stems not from deliberate evasion but from sheer administrative impossibility. "A lot of that will, maybe some of it is deliberate, but a lot of it is just: I can't do this admin. This is just too hard."

    Haldane agrees wholeheartedly. "As someone who sent me around small business a few years ago, I've felt the full force of that, and I've been absolutely... Now having sampled this myself, I'm completely with small business owners and thinking this is an endless and rising tide of compliance." He extends this insight to personal finance: "There is nothing in the world that people hate more apart from... In general, admin. Admin is everybody's personal hell."

    The critique applies equally to regulation. "This is an interesting utter failure of the public sector to understand the extent of the friction that is involved," Haldane observes. Governments design rules with good intentions; they rarely account for how those rules interact with hundreds of other well-intentioned rules to create an impenetrable thicket for businesses trying to operate and grow.

    Haldane's verdict on successive governments is damning: "On both regulation and, to a lesser extent, on tax, this government, like its predecessors, has talked big and acted small. And that will need to change."

    Britain's Innovation Paradox

    Despite these structural problems, Haldane identifies a remarkable strength that makes him "excited about what's possible." The UK, he argues, "might be the most innovative nation on the planet." The evidence lies in the pipeline of venture capital-backed businesses growing rapidly—"not yet unicorns, not yet with wings, but the kilties and thoroughbreds that are rising at real pace."

    Haldane defines these as businesses with revenues exceeding £25 million annually—not yet household names, but the thoroughbreds that become tomorrow's industry leaders. "The businesses we know are the wellspring of job creation, of value creation, of productivity generation." By the absolute number of such businesses, the UK ranks third globally, behind only the United States and China. Adjusting for population—where China and the US possess vastly larger populations—the UK's innovation performance is even more striking. "We are smashing the lights out relative to the rest of Europe, and arguably even relative to the US."

    Yet this success creates its own frustration. "We can be first in terms of exciting companies and still twenty-seventh in terms of GDP per capita," Somerset-Webb interjects. "How do we keep those companies here?" This is the paradox Haldane confronts: Britain invents but does not retain, nurtures but does not harvest.

    The Scale-Up Problem and Capital Plumbing

    Haldane traces the problem to three interconnected issues. First, many promising businesses are staying private longer than they would have two decades ago, meaning wealth creation bypasses public markets and retail investors. Second, when these companies do seek capital, the financial infrastructure to support their growth domestically remains underdeveloped. Third, British institutional capital—particularly pension funds—continues to flow overseas rather than back into UK plc.

    On the third point, Haldane makes a striking observation: "We are the only pension fund on the planet that does not have a home bias towards our own companies." Every other major economy's retirement savings system channels capital toward domestic enterprise; Britain's uniquely globalized pension architecture does not. "And that is ridiculous, and that needs to change."

    He stops short of recommending a government mandate requiring domestic investment, instead favoring a suite of facilitative measures: examining whether existing regulations (such as LTAF/SombCT UK) impede domestic allocation; addressing trustee risk aversion through guidance or incentives; and creating tax-efficient wrappers that make UK-focused investment more attractive to individual savers. "We can provide more tax-efficient wrappers to enable people to put their money towards UK companies rather than overseas ones. Yes, we can do that. We should do that."

    The underlying diagnosis is what Haldane terms a "replumbing problem." The capital exists—trillions of pounds in British pension funds and insurance companies, vast sums sitting in cash earning negligible returns. The businesses exist—the pipeline of innovative firms ranks among the world's best. The missing element is the infrastructure to connect capital to opportunity. "The money is there. The money is there," Haldane emphasizes. "But the plumbing to get that money into our businesses... isn't there. So that's a replumbing problem. We can solve plumbing problems. We can solve the problem."

    The Sustainable Growth Model

    What Haldane explicitly rejects is the notion that government spending can substitute for business-led growth as the driver of economic expansion. "Business-led growth... is the only type of growth you can have that's sustainable over the longer term." Government has a role in creating the enabling environment—fixing the tax system, streamlining regulation, building the financial infrastructure—but the actual wealth creation must come from enterprise.
