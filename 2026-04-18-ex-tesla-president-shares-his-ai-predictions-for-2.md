# Ex Tesla President Shares His AI Predictions for 2026: A Deep Dive into the Entrepreneurial Opportunity

## Introduction

Jon McNeill spent nearly eight years as President of Tesla, overseeing global sales, service, and delivery operations during the company's most explosive growth phase. But before he became one of the most influential executives in the electric vehicle industry, McNeill was a college student in Chicago working afternoons writing trading algorithms at the world's largest options and futures trading platform—using neural networks so primitive they could barely be called artificial intelligence. Now, watching the technology he glimpsed three decades ago finally mature into something powerful, McNeill has returned to his entrepreneurial roots, applying AI at the core of each of his new ventures while keeping a keen eye on the seismic opportunities he believes are just beginning to emerge.

In a wide-ranging conversation about artificial intelligence and its implications for business and society, McNeill offers a perspective that is both historically grounded and forward-looking. Rather than dwelling on the anxiety that typically accompanies technological revolutions, he draws on historical parallels to argue that AI will follow the same pattern as every major technological breakthrough before it—destroying some jobs while simultaneously creating far larger numbers of new ones that we cannot yet imagine. His analysis blends the optimism of a seasoned entrepreneur with the analytical rigor of someone who has spent decades studying how technology reshapes economies.

## The Entrepreneur's Perspective on AI: From Caveman Computing to Real Power

McNeill begins by acknowledging a sentiment that many veterans of early computing and AI research probably feel: a sense of vindication mixed with wonder. "I'm excited," he says, describing how he worked his way through college coding trading algorithms in Chicago using the earliest neural networks, attempting to gain an edge in markets through automated trading at speeds and accuracy levels that were revolutionary for their time. "We were using what looked like rudimentary computers to do this stuff," he recalls with a laugh. "Caveman AI."

The comparison is striking because it captures both how far the technology has come and how long certain minds have been working toward this moment. For McNeill, the emergence of genuinely powerful AI is less a disruption than a fulfillment—finally having access to the computational muscle that early pioneers could only dream about. "Now you got real compute power," he says. "And now that you see this unleashed, I'm like, 'Ah, this is what we were trying to do three decades ago.'"

This personal history is more than anecdote; it establishes McNeill's credentials as someone who understands AI not as a futuristic abstraction but as a practical tool he has used to build businesses and generate returns. His excitement is the excitement of a craftsman who finally has the right tools.

## The Historical Pattern: Why Every Revolution Creates More Than It Destroys

Central to McNeill's argument is a thesis about technological revolutions that he has clearly thought about extensively: every major breakthrough in history creates enormous opportunities for entrepreneurs, and he cannot name a single exception where the result was less economic activity and fewer jobs. "Every technical revolution and every breakthrough like this that has happened in history creates enormous opportunities for entrepreneurs," he explains. "I cannot name a technical revolution that's happened that's resulted in less GDP and less jobs. It just doesn't work that way."

The reason, according to McNeill, lies in the cognitive limitations of human prediction. "There's a lot of hand-wringing at the beginning of every technological revolution because humans are really good at seeing the first-order effect, which is the job destruction, but they're not good at seeing the second-order effect, which is the job creation that happens by entrepreneurs on the back end." He positions the current moment as a classic inflection point where anxiety about displacement dominates public discourse even as the seeds of the next generation of industries are being planted.

## The Telephone Operator Example: A Blueprint for Understanding Disruption

To illustrate this pattern concretely, McNeill walks through one of his favorite historical examples—the disruption of telephone operating jobs in the 1960s and 1970s. When he was growing up, making a long-distance phone call required dialing zero and speaking with a human operator—typically a woman in the local community who would literally plug cords into a switchboard to connect local phone lines with the national network. "In other words, humans were switches," McNeill explains. This was not a marginal occupation; by the late 1970s, approximately 800,000 people were employed as telephone operators across the United States, representing a significant pillar of local employment.

The invention of the electronic switch by Bell Labs in the late 1960s and early 1970s rendered these jobs technically obsolete. By the late 1970s and early 1980s, McNeill notes, "100% of those jobs had gone away. Those humans were replaced by electronic switches." The hand-wringing was intense and, from the perspective of those who lost their livelihoods, entirely justified. But what was invisible to the worriers was what would emerge on the other side of this disruption.

Once long-distance calling became essentially free—requiring only electrons rather than human labor—entrepreneurs could suddenly offer services that had been economically impossible before. This is how toll-free calling was born. Businesses like 1-800-Flowers, 1-800-JUNK, and 1-800-INSURANCE emerged as entirely new commercial concepts enabled by cheap, automated long-distance infrastructure. These businesses required centralized call answering, which created an entirely new industry: call centers. "A few years into the 1980s, there were now millions of people employed in call centers," McNeill notes. "Hundreds of software firms were created." His own first startup in the 1990s was a software firm serving call centers, precisely because the technology was still nascent when companies were deploying electronic switches and lamenting the loss of operator jobs.

The lesson McNeill draws is not merely historical but operational: "Nobody could see on the other side that these entrepreneurs were going to create toll-free dialing. They were going to create businesses on top of toll-free dialing. And on top of that, there were going to be this whole layer in our economy called call centers that millions of people are going to be employed in." The parallel to the current AI moment is explicit in his framing.

## The Spreadsheet Example: How Job Destruction Became Trillions in Market Cap

McNeill offers an even more powerful example: the digital spreadsheet. In the 1950s, skyscrapers were full of humans performing manual calculations for financial analysis, he explains. When personal computers and spreadsheets arrived, these jobs were eliminated. Critics of technology typically end their analysis there—but McNeill pushes further. "I was just in New York City. Those skyscrapers aren't empty. What happened?"

What happened, he argues, is that the spreadsheet enabled something entirely new: sophisticated financial modeling that was previously impossible. Once digital spreadsheets existed, firms could deploy the Black-Scholes model for pricing options and derivatives—calculations so complex that they could not be performed manually on a large scale. "All of a sudden you could have sophisticated pricing engines like Black-Scholes at your fingertips," McNeill explains. "And that meant that you could now price options and futures and derivatives that didn't exist before."

The result was the creation of entire new markets: options exchanges, futures markets, derivatives trading desks, and sophisticated loan syndication capabilities. These markets employ hundreds of thousands of people and represent trillions of dollars in market capitalization. "Again, that was unknowable on the other side when the spreadsheet was just being invented—that somebody would deploy Black-Scholes, somebody would figure out how to price puts and calls and derivatives and make entire markets like the options exchange, the Merc, possible."

For McNeill, this is the definitive argument against technological determinism in the negative direction. We cannot see the other side clearly, but history consistently shows that the other side contains opportunities that dwarf what was lost.

## The New Venture: AI-Powered Supply Chain Automation

Shifting from historical analysis to present-day application, McNeill describes one of his current businesses that he finds most exciting: a supply chain automation platform built by a team pulled directly from Tesla. This team originally built Tesla's internal supply chain automation platform, which McNeill describes as "super sophisticated and way beyond anything anybody had in the industry." They constructed this system using machine learning around 2017—ahead of most of the industry.

Now, McNeill and his team have asked this group to rebuild that capability with modern AI tools. The results have been transformative. The AI allows the team to solve complex supply chain problems rapidly while using agents to understand the workflows of clients almost instantly. As an example, McNeill describes how they recently engaged with one of the largest grocery delivery platforms in the country (though he cannot yet name them publicly). Their agents were able to understand the work rules of this massive platform within hours and design a system and workflow within hours as well.

This capability represents something genuinely new in the enterprise software space. McNeill notes that traditional ERP systems typically take 9 to 12 months to implement. His company's AI-powered solution can accomplish the same outcome in a matter of days. The value proposition is not just speed but the combination of deep expertise in supply chain optimization—knowing which rules are good, which are bad, what to tell agents to do—coupled with the ability to verify whether the agents' outputs are correct or need adjustment.

"What they're essentially doing is a consultant," McNeill explains. "It comes in and it learns your workflows. It documents it, figures out how things are currently working and then it starts to improve or automate or whatever." The AI, in this framework, functions as an exoskeleton for human expertise, dramatically amplifying what a small team of domain experts can accomplish.

## Addressing the Hard Question: Is This Time Different?

The interviewer raises a pointed question that represents the most sophisticated objection to techno-optimism: if software now functions essentially like human labor, does this change the pattern? If AI can do the work that previously required human judgment, then even the new businesses created might not employ many people. "Do we all just become sort of like mechanics for the robots and for AI?" the interviewer asks. "That's one part I haven't been able to wrap my head around."

McNeill's response is notable for its intellectual honesty. "I haven't either," he admits. "And I haven't heard the case for why we do that is compelling enough." He acknowledges that this concern represents a legitimate philosophical question that he has not fully resolved. But his bet—and it is positioned as a bet informed by historical study rather than certainty—is that the pattern will hold.

His reasoning is empirical rather than theoretical. In every major technological revolution he has studied or experienced personally, the naysayers' predictions of permanent job destruction have proven wrong. "Make me the case that all of a sudden now the computers do all of that and take all that creativity and all those jobs," he challenges. "I just haven't seen that in history, and I don't know that I can fully buy into that ending point."

This represents McNeill's core epistemic position: uncertainty acknowledged, but confidence that the historical record provides sufficient grounds for optimism. The future will include jobs we cannot currently imagine, created by entrepreneurs who will seize opportunities that are invisible today.

## The AI Competitive Landscape: A High-Stakes Chess Game

The conversation briefly touches on the competitive dynamics among AI leaders. McNeill references Elon Musk's characterization of the AI race as "the highest ELO game in the world"—meaning the most intense strategic competition on the planet, where the smartest people with the most capital are going all out for the biggest prize. The players he mentions include Mark Zuckerberg, Google, Sam Altman of OpenAI, and Anthropic.

While McNeill does not offer a detailed analysis of competitive positioning, his framing suggests that he views this competition as both intense and potentially decisive. Companies and investors who are not paying attention to this race risk missing the most significant technological and economic shift of their careers.

## Key Takeaways

**The Second-Order Effect Principle**: McNeill's most valuable mental model is the distinction between first-order and second-order effects of technology. The first-order effect of any disruption is visible immediately—jobs destroyed, processes eliminated, familiar ways of working rendered obsolete. The second-order effect, which is always larger and always positive in aggregate, is the entrepreneurial response that creates new industries, new job categories, and new forms of value. The practical application is to train yourself to ask not just "what disappears?" but "what becomes possible now that this barrier has been removed?"

**History Is the Best Predictor of Technology's Economic Impact**: McNeill's telephone operator and spreadsheet examples are not mere analogies; they are the foundation of his entire worldview. Every major technological revolution has followed the same pattern: initial disruption, hand-wringing, invisible opportunity, eventual creation of more jobs and wealth than existed before. The confidence to act boldly in the present comes from understanding that this pattern has never been broken.

**AI as Human Exoskeleton, Not Replacement**: McNeill's current business model rests on a specific vision of human-AI collaboration. The most powerful applications are not fully automated systems but tools that amplify human expertise. A team with deep knowledge of supply chain optimization, equipped with AI that can rapidly learn any company's workflows, can deliver value in days that traditional consulting or implementation firms require months to match. The skill lies in knowing how to deploy the AI effectively and how to verify its outputs.

**Speed and Adaptability as Competitive Advantages**: The ability to implement supply chain solutions in days rather than months represents a fundamental shift in what is economically viable. Problems that were too complex or too expensive to solve with traditional approaches become solvable when the implementation timeline shrinks by orders of magnitude. This suggests that businesses built around AI-first architectures will increasingly compete not just on the quality of their solutions but on the speed at which they can be deployed and adapted.

**The Unknown Future Favors Action Over Paralysis**: McNeill's final philosophical point is that uncertainty about the future should not lead to inaction but to strategic engagement. "We cannot see through the other side," he acknowledges, "but we can get a hint of where this stuff is going to go if you think creatively." His own career—from algorithmic trading to Tesla to new AI ventures—exemplifies this philosophy. The goal is not to predict the future perfectly but to position yourself to seize opportunities as they emerge.

## Who This Is For

This conversation will be most valuable for entrepreneurs, business leaders, and investors who are trying to develop a framework for thinking about AI's economic implications. McNeill offers something rare: a perspective grounded in deep technical experience, shaped by multiple business cycles, and articulated with the clarity of someone who has thought through these arguments carefully rather than merely absorbing conventional wisdom.

For **founders and executives** at companies considering how to integrate AI into their operations, McNeill's discussion of his supply chain automation business provides a concrete model for how AI can be deployed not as a chatbot or novelty application but as core infrastructure that changes what a small team can accomplish. His emphasis on human expertise amplified by AI rather than replaced by AI offers a more sustainable and probably more profitable vision of AI deployment.

For **investors** trying to identify where value will be created in the AI era, McNeill's historical analysis suggests looking not at the obvious AI infrastructure plays but at the second-order opportunities—the toll-free dialings and derivatives markets of the AI era that are not yet visible but will emerge from the collision of cheap AI capabilities with unmet needs.

For **policymakers and commentators** engaged in debates about AI's impact on employment, McNeill's historical framework offers a valuable counterweight to narratives that focus exclusively on disruption without accounting for creation. While his optimism is not guaranteed to be correct, his arguments deserve engagement rather than dismissal.

The common thread across all these audiences is the need to think beyond immediate visible effects toward the creative destruction that always follows major technological breakthroughs. McNeill's conviction—that we are right in the middle of this pattern and that the entrepreneurial opportunities will be prolific—may not be universally shared, but it is articulated with enough depth, historical grounding, and practical example to make it worth taking seriously.
