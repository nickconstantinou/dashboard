# The Unreleased Rollable Smartphone: LG's Final Wild Gamble Before the End

## Introduction: A Company That Refused to Play It Safe

LG is dead. The South Korean electronics giant, once a titan of the mobile industry, shuttered its smartphone division in 2021 after years of financial struggles. But here's the cruel irony that makes their demise so bittersweet for tech enthusiasts: LG didn't fail because they made boring phones. They failed because they made *too many* crazy phones without ever establishing a steady revenue stream to keep the lights on. The Velvet, the Wing, the dual-screen experiments—the engineering ambition was undeniable, but the business reality was unforgiving.

Yet even as the company was bleeding out, one final project was still churning in development. Right before the end, LG was working on what may have been their most ambitious device yet: a rollable smartphone that could transform from a compact 6.7-inch slab into a 7.4-inch mini-tablet with nothing more than a three-finger swipe gesture. Unlike the folding phones that were beginning to dominate headlines from Samsung and Huawei, LG's approach used actual rolling mechanics to extend the display—eliminating the crease while adding a unique form factor that had never been commercially attempted.

Now, in 2026, thanks to case and skin manufacturer dbrand, that unreleased device has surfaced in the hands of reviewers, offering a rare glimpse into what might have been LG's ultimate statement piece. This isn't a concept render or a leaked patent diagram—it's fully functional hardware with working software, complete with animations, motor sounds, and all the polish that comes from genuine product development. And having held it, the reviewer makes an observation that carries the weight of posthumous vindication: this felt like LG getting "really close to releasing this."

## The Legacy of LG's Mobile Gambles

To understand why the rollable phone matters, you need to understand the company that built it. LG wasn't content to simply compete in the spec wars or chase iPhone clones. Instead, they pursued form factors that no other major manufacturer would touch.

The LG Wing remains perhaps their most memorable eccentricity. At first glance, it appeared to be a normal smartphone, but the spring-loaded display was mounted on a swivel mechanism that allowed the entire top layer to rotate into a horizontal orientation, revealing a second, smaller square display underneath. The result was a T-shaped viewing experience that seemed absurd on paper but demonstrated genuine engineering ingenuity. The software had to account for this unusual geometry, ensuring that apps and interfaces felt intentional rather than gimmicky. The mechanical precision required to make that rotation smooth and reliable—while keeping the whole package reasonably slim—was staggering.

Then there was the LG G8X, which arrived right at the dawn of the folding phone era. Rather than investing in expensive flexible display technology, LG took a different approach: they created a case with a built-in second display that users could plug into their phone. This offered dual-screen functionality at a fraction of the cost of actual foldables, and the engineering here was equally impressive. The case featured a fully folding 180-degree hinge and pass-through magnetic charging adapters so users could still fast-charge while the USB port was occupied. It was clever, pragmatic, and distinctly LG in its willingness to solve problems in unconventional ways.

But here's the thing that ultimately doomed them: none of these experiments ever became a consistent moneymaker. LG spent years pushing boundaries while never establishing the kind of reliable, mainstream success that competitors like Samsung and Apple took for granted. The company was essentially running a research lab that also sold phones, and eventually, the money ran out.

## The Rollable Concept: Elegant Theory Meets Mechanical Reality

The core idea behind the rollable smartphone is elegantly simple. Instead of folding a display in half around a tight radius—which creates the creases that have plagued every Samsung Galaxy Z Fold—why not roll the screen out like a scroll? The display could expand from the side of the phone, stored in a hidden compartment until needed. This approach promised two significant advantages: no crease and a unique pocket-friendly form factor that felt distinctly different from the book-like foldables flooding the market.

When closed, the LG rollable phone measures approximately 6.7 inches diagonally—comparable to a standard modern flagship. The screen appears to wrap around both edges in a way reminiscent of Samsung's older Edge display phones, with the curved portions bending over the sides. But the real display is hidden in plain sight, tucked away behind a rail on the right side. Flip the phone over, and you can actually see the remainder of the display curving over the back, protected by a clear glass panel that LG implemented deliberately. This back-facing portion wasn't wasted space—LG developed software features that utilized it for displaying notifications, messages, or even a camera interface, allowing users to take selfies with the primary rear cameras without unfolding the device. This design choice echoes the Xiaomi Mi Mix Alpha, a concept phone from 2019 that wrapped a display entirely around its body but never went on sale either.

To activate the expansion, users perform a simple three-finger swipe gesture across the closed display, and the entire device transforms. The screen unrolls to become a slightly more square 7.4-inch mini-tablet, adding meaningful screen real estate without requiring a fundamentally different device. The transformation takes just a few seconds, and LG added several sound effects that play through the speakers during opening and closing—ostensibly to mask the audible whirring of the motors, though the reviewer notes this effort is "about as effective as coughing to cover up a fart." Still, the existence of these sounds suggests someone at LG spent real time engineering matching audio profiles, which the reviewer finds darkly hilarious.

## Engineering Deep Dive: What Makes This Phone Tick

Holding the device reveals just how far LG pushed the mechanical engineering. The motors responsible for rolling and unrolling the display are surprisingly powerful—strong enough to push a 16-inch MacBook Pro across a table, complete with rubber feet, which weighs approximately five pounds. If you try to resist the rolling motion with your hand, the motors push back with notable force. However, LG did implement safety features: if the phone is open and you squeeze it hard enough, the motors will actually trigger the closing sequence to prevent damage, followed by an error message warning you about the obstruction.

The pressure to accommodate rolling mechanics created interesting design constraints elsewhere. With the sides of the phone dedicated to housing the extended display and mechanical components, there wasn't room for traditional physical buttons. So LG got creative. The volume buttons on the left side are actually pressure-sensitive areas positioned where buttons would normally sit. The power button, meanwhile, lives on the back of the phone—positioned near the cameras—and doubles as a fingerprint reader. It sounds awkward in description, but the reviewer found it surprisingly natural and reachable in practice.

Durability, however, presents concerns that likely contributed to LG never bringing this to market. When the phone is fully open, you can see the gear teeth at the top and bottom of the mechanism, along with visible air gaps throughout. Unlike modern folding phones where the flexible display is protected inside the chassis when closed, the rollable's display mechanism is inherently exposed to the elements. The flexible display itself is continuously present in the rolling mechanism, subject to stress that foldable displays don't face. LG never achieved an IP rating for water or dust resistance, which would have been a significant marketing disadvantage in 2021's premium smartphone market.

## Software Polish: The Details That Almost Made It Real

What strikes the reviewer most is how much genuine software development had gone into this device. This wasn't vaporware or a rushed prototype—it was a product approaching launch readiness.

The lock screen features a unique blooming flower animation when the phone expands, with the petals visually unfurling as the display grows. When contracting back to closed mode, the animation reverses, with the flower collapsing back into a single petal. It's a small touch, but it demonstrates that LG's software team had considered how to make the mechanical transformation feel magical rather than merely functional.

The Settings app shows more practical consideration. When expanding from closed to open, the interface smoothly adds an additional column to accommodate the extra screen space, with a subtle slide-over animation. Closing the phone reverses this process, with the column sliding away as the screen contracts. These details suggest months of user experience testing and refinement—the kind of polish that typically only happens in the final stages before launch.

The reviewer discovered additional settings that let users customize or disable the motor sounds entirely. With sounds turned off, the mechanical nature of the device becomes unfiltered: you hear the whirring motors and the subtle clicks of the internal mechanisms. It's a reminder that beneath the sleek exterior, this is a machine with moving parts—something no current mainstream smartphone can claim.

## Specs, Internal Engineering, and What Might Have Been

Digging through the settings reveals some hardware specifications that LG never officially announced. The device contains a 4,500mAh battery and 12GB of RAM—respectable numbers for its era, though of course, none of this ever shipped to consumers. Without a proper name or price point, the rollable phone exists in a strange limbo, a product that was developed extensively but never officially acknowledged by LG.

For those curious about the internal engineering, the reviewer recommends watching Zack from JerryRigEverything's teardown video, where he examines the device's guts after obtaining one through the same dbrand channels. According to his analysis, the phone contains not one but two motors working in concert to achieve the rolling motion, along with three spring-loaded arms that push the chassis over as it unrolls. The complexity is described as "ridiculous" and "wild"—testament to just how seriously LG took this project.

The company clearly got far enough in development that they were considering the fine details of the user experience. But without a financial lifeline or mainstream appeal, all that engineering expertise never translated into a product you could buy.

## Key Insights: What This Device Tells Us About Innovation and Risk

**The Rollable Form Factor Solves Real Problems That Foldables Don't**  
Folding phones have improved dramatically since their introduction, but the crease has never fully disappeared. The rollable approach sidesteps this issue by using a larger radius curve rather than a tight fold, resulting in only a subtle waver in the display rather than a visible line. LG's engineers recognized that some users might want large-screen functionality without accepting the compromises of current folding technology. Whether this trade-off was worth the mechanical complexity remains an academic question now, but the reasoning was sound.

**Courage Without Strategy Is Just Spectacle**  
LG's problem was never a lack of imagination—they had plenty. The Wing and the G8X and the rollable phone all demonstrated creative thinking. But innovation requires more than novel ideas; it requires products that people actually want to buy in sufficient quantities to sustain a business. Samsung's foldable division has survived because the company also sells conventional phones at scale. LG bet everything on differentiation and had no fallback when those bets didn't pay off. The rollable phone stands as a monument to what happens when a company prioritizes ambition over sustainability.

**Sound Design in Engineering Is an Overlooked Art Form**  
The existence of those motor-masking audio tracks—engineered to match the frequency profile of the rolling mechanisms—represents a fascinating intersection of acoustics and mechanical engineering. Someone at LG spent real hours developing audio that would make the device feel more refined. Whether customers would have noticed or appreciated this detail is unknowable, but it reveals the depth of thought that went into making the rollable feel premium, not prototype-like.

**Mechanical Complexity Creates Trade-offs That Software Can't Fix**  
Every engineering decision involves compromises, and the rollable form factor's compromises were substantial. The exposed mechanism, the inability to achieve water resistance, the need for pressure-sensitive buttons instead of real ones—all stem from the fundamental requirement of having moving parts inside a smartphone. Modern smartphones have become increasingly sealed and simplified; the rollable phone went in the opposite direction, trading ruggedness and simplicity for uniqueness.
