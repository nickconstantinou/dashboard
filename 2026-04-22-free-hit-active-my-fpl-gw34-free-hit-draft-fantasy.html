However, Van Dijk "always has goal threat" according to Harry, making him a viable option despite the personnel changes behind him. The takeaway is clear: Liverpool's attacking assets offer safer floors than their defense given the injury situation, with Salah and Szoboszlai representing the most straightforward selections and Gakpo as a differentials-friendly alternative.

The pressure and expectation on Spurs to secure maximum points here could manifest in either determined performance or nervous collapse.

Alternatively, managers could target Wolves players under the theory that Tottenham's emotional instability and mounting pressure might prove too much for them to handle. The broader lesson here concerns high-risk, high-reward Free Hit strategy. When a team faces a must-win situation against apparent inferiors, their attackers can either deliver spectacular returns or produce nothing if nerves and pressure overwhelm them. Identifying these scenarios and deciding whether to punt or avoid them represents one of the most difficult decisions Free Hit managers face.

Strategic Principles for Free Hit Success

Throughout his analysis, Harry emphasizes several key principles that should guide any Free Hit decision. First, fixture quality and matchup dynamics matter more than general team quality when selecting a temporary squad. An attacking player from a team facing a defensively vulnerable opponent often outperforms a premium asset against a defensive specialist.

Second, teams with clear motivation—whether fighting relegation, chasing European qualification, or desperate for wins—tend to produce more consistent attacking returns than teams with nothing to play for. West Ham's survival battle makes them attractive despite their general inconsistencies, while teams coasting toward mid-table security offer limited ceiling potential.

Third, historical precedent can provide valuable signals. Harry references Isma's history against Liverpool as evidence that even lesser-known players can deliver on specific matchups, while Gibbs White's emergence for Forest suggests emerging form should override past reputation when making Free Hit selections.

Finally, Free Hit flexibility allows managers to ignore popular picks entirely. Brentford may be widely owned among non-Free Hit managers, but Free Hit users can simply avoid them entirely and target alternatives with better expected returns against favorable opposition.
