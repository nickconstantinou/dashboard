<p># EP34: Atomic Accountability

## A Conversation with Professor Alex Wellerstein on Re-examining the Decision to Drop the Bomb

---

## Introduction

In an episode that forces listeners to sit with uncomfortable questions about one of the most consequential moments in human history, Dan sits down with Professor Alex Wellerstein—a historian who has spent his career excavating the forgotten details, classified documents, and complicated truths surrounding America's development and use of atomic weapons. Wellerstein, who runs the widely-read blog *Restricted Data* and serves as a professor at the Stevens Institute of Technology, brings a scholar's rigor and a storyteller's instinct to a subject that most Americans believe they already understand. His work has consistently challenged the tidy narratives that have calcified around August 1945, and this conversation proves no exception.

The episode arrives at a moment when the atomic bombings remain a subject of intense cultural and political debate, yet also a subject where most public discourse operates on assumptions that rarely get interrogated. We tell ourselves a story about Harry Truman receiving a fateful briefing, weighing the options, and making the agonizing but necessary decision to end the war and save American lives. We tell ourselves the bombs were both militarily essential and morally justified. Wellerstein's scholarship suggests that this story—comforting in its clarity—obscures a far messier reality in which the decision was more contingent, the evidence more ambiguous, and the historical record far more complicated than most Americans have ever been permitted to know.

What makes this conversation particularly compelling is not merely Wellerstein's willingness to challenge conventional wisdom, but the manner in which he does so. He is not interested in present-day moralizing or in retrofitting history to fit contemporary political agendas. Instead, he insists on understanding the past on its own terms, which means grappling with the genuine uncertainties, competing pressures, and institutional dynamics that shaped decisions in real time. The result is an episode that respects its audience enough to present complexity rather than offering easy answers.

---

## Key Concepts & Arguments

### The Myth of the Single Decision

One of the central arguments Wellerstein advances is that the decision to use atomic weapons has been transformed in collective memory into something cleaner and more deliberate than it actually was. In reality, there was no single "decision" in the way the term is commonly understood—no moment where Truman sat down with comprehensive information, weighed the options clearly, and chose the path forward. Instead, the use of the bombs emerged from a cascading series of smaller decisions, institutional momentum, and circumstances that constrained options in ways that often go unrecognized.

Wellerstein explains that by the time the bombs were ready for use in the summer of 1945, a substantial infrastructure had been constructed around the Manhattan Project, and that infrastructure had its own logic and imperatives. Thousands of scientists, engineers, and military personnel had devoted years to developing these weapons. The organizational culture that had been built was oriented toward deployment, not toward second-guessing or reconsideration. When the bombs became available, the question for many within the system was not *whether* to use them but *how* and *when*. This bureaucratic and institutional dimension is consistently absent from popular accounts, which prefer the drama of a lone decision-maker wrestling with profound moral questions.

The president, Wellerstein argues, was in many ways reactive rather than proactive in his engagement with atomic weapons policy. Truman was not deeply involved in the detailed planning of how the bombs would be used, and much of the operational decision-making fell to military commanders and scientists operating with considerable autonomy. The narrative of Truman as the man who "decided to drop the bomb" obscures the degree to which the decision was distributed across multiple actors and driven by forces beyond any single individual's control or even full understanding.

### What Truman Actually Knew

Central to Wellerstein's critique is the claim that Truman's understanding of what he was authorizing was far more limited than most people assume. When Truman approved the use of atomic weapons, he did so based on information that was incomplete, sometimes misleading, and often shaped by officials with their own interests and perspectives. The president received briefings that emphasized certain facts—particularly the anticipated casualty savings from avoiding an invasion—while minimizing or omitting information that might have complicated the picture.

Wellerstein discusses how the intelligence available to Truman about Japanese willingness to surrender was more nuanced than the standard narrative suggests. There had been communications from Japanese officials suggesting openness to capitulation, though under conditions that American leaders found unacceptable. The question of whether additional diplomatic efforts might have produced Japanese surrender without the use of atomic weapons is not a simple one with a clear answer, yet it is often treated as settled in the affirmative by those defending the bombings. Wellerstein's scholarship suggests that the evidence is more ambiguous, and that the assumption of unconditional necessity deserves more scrutiny than it typically receives.

The president was also operating with limited understanding of the weapons' effects. While he knew atomic bombs were enormously destructive, the reality of what they would do to urban populations—the specific horrors of radiation, the long-term suffering, the annihilation of civilians including women and children—was not fully conveyed to him in ways that would have given him a complete picture of what he was authorizing. This is not to excuse the decision but to understand it more accurately, which is the historian's fundamental task.

### The Role of Secrecy and Classification

One of Wellerstein's signature contributions to this field is his attention to the role of secrecy in shaping both the historical events and the historical record. He argues that the classified nature of atomic weapons policy during and after the war created conditions in which certain options were never seriously explored, certain information was systematically suppressed, and the subsequent historical narrative was heavily managed by officials with interests in particular interpretations.

In the immediate aftermath of the bombings, the United States engaged in extensive efforts to control information about what had happened, why it had happened, and what alternatives had existed. Scientists who had worked on the Manhattan Project were subject to security constraints that limited what they could say publicly. Government officials shaped the emerging narrative to serve diplomatic and political interests. The result was a historical record that was not simply incomplete but actively constructed to support particular conclusions.

Wellerstein is particularly interested in how this secrecy continues to affect what we know and believe about the bombings. Documents that might illuminate key questions remain classified or were destroyed. The voices of those most directly affected—Japanese civilians, survivors, ordinary soldiers on both sides—are systematically underrepresented in the archives that historians work with. The American perspective, and specifically the perspective of American decision-makers, dominates the historical record in ways that reflect power dynamics as much as historical truth.

### Challenging the "Saved a Million Lives" Argument

Perhaps the most contested terrain in debates about the atomic bombings concerns the claim that the bombs were necessary to save American lives by avoiding an invasion of Japan. This argument, which has been standard in American discourse for decades, holds that an invasion would have cost hundreds of thousands of American casualties, and that the bombs, by forcing Japanese surrender, made this sacrifice unnecessary.

Wellerstein engages with this argument seriously but questions its certainty. He notes that the projected casualty figures for an invasion—which were themselves estimates based on limited intelligence and institutional incentives to make the case for the bombs—were not as definitive as the narrative suggests. Military planners had reasons to support higher estimates, and the assumption that the invasion was the only alternative to the bombs deserves examination.

More fundamentally, Wellerstein emphasizes that the argument "the bombs saved lives" does not necessarily mean "the bombs were the only way to save those lives." Japanese surrender was the goal, and the question historians should ask is whether there were pathways to that outcome that did not involve the destruction of Hiroshima and Nagasaki. The existence of Soviet entry into the war, diplomatic communications, and Japanese internal debates about surrender suggest that alternatives existed, even if we cannot know with certainty whether they would have succeeded.

The moral weight of this discussion is significant. Wellerstein is careful not to claim certainty about counterfactuals that cannot be verified. But he insists that treating the "saved a million lives" argument as established fact rather than contested interpretation does a disservice to historical understanding and to the people who died in the bombings.

### The International Dimension

The conversation also addresses how the atomic bombings fit into the broader context of the war and of emerging Cold War dynamics. Wellerstein discusses how American officials were aware that the Soviet Union was developing its own atomic weapons, and how considerations about demonstrating American power to a potential future adversary influenced thinking about how the bombs should be used.

The decision to drop the bombs on cities rather than military installations or uninhabited areas was not as obvious as it might seem. Some military officials had advocated for demonstrations that might have demonstrated the weapon's power without causing the same level of civilian casualties. The fact that these options were rejected in favor of direct attacks on urban populations reflected choices that deserve examination rather than assumption.

The postwar implications are equally significant. The bombings established a precedent that subsequent nuclear powers would invoke, and they shaped international norms around weapons of mass destruction in ways that continue to affect global security. Understanding the decision to use the bombs thus requires attention not only to the immediate context of 1945 but to the long-term consequences and the ways in which those consequences were, or were not, anticipated.

## Detailed Takeaways

**Historical narratives are always constructed, and the construction itself is historically significant.** Wellerstein's work demonstrates that the story we tell about the atomic bombings is not simply a record of events but a product of choices about what to emphasize, what to suppress, and what questions to ask or avoid. Understanding how and why certain narratives became dominant—the institutional interests they served, the emotional needs they met, the political purposes they accomplished—is itself essential historical knowledge. This insight extends far beyond the atomic bomb; it applies to how we understand all controversial events in which there are winners and losers, perpetrators and victims, justified and unjustified actions.

**The complexity of historical decisions does not make them less significant—it makes them more instructive.** One response to Wellerstein's arguments is to say that insisting on complexity is a way of excusing or minimizing the bombings. The opposite is closer to the truth. When we reduce the decision to a simple moral calculus—tragic but necessary—we diminish the genuine horror and significance of what occurred. Recognizing the full complexity of the situation, including the institutional forces, the information gaps, the competing pressures, and the human fallibility involved, actually deepens our moral engagement rather than undermining it. It reminds us that these were real people making real decisions under conditions of uncertainty, and that the outcomes were not inevitable.

**Understanding history requires engaging with what people actually knew and believed, not what we wish they had known or believed.** A common error in historical interpretation is to judge past actors by standards they could not have met—either by holding them to contemporary moral enlightenment or by assuming they had access to information and perspectives that were not available to them. Wellerstein's approach insists on reconstructing the actual context of decisions: what did Truman actually know? What information was available to him and what was withheld? What framings and assumptions did he bring to the decision? Only by answering these questions honestly can we assess the decision fairly and learn from it appropriately.

**Secrecy in government creates both immediate and long-term harms that persist across generations.** The classification system that surrounded atomic weapons policy from the beginning served immediate military and diplomatic interests, but it also created conditions for historical misunderstanding and public manipulation that continue to shape how Americans understand their own history. Wellerstein's emphasis on the role of secrecy is not merely academic; it has implications for contemporary debates about government transparency, accountability, and the public's ability to engage meaningfully with decisions that affect their lives. The atomic bomb provides a particularly dramatic example of a broader pattern.

**Moral clarity about the horrors of the bombings is compatible with historical complexity about the decision.** One of the episode's most important contributions is modeling how one can simultaneously hold two things: a clear moral recognition that the atomic bombings were catastrophic acts that killed tens of thousands of civilians, including children, in unspeakable ways, and an intellectual commitment to understanding the historical situation as it actually was rather than as we might wish it to have been. The first does not preclude the second, and the second does not diminish the first. Wellerstein models a kind of moral-historical integrity that refuses both the comfort of easy justification and the retreat into relativism.

## Who This Is For

This episode is essential listening for anyone who wants to move beyond the simplified narratives that dominate public discourse about World War II, nuclear weapons, and American foreign policy. It is particularly valuable for educators and students of history who may have encountered the atomic bombings primarily through textbooks that present a sanitized and settled version of events. Wellerstein's scholarship demonstrates that far from being a closed question, the decision to use atomic weapons remains a subject of genuine historical debate with significant implications for how we understand military power, moral responsibility, and the relationship between government and governed.

The episode also speaks powerfully to readers interested in the nature of historical knowledge itself—how narratives are constructed, how evidence is selected and suppressed, and how the past is continuously contested and reconstructed in service of present needs. Wellerstein's insistence on complexity without moral relativism, on factual accuracy without presentism, offers a model of engaged historical thinking that applies far beyond the specific subject matter.

Finally, for those who find themselves reflexively defensive about the bombings when confronted with criticism, this episode offers an invitation to sit with discomfort and to recognize that genuine understanding often requires letting go of the narratives that make us feel good about our collective past. That is not an easy thing to do, but it is, as Wellerstein demonstrates, the essential work of historical understanding.</p>
