    How to Win with AI Agents in 2026: A Deep Dive into the Trillion-Dollar Opportunity

    The $1 Trillion Bet Nobody's Talking About

    When Sequoia published their data on AI agent deployment across industries, most observers saw confirmation of what they already believed. Software engineering leads at nearly 50% adoption, with back office, marketing, and sales trailing behind at single-digit percentages. It's a tidy chart that suggests AI agents are making steady, predictable progress. But when Howie Lew looks at that same data, he sees something else entirely: evidence of profound underpenetration.

    "Two things," Lew begins, his voice carrying the weight of someone who's spent the last decade building Airtable into a billion-dollar business. "One is I think it absolutely reflects the underpenetration of AI in industries that clearly could already be disrupted or benefit with even today's AI capabilities. If you took frontier agents today and deployed them into every one of these categories, you should get to 100%."

    The second point is more striking: even that 50% figure for software engineering is, in Lew's assessment, an overestimate. Not because adoption is slower than reported, but because the definition of "AI-assisted development" has fundamentally shifted. The frontier companies—the ones Lew watches most carefully—have already moved beyond what he calls "gen one AI," which is essentially AI augmentation for still very human-driven workflows. Think GitHub Copilot's autocomplete, the breakthrough that seemed revolutionary just three years ago.

    The new paradigm is something else entirely. Lew describes his own development process using HyperAgent: "I have 30 different cloud code instances running in parallel. Each one is coupled up to a browser, fully autonomous. It can go and get other agents to comment on any PRs it creates." This isn't human developers using AI tools. This is autonomous AI systems executing software development with humans serving as reviewers. Andrej Karpathy, the renowned AI researcher, publicly acknowledged in October and November that he had "completely inverted" from mostly human-written code with AI augmentation to the opposite—mostly AI-written code with human oversight.

    What Sequoia's chart actually measures, Lew argues, is the lag between frontier capability and mainstream adoption. "Many companies and many industries and many functions are barely catching up to the three-year-old state of the art, let alone disrupting themselves with the new state of the art."

    This gap between what's possible and what's deployed is precisely where Lew sees the opportunity. Sequoia's trillion-dollar estimate for AI agent value creation strikes him as conservative. "It's not even a trillion," he says flatly. "It's probably the whole GDP of all white collar labor, which is obviously many tens of trillions in the Western Hemisphere alone."

    The Autonomy Breakthrough: Why Now Is Different

    For years, AI tools could assist humans but couldn't replace them. They could suggest code completions, draft marketing copy, answer customer questions—but always with a human in the loop, reviewing, correcting, approving. That constraint is gone, according to Lew. The breakthrough happened roughly four to five months ago, and it arrived with a model name most people recognize: Opus 4.5.

    "You know, Opus 4.5 just kinda set a new high watermark," Lew explains. "This thing, for the first time, really feels like a true software engineer that's able to work on a task that would have taken a real human engineer maybe many hours, if not days. It can go do it completely autonomously, and it ships me a perfect clean PR that I can just review like a reviewer would."

    The key insight isn't just that the models became smarter—it's that they became capable of coherent execution across multiple turns with lots of tools and context. A chatbot can answer a complex question in isolation. An agent can pursue a complex goal over hours, hundreds of steps, and multiple tools, course-correcting as it goes. That distinction fundamentally changes what can be automated.

    Lew draws an analogy to aviation: "There's copilot territory. There's autopilot territory." The copilot suggests and assists. The autopilot flies the plane. We're no longer debating whether AI can be a good copilot. We're discovering what becomes possible when AI takes the controls.

    The implications extend far beyond software engineering. Lew points to his own experience using frontier models in his CEO role at Airtable: "You give it really complicated subject matter content. Management consulting. You give it some really hard meeting problems in the context thereof, and it gives you really smart answers that truly are like expert level." The model intelligence is there, he argues. The models are smart enough. The remaining challenge is deployment—not building smarter AI, but integrating AI into every role and industry that could benefit.

    Rethinking the Economics: Why $150 Isn't Expensive

    One of the most significant barriers to AI adoption isn't technical—it's psychological. Many potential users anchor their expectations to subscription software pricing. Netflix raised its price from $12.99 to $20, and people complained for months. When frontier AI models cost significantly more per task, the sticker shock can feel prohibitive.

    "There's this mental model that people have to get over," Lew says. "In the old days of software, a lot of stuff was free. Even ChatGPT has a free version you could use however much you want. You get a cheap, dumb model. But you're not expending that many tokens because it's not doing autonomous multi-turn work and expending a billion tokens every few days."

    The comparison that matters, Lew argues, isn't token cost versus monthly subscription fees. It's token cost versus the human time the task would otherwise require. He illustrates with a specific example from his own work: "One of our recent board memos that I wrote and sent out to our entire board and investor list—a lot of it was researched and crafted by HyperAgent. Obviously with my instincts and context and whatever imbued into the agent. And of course I oversee it at the end. But I got feedback that that was the best memo from some of our best investors that I'd ever written."

    The reason wasn't magic. It was depth. The agent could research extensively, pull in competitive intelligence, analyze market dynamics, and draft polished prose—all tasks that would have consumed many hours of Lew's attention. "I got to do it in ten times less time," he notes. "And so even if it cost me, let's call it, $150 of tokens to generate that output—think about the opportunity cost of my time."

    This reframe transforms the economics entirely. A $150 board memo that would have required $1,500 or $15,000 worth of human executive time isn't expensive. It's a bargain. The constraint isn't what AI costs; it's what humans cost. And the gap between those numbers is where enormous margin businesses can be built.

    Lew applies the same logic to development: "How much would it have cost a human to do the thing? If I wanted to go and create an entire marketing campaign... if it cost me, let's say, $500 in tokens, but it would have taken a human team two weeks and $50,000 in labor... I think that is a real reframe moment that's needed."

    The Fastest Adoption Curve in Enterprise History

    Sequoia's data on enterprise AI adoption shows a curve steeper than anything in software history. By the metrics that matter—revenue creation from zero—AI companies have accomplished in three years what took previous technology waves a decade or more.

    "Take OpenAI and Anthropic alone," Lew says. "Let's just say they have a combined revenue probably of $80 billion plus right now, up from basically zero a few years ago." He pauses for emphasis. "What in the history of software has there ever been an industry where any company, let alone in aggregate across all the companies, you've got a category that went from zero to $80 billion plus?"

    The numbers exclude all the other AI providers—inference providers, tooling companies, application builders. The total AI economy is already substantially larger than the headline figures suggest. And the growth isn't slowing; it's accelerating as enterprises move from experimentation to production deployment.

    This creates a bifurcated opportunity for entrepreneurs, according to Lew. The first path is product-led growth (PLG): build a product so effective at solving real problems that users adopt it organically and spread it through their organizations. "Obviously we see a lot of these PLG products," he notes. "I kind of put OpenAI itself in this category because even though it's not actually a monetized business, it is getting massive adoption. The raw token consumption through OpenAI is in the many hundreds of millions, if not billions, already."

    The second path is top-down enterprise sales—going to corporate boards and CEOs with a simple value proposition: "Pay us a massive check. Give us a $100 million plus check, and we will purportedly solve your AI problems for you." Lew calls this "Palantir style," and he sees it as an almost rational response to existential uncertainty.

    "CEO's choice is either I pay it and I risk wasting $100 million and maybe getting fired over it," he explains, "or I don't do anything with AI and I'm definitely getting fired over it. On a game theory level, it's like everybody's gonna pay it."

    The irony, Lew suggests, is that many of these massive enterprise AI transformation projects will fail to achieve their stated goals—not because the technology is inadequate, but because the technology is so powerful that a small team with the right agents could accomplish what entire departments currently do. The $100 million enterprise engagement might accomplish what $10 million worth of HyperAgent usage would achieve. But the incumbents selling those massive engagements have no incentive to reveal that efficiency.

    The Future of Work: Fleets, Not Individuals

    The most striking image from Sequoia's data shows an "agent command center"—a dashboard displaying multiple specialized agents handling different functions simultaneously. Customer intelligence. Content production. Competitive research. Lead enrichment. Each agent executing autonomously, coordinating with humans who oversee rather than execute.

    "That literally is a view in HyperAgent," Lew says when the chart appears. "I feel like I'm looking at HyperAgent, and I think this is the future. We are building towards a world where it may not be that every company is literally one person, but I do think every company will have a fleet of agents."

    What's fascinating is how this fleet organizes itself. Lew describes an emergent phenomenon: agents naturally converge on structures that mirror human organizational hierarchies. "Agents are converging on these purposeful—they almost map to job roles that humans were playing."

    He draws an analogy to robotics: humanoid robots are converging on human form factors not because it's optimal from a pure engineering standpoint, but because the physical world—door handles, stairs, workstations—is designed for human ergonomics. Similarly, AI agents naturally settle into roles that align with human job functions because that's how work is organized in the systems they're designed to interact with.

    There's also a fundamental technical reason for this convergence, according to Lew: context window limitations. The dream of a single omniscient AI—something that can look at everything, know everything, and execute everything simultaneously—may be structurally impossible.

    "I just don't think we're ever gonna get to a point where an AI model can have infinite context window," Lew says. "I think there's physics to that. You could just literally only have so much attention, so much context at once."

    Just as human organizations need different people handling different responsibilities because no single person can know everything, AI systems will need to partition capabilities across specialized agents. This isn't a limitation to overcome—it's a fundamental architectural principle of how AI workforces will be organized.

    "Agents are converging on these purposeful roles," Lew observes. "It's not like five years ago when people talked about superintelligence, I always imagined there's gonna be just the single omnipotent AI that just figures everything out and looks at everything all at once. I'm more and more of the belief that there are gonna be fundamental and always present limitations on context windows."

    HyperAgent: The Mac to Linux's Command Line

    After discussing the strategic landscape at length, Lew pivots to demonstration. The product is HyperAgent, and it's designed to solve a problem that has frustrated enterprise AI adoption: the gap between what frontier AI can do and what ordinary users can access.

    "Think of HyperAgent as, if all of these other agent products out there are more like Linux, HyperAgent is our take on the Mac version of it," Lew explains. "We want it to just work, to be secure. It's cloud native. You don't have to run a Mac mini. And perhaps most importantly, HyperAgent is applying a lot of the same design philosophy and obsession with great UX that we applied to the no-code app category ten years ago, but now to agents."

    The analogy to Airtable's origins is deliberate. A decade ago, application development required technical expertise—data layers, logic layers, view layers, all written in code. Airtable distilled that complexity into an intuitive visual experience that non-technical users could grasp immediately. Lew and his team are attempting the same transformation for AI agents.

    "We were very inspired by the Macintosh, the GUI, taking terminal-based command line computing and making it into something that people could just grok immediately," Lew says. "HyperAgent is really intended to be a very intuitive and visual way of using agents."

    The product demo Lew walks through starts with what he calls a "task thread"—a visual representation of an agent's work on a specific objective. The example shown is one the podcast host (Greg) had pitched: hyperlocal market reports for real estate agents, generated from public data.

    The agent went through its work systematically. It researched the landscape of the market. It ran analysis. It generated code. It produced deliverables. The demonstration shows what the product looks like when it's working as designed—not a chat interface waiting for prompts, but an autonomous system executing a business objective with minimal human intervention.

    "The pitch was hyperlocal market reports for real estate agents generated from public data," Lew describes. "This agent went around and did research on the landscape of the market. It ran a bunch of analysis. It's got full coding capability. It's got a full sandbox environment. So it is running a full computer—just one of the cloud, not your own computer."

    Agents can connect to external systems if users authorize them. "It can access your Slack and Granola and email. It can send stuff on your behalf or just pre-draft emails. It's got preconfigured ability to do things like pull from Twitter, use advanced tools like generate imagery or use Google Maps."

    The result for the real estate market report concept was a comprehensive business case: market size analysis, competitive landscape assessment, revenue projections, technical requirements. The agent didn't just gather information—it synthesized it into actionable strategic guidance.

    Lew expresses genuine enthusiasm for what he calls "medium-sized markets"—not the $100 billion opportunity that attracts massive incumbents, but the couple-billion-dollar total addressable market where a focused startup can capture meaningful share. "It's not like a $100 billion market that's gonna be super competitive," he notes. "I really love this idea of the kind of mid-mini market. Like, couple billion TAM large, which is to say you can build a very lucrative business even capturing a double-digit percent chunk of this."

    The Hands-On Imperative: Using Is Believing

    Throughout the conversation, Lew returns to a central theme: the gap between understanding AI's potential intellectually and experiencing it firsthand is enormous. Most people who haven't spent serious time with frontier agents don't grasp what they're capable of.

    "It's really hard to fully grok the power here if you haven't actually gone and hands-on spent at least a full weekend playing with agents," Lew says. "And that means more than just a superficial one-shot thing. Hey, who's gonna win the next presidential election? Like, kind of question you could have asked a chatbot."
