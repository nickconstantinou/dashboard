What Should You Do With Money Once You're Rich? | Scott Galloway

Introduction

In a world obsessed with how to *make* money, far less attention is devoted to the far more nuanced question of what to do with it once you've accumulated enough. This conversation featuring Professor Scott Galloway— NYU Stern School of Business professor, entrepreneur, and author of *The Algebra of Wealth*—tackles that exact paradox with characteristic bluntness and self-awareness. Rather than offering a polished financial advisory framework, Galloway strips away the pretense and delivers an unfiltered meditation on purpose, mortality, and the responsibilities that come with extraordinary wealth.

The discussion emerges from what appears to be a podcast setting between Galloway and hosts who probe him on his relationship with money at different life stages. What makes this conversation particularly compelling is Galloway's willingness to be vulnerable about his own journey—from a man driven by greed and vanity in his younger years, to someone grappling with what it means to have "enough" and how to deploy resources meaningfully. He addresses the practical question of *how* to spend money that actually improves quality of life, while simultaneously exploring the deeper existential terrain of what wealth cannot buy. The conversation is peppered with Galloway's signature dark humor and provocative statements, creating an entertaining yet surprisingly substantive exploration of a topic that remains largely taboo in public discourse.

The Evolution of Purpose: From Greed to Legacy

Galloway's philosophy on wealth has undergone a dramatic transformation over his lifetime, and understanding this evolution is essential to grasping his current worldview. In his younger years, he openly admits his purpose was remarkably simple and perhaps embarrassingly honest: he wanted to become "really rich and really awesome" so he could take care of his mother and be more "attractive to strange women." This candid self-assessment covers the first forty years of his life—a period he describes as defined by work, ambition, and little else. There was no grand humanitarian impulse, no vision for changing the world. Just raw, unapologetic self-interest driven by the dual engines of familial obligation and sexual competitiveness.

The transformation began, as it does for many men, through profound loss and upheaval. When his mother became seriously ill and he found himself unable to provide adequate care, everything shifted. Money, which had previously represented a trophy or a status symbol, suddenly revealed itself as something far more practical: a means of protecting people. This painful experience crystallized into a guiding principle that would shape the rest of his life. Beyond his mother's illness, the birth of his sons in 2008—during the financial crisis, when he had "lost everything"—served as another inflection point. He describes feeling shame despite his previous success, recognizing that he was "near broke" and now "responsible for this little science experiment." These twin experiences of death and birth, trauma and responsibility, became the most significant influences on his worldview.

Today, his stated purpose has fundamentally changed. "I want to launch good men," he says, expressing what he describes as a deficit he feels he needs to repay. He speaks of wanting to reach "a point of surplus value" after years of what he perceives as taking more from the country, taxpayers, and freedoms than he gave back. His ultimate measure of success is no longer net worth but something far more personal: knowing that when he reaches the end of his life, he raised "really confident, loving men." This shift from accumulation to contribution represents the philosophical backbone of his current approach to wealth.

The Mathematics of Enough: A Practical Framework

Perhaps the most actionable insight Galloway offers is his concrete framework for deciding when to stop accumulating and start living. He describes a decision he made during his working years: once he reached a certain number—which he estimates was somewhere between 25 and 45 million dollars—he would consider himself finished with the accumulation phase. However, he acknowledges the uncomfortable truth that "your number keeps going up." As he explained in a previous podcast conversation with the hosts, the goalposts shift continuously, making it dangerously easy to never feel satisfied.

His solution is elegant in its simplicity. Once a year, Galloway meets with his financial advisors to review his net worth. If his net worth exceeds his target number—which it has for the past twelve years due to what he calls "an amazing economy"—he follows one of two paths with the incremental wealth. First, he spends it. Enthusiastically. He describes himself as "great at spending money" and "so good at spending money," claiming to spend "like a 50s gangster just diagnosed with ass cancer." This memorable line, which he admits to using "not more than two or thirty hundred times," has become something of a signature phrase. It captures his philosophy: when you have finite time, there's no point in hoarding resources you can't take with you.

Second, anything above his target number that he doesn't spend, he gives away. Importantly, he doesn't frame this as philanthropy or altruism—though he acknowledges it produces those outcomes. Instead, he describes giving away money as something that "makes me feel masculine and American." This reframing is characteristic of Galloway's approach: rather than moralizing about obligation or virtue, he wraps practical behavior in language that resonates with his own identity and values. The result is a system that ensures he never becomes one of those wealthy people he criticizes—"rich friends that don't know how to spend their money or they don't give it away."

The Best Things to Spend Money On

When pressed on what actually constitutes good "bang for your buck" in terms of spending, Galloway doesn't hesitate. His answer is immediate and emphatic: "Hands down. A private plane." He explains that private aviation "lowers the bar for fun"—meaning it makes recreational travel genuinely enjoyable rather than a stressful endurance test. The practical benefits are substantial: he estimates that having a private plane has freed up approximately seventeen additional days per year that would otherwise be lost to airport delays and connecting flights through hubs like Dallas Fort Worth.

Galloway is careful to distinguish between different models of private aviation ownership. Owning a plane outright, he notes, is "a ton of work" because it involves managing pilots, safety checks, and what essentially amounts to a small business. Chartering flights comes with its own frustrations, including constantly comparing prices and coordinating logistics. His recommendation, "hands down," is fractional ownership—essentially subscribing to a private aviation service that allows you to scale up or down based on each specific mission. For solo travel, you take a smaller plane; for multi-family trips to India, you upgrade to something larger. The system essentially just requires a text message, removing virtually all friction from the process.

He spends approximately $1.5 to $2 million annually on this luxury, and he considers it worth every penny—not because of status signaling, but because of the tangible improvement in quality of life and the reclaiming of finite time. His closing comment on this subject reveals just how much value he places on this expenditure: "I hate to say take my house, don't take my plane." This prioritization is significant coming from a man who clearly enjoys material comforts but has thought deeply about where money actually produces happiness versus where it merely produces appearance.

What Wealthy People Waste Money On

In classic Galloway fashion, he's equally willing to critique the spending habits of his fellow ultra-wealthy individuals. When asked about things his "billionaire buddies" spend on that he considers stupid, he doesn't hold back: "Art and wine." His reasoning is both practical and characteristically self-deprecating. Regarding art, he suggests that wealthy people are essentially trying to "buy culture" and by extension, interestingness. "I've been a boring douchebag my whole life," he says, "so all of a sudden I'm going to start overspending on art and it makes me interesting. No, it doesn't. You're still the same douchebag." It's a brutal assessment, but one that contains a genuine insight about the limits of material acquisition in creating authentic identity or fulfillment.

Wine, he implies, falls into a similar category of performative consumption—expensive vintages purchased more for the story they tell about the owner than for any actual enjoyment they provide. While Galloway doesn't elaborate extensively on wine, his contempt for it seems to stem from the same logic: spending that signals wealth rather than enhancing life. This critique ties into a broader theme in his philosophy: the importance of spending on things that actually produce experiences and utility rather than status and appearance.

The Golden Decade: Traveling With Children

Given that one of the hosts mentions having a two-year-old and a three-month-old, Galloway offers his perspective on traveling with children at different ages—and his advice is both practical and somewhat brutal. He suggests that the age range from zero to three is "awful" for travel, going so far as to say that the three-month-old is "actually easier to travel than a two-year-old." His prescription for this phase is stark: parents—fathers, specifically—have essentially no value to add during this period. "Your job is to be supportive of your wife and make money," he says. "That's it. You add no value." He advises keeping infants away from large bodies of water and suggests fathers should simply "pretend to like it" when they don't.

However, from approximately age three onward, everything changes. He describes the period from ages four to fourteen as "the golden decade" for family travel. This is the window when children are old enough to form and retain memories, appreciate experiences, and genuinely engage with the world—yet young enough to still want to spend time with their parents. His recommendation is emphatic: "Go. I mean, again, these are incredible stories of privilege. But even if you don't have money, take your kid."

Galloway recently experienced this golden decade firsthand during a college tour with his son. Using AI to help plan the trip, they explored potential universities together—visiting Indiana, Washington University in St. Louis, Michigan State, the University of Michigan (where they caught a football game), and the University of North Carolina (where they caught a basketball game). He describes this trip as "the best week I've had in probably the last three years"—a remarkable statement from a man who has access to virtually any luxury experience in the world. The key insight here is that the value came not from the expense or the prestige but from the shared experience and the opportunity to connect with his son during a formative life stage.

The Relationship Between Mortality and Generosity

Throughout the conversation, Galloway's atheism surfaces as a significant influence on his relationship with money and time. He describes his lack of religious belief as providing a certain "power" derived from "the finite nature of life." This perspective—that this life is all there is—removes any metaphysical reasons for deferring gratification or accumulating beyond what can be enjoyed. If there's no afterlife in which your virtues will be rewarded or your greed punished, then the math becomes straightforward: money is only valuable insofar as it can improve your limited time on Earth or be transferred to causes and people who can benefit from it while you still exist.

This mortality awareness also explains his impatience with wealthy people who fail to enjoy their resources or give them away. "What are you doing?" he asks, addressing these friends directly. "Enjoy your life." It's a question born not of judgment alone but of genuine bafflement at behavior that seems to contradict the obvious reality of human finitude. For Galloway, the atheist framework strips away both the carrot of heavenly reward and the stick of hellish punishment, leaving only the individual responsibility to make the most of their brief existence—and, perhaps, to leave the world in marginally better condition than they found it.

Detailed Takeaways

Takeaway One: Purpose Evolves, and That's Normal. Galloway's journey from pure self-interest to legacy-building demonstrates that it's neither necessary nor expected to have everything figured out in youth. The purpose that drives accumulation in your twenties and thirties may look entirely different from the purpose that guides your giving in your fifties and sixties. This evolution should be embraced rather than feared, and the key is simply to remain reflective about what matters at each life stage.

Takeaway Two: Set a Concrete "Enough" Number and Stick to It. The danger of infinite wealth accumulation is that there's always another milestone. Galloway's framework of setting a target number and then aggressively spending or giving away everything above that threshold provides a practical mechanism for breaking this cycle. The specific number matters less than having an intentional threshold that forces behavior change rather than endless escalation.

Takeaway Three: Invest in Time, Not Just Things. Private planes, travel experiences, and time with family all share a common thread: they represent investments in time rather than accumulation of objects. Galloway's prioritization of the plane because it "lowers the bar for fun" and recovers seventeen days annually illustrates his core thesis that money's highest purpose is buying back hours that would otherwise be wasted on friction and delay.

Takeaway Four: Travel With Children During the Golden Years. The ages of four to fourteen represent a unique window when children are capable of forming lasting memories, appreciating new experiences, and genuinely enjoying parental company. Galloway's enthusiasm for college tours and international travel during this period stems from his belief that these shared experiences create bonds and memories that justify the expense many times over. Parents should be intentional about maximizing this relatively brief window.

Takeaway Five: Reject Performative Spending in Favor of Authentic Enjoyment. His critique of art and wine as attempts to "buy culture" or interestingness speaks to a broader principle: spending that aims primarily at status signaling often fails to produce genuine satisfaction. The question isn't "what will impress others?" but rather "what will I actually enjoy or benefit from?" This reframing can help direct resources toward experiences and possessions that enhance life rather than merely projecting wealth.

Who This Is For

This conversation will resonate most with individuals who have achieved significant financial success and are grappling with questions of purpose and stewardship—though Galloway's insights also apply to anyone at earlier stages who wants to think more intentionally about the relationship between money and meaning. His frank, sometimes provocative style particularly appeals to those who find traditional financial advice too sanitized or moralistic. The audience that will benefit most includes successful entrepreneurs, executives, and professionals who have reached or are approaching financial independence but haven't yet developed a clear framework for thinking about what comes next.

What distinguishes this from standard wealth management advice is its emphasis on psychological honesty and personal values rather than abstract principles. Galloway doesn't tell listeners what they should care about; he shares what he cares about and explains the experiences and reasoning that shaped his perspective. For readers willing to engage with uncomfortable truths about motivation, mortality, and privilege, this conversation offers a refreshingly candid alternative to the platitudes that typically surround discussions of wealth and purpose.
