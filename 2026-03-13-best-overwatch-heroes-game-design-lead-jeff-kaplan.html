Best Overwatch heroes - game design lead Jeff Kaplan picks his favorites | Lex Fridman Podcast Clips

In the world of competitive gaming, few titles have left as indelible a mark as Blizzard Entertainment's Overwatch. Released in 2016, the hero shooter revolutionized the genre and became a cultural phenomenon, spawning esports leagues, passionate communities, and a sequel that continues to evolve the formula. At the helm of its development was Jeff Kaplan, the game's design lead who shaped not only the mechanics but the very philosophy behind the game's world. This conversation between Kaplan and Lex Fridman offers a rare, intimate look into the creative process behind one of gaming's most beloved properties—revealing the intentionality, influences, and philosophy that transformed Overwatch from a concept into a cultural touchstone.

The discussion spans far beyond simple game design tips, touching on fundamental questions about what kinds of worlds we want to inhabit, both virtually and metaphorically. Kaplan's insights provide a window into how deliberate creative choices—about tone, location, character simplicity, and mechanical philosophy—can shape player experience in profound ways. For game developers, storytellers, and anyone interested in how worlds come to life, this conversation distills years of creative iteration into a coherent design philosophy that prioritizes joy, aspiration, and accessibility.

---

The Philosophy of a Hopeful Future

One of the most distinctive aspects of Overwatch's identity is its tonal departure from the prevailing trends in competitive gaming. While many shooters embrace dark, gritty, post-apocalyptic aesthetics—settings that had become almost formulaic by the mid-2010s—Kaplan and his team deliberately charted a different course. The goal was to create what Kaplan describes as "a bright, hopeful future" and "a future worth fighting for." This wasn't merely an aesthetic choice; it was an ideological stance that permeated every aspect of development, from the characters players would control to the environments they would inhabit.

The decision to craft a hopeful world came from a deep respect for what had come before, paired with a desire to offer something genuinely different. Kaplan acknowledges the influence of games like Warcraft, StarCraft, and Diablo—each of which established distinct tonal identities within the fantasy and science fiction spaces. Rather than competing directly with the ultra-realistic military shooters that dominated the market, Overwatch would paint "a vision of a near-term hopeful future and fun and more sort of surreal versus ultra real." This meant rejecting the claustrophobic, oppressive environments common in many shooters in favor of spaces that invited exploration and aspiration.

---

Building the World: Locations as Characters

The location selection process became one of the most enjoyable aspects of development for Kaplan and his team. Rather than defaulting to familiar, convenient settings—those "cargo container mazes" that appear in countless games—the Overwatch team approached world-building as a kind of curated tourism. Kaplan describes sitting around with colleagues, looking at photographs of real locations like Santorini and asking themselves: "Where do you want to go? Santorini looks amazing, let's make that place in a video game." The philosophy was straightforward: players would spend hours upon hours in these environments, so they deserved to be visually stunning and emotionally uplifting rather than oppressive.

This approach extended to more complex geopolitical contexts as well. The map Oasis, for instance, was inspired by Iraq but transformed into an aspirational vision of what that region could become in a hopeful future. Kaplan emphasizes that the goal was twofold: first, to get players "thinking about different places on planet Earth and how awesome they all are," and second, from a pure game design perspective, to ensure that the environment "should be pleasing and not oppressive." This attention to tonal consistency—where every element reinforces the game's core philosophy—demonstrates how environmental design can profoundly shape player mood and engagement.

---

The Hero Design Philosophy: Simplicity as Virtue

When discussing his favorite heroes in Overwatch, Kaplan reveals a design philosophy that prioritizes elegance over complexity. His first and most obvious favorite is Tracer, the fast-moving, time-manipulating British assassin who became the game's mascot. Describing her as "the OG, the cornerstone," Kaplan explains that Tracer represents the distillation of game design to its purest form. Her mechanics—the ability to blink forward in short bursts and "recall" to her position from moments before—are "very easy to explain to somebody" and "very easy to pick up the first time."

What makes Tracer particularly brilliant, according to Kaplan, is how her simplicity masks her depth. When a new player hits recall for the first time, they immediately begin mapping out the possibilities: "Wait, does that mean if I [move forward] and then I recall..." The mechanics invite experimentation and mastery without requiring tutorialization. This reflects Kaplan's broader belief that the best game designs create layers of complexity that emerge from simple foundations.

Kaplan's preference for simplicity becomes even more apparent in his discussion of McCree (now known as Cole Cassidy in Overwatch 2). Attracted to "the simplicity in design," Kaplan gushes about the six-shooter that defines McCree's playstyle: "The way that gun feels is phenomenal." He specifically credits designer Mike Hyberg with capturing the spirit of the character's ultimate ability, High Noon, and notes the small but meaningful touches—like rolling a tumbleweed across the screen every time the ultimate activates—that bring personality to the character without adding mechanical complexity.

This philosophy extends to Kaplan's criticism of overly complicated hero designs. He describes his reaction to learning complex ability descriptions in other games: "My head is spinning and I'm like, just give me a good gun." While he acknowledges that games like MOBAs and hero shooters often thrive on intricate ability kits, his personal preference—and the guiding philosophy for Overwatch—was to create "explosive, larger-than-life, fun arcadey" characters whose depth emerged from mastery rather than memorization.

---

Reinhardt: The Heart of the Game

Perhaps no hero exemplifies Overwatch's design philosophy better than Reinhardt, the hulking German tank who became Kaplan's personal main. Kaplan describes the character as "another amazing Jeff Goodman design" featuring a simple but revolutionary mechanic: a shield that teammates could shoot through. "As soon as you give somebody a shield, they know what to do," Kaplan explains. "They go into protector mode." The shield became so influential that it has since been "copied by like every hero shooter since and even non-hero shooters."

The inspiration for Reinhardt's signature charge ability came from an unexpected source: Left 4 Dead 2. Kaplan was playing the cooperative zombie shooter in versus mode, where players could control the enemy zombies, and encountered a boss zombie called the Charger who possessed a similar charge ability. What fascinated Kaplan about the ability was its nature as a "commit"—once you press the button, "you're a runaway train." This mechanical philosophy of forced commitment created dramatic, memorable moments that could go hilariously right or spectacularly wrong. Watching Reinhardts charge to their deaths, Kaplan notes, "is kind of hilarious" and represents "what separates a great hero from a shitty hero."

The Arcade Shooter Heritage

Throughout the conversation, Kaplan emphasizes Overwatch's lineage within the arcade and arena shooter tradition. While the term "arcade shooter" sometimes feels like an insult—Kaplan notes it "pains" him because he never thought of Quake as an arcade shooter—the mechanical DNA is unmistakable. The game hearkens back to "fast movement, really epic over-the-top weapons" and a "low time to kill" that makes players feel survivable and powerful. This stands in contrast to games like Call of Duty or Counter-Strike, where a single headshot can end a life instantly.

The influence of Team Fortress 2 is particularly significant. Kaplan describes Team Fortress as originally starting as "a Quake mod, which was brilliant," with Team Fortress Classic following for Half-Life and then Team Fortress 2 in 2007, which "blew everybody away when it came out." Overwatch was designed as a homage to these predecessors while carving out its own identity. The six-versus-six format with three distinct roles—tank, support, and damage—provided enough structure to enable teamwork while preserving the chaotic, explosive fun of classic arena shooters.

Key Takeaways for Game Designers and Creators

Deliberate Tonal Choice Shapes Everything: Kaplan's insistence on a "hopeful future" rather than a dark one demonstrates that world-building isn't just about setting—it's about what emotions you want to evoke. Every creative decision, from map design to character personality, should reinforce that core tone. The choice to make Overwatch optimistic wasn't cosmetic; it informed every subsequent decision about color palette, character design, and environmental storytelling.

Simplicity Enables Mastery: The most enduring game designs often have the shallowest learning curves but the deepest mastery ceilings. Tracer's mechanics can be explained in seconds, but mastering her movement, timing, and game sense takes thousands of hours. Designers should resist the temptation to add complexity for its own sake and instead ask whether additional mechanics add meaningful depth or merely cognitive load.

Environmental Design Matters More Than You Think: Players spend far more time looking at and moving through environments than they do reading lore or watching cutscenes. Making environments "pleasing and not oppressive" isn't just an aesthetic preference—it fundamentally affects player mood, retention, and overall experience. Investing in environmental beauty pays dividends in player satisfaction.

Influence Is a Starting Point, Not a Destination: Overwatch owes clear debts to Team Fortress 2, Quake, and Left 4 Dead 2, yet it created something entirely its own. Understanding and respecting your influences allows you to build on their successes while finding your unique contribution to the conversation.

Constraints Breed Creativity: The decision to reject "cargo container mazes" and create aspirational real-world locations required more effort than defaulting to familiar industrial environments. Sometimes deliberately making things harder on yourself—choosing the harder path—produces the most distinctive and memorable results.

Who Should Watch This Conversation

This conversation holds particular value for several audiences. Game designers and developers will find Kaplan's philosophy on simplicity, environmental design, and tonal consistency directly applicable to their own work. His honest reflections on what made certain heroes successful—and his willingness to credit other designers like Jeff Goodman and Mike Hyberg—demonstrate the collaborative nature of great game development.

Storytellers and world-builders in any medium will appreciate Kaplan's insights on creating cohesive fictional worlds. His emphasis on tonal intentionality and the "future worth fighting for" approach offers a framework for building aspirational narratives that resonate emotionally.

Overwatch fans and esports enthusiasts will enjoy the behind-the-scenes glimpse into the creation of their favorite heroes, understanding not just what makes them mechanically interesting but why certain design decisions were made. The revelation about Reinhardt's charge coming from Left 4 Dead 2 provides a concrete example of how cross-pollination between games creates new ideas.

Finally, anyone interested in creative process will find Kaplan's enthusiasm infectious and his methodology instructive. His approach—combining deliberate philosophy with playful experimentation, respecting influences while charting new territory—offers a model for creative work that is both professionally rigorous and genuinely joyful.
