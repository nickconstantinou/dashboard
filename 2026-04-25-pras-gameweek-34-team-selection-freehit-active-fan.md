## Understanding the Template Landscape

### Attack: The Four-Team Consensus

According to Pras, the FPL community has essentially reached unanimous agreement on which teams to target in attack this gameweek. Liverpool, Arsenal, Manchester United, and Tottenham Hotspur represent the four attacking options that dominate virtually every free hit draft. Within these teams, West Ham's Mohammed Bowen emerges as the standout differential because, as Pras explains, "he's the best of the rest and he does a lot more than just what a striker does. He can assist, he can defcon, he's on penalties." This multi-faceted contribution makes him arguably the most valuable non-template asset available to free hit managers.

The forward line options have crystallized into four primary picks: Bowen, Sulanki, Watkins, and Thiago, with Isak mentioned as a fifth consideration—though Pras playfully notes that "people will get put off if I say Isac" and advises viewers not to select him, suggesting the Newcastle striker carries ownership concerns or fixture-based hesitation that makes him less attractive despite his obvious quality. For managers debating which forwards to include, Pras's guidance suggests these four represent the safest paths to returns, with the choice between two or three of them forming the core strategic decision.

### Defense: Clean Sheet Probability

When examining defensive options, the analysis becomes equally straightforward. Arsenal, Liverpool, Tottenham, and Sunderland represent the clean sheet probabilities that most managers are targeting. Pras identifies Gabriel and Van Dijk as the premium defensive picks, supplemented by one Spurs defender—typically Porro or Dano. This means Dano's involvement in defensive duties partially offsets his attacking potential, making him a comparable option to Porro despite different underlying profiles.

Mavranos at 4.3 million also enters the conversation as an attractive option because he carries "defcon baked in and a bullet header baked in"—meaning he combines clean sheet probability with occasional goal-scoring opportunities from set pieces. For managers building their back four, Pras indicates that selecting three or four defenders from this premium group presents "absolutely no issues" from a strategic standpoint, as the expected points differential between options is minimal compared to the risk of pivoting to inferior alternatives.

## Key Strategic Insights

### First Insight: Fixture Independence Matters More Than You Think

The free hit value proposition fundamentally depends on identifying fixtures with limited ownership that still carry legitimate return potential. With no Burnley fixture available this gameweek—the team that traditionally represents the easiest fixture target—the realistic chase options become extremely limited. This forces managers to accept that "whichever fixture you think has some potential and pretty much limited ownership that's the one you chase," even if that fixture isn't obviously favorable. The Wolves game for Spurs represents precisely this type of opportunity: a fixture with reasonable scoring potential but enough uncertainty to keep ownership manageable.

### Second Insight: Midfield Differentiation Is Primarily Psychological

Pras's assertion that all midfielders between 4.5 and 6.0 million carry similar expected points should fundamentally reshape how managers approach their free hit drafts. Rather than spending hours analyzing marginal expected point differences, managers should focus on which picks align with their team structure, captaincy plans, and personal conviction. "Just flip a coin" might sound flippant, but it reflects the mathematical reality that one-week samples are heavily influenced by variance, making theoretical expected point advantages largely irrelevant over a single gameweek horizon.

### Third Insight: The Locked Core Approach Minimizes Regret

By establishing a locked core of seven assets—Raya, Gabriel, Porro, Bruno, Salah, Bowen, and either Javi Simons or Sulanki—managers can reduce decision fatigue while ensuring they're not completely off-template. The remaining three spots become the designated "differential zone" where managers can pursue the upside necessary for rank improvement without sacrificing the foundation of likely returns. This framework prevents the common free hit mistake of over-differentiating across the entire squad, which increases variance to the point where results become essentially random.

### Fourth Insight: Multiple Assets From Single Teams Amplify Variance

Including three Spurs assets—as many template teams do—creates concentrated exposure that "could completely smash them in the face" if results go poorly. Managers should consciously accept this variance when building their squads rather than accidentally accumulating team-specific exposure. The alternative of spreading differential picks across multiple teams reduces the ceiling but also limits the floor risk, which may be preferable for managers with limited rank mobility to protect.

### Fifth Insight: Don't Panic If You're Not On A Free Hit

The anxiety around free hit weeks often exceeds the actual strategic advantage they provide. Pras's assessment of non-free hit assets—"these are as good as any other pick, right?"—suggests that managers with well-constructed regular squads shouldn't feel pressured to make sub-optimal moves simply to manufacture a free hit activation. The chip provides ceiling potential, but the floor isn't as low as fear might suggest.
