Dan Carlin's Hardcore History: Addendum

EP27 More Steppe Stories

In this episode of Hardcore History, Dan Carlin returns to one of his most enduring intellectual fascinations: the peoples of the Eurasian steppe.

Introduction

In this episode of Hardcore History, Dan Carlin returns to one of his most enduring intellectual fascinations: the peoples of the Eurasian steppe. Carlin has long been captivated by the nomads, tribes, and tribal confederacies who shaped civilization from the edges of Central Europe to the borders of China. These aren't merely historical footnotes—their names alone have entered the collective vocabulary: Mongols, Turks, Huns, Scythians, and countless others whose influence rippled across centuries and continents.

The episode features a rich interview with Professor Kenneth W. Harl of Tulane University, whose recent book *Empires of the Steppe: A History of the Nomadic Tribes Who Shaped Civilization* represents a sweeping synthesis of decades of archaeological, linguistic, and historical scholarship. The conversation explores how our understanding of steppe peoples has transformed fundamentally in recent decades, driven by dramatic discoveries ranging from the Tarim Basin mummies in China to newly deciphered inscriptions in Afghanistan and revolutionary DNA evidence.

What emerges is a nuanced portrait of populations often dismissed as mere "barbarians" by settled civilizations. Instead, Carlin and Harl paint a picture of highly adaptable, strategically sophisticated peoples whose unique relationship with their environment—harsh, extreme, and demanding—produced innovations in warfare, trade, and culture that would influence societies from Ireland to India. The episode serves both as an accessible introduction for newcomers and a thought-provoking synthesis for those already familiar with steppe history.

The Geography That Shapes a People

The conversation begins with a fundamental question: what exactly are "the steppes," and why did they produce such distinctive civilizations? Professor Harl confirms Carlin's intuition that geography and environment created the conditions for an entirely different way of life.

The Eurasian steppe is not a monolithic region but a vast, heterogeneous landscape that stretches approximately 10,000 kilometers from the Pannonian Plain in Hungary eastward to the foothills of Manchuria. Harl divides this immense territory into three convenient zones. The western steppe extends eastward from Central Europe, traditionally bounded by the Volga or Ural Rivers. The central steppe encompasses what is today Kazakhstan—an area so vast it approaches the size of Western Europe. The eastern steppe consists of the Mongolian grasslands, bounded to the south by the formidable Gobi Desert and beyond that by the great river systems of China.

This narrow band of grassland exists in a precarious geographic position, bordered to the north by the taiga (Siberian forest), tundra, and ultimately the frozen Arctic regions. To the south, it meets various deserts—the Kyzylkum and Karakum of Central Asia, and the Gobi in the east. This positioning created a natural corridor for migration, trade, and conflict that would prove central to world history.

The climate of these regions defies easy description. Medieval travelers provided vivid accounts of the extremes. Papal Envoy William of Rubruck, journeying to the Mongol court in the thirteenth century, noted that the only shade available during brutal summer heat was beneath his ox-drawn cart. Initially cursing his decision to travel by cart rather than mounted, he came to appreciate its shade when he realized there simply was no natural shelter from the relentless sun. Winter presented equally brutal challenges. Arab traveler Ibn Fadlan, famous for his account of the Vikings on the Volga, was hosted during winter by Oghuz Turks huddled in their portable felt tents. His writings describe months of survival against bitter cold, emphasizing how hospitality and one's word in transactions became sacred bonds—essential for collective survival when the environment could kill without warning.

This environment, Harl emphasizes, bred a particular type of person. "No weaklings survive in this environment," he notes. All adult free males were both hunters and warriors, a dual identity that would prove decisive in shaping steppe military traditions.

The Steppe Economy: Nomads and Trade

The steppe lifestyle emerged sometime between 4000 and 3000 BCE, when peoples dwelling on these grasslands discovered they could exploit the grasslands to sustain great herds of animals—sheep, goats, and cattle. But it was the domestication of the horse that proved truly decisive.

The horse became the beast of burden that enabled the entire nomadic way of life. It could serve as a source of winter meat when other resources ran thin. It became the prime animal of war, transforming military affairs across the Eurasian world. And critically, it enabled mobility across vast distances in a harsh environment where agriculture was marginal at best.

The primary economy of the steppe peoples was pastoral: they consumed and traded the products of their animals—skins, furs, dairy products, and meat. But they could not produce certain essential goods themselves. This created an inherent need for trade with the sedentary civilizations on the edges of the steppe. The desire for goods they couldn't produce—metals, textiles, luxury items—drove them into complex economic and diplomatic relationships with China, Persia, Rome, and Byzantium.

Harl notes that scattered agriculture did exist in some river valleys, documented by Herodotus among the Scythians and by Chinese sources. But the fundamental economic model remained pastoral nomadism, and this economic reality shaped steppe politics, diplomacy, and warfare for millennia.

The Steppe Peoples and Indo-European Origins

One of the most significant revelations of modern scholarship concerns the early history of steppe peoples. The domestication of the horse first developed on the western steppe among speakers of what linguists call Proto-Indo-European (PIE)—the reconstructed mother tongue of hundreds of languages from Ireland to India.

This is remarkable. The same linguistic family that produced Greek and Latin, Sanskrit and Persian, Germanic and Celtic languages originated, at least in part, from successful pastoral nomadic peoples on the western Eurasian steppe who harnessed grass as a way of sustaining themselves and their animals.

The evidence for this deep history has grown substantially. Harl specifically cites the Tarim Basin mummies, discovered and analyzed in recent decades. These remains, preserved in the arid conditions of western China, proved that Indo-Europeans occupied this area at a very early time—far earlier than previously suspected and much farther east than many scholars had imagined. DNA analysis and isotope studies have corroborated and expanded these findings, providing physical evidence for population movements that previously were known only through linguistic reconstruction and scattered archaeological finds.

The Weapon System That Changed History

Carlin, ever the military history enthusiast, returns repeatedly to what he considers one of the most remarkable phenomena in the history of warfare: the combination of the horse and the composite bow in the hands of people who grew up with these skills from earliest childhood.

Once steppe peoples learned to ride horses—beginning around 2000 BCE with innovations first in light chariots and then, about a thousand years later, in fully mounted warriors—they combined this mobility with another devastating technology: the composite bow. This recurved bow, made from layers of wood, horn, and sinew bonded under tension, was extraordinarily powerful yet compact enough to use effectively from horseback.

The effect on military history was profound and long-lasting. Carlin marvels at the fact that this weapon system—mounted archery combining elite horsemanship with powerful bows—wouldn't be effectively countered by settled civilizations until gunpowder became commonplace. "You've created a weapon system that won't be solvable for the people, the enemies of the steppe people, until gunpowder becomes commonplace," he notes. "If you're taking some Byzantine peasant and training them as a mounted archer, it's very different when you take a Hunnic kid who's been on a horse since three years old, shooting at rats and mice as a little child."

The ripple effects of this military innovation spread outward like drops in water. Assyria adopted cavalry techniques. China transformed its western frontier peoples from foot soldiers to mounted archers, fundamentally changing its security calculus. The Comanche warriors of the American Great Plains, centuries later and half a world away, would adopt the same basic system once horses—escaped from Spanish settlements in the 1680s—became available on the North American grasslands. The mustangs that survived and bred on the Great Plains created the same conditions that had existed on the Eurasian steppe: horses that could live on the grasslands, enabling a complete transformation of culture and political landscape among populations that European Americans initially dismissed as inhabitants of "the great American desert."

The Transformation of Scholarship

Professor Harl's book represents a new generation of steppe scholarship, one that has benefited from dramatic advances across multiple disciplines. He explains how our understanding has deepened fundamentally in recent decades.

Archaeological discoveries have been crucial. The Tarim Basin mummies mentioned above were only discovered and properly analyzed in recent decades. New excavations across Central Asia have revealed unexpected complexity in steppe cultures. The study of the Silk Road—once viewed primarily in terms of luxury trade—has expanded to consider how steppe peoples facilitated, controlled, and sometimes disrupted these commercial networks.

Linguistic scholarship has advanced hand-in-hand with archaeology. Harl's own training in classical history, Greek and Latin, and comparative Indo-European linguistics enables him to draw connections that specialists focused on narrower periods might miss. The same applies to specialized knowledge: scholars who read Mongol language sources and understand Chinese texts have made crucial contributions to our understanding of the Mongol Empire, while others focus on Turkish-speaking peoples or the eastern steppes adjacent to China.

Numismatic studies have clarified relationships between steppe confederacies and their neighbors. Studies of ancient coins have revealed who issued them and how steppe peoples connected with broader Eurasian economic networks.

Most dramatically, genetic evidence has transformed the field. DNA testing of both ancient remains and modern populations has provided new evidence for population movements, genetic intermixing, and the spread of languages and cultures. Isotope analysis of skeletal remains can reveal ancient diets, migration patterns, and origins with precision impossible a generation ago.

Harl notes that his own work benefited from the COVID-19 pandemic, which confined him to his home and provided the concentrated time necessary to synthesize decades of notes and research into a coherent narrative. "I kept myself occupied, putting all of my notes and thoughts together, which I've been looking at for a long time," he explains.

Challenging the "Barbarian" Narrative

Perhaps the most important theme emerging from this conversation is a fundamental critique of how steppe peoples have been portrayed historically—as mere barbarians, wild horsemen at the edges of civilization, lacking the accomplishments of settled urban societies.

Carlin and Harl both push back against this characterization. "It's really wrong to simply think of them as barbarians who don't live in cities and don't write great books and don't eat bread as the ancient people of the Near East would say, that they're just wild, and that's a misrepresentation," Harl states flatly.

What the steppe peoples developed was a different kind of sophistication adapted to their environment. Their political structures—often confederacies of tribes that could unite under charismatic leaders—were highly effective for their circumstances. Their military organization produced innovations that settled civilizations struggled to counter for millennia. Their economic networks connected distant civilizations and facilitated the exchange of goods, ideas, and technologies across continental distances.

In essence, the steppe peoples were not failed versions of settled civilizations but successful adaptations to an environment that made sedentary agriculture impossible. Their accomplishments should be measured by the standards of their own circumstances, not by how well they approximated the city-building, bread-eating, book-writing civilizations they traded with and sometimes raided.

Detailed Takeaways

The Eurasian Steppe Was a Crucible of World History: The vast grassland corridor stretching from Hungary to Manchuria served as a highway for migrations, invasions, and cultural transmission that shaped civilizations from Ireland to Japan. The Huns triggered the Great Migration that helped topple Rome. The Mongols created the largest contiguous land empire in history. Turkish peoples established states across Anatolia, the Middle East, and Central Asia. Understanding the steppe means understanding one of the major engines of Eurasian history.

Geography Determines Possibility, Not Destiny: While the harsh steppe environment shaped the nomad lifestyle, it did not predetermine specific outcomes. Steppe peoples proved remarkably adaptable, learning rapidly what was necessary to survive and thrive. They adopted new technologies (the horse, the composite bow, later firearms) with impressive speed. They created complex political structures when circumstances warranted. Environmental determinism explains the general conditions of steppe life, but human ingenuity determined specific historical developments.

Technological Advantages in Warfare Can Persist for Millennia: The combination of mounted archery represents one of the longest-lasting military advantages in human history—effective from approximately 1000 BCE until the widespread adoption of gunpowder in the 1500s CE. This remarkable persistence resulted from the near-impossibility of replicating the training, instincts, and physical conditioning of peoples who grew up in the saddle. Settled civilizations could recruit steppe mercenaries or attempt to copy techniques, but they rarely matched the native proficiency of peoples for whom mounted archery was a way of life developed from earliest childhood.

Modern Scholarship Has Revolutionized Our Understanding of Nomadic Peoples: The old image of steppe peoples as shadowy, barely-known barbarians is obsolete. Archaeological discoveries (the Tarim Basin mummies, Kushan inscriptions in Afghanistan), advances in DNA analysis, isotope studies of remains and artifacts, and the application of multiple disciplinary approaches have created a far richer picture. The steppe is no longer a dark age in our knowledge but a rapidly evolving field where new discoveries regularly reshape our understanding.

The "Barbarian" Label Reflects Civilizational Bias, Not Reality: Settled civilizations throughout history have dismissed steppe peoples as barbaric because they didn't build cities, write books, or eat bread. This perspective reveals more about the biases of urban historians than about the actual accomplishments of nomadic societies. Steppe peoples developed highly effective political organizations, military techniques, economic networks, and cultural traditions suited to their circumstances. Their failure to produce written records or monumental architecture reflects their mobile lifestyle, not intellectual or organizational deficiency.

Who This Is For

This episode serves multiple audiences effectively. Military history enthusiasts will find rich material on the evolution of cavalry, the composite bow, and the transformation of warfare that steppe peoples catalyzed across Eurasia. The discussion of how mounted archery represented a 3,000-year military advantage should fascinate anyone interested in how technological breakthroughs reshape conflict.

General history readers seeking to understand Eurasian history will benefit from the comprehensive overview of steppe geography, economy, and peoples. The episode provides an accessible entry point into a region and topic often neglected in standard historical education, which tends to focus on Mediterranean, Mesopotamian, and Chinese civilizations while treating steppe peoples as peripheral.

Academic readers interested in historiography will appreciate the discussion of how scholarship on the steppe has transformed, particularly the integration of archaeological discoveries, genetic evidence, and interdisciplinary approaches. Professor Harl's explanation of how different scholars specialize in different linguistic areas (Turkic specialists, Mongol specialists, Chinese source specialists) and how synthesis across these boundaries remains challenging illuminates important questions about how historical knowledge is produced.

Finally, anyone interested in understanding the origins of the modern world will find material relevant to topics from the fall of Rome to the establishment of the Ottoman Empire to the cultural and linguistic map of modern Eurasia. The steppe was not an isolated backwater but a central arena of world history whose influence continues to the present day.

Conclusion

This episode of Hardcore History offers a masterclass in why the Eurasian steppe matters to understanding human history. Through Dan Carlin's enthusiastic questioning and Professor Kenneth Harl's deep scholarly expertise, listeners receive both an accessible introduction to steppe peoples and a sophisticated analysis of how modern scholarship has transformed our understanding of these often-misunderstood populations.

The conversation reminds us that history is not merely the history of cities and empires but also the history of the vast grasslands that shaped—and were shaped by—the peoples who moved across them. The Mongols, Huns, Scythians, Turks, and countless other peoples of the steppe were not footnotes to the great civilizations but active participants in making Eurasian history, their influence felt from the walls of Constantinople to the courts of Chinese emperors.

For those seeking to understand how the world came to be as it is, the steppe cannot be ignored. And thanks to recent scholarship, we are finally developing the tools to tell its story properly.
