Let's Get Back On Track: FPL Gameweek 30 Strategy and FA Cup Ramifications

Introduction

The Fantasy Premier League landscape is entering a crucial phase as managers navigate the chaotic spring schedule, and the latest episode of this popular FPL podcast provides exactly the kind of strategic insight that could separate those who finish the season in style from those who falter. Hosted by Tom and joined by Sam (known by his podcast handle Maverropos), this episode was recorded on Tuesday, March 10th, as managers contemplated their moves ahead of gameweek 30.

The conversation arrives at a pivotal moment in the FPL calendar. With FA Cup results now confirmed, the fixture schedule for the critical double gameweeks in gameweeks 33 and 34 is becoming clearer, while the looming blank gameweek 31 is forcing managers to make difficult decisions about their squads. The podcast also touches on the ongoing Champions League fixtures, which are adding another layer of complexity to planning, particularly for teams like Liverpool who are still in European competition.

What makes this episode particularly valuable is the hosts' willingness to be candid about their own struggles. Both Tom and Sam are hovering around the 140k and 350k marks respectively, experiencing what Tom describes as "bumping up and down holding position" – a situation that resonates with thousands of managers who find themselves stuck in neutral as the season reaches its business end. Their honest assessment of their own performances, including some notable benching decisions that backfired, provides relatable context for the strategic discussions that follow.

Gameweek 29 Recap: The Reality of Variance

The episode begins with a thorough debrief of gameweek 29, which felt like "a little while ago" according to Tom due to the unusual scheduling around the FA Cup weekend. Both hosts paint a picture of a frustrating week that exemplifies the randomness inherent in FPL, where even the best-laid plans can be derailed by a single moment of bad luck or an unexpected team selection.

Tom's week yielded exactly 60 points, a score that he acknowledges was "not very good" – though he does point to the unusual situation where the FPL skill of Tottenham's goalkeepers apparently played a role in his outcome. He describes his experience as "treading water," having rolled his single transfer in the end after deciding to bench Liverpool's Hill in favor of Chaliba. The decision proved relatively correct given that Villa scored early, though Tom notes that his second-choice bench option, Chiao, actually got Defcon (presumably a clean sheet bonus) while his chosen starter didn't. He ended up hitting the bench when Anderson was benched out of nowhere, bringing him eight points – a small mercy in an otherwise forgettable week.

His captain, Smeno (Alexander Isak), came within inches of a double, hitting both the woodwork and requiring a Sangari arm save to prevent a goal that would have given Tom a "mega haul." Tom reflects that he feels Isak "doesn't get bonus if he does score," suggesting frustration with the bonus point system that often fails to reward players who create but don't convert chances. The combination of his captain's near-miss and minimal returns elsewhere meant Tom slipped to around the 140k mark, a position he describes as "bumping up and down holding position" with no differentials making a meaningful impact.

Sam's week was marginally better in terms of points – 62 points – but he still dropped approximately 8,200 ranks to around 350k. He describes himself as "basically a gray arrow," indicating a small but measurable decline. However, he's at least somewhat philosophical about it, noting that his team performed reasonably well in isolation, with several players delivering returns they were "due" after periods of underperformance.

The key difference in Sam's week came from his Arsenal defensive assets. Timber delivered 13 points with an attacking return, a clean sheet, defcon (clean sheet bonus), and some bonus points – a performance Sam describes as "very warmly received." Gabrielle contributed 11 points, meaning Sam's back line delivered two double-digit hauls. His goalkeeper Keller managed a six-pointer against Bournemouth in what Sam admits was a somewhat surprising clean sheet given the defending required.

The hosts also discuss some notable mistakes and lessons. Sam brought in Anderson for Anderson (presumably a transfer involving a player with the same surname), which yielded 12 points, though he apologizes to his "favorite player of the season" – Elliot Anderson – for having previously removed him from his squad. Sam acknowledges he's "not going to be making that mistake again by benching him" after the Newcastle midfielder finally got his goal with a "well-taken finish." Virgil Van Dijk, meanwhile, contributed just one point in Sam's midfield, prompting a discussion about the wisdom of playing a defender in midfield when that player plays in a defensive role.

The biggest disappointment for both hosts was Harland (Erling Haaland). Tom predicted he would start and play 90 minutes, and both predictions proved correct – but the Norwegian striker failed to deliver an attacking return. Tom describes it as the "biggest disappointment of the lot," noting that Haaland missed what "doesn't go down as even a chance because he didn't quite get his foot to it" – a "pretty much an open goal" that flashed across the goal from Smeno's pass. The xG on that chance was 0.9, meaning it was a high-quality opportunity that both managers would have been "very happy" had he converted. Tom also argues it was a "stonewall pen" that he would have given if refereeing, though he acknowledges his captain bias is showing.

The cumulative effect of these near-misses and blanks has left both managers feeling stuck. Tom explicitly states that "this interchangeable weight to wildcard does feel like it's dragging a bit," suggesting he's strongly considering using his wildcard soon rather than holding it for an ideal moment that may never arrive. With two free transfers available to both hosts, the question becomes what moves can break them out of their respective funks.

FA Cup Results and Their FPL Implications

The FA Cup draw has significant ramifications for the FPL fixture calendar, and the hosts spend considerable time analyzing what the results mean for chip strategy in the coming weeks. The draw produced several notable matchups that will directly impact which teams receive double gameweeks and which managers face blank weeks.

The most significant outcome is that Manchester City and Liverpool have been drawn together, meaning one of these powerhouse teams will be eliminated from the FA Cup and will therefore have a free week in the schedule – potentially creating a double gameweek for their opponents or simply reducing the number of matches in that gameweek. This has major implications for managers who have invested heavily in either City or Liverpool assets.

Chelsea were drawn against Port Vale in what Tom describes as a "difficult game" for Chelsea – though presumably a winnable one that could still yield clean sheet potential. More concerning from an FPL perspective is Arsenal's away fixture at "St. Aries" (likely St. James' Park, home of Newcastle United) and then West Ham, after Arsenal won on penalties against Brentford in their replay. This creates an unusual schedule for Arsenal that could affect their player availability and rotation in the coming gameweeks.

The hosts acknowledge this "has a lot of ramifications" in terms of "who's in who's out" for the upcoming double gameweeks. They note that some fixtures for gameweek 34 are now confirmed, giving managers the ability to identify probable doublers in gameweek 33 with "probable cause" to plan accordingly.

The FA Cup results intersect with the broader challenge of blank gameweek 31, which is "coming into view" according to Tom. With some teams advancing in cup competitions and others being eliminated, the fixture list is becoming increasingly chaotic, and managers need to plan ahead to ensure they don't get caught with players who don't play in critical weeks.

European Competition and Rotation Concerns

The discussion then shifts to the ongoing European competitions, particularly the Champions League matches occurring "in the background" as the podcast was recorded. Tottenham was "currently 4-1 down" in their Champions League fixture, with Tom noting that "the first 20 minutes looked absolutely manic" and questioning whether "TUDA's going to be around for much longer" – presumably referring to Tottenham manager Ange Postecoglou's future.

This European presence creates rotation risk for gameweek 30 and beyond. Teams still in European competition may need to rest key players in domestic fixtures to manage fatigue and prioritize cup competitions. This affects the value of assets from clubs like Liverpool, Manchester City, and others who are still competing on multiple fronts.

For gameweek 30 specifically, Tom flags that there might be "a few assets that we might be willing to get rid of a week early" before blank gameweek 31 arrives. This could mean selling players from teams that might not have fixtures in gameweek 31 or those whose fixtures look particularly unfavorable in the coming weeks.

The European situation adds another layer of complexity to what is already a challenging decision-making environment. Managers who have invested in players from clubs still in European competition need to monitor their availability carefully and may need to pivot to teams with less demanding schedules as the season reaches its business end.

Strategic Decisions: To Keep or Dump Haaland?

One of the central strategic discussions centers on the question that many FPL managers are facing: should they keep Erling Haaland or look to move to Liverpool assets ahead of their fixture against Tottenham at home in gameweek 30?

Tom explicitly raises this as a possibility, asking whether "we be looking at bringing in Liverpool players a week this week ahead of Spurs at home?" He acknowledges that Liverpool are "struggling in the background" as the podcast is recorded – specifically referencing a poor performance "earlier this evening" that both hosts are "quite glad" happened before they "potted" because they would have been "a little bit more bullish on MoSalah" (Mohamed Salah) if they hadn't seen how that match transpired.

This illustrates the real-time nature of FPL decision-making and how results can dramatically shift the strategic landscape. The hosts were considering advocating for Liverpool assets, but Liverpool's poor performance gave them pause – though both acknowledge the irony that Tottenham also lost heavily in the Champions League.

The broader question of Haaland's value is nuanced. While he remains the most expensive player and a captain option for many managers, his recent blank against Manchester United, combined with Manchester City's fixture schedule and potential FA Cup distractions, makes him a tradeable asset for managers looking to reallocate funds to Liverpool or other emerging options.

The hosts also discuss the captaincy question more broadly. While Haaland is generally considered the best captain option, the discussion acknowledges that "if we were to look past him, who would those prime options be?" – implying that there are viable alternatives for managers who want to captain a differential or who are considering moving away from the Norwegian altogether.

Form vs. Fixtures and Transfer Plans

The podcast promises a return of "form versus fixtures" analysis and "one-week punts," though the transcript cuts off before these sections are fully developed. The hosts indicate they will discuss "captaincy makes return once again" and that there will be "a bit of discussion as to whether Harland is indeed the best captain" before looking at alternatives.

Both managers have two free transfers available going into gameweek 30, and both acknowledge they need a "big game week soon" to break out of their respective malaise. Tom explicitly states he's "looking at kind of using both my transfers this week potentially," indicating he's ready to make significant changes rather than continuing to roll transfers.

The key decision points appear to center on whether to invest in Liverpool assets ahead of their favorable fixture, whether to hold onto Haaland despite his recent blank, and how to structure the squad to maximize returns in the upcoming double gameweeks while also preparing for blank gameweek 31.

The discussion around "differentials" – players with low ownership who can provide rank-boosting returns – is notable. Tom explicitly mentions he has "not much differential at the moment" and that "all in all it feels a bit like Bork's quiet for me at the minute" – suggesting his team lacks the kind of low-owned players that can dramatically improve rank position when they deliver.

Detailed Takeaways

The value of honest self-assessment in FPL management. Both Tom and Sam demonstrate a willingness to admit their mistakes – from Sam benching Elliot Anderson to Tom's captaincy decisions and bench choices. This transparency helps listeners learn from others' errors while also providing realistic expectations about the variance inherent in FPL. The hosts' positions around 140k and 350k demonstrate that even experienced managers can find themselves stuck, reinforcing that consistency and patience matter more than chasing every trend.

FA Cup results directly impact the double gameweek calendar. The draw produced Manchester City vs. Liverpool, eliminating one of those clubs from FA Cup contention and affecting their fixture schedule. Managers need to track cup results carefully because they determine which teams have blank or double gameweeks in the coming weeks. The "ramifications" as Sam describes them, affect everything from squad construction to chip strategy.

European competition creates rotation risk. With Champions League matches ongoing, teams like Liverpool face squad rotation concerns that affect the reliability of their assets. Tom's caution about Liverpool players reflects the need to monitor European schedules when making transfer decisions, particularly for teams still in contention in multiple competitions.

Blank gameweek 31 planning is essential. The hosts repeatedly reference the need to prepare for gameweek 31, which is "coming into view" as Tom notes. Managers who haven't already started planning for this blank week risk being caught short with players who don't play. The strategic use of transfers now can help ensure a full squad for the upcoming blank.

Two free transfers represent a valuable resource. Both managers have two free transfers available and are considering using them both in gameweek 30. This contrasts with the common strategy of rolling transfers to save for a future gameweek, suggesting that both Tom and Sam see sufficient value in the current market to justify spending their transfers now rather than waiting.

Captaincy decisions require ongoing evaluation. Even when Haaland is the default captain choice, the hosts acknowledge the need to evaluate alternatives and consider whether his price tag and ownership justify continued faith. The discussion implies that form, fixtures, and team news should continuously inform captaincy decisions rather than defaulting to the most expensive player automatically.

Who This Is For

This episode is particularly valuable for FPL managers in the 100k to 500k rank range who find themselves stuck in neutral as the season reaches its critical phase. The hosts' candid discussion of their own struggles around the 140k and 350k marks provides relatable context for listeners in similar positions.

The episode delivers the most value to managers who are making decisions about whether to hold or sell premium assets like Haaland, those looking to understand the implications of FA Cup results on the fixture calendar, and anyone trying to navigate the complex spring schedule with its blank and double gameweeks. The discussion of Liverpool's upcoming fixture against Tottenham and the potential to bring in Mohamed Salah or other Liverpool assets provides actionable tactical insight that managers can apply immediately.

Additionally, managers who are holding onto their wildcard and deciding whether to use it will find the hosts' discussion of their own wildcard plans useful. Tom's feeling that "this interchangeable weight to wildcard does feel like it's dragging a bit" may resonate with other managers who are waiting for the perfect moment that may never arrive.

The episode serves as a strategic roadmap for the coming weeks, combining tactical analysis with honest reflection on the randomness that makes FPL so challenging. Whether you're a veteran manager or newer to the game, the insights about squad construction, chip strategy, and transfer planning provide a solid foundation for making better decisions as the season reaches its conclusion.
