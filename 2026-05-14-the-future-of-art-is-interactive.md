**Introduction**

In a candid conversation that spans childhood nostalgia, high-pressure media careers, and the bleeding edge of digital art, Raoul Pal—former Goldman Sachs trader turned macro-crypto investor and founder of RealVision—sits down with Kaldi, an artist who has become one of the most prescient chroniclers of blockchain culture. The interview, recorded during an "art camp" experience in Marfa, Texas, offers an unusually intimate look at how an artist who never envisioned himself as creative transformed into a pioneering figure at the intersection of art, technology, and cryptocurrency.

The conversation matters precisely because it traces a path that few understood at the time and even fewer have documented with such self-awareness. Kaldi entered the blockchain art space in 2017, when the ecosystem consisted of barely a handful of platforms and a handful of artists working in near-total isolation. Today, as the NFT market has exploded into a multi-billion dollar phenomenon, understanding the origins and philosophies of early practitioners offers invaluable context for where digital art is heading.

What emerges from this discussion is not merely an artist's biography but a meditation on how cultural moments get recorded, how technology reshapes artistic expression, and why the conventional museum规矩 of "don't touch the art" represents everything wrong with how we experience creativity. Kaldi's work—ranging from lenticular portraits of crypto luminaries to satirical pieces that capture the absurdities of financial promise—serves as both artifact and commentary on an era defined by technological disruption and cultural upheaval.

**From ViewMaster to Blockchain: The Formation of an Artist**

The conversation opens with a confession that subverts every expectation about what an artist looks like. "Growing up, I would never was thought of as, like, a artist or a creative," Kaldi admits. "I was more like I was kind of a jock kid. So I was always doing sports and staying active with that. I never really cared about drawing." This framing matters because it establishes that his artistic identity emerged not from childhood prodigy迹象 but from a series of seemingly random encounters that only reveal their coherence in retrospect.

The thread connecting those encounters is stereoscopic imagery. Kaldi recalls discovering ViewMaster devices—the classic plastic viewers containing discs of paired images that create a 3D effect when viewed through the optical apparatus. One particular device came from a Taiwanese street market when he was eight years old, living in Orange County, California. When he looked through the viewer, he didn't merely see images—he experienced transportation. "I was living this scene because it had depth and it was real," he explains. "And I was eight years old and it made this, like, deep-seated connection in my brain."

That connection might have remained dormant had it not been for another encounter: his grandparents possessed stereoscopic cards from the 1910s, Wild West scenes and similar Americana that prefigured the ViewMaster technology by decades. "Instead of watching a movie, I'd look at 3D cards," Kaldi recalls. "It was very odd, but it was there, and I liked it." These experiences installed a潜意识 fascination with depth, perception, and the question of how images can create experiential presence rather than mere representation.

High school brought an unexpected acceleration when Kaldi enrolled in a video production and graphic design class populated by classmates who would later become high-end directors of photography for major music videos. "The cosmos aligned, and we were doing cool shit with each other," he remembers. "And then we all went off into the world." This peer group exposed him to professional creative tools and workflows, but more importantly, it introduced him to the idea that technical skill and artistic vision could coexist and amplify each other.

When he eventually pursued an art degree, those scattered interests coalesced into intentional direction. "It was a weird, like, mix of science and math and art in these weird ways," he reflects. "And I guess, long story short, that's what kind of wove me into what I'm doing now." The recognition that art could incorporate technical and scientific elements—not despite them—became foundational to his later embrace of blockchain technology as an artistic medium rather than merely a commercial opportunity.

**The LA Weekly Crucible: Learning Under Pressure**

Art school opened doors that led directly to one of Los Angeles' most influential alternative publications. A professor connected Kaldi with an interview at LA Weekly—the city's dominant alternative newspaper, comparable to New York's Village Voice—with a readership of 100,000 people every week. He arrived right out of school and immediately began designing covers. "You're jumping into the fire," he notes. "If you fuck up on that cover and it goes to print with a typo, 100,000 people are gonna dog on you."

The pressure was immediate and unrelenting. Covers would go to press on Tuesday nights, sometimes after Kaldi had worked until 2 AM making final adjustments. Occasionally, news events would force last-minute changes, prompting editors to appear at 11 PM with entirely new assignments. "Here's your new cover. I need it in a half an hour. Let's go." The speed required—conceiving, designing, and perfecting a professional publication cover in thirty minutes—created habits that would serve him throughout his career.

But the job offered compensations beyond the professional training. Because he worked at the paper, Kaldi gained access to art show openings, concert events, and cultural happenings throughout Los Angeles. He reviewed Tool concerts and Obama campaign events in the same week. He attended major graffiti exhibitions and curator Roger Gasman's shows. Most significantly, he went to a Banksy exhibition before it was publicly announced—an experience that crystallized his understanding of what art could achieve.

His boss simply told him one Tuesday afternoon: "You're done for today. Here's an address, and just go and pretend like you're supposed to be there." Kaldi walked into the famous "painted elephant" installation in Los Angeles while it was still being set up, encountering a level of artistic ambition that exceeded anything he'd previously experienced. "I was not prepared for that," he admits. "I was like, holy shit." He maintained his composure, wandering through the space as though he belonged, and found himself standing next to Macaulay Culkin—who was actively purchasing artwork. "You know, it's just some weird LA shit."

This experience reinforced a practice Kaldi had developed unconsciously: attending every exhibition as if he were a collector with resources and taste. "I would go into every single show with the vision that I want to pick my two favorite pieces from this show," he explains. "And if I was a collector, I would buy those." Over dozens and hundreds of shows, this mental exercise trained his critical faculties, his understanding of what made certain works significant, and his sensitivity to artistic signatures, imperfections, and the intangible qualities that distinguished memorable pieces from forgettable ones.

"I always looked at the side of canvases," he recalls. "I always looked at the imperfections of the signature." This obsessive attention to materiality—understanding what it meant to be an artist "in the gallery," what that positioning required and what it excluded—became crucial when he later entered the digital art space, where questions of authenticity, ownership, and presence were radically unsettled by new technology.

**The Crypto Awakening: Finding Blockchain Art in 2017**

The transition from traditional art world observer to blockchain art pioneer emerged from an unexpected source: podcasts about money. While working at LA Weekly, Kaldi began listening to discussions about gold, silver, and fundamental questions about what money actually is. "I went to art school," he explains. "I literally didn't know shit about finance." The educational podcasts led inevitably to discussions of cryptocurrency, and something clicked.

"All of a sudden, it disregistered, and I quickly saw this, like, emerging, you know, it'd been around for, what, six years, but, like, it was still very early." Kaldi recognized in blockchain technology a new medium with artistic possibilities that hadn't been explored. "How do I help promote this new thing?" he wondered. "My tangible skill on this earth is art. So I typed in blockchain art in Google."

The search results in 2017 revealed a space in its most embryonic stage. "We got CryptoPunks, Rare Pepe, CryptoKitties. There's not very much." He also discovered Rare Art Labs, a platform that allowed artists to tokenize digital artworks and mint them as ERC-20 tokens on the Ethereum blockchain. He approached the platform as "a dating site" for his art—a zero-risk opportunity to share his work with a potential global audience, even though "there was no one buying anything."

The community at that time consisted of a handful of artists who would later become significant figures in the NFT space: X*Copy, Hackatao, Osinachi, Missales Simpson, and Rare Skrill among them. Kaldi minted work on Rare Art Labs approximately one day before X*Copy joined the platform, though neither artist was aware of the other. "None of us knew each other," Kaldi recalls. "It was just some random dude over in UK. I don't know." There was no community infrastructure, no Discord servers, no Twitter Spaces connecting these early practitioners.

Kaldi's early pieces sold for $10-15 when they sold at all. He remembers burning through 25 tokens from a metallic portrait series that never found a buyer, only to remint one of those pieces years later as "Lost in Italic," which eventually sold. The experience taught him valuable lessons about patience and the eventual recognition of early work, but at the time, "nobody gave a shit. It was weird." The platform eventually shut down, its brief existence a footnote in crypto art history that would only gain meaning in retrospect.

**Documenting the Crypto Era: Art as Historical Record**

What distinguishes Kaldi's work from purely decorative digital art is his insistence on capturing cultural moments with documentary precision. He gravitates toward figures who shaped the cryptocurrency space—Vitalik Buterin, Andreas Antonopoulos, John McAfee—and renders them in styles that comment on their significance while preserving their visual presence for future audiences.

"This is an old lenticular of Andreas Antonopoulos," he notes, showing Pal a piece from his collection. The stereoscopic effect he pioneered as a child manifests in his adult work, combining analog visual technology with digital ownership tokens. "In terms of crypto, I was making portraits and things that were a scrapbook item for me that I was living through."

The scrapbook metaphor is crucial to understanding his artistic philosophy. Just as Andy Warhol's exhibitions document an era through assembled portraits of its cultural figures—"it's a time and a place, each thing is there for a reason"—Kaldi sees his work as recording what it meant to be alive during the blockchain revolution. "Because it's the recorded storytelling of what it meant to be alive today," he tells Pal.

His portrait of John McAfee exemplifies this approach. In 2017, McAfee became infamous for promising that he would eat his own genitals on national television if Bitcoin didn't reach $1 million. "We that has to be recorded, and it was funny as shit to put that in there," Kaldi explains. "But I'm like, he said it. Like, yeah. I'm just the recorder here." The artist positions himself not as interpreter or critic but as witness and archivist, preserving the absurdity and intensity of the moment for future generations who might otherwise only read sanitized histories.

This documentary impulse coexists with satirical instincts. When Pal notes that Kaldi's work is "super appealing" because "it's the language I speak and how I think," he's acknowledging the way Kaldi's pieces encode insider knowledge about crypto culture in visual form. The works reward familiarity with their references while remaining visually engaging even to viewers who don't catch every allusion. The satirical element ensures they remain culturally relevant beyond their immediate subject matter, capturing the humor and excess that characterized the period.
