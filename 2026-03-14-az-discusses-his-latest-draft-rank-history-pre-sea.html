Az Discusses His Latest Draft & Rank History: An Honest Look at Fantasy Premier League Success and Struggle

Introduction

With the 2025/26 Fantasy Premier League season now just over a week away, the FPL community finds itself in that familiar preseason limbo—tweaking drafts, analyzing fixtures, and devouring any content that might provide that elusive edge. For fans of the FDL Blackbox channel, this period brings a particular treat: the team's traditional "team reveal" videos, where each creator walks through their draft strategy and the thinking behind their player selections. Following Andy's contribution earlier in the week, Az has now taken his turn, delivering a video that proves to be far more than a simple team preview.

What makes this particular video stand out is Az's candid discussion of his own FPL journey—not just his draft choices, but his complete transparancy about his recent performances, the criticism he's faced, and his honest assessment of where things have gone wrong. In a landscape where FPL content creators often present an aura of infallibility, Az's willingness to acknowledge his struggles and examine their root causes offers something genuinely valuable: a window into the realities of FPL success and failure, and what it takes to bounce back.

The Context: Preseason, Team Reveals, and Community Expectations

Az opens by welcoming viewers to his second video on the channel focused on team reveals, noting that Luke will hopefully be producing his own contribution as well—though Az jokes that Luke is "scared of going solo" after being away on holiday and not yet producing much content. The tone is light and familiar, reflecting the comfortable dynamic between the three Blackbox collaborators. With the new season rapidly approaching, Az explains that his video will cover his current draft position, the fixture analysis that informs his choices, and the reasoning behind specific player selections.

Before diving into the team itself, Az addresses what he calls "the elephant in the room"—the considerable negativity directed at content creators on platforms like Twitter, with himself being a frequent target. This preamble sets the stage for what becomes the video's most compelling content: an unvarnished look at his FPL track record and the lessons he's drawing from his recent performance.

A Candid Assessment: The Numbers Don't Lie

Az begins his transparency exercise by presenting his complete FPL history, starting from his first season in 2007. "I've had a pretty good spell," he acknowledges, but the full picture reveals considerable variance. His first three years (2007-2009) produced an average rank of approximately 186,000—which he characterizes as a learning period. Things improved markedly from 2010 to 2012, when he achieved his first top 5,000 finish (3,800th), followed by ranks of 175,000 and 10,600—the latter particularly painful as he finished just one point outside the top 10,000.

The period from 2013 to 2016 represents what Az considers his prime: an average rank of around 20,000 over those four years, including another 5,000 finish and a remarkable 2,000th place in 2014. "That was a good couple of years," he admits, though he doesn't particularly remember those seasons in vivid detail. This era coincided with his emergence in the FPL content space, as he came on toward the end of the 2016-17 season, finishing 59,000th before achieving his personal best: an 800th-place finish in 2017-18, followed by 62,000 the next year for a two-year average of around 40,000. "That's my best ever year," he notes.

However, Az is quick to acknowledge that the subsequent years have been considerably less impressive. The 2020-21 season—his first year doing Blackbox—yielded a rank of 135,000, which he admits was "lousy," though he recalls being in much worse positions before managing a recovery. The 2021-22 season was better, forming part of a three-year average of 52,000 that he describes as a strong period. "Over those four-year period, I did well," he reflects. "My three-year average was—you know, the lowest was 63,000. So, you know, I had pretty good—I was quite high up in some of the hall of fame tables and things."

The Recent Struggles: A Three-Year Slide

It is the last three seasons that have drawn the most criticism and that Az addresses with remarkable honesty. The 2022-23 season saw him finish 143,000th—a result he actually views more positively than the numbers might suggest, since he was around one million in the world with roughly six weeks remaining before mounting a recovery that saw him pip his colleague Mark "by one point" on the final day. "I'm overall quite happy with how that season went," he explains. "I managed to turn things around. It could have been a hell of a lot worse."

The 2023-24 season, however, represents what he openly calls "embarrassing"—a 445,000 finish that he describes as "really bad." Az explains that he found himself around 200,000-250,000 with a few weeks to go and made a decision that proved catastrophic: "I was like, 'Okay, I can sort of settle for a pretty rubbish finish, or I can really push and try and get into the top 100k.' And I did push. I ignored Haaland, home to Wolves, captained Palmer. I think I went for Romero over Porro. I made lots of tilt decisions and hope some of them would pay off, and absolutely none of them paid off. So I ended up tanking down the ranks."

The 2024-25 season saw him finish 265,000—a result he attributes to struggling to find a groove, starting reasonably well but then ignoring Mohamed Salah "for far too long" and being "too slow for like some of the top picks." This, he believes, encapsulates the story of his last three seasons: "I've just been too slow to react. I've tried to go against the template too much."

Root Cause Analysis: Template Resistance and Data Skepticism

Az's introspection goes beyond simply acknowledging poor results; he attempts to diagnose their cause. "I've completely ignored things like the template," he explains. "Always thought, you know, this isn't the way to play the game. I want to pick players based on what I've done before." He acknowledges that this approach was perhaps more viable in earlier seasons when "there's been far fewer people" and "less tools available," creating a more natural balance. He notes that he used to watch "a lot more football" and relied more heavily on that viewing knowledge for decision-making.

Crucially, Az suggests that he and his Blackbox colleague Mark may have "pushed too much against" data-driven approaches in recent years. "The models and things like they need to be incorporated a little bit because they are so good at synthesizing that data," he admits. "And Mark and I think on Blackbox have pushed too much against that over the last few years, and you know, neither of us have had particularly good years." He notes that Mark managed a top 100,000 finish last year, but acknowledges that their collective resistance to template plays has likely cost them both.

This represents a significant admission from a content creator who has built part of his brand on offering an alternative to mainstream FPL groupthink. "We've tried to fly the flag maybe too much against that, and it hasn't really worked," Az concedes.

The "Expert" Question: Branding and Reality

Az also addresses directly the criticism he faces for being associated with the label "expert"—a term used by the FPL Show and Scout (for whom he works) in their marketing. "Never something I would ever call myself with anything really, let alone FPL," he states flatly. "That's obviously the marketing term they use." He explains that he's been asked why he doesn't tell them not to use it, but points out that he's "not going to tell the Premier League how to market me" and that "if they don't want to use me, if they don't think I'm an expert, then they can not use me. That's absolutely fine."

The choice, as he sees it, is simple: "If the choice is I'll be called an expert reluctantly, you know, reluctantly, but carry on doing it, or not do it, then obviously I'm going to take the former." He expresses genuine enjoyment of his work for the Premier League and Scout, and isn't willing to turn down opportunities he loves simply because of a marketing label he doesn't personally endorse.

Motivation and Moving Forward

Despite the criticism—which he acknowledges is sometimes justified—Az makes clear that he has no intention of stepping away. "I've got a full-time job. I do this for fun. This is like a passion that I've had since 2007-08," he explains. "I love making content. I get great opportunities. I really enjoy doing Blackbox with Mark and now with Andy and Luke. So, I'm not going to stop that while I'm while I'm enjoying it."

He does acknowledge that if the negativity becomes too much, he might reconsider, but emphasizes: "At the moment, no plans to." The final motivation he mentions is characteristically personal: he notes that his wife Yelena "beat me three years in a row, and it's just getting a bit embarrassing. So let's see if we can change that."

The Draft Discussion: Fixtures and Strategy

With the personal background established, Az transitions to discussing his actual draft for the upcoming season. He explains that the video will cover his current team selection, the fixture analysis that informs his choices, and the reasoning behind specific player picks. While the transcript doesn't include the full draft details, the framework he establishes involves looking at fixtures—particularly early-season fixture runs—and making selections based on those assessments combined with his broader FPL philosophy.

Key Takeaways

The Value of Radical Honesty in FPL Content

Az's willingness to publicly display his complete ranking history—including three consecutive poor seasons—represents a refreshing departure from the typical content creator persona. Rather than glossing over failures or focusing solely on successes, he presents the full picture, allowing viewers to make their own judgments. This transparency serves a dual purpose: it builds credibility through honesty, and it provides genuine learning material by showing exactly what doesn't work. For viewers, this kind of raw self-analysis is far more valuable than polished narratives of success.

Template Resistance Has Real Costs

Az's admission that he and Mark "pushed too much against" data-driven template plays offers an important lesson for FPL managers who pride themselves on contrarian thinking. While being different from the crowd can certainly yield advantages, Az's three-year struggle suggests that complete rejection of consensus picks—particularly obvious premiums like Salah and Haaland—carries significant risk. The models and tools available to FPL managers today are extraordinarily sophisticated; ignoring them entirely means sacrificing a meaningful edge. The lesson isn't to follow the template blindly, but to understand what the template is capturing and when deviation makes sense.

The Danger of "Tilt" Decision-Making

Az's description of his 2023-24 season—where he deliberately pushed for a top 100k finish from a comfortable position and "made lots of tilt decisions"—serves as a cautionary tale about the psychology of FPL. The desire to improve rank can lead to increasingly risky choices that actually accelerate decline. His story of captaining Haaland away to Wolves and choosing Romero over Porro illustrates how a single bad decision can cascade into many more. Recognizing when to be conservative versus when to take risks requires honest self-assessment that Az demonstrates here.

Experience and Intuition vs. Data: Finding Balance

Perhaps the most nuanced insight Az offers is his reflection on how his traditional approach—watching football, using accumulated knowledge—served him well when fewer people played and fewer tools existed, but has become a liability as the game has evolved. His acknowledgment that "the models and things like they need to be incorporated a little bit" represents a middle path between complete data dependency and intuition-only management. The most successful FPL managers likely combine both: using data to identify high-probability picks while retaining football knowledge for transfers and captaincy decisions.

Long-Term Perspective Matters More Than Single-Season Results

Az's career demonstrates remarkable variance—from 800th place to 445,000—yet he continues playing and creating content. His approach suggests that FPL should be viewed as a multi-year endeavor rather than a weekly battle. Even his worst seasons contained moments of recovery (like pipping Mark by a point in 2022-23), and his best years came after periods of learning. This perspective can help managers avoid the psychological damage that tilt and frustration cause.

Who This Is For

This video—and by extension this summary—will prove most valuable for several distinct audiences. FPL managers who have struggled with their own results will find Az's transparency validating and his self-diagnosis instructive. Content consumers curious about the personalities behind FPL content will gain insight into how creators actually perform relative to their public personas. And managers who consider themselves "anti-template" or who rely heavily on intuition over data may find Az's honest assessment of his own resistance to analytics thought-provoking.

The core value here lies not in any specific FPL tip or draft pick, but in the meta-lesson about honest self-assessment and intellectual humility. Az demonstrates that even experienced, credentialed players can fall into systematic errors—and that acknowledging those errors is the first step toward improvement. For anyone approaching the 2025/26 season looking to refine their approach, this honest retrospection offers a model for how to examine their own FPL history and identify patterns worth changing.
