Introduction

With just ten weeks remaining in the campaign, the decisions made in the coming gameweeks could make or break thousands of fantasy teams. In this comprehensive analysis, FPL Harry walks through his gameweek 30 team selection, his recent performance trajectory, and most importantly, the chip strategies that are dominating conversations among serious FPL managers. The timing of this content is particularly valuable because the FA Cup draw has just been completed, introducing new variables into an already complex strategic landscape. Between gameweek 31 and gameweek 32, there will be a significant break—including international fixtures, an FA Cup weekend, and then a full week until the gameweek 32 deadline—which Harry describes as "probably going to be the biggest week of the entire season for planning."

Harry opens by acknowledging that while gameweek 29 was "a long time ago" in FPL terms, it's worth reviewing to understand the momentum behind his current decision-making. Before gameweek 28, he was sitting at 167,000 in overall rank—a position that he admits he didn't believe was realistic for a top 10K push. However, after two remarkably fortunate gameweeks, he finds himself just outside the top 60,000, a position where "top 50K is a realistic target." This dramatic swing from 170K to the cusp of the top 60K in just two weeks serves as a powerful reminder that "all it takes is a couple of lucky picks that I've had over the past couple of gameweeks to massively swing and change the way that your season is looking." The lesson here is clear: even at this late stage of the season, substantial rank gains remain possible, and strategic planning around fixtures and chip usage can dramatically alter outcomes.

Recent Performance and Current Standing

The specifics of Harry's recent gameweeks reveal both the volatility and the importance of differential picks in FPL. In gameweek 28, he scored 72 points, moving from 88,000 to 64,000 overall. While he acknowledges this was "a very lucky and a very good gameweek 28," the key insight is how he achieved a green arrow despite several of his premium assets blanking. His goalkeeper, Sename, failed to score, and his Chelsea assets didn't perform particularly well. However, Anderson from Nottingham Forest became his "absolute hero" with a 12-point haul, coming off the bench as an "auto-sub stopper."

The luck continued in gameweek 29, where Harry further benefited from fortunate circumstances. He had no Wilson and no Anderson from Fulham playing any minutes against West Ham, meaning Elliot Anderson was second on his bench as an auto-sub. Jbury Hall and Taroski also delivered, making him "big for the luck with those cheap options." The result was a green arrow despite neither Semeno nor J Pedro scoring—a testament to the value of having reliable cheap options on the bench who can step up when premium assets fail. Harry's philosophy here is pragmatic: "I'm very happy with that. Let's try and do it again in gameweek 30."

His current team composition reveals an interesting challenge: he has 11 players predicted to have fixtures in gameweek 34, which is the blank gameweek, but he doesn't have many doublers—players who play twice in a particular gameweek. This asymmetry is driving much of his strategic thinking about which chips to play and when.

The Four Chip Strategies Under Consideration

With the FA Cup draw now complete, particularly the mouth-watering matchup between Manchester City and Liverpool, Harry outlines four distinct chip strategies that both he and other FPL managers are weighing. Each strategy accounts for the complex fixture schedule involving potential doubles in gameweek 33, a blank in gameweek 34, and the remaining fixtures through the end of the season.

Option One: Wildcard 32, Bench Boost 33, Free Hit 34 represents the most popular and "obvious" approach. This strategy involves using your wildcard in gameweek 32 once you have clarity on what the rest of the season looks like, then deploying the bench boost in the biggest double of gameweek 33, and finally using the free hit to navigate the blank gameweek 34 without having to worry about fielding a team with limited options. The free hit essentially allows you to completely reset your team for that one gameweek.

Option Two: Free Hit 33, Wildcard 35, Bench Boost is gaining traction, particularly among managers like Harry who have "a very good team in gameweek 32" but "don't have many doublers." The logic here is that many managers have squads filled with players who have good fixtures in gameweek 32, don't double in gameweek 33, but do have good fixtures in gameweek 34. By free hitting in gameweek 33 instead, you can load up on doublers for that specific week without affecting your team for the blank. The only challenge is determining when to use the bench boost—whether in a single gameweek, or saving it for gameweek 36, 37, or even 38, though Harry notes "there's often rotation there" in the final weeks.

Option Three: Bench Boost 33, Free Hit 34, Wildcard 35 differs from option one only in the timing of the wildcard. This approach suits managers who already have "lots of players predicted to double" in gameweek 33 and perhaps "lots of transfers in your back pocket" like Harry does. If you can construct a bench boost team without using your wildcard, this preserves flexibility for later in the season.

Option Four: Wildcard 33, Free Hit 34, Bench Boost 35 appeals to managers who want to wildcard early to acquire players who double in gameweek 33 while also having "a good long-term run of fixtures till the end of the season with motivation and things to play for like the league, like Champions League football, like European football in general." Harry notes that gameweek 35 "is quite a good week to bench boost as opposed to gameweeks 36, 37, and 38, which has some more difficult fixtures."

At the time of recording, Harry was "really flexible" on his choice, admitting "option one is probably the most likely, but I am looking at all the other options, considering them what it means for my team going forward." He emphasized that he "probably won't lock into it until we get those cup results between gameweek 31 and 32," when the FA Cup results provide clarity on which teams will have doubles and which will blank.

For managers without all their chips available, Harry offers additional guidance: if you've lost your wild card, save the free hit for either gameweek 33 or 34 and "plan to use it in the week that your team is least well set up for." If you've lost your free hit, you're likely looking at "a wild card in gameweek 34 to buy lots of doublers until gameweek 33" and then wildcarding again to set up for the remainder of the season.

Transfer Decisions: The Haaland Question and Wilson Uncertainty

The heart of Harry's gameweek 30 decision revolves around two key questions: whether to sell Erling Haaland and how to handle the uncertainty surrounding Harry Wilson.

On Haaland, Harry admits he is "considering selling Erling Harland ahead of West Ham away because I'm not certain that he definitely starts that game." This is a significant admission for a player who has been a cornerstone of countless FPL teams this season. The concern isn't necessarily about Haaland's form but about rotation risk—Manchester City may look to rest key players ahead of their FA Cup tie against Liverpool, and with the season reaching its business end, rotation becomes increasingly likely. Harry wants to "upgrade my midfield" and specifically mentions someone like J Pedro, though notably J Pedro blanks in gameweek 31, adding another layer of complexity to any transfer decision.

The Harry Wilson situation adds further complication. Wilson is currently in Harry's midfield away at Nottingham Forest, a fixture Harry likes for him given Forest's defensive vulnerabilities. However, Wilson "didn't feature at all in the FA Cup" and Harry revealed on a podcast earlier this week with Peter Crouch that Wilson "is still carrying a knock." The timing is uncertain—the information was recorded on Monday but published later—leaving Wilson "still a doubt going into the weekend."

The bench situation compounds this concern. Harry's current bench includes Anderson from Fulham away at Nottingham Forest, which he describes as "okay. Not bad. Defcon." However, he acknowledges there's "a chance... that he might not start that game away at Nottingham Forest, the way that he performed in the cup and the way that the other center backs performed while he was out." His other two options are Jbury Hall and Tarovski, both away at Arsenal—a fixture that offers minimal appeal. In his own words: "I don't really have much reliable cover on my bench if Harry Wilson is going to be out."

The potential solution Harry is considering involves buying a better defender and playing a back four this week, possibly moving Tarovski out to bring in James Hill, who has "a great fixture this week" according to the transfer targets video he published yesterday. Arsenal away follows for Tarovski, making this a particularly difficult decision.

Detailed Takeaways

The importance of bench depth cannot be overstated at this stage of the season. Harry's experience demonstrates how critical it is to have reliable cheap options who can deliver points when premium assets blank. His green arrows in gameweeks 28 and 29 came despite his top performers failing to deliver, precisely because he had Anderson, Hall, and Tarovski available to contribute. Managers should ensure their bench includes at least two players with appealing fixtures who are likely to start.

Chip strategy should be determined by your team composition, not by what everyone else is doing. Harry's analysis shows that while option one (wildcard 32, bench boost 33, free hit 34) is the most popular approach, it's not automatically right for everyone. His lack of doublers makes options two and three increasingly attractive, and he correctly identifies that the FA Cup draw has "opened up" new possibilities that didn't exist before.

Being flexible with transfers until you have complete information is often the right approach. Harry plans to wait until after the FA Cup results between gameweeks 31 and 32 before locking in his chip strategy. This patience allows him to make decisions based on confirmed fixture information rather than predictions, reducing the risk of making expensive errors.

Differential picks can transform your season in a matter of weeks. Harry's jump from 170K to just outside the top 60K happened primarily because he had "lucky picks" with Anderson and other under-the-radar options. While luck plays a role, having the courage to pick players that few other managers have can pay enormous dividends, especially in the closing weeks when everyone converges on the same popular picks.

Understanding fixture run-ins is about more than just the next gameweek. Harry's analysis of players like those from Arsenal, Newcastle, Manchester City, and Chelsea considers not just their immediate fixtures but their "long-term run of fixtures till the end of the season with motivation and things to play for." Teams fighting for league titles, Champions League qualification, or European places will continue to field strong XIs, making them better long-term investments than teams with nothing to play for.
