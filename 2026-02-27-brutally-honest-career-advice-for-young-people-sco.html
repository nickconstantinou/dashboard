Brutally Honest Career Advice for Young People | Scott Galloway

Summary: Brutally Honest Career Advice for Young People | Scott Galloway

In this candid conversation, Professor Scott Galloway shares his brutally honest career advice for young people, centered around the concept of "density" ‚Äì the idea that opportunity is a function of where you live. He references research showing that we become the average of our five closest friends, including similarities in body mass index, political values, and career outcomes. However, he notes that even within peer groups making similar incomes, there's significant variance: typically one ends up wealthy, three do okay, and one falls behind. Galloway's mission is to understand what behaviors and strategies separate the financially secure from the rest.

Key takeaway: Location is everything. Galloway strongly advises ambitious young people to move to the biggest city in their nation ‚Äì whether that's New York, London, or San Francisco for tech. Two-thirds of economic growth happens in just 20 cities, and in major metropolitan areas, you're forced to level up your skills because you're competing against the best players in the world. While living in expensive cities like Brooklyn is challenging, the pressure forces excellence and surrounds you with high-achievers who will elevate your game.

Galloway offers a provocative comparison between the US and Europe: "The US is still the best place to make money. Europe's the best place to spend it." He believes America uniquely embraces risk-taking and rewards those who "get after it" ‚Äì even if they fail. In contrast, European cultures often exhibit "tall poppy syndrome," where sticking out earns criticism rather than admiration. This American tolerance for failure is what allows entrepreneurs to succeed; Galloway's strategy was to start nine businesses, knowing that only one big win would make everything worthwhile.

  • Geographic density: Why moving to major cities with high concentrations of successful people is crucial for ambitious young professionals
  • US vs. Europe: The American culture of risk-taking and tolerance for failure versus European tall poppy syndrome
  • Entrepreneurial mindset: Why failure is an essential part of the path to success and should be embraced, not feared

    Main Takeaways

  • Get to a major city: Move to the biggest city you can afford early in your career ‚Äì the competition will elevate your skills and connect you with opportunities
  • Choose your peer group wisely: Surround yourself with high-character, successful people you admire; you rise or fall to the level of your closest friends
  • Embrace failure as a pathway to success: In America, failure is tolerated and even respected ‚Äì start multiple ventures and learn from each attempt
  • Take risks while you're young: The US rewards those who work hard and take chances; this is harder to do later when you have more responsibilities
  • Treat people well: Galloway notes that past failures haven't hurt his ability to raise capital ‚Äì what matters is being ethical and decent
