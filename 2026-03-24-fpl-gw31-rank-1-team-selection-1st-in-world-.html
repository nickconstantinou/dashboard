FPL GW31 RANK 1 TEAM SELECTION 🌍 1ST IN WORLD 🏆

Introduction

In the ultra-competitive world of Fantasy Premier League, where approximately 13 million managers battle weekly for supremacy, reaching the top spot represents the pinnacle of strategic achievement. Yet maintaining that position against thousands of dedicated rivals requires more than just luck—it demands calculated decision-making, deep understanding of player form, and unwavering conviction in one's transfer philosophy. In this Gameweek 31 analysis, we sit down with Eric, the current world number one FPL manager, who claimed the top spot for the first time in Gameweek 29 and successfully defended it in Gameweek 30, extending his lead to 15 points over second place.

The timing of this conversation proves particularly valuable. We're approaching a blank gameweek—a period when several teams have no fixture—meaning managers must carefully navigate their squad selections to avoid fielding players who won't play. Eric's approach to this challenge, combined with his insights on captaincy choices, ownership considerations, and his remarkable hit-free season, offers a masterclass in FPL management that readers can apply immediately to their own teams.

Last Gameweek Performance: A 71-Point Masterclass

Eric's Gameweek 30 proved exceptional by any measure. Scoring 71 points while simultaneously extending his lead at the top of the rankings demonstrates the compounding advantage of consistent excellence. When asked to break down what made the week successful, Eric highlighted several key factors that defined his approach.

The decision to captain Bruno Fernandes proved transformative. "Bruno captain—I don't think that was great," Eric joked, clearly being self-deprecating about what was actually an excellent choice. "I was sitting there celebrating every single one of his assists. It was huge." Manchester United's playmaker delivered multiple assists, providing the attacking returns that made the captaincy choice pay off handsomely.

Eric also discussed his transfer strategy, which involved moving away from Erling Haaland. "I sold Haaland fit," he confirmed, explaining the rationale behind the move. While the decision to transfer out the league's top scorer might seem counterintuitive, Eric's approach reflects his broader philosophy of value optimization rather than simply owning the most expensive players. He also brought in Mohamed Salah during this transfer window, a move that proved fortunate given Liverpool's favorable fixture.

A significant talking point was his decision to bring in Son Heung-min over other premium alternatives—a differential choice that didn't ultimately pay off with only one point in Gameweek 30. "I did post about it a bit like analysis behind it but statistically I just looked like the better play, especially with the budget now that I have—I can really pick anyone I want—but it didn't pay off," Eric acknowledged. "But with one point I was fine with it."

The Arsenal defense also delivered, providing clean sheet points that contributed to the strong overall return. "Arsenal saw defense heal paid off—yeah, can't complain," Eric summarized with evident satisfaction.

The Ownership Equation: Why High-Effective-Ownership Players Matter

A fascinating dimension of Eric's strategy involves his consideration of player ownership percentages, particularly effective ownership among top managers. When asked whether ownership influences his decision-making, Eric confirmed it plays a significant role in his approach.

"It definitely matters, especially now when effective ownership is like such a big deal in the top 100 or top thousand," Eric explained. "Effective ownership can be such a swing in rank. Like selling Bruno is out of the question because it has such a high effective ownership that I can't even consider it."

This insight reveals a crucial strategic layer that separates elite managers from casual players. When everyone in the top ranks owns the same high-performing player, that player's points effectively become "neutralized" in terms of rank movement—you gain what others gain. The real rank swings come from owning differential picks that others don't have, or from avoiding players that everyone else owns but who underperform.

Eric maintains a balance between ownership considerations and his own convictions. "I have like a couple differentials with like Saka and maybe like Hojlund, I guess, but he's also very high in ownership," he noted. "But I do keep an eye on ownership now. It's definitely a bigger role."

This doesn't mean Eric blindly follows ownership trends. Rather, he uses ownership data as one input among many, particularly for high-stakes decisions like selling established premium players. The key insight is that in the upper echelons of FPL, where point differences between managers are minimal, understanding ownership dynamics becomes essential for maintaining rank advantage.

Regrets and Lessons: What Would the World's Number One Do Differently?

Every manager accumulates regrets throughout a season, and Eric is no exception. When prompted about his biggest mistakes, he identified several areas while maintaining perspective on his overall success.

"The Saka December and January—that was just so many points where I tried to go for a different differential play and then Saka just ended up being the right play and he could have harvested so many points," Eric recalled. This sub-optimal timing with Saka represents one of the key differentiators between good and great FPL managers—the ability to identify the exact moment to jump on a trending player rather than either too early or too late.

Beyond tactical decisions, Eric acknowledged bench management mistakes, particularly with Thiago Alcântara. "I've also made such bench mistakes and should have trusted Thiago earlier," he admitted. These moments where a player on the bench would have outscored a starter represent common frustrations for FPL managers, but Eric's willingness to analyze these situations critically demonstrates his commitment to continuous improvement.

Despite these regrets, Eric maintained appropriate perspective: "I mean plenty of mistakes but I mean I'm number one so I don't know if it's been that bad." This balance between self-critique and confidence characterizes the mindset of successful managers who can acknowledge errors without becoming paralyzed by them.

The Hit Philosophy: Why Eric Has Never Taken a Penalty This Season

One of the most remarkable statistics about Eric's season is that he hasn't taken a single hit—meaning he hasn't used a transfer that incurred a minus-four-point penalty. In the top 10 of the overall rankings, this stands in stark contrast to other managers who have accumulated significant penalty debt.

"Number five in the overall rankings—he's taken minus 20 in hits this season," Eric noted, highlighting the contrast. "You've not taken a single one yet."

When asked what would trigger him to take a hit, Eric outlined his threshold clearly: "If I had like just blanks on my team that I don't have free transfers for, I wouldn't—I can't just have a blank zero player. I think at that point I just take the hit."

However, his analysis suggests he rarely sees value in hits. "Every single time I've like tried to mathematically break it down, a hit has never—I've never seen the value in a hit," he explained. "You know that I'd rather bench a player and play someone else on my team."

This philosophy connects directly to Eric's emphasis on building a strong bench. "That's also why I've been really focused on having like a good bench so that if a player doesn't do well, I can just swap them out instead of taking a hit for that game."

Eric would only consider taking a hit in an extreme scenario: "If I really were in a crisis with a starting 11 and didn't have any a wild card or anything, that might just be the tipping point then."

Goalkeeper Analysis: The Pickford Decision

For Gameweek 31, Eric maintains Jordan Pickford in goal, facing Chelsea at home. As an Everton fan, there's obvious appeal in the pick, but Eric explained the strategic reasoning extends beyond sentimentality.

"Even if Everton are at risk of losing the clean sheet, I'd rather that he maybe plays for the save points than swap him out for someone else," Eric reasoned. "And using a transfer—which could be so valuable later to maybe get a clean sheet with someone else—I wouldn't change that."

This demonstrates the forward-thinking approach that characterizes elite managers. Pickford faces a challenging fixture against Chelsea, but the opportunity cost of using a transfer on goalkeeper improvements—even to a theoretically better option—doesn't make sense when considering the broader squad needs. The transfer saved today might prove crucial during the crucial run-in.

Defensive Dilemmas: Navigating the Tarowski Injury

Eric's defensive setup currently features Tarkowski, Van Dijk, and Hall. The immediate concern is Tarkowski's injury status, with West Ham manager David Moyes notably unhelpful when pressed for details in his press conference.

"We're actually yet to find out exactly how bad that one is," Eric acknowledged. "So assuming Tarkowski is out or in major doubt, which players would have to come in?"

The implications are significant—if Tarkowski doesn't play, Eric would effectively be reduced to 10 players unless he makes a transfer. His planned approach demonstrates careful thinking about roster construction: "My plan would be to sell GKep to have an extra player on the bench instead of just taking out Tarkowski directly, because if I had blanking players in the bench anyway, rather have the extra guy on the bench to give flexibility if anything would happen."

Eric identified two primary targets for defensive cover, both with appealing longer-term prospects. "Chiao looks really set up because he's going to be the double game week for later, so getting him sounds like a great preparation," he explained. "Van Dijk has also been my thoughts, but I think for this week it just be like a one-week punt if I've planned a wild card next week."

He also noted that Ben White represents a potential punt, given Manchester United's recent lack of clinical finishing. "I don't really have the match and maybe even a United defender because Bournemouth have been so unclinical as of recently."

For immediate Gameweek 31, Eric identified center-backs against Sunderland as particularly appealing, with both Anderson and Chiao standing out as strong options.

Midfield Strategy: The Saka Situation

Currently, Eric's midfield features only three players—Bruno Fernandes (captain), Wilson (vice-captain), and Saka alongside Zabanci. This thin midfield coverage creates a potential issue during the blank gameweek.

"Saka will come out," Eric confirmed, indicating a planned transfer. "I have some free transfers. So I think again that'll just be like a one-week punt for however I feel."

The replacement options Eric considered reveal his thought process around fixture appeal and potential returns. "I'm looking at Gordon because now when he's playing at the 10 as a midfielder and on penalty—that's exactly what you want as a Premier League manager or like an FPL manager," he explained. "So that looks ideal, especially against Sunderland where I can really imagine it being a bloodbath with yellow cards. I would pick him just on the bet of a penalty—just the hope that he maybe gets a penalty."

Eric also considered Cole Palmer but felt less confident about that option: "I have a hard time picking Palmer against Everton."

The broader point about penalty potential demonstrates sophisticated FPL thinking. "People often understate the power of penalties," Eric noted. "You can have someone like Gordon—he's had an absolutely horrendous game and then someone wins a penalty. Boom—he's got you five points, maybe some bonus despite having a bad game. Penalties are just so powerful."

Other options discussed included Gordon and Damsgard against Leeds, which Eric acknowledged "also looks really ideal."

Forward Options: The Attacking Setup

Eric's front three features Ekit, Thiago, and Pedro. "That looks perfect to me," he confirmed.

A key observation about Ekit relates to his benching in the previous gameweek. "I know Ekit was benched last game. I think if anything that means his chances of starting in Gameweek 31 go up."

This counterintuitive analysis—viewing a benching as potentially positive for future selection—reflects understanding of squad rotation patterns. When a player gets rested, it often means they're being preserved for upcoming matches rather than being dropped due to form concerns.

Detailed Takeaways

Takeaway 1: Free transfers are more valuable than you think. Eric's refusal to take hits throughout the season, combined with his careful goalkeeper management, demonstrates that preserving transfer flexibility often matters more than optimizing every single position immediately. The transfer you don't use today might be crucial during the run-in.

Takeaway 2: Effective ownership matters at elite levels. Understanding what percentage of top managers own specific players helps predict rank movements. High effective ownership means your points gains are "neutralized" while differential picks can provide meaningful rank swings. Selling players everyone else owns (like Bruno) is strategically difficult despite their point potential.

Takeaway 3: Penalties represent hidden value. Gordon's potential for penalty wins illustrates how a single event can transform a poor individual performance into acceptable returns. Managers should value players who regularly win penalties, even when their underlying performance looks mediocre.

Takeaway 4: Bench management prevents hits. Eric's hit-free season stems partly from maintaining a strong bench that allows rotation when players blank. This approach requires squad depth investment but pays dividends by avoiding the mathematical disadvantage of taking hits.

Takeaway 5: Gameweek-specific punts make sense. For a blank gameweek, Eric's approach involves targeted one-week punts rather than wholesale changes. Identifying players with favorable fixtures and using free transfers to bring them in represents efficient roster management without long-term commitment.
