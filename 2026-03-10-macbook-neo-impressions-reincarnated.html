Macbook Neo Impressions: Reincarnated!

MacBook Neo Impressions: Reincarnated!

Apple's New $599 Laptop Aims to Dominate Education Markets

The technology world has been buzzing with anticipation as Apple unveiled its latest product lineup, but the most significant story to emerge from this week's announcements may be the device that represents a dramatic shift in Apple's pricing strategy. The MacBook Neo, Apple's newest and cheapest entry into the MacBook family, has officially arrived with a starting price of just $599—a figure that immediately signals Apple's intent to compete directly with Chromebooks and budget Windows laptops in the education sector.

Marques Brownlee, known online as MKBHD, provides his first impressions of this intriguing new device, offering insights into what makes this laptop both a technological marvel and a strategic gamble for Apple. The name itself is noteworthy: "Neo" is a designation Apple has never used for a consumer product before, leading Brownlee to speculate whether it was merely a codename that somehow made it to the final product branding. Whatever the origin, the MacBook Neo represents something Apple hasn't attempted in nearly a decade—the resurrection of an ultra-portable, budget-conscious laptop that actually has the hardware chops to be useful.

Design Philosophy and Build Quality

The MacBook Neo arrives as a 13-inch laptop that immediately distinguishes itself through its remarkably thin and lightweight all-aluminum construction. Brownlee notes that despite being Apple's most affordable laptop, the device doesn't feel cheap in any meaningful way—a testament to Apple's commitment to build quality even at lower price points. The laptop features two USB Type-C ports on the left side, though notably these are not Thunderbolt ports, a distinction that matters for users who need the fastest possible data transfer speeds. A headphone jack sits on the left as well, and the device includes side-firing stereo speakers that provide adequate audio for casual use.

The color options represent a departure from Apple's traditional Silver and Space Gray offerings, instead embracing a more vibrant palette that includes indigo, blush, citrus, and silver. Each laptop comes with a keyboard that features a subtle tint matching the laptop's exterior color—a design detail that becomes more apparent when comparing units side by side. The 1080p webcam hides discretely within the bezel at the top of the display, and notably, there is no controversial "notch" housing the camera, a design choice that gives the Neo a cleaner aesthetic compared to some other MacBooks in the current lineup.

The display itself measures 13 inches corner to corner with bezels that Brownlee describes as "pretty normal looking" rather than groundbreaking. The screen maxes out at 500 nits of brightness and offers a resolution slightly higher than 1440p at a standard 60Hz refresh rate. While this won't satisfy professionals who demand the highest display quality, it's entirely adequate for the target audience of students and casual users. Perhaps surprisingly given its compact size, the MacBook Neo weighs approximately 2.7 pounds—essentially the same as the slightly larger MacBook Air, a fact that Brownlee found slightly counterintuitive but ultimately irrelevant given the solid overall build.

Performance: The A18 Pro in a Laptop Context

The most compelling question surrounding the MacBook Neo centers on how Apple's A18 Pro chip—the same processor found in the iPhone 16 Pro lineup—performs when powering a full laptop experience. Brownlee acknowledges this is a "bin version" of the chip, featuring a five-core GPU rather than the six-core version in the iPhone, but suggests this distinction likely won't matter for the intended use cases.

The philosophy here seems clear: Apple is betting that most everyday computing tasks don't require the massive power of their M-series chips. Web browsing, email management, shopping, and basic spreadsheet work represent the vast majority of what average users do on their computers, and Brownlee suggests the A18 Pro should handle all of these tasks "totally fine" based on his brief hands-on experience. He explicitly cautions against expecting to edit videos or perform intensive tasks on this machine, calling such expectations "kind of crazy," but also expresses confidence that light photo editing and general multitasking should pose no problems.

The comparison Brownlee draws is particularly apt: this is essentially "a Chromebook, but the Apple one" or "the Safari book" as he describes it. For users who fall into the category of technically-minded viewers watching this video on the day it was uploaded—people who might immediately ask about Thunderbolt ports or RAM limitations—Brownlee suggests this probably isn't the right computer for them. The Neo is designed for a different audience entirely: users who simply need a reliable, well-built machine for basic tasks without any technical hand-wringing.

Strategic Positioning: Taking Back the Education Market

Perhaps the most significant aspect of the MacBook Neo isn't its specs or design, but rather Apple's strategic intent behind its creation. Brownlee articulates this clearly: this laptop is designed to compete with Chromebooks that have "been killing it for years" in educational environments. High schools and even younger students have increasingly relied on Chromebooks due to their low cost and functional simplicity, and Apple has clearly decided they want "a piece of that pie."

The pricing makes far more sense when viewed through this lens. The previous cheapest Apple laptop, the MacBook Air, received a price bump with the M5 update and now starts at $1,100—a significant barrier for budget-conscious students and families. At $599, the MacBook Neo brings Apple much closer to Chromebook pricing while maintaining the build quality and ecosystem integration that Apple products are known for. With the educational discount bringing the price down further to $499, the value proposition becomes even more compelling.

Brownlee makes an important observation about the iPad as an alternative: while the base iPad costs only $329, adding Apple's keyboard accessory brings the total to approximately $600 before even purchasing a pencil—and that configuration offers only 128GB of storage and an A16 chip. The MacBook Neo at $599 with 256GB of storage suddenly looks like a dramatically better value for students who need a proper computer experience rather than a tablet with keyboard aspirations.

The Return of the 12-Inch MacBook

For those who remember Apple's earlier attempts at an ultra-portable laptop, the MacBook Neo might feel suspiciously familiar. Brownlee draws an explicit connection to the original 12-inch MacBook from approximately ten years ago, a machine that represented Apple's first attempt at creating an even thinner and lighter laptop than the MacBook Air. That device, powered by a 1.1 GHz dual-core Intel processor, was notorious for thermal throttling issues and cost $1,300 despite its compromised performance.

The parallel is striking: both machines feature USB Type-C only connectivity, both aim for extreme portability, and both represent attempts to create a "netbook" category device. However, Brownlee theorizes that Apple silicon has finally made this vision achievable in a way that Intel processors never could. The efficiency gains and tight integration of Apple's own chips mean they can now create an ultra-portable machine that doesn't sacrifice real-world performance for its thin profile. In this sense, the MacBook Neo represents "that old 12-inch MacBook reincarnated"—but this time, actually good.

The Missing Features: What You're Giving Up

To hit that $599 price point, Apple made some intentional trade-offs that buyers should understand. The base model with 256GB of storage lacks Touch ID, featuring only a standard lock button rather than a fingerprint sensor. Upgrading to the 512GB configuration adds Touch ID but costs an additional $100. The trackpad represents another notable compromise: unlike other modern MacBooks that feature haptic (non-moving) trackpads, the Neo uses a traditional clicking trackpad with actual physical movement. Brownlee notes this feels "totally fine" but represents a cost-saving measure that distinguishes it from the premium lineup.

These compromises make sense when viewed through the lens of the target audience. Students and casual users likely won't miss Thunderbolt ports, haptic trackpads, or Touch ID—features that matter more to power users and professionals. The core experience remains distinctly "MacBook" in feel and quality, just with the essential features that matter most to everyday users.

Battery Life and Real-World Expectations

Battery life on the MacBook Neo could represent one of its most compelling advantages. Apple is quoting approximately 16 hours of battery life, which Brownlee notes places it in "the same ballpark" as the MacBook Air's quoted 18 hours. Given that the A18 Pro chip is essentially the same silicon found in the iPhone, pairing it with a laptop-sized chassis and battery seems to create an ideal efficiency scenario. The integration between Apple's hardware and software that has made iPhones famous for battery life may translate remarkably well to this laptop form factor.

Other Announcements from Apple's Busy Week

While the MacBook Neo dominates the conversation, Brownlee briefly notes several other announcements from Apple during this product week. These include new Studio Display and Studio Display XDR monitors to replace the Pro Display, an iPhone 17e featuring MagSafe and doubled starting storage along with a new pink color option, updated M5 Pro and M5 Max MacBook Pros with slightly revised keyboard branding, and a spec-bumped M4 iPad Air. These additions round out what Brownlee describes as a substantial week of Apple news.

Key Takeaways

The MacBook Neo targets the education market aggressively. With a $599 price tag (or $499 with student discounts), Apple is making a deliberate play for the Chromebook-dominated education sector. This represents a fundamental shift in Apple's strategy toward more affordable computing options.

Build quality remains high despite the low price. The all-aluminum construction, solid hinge, and overall premium feel distinguish the Neo from budget competitors. Apple isn't sacrificing the essential MacBook experience to hit this price point.

The A18 Pro chip is likely sufficient for everyday tasks. While it won't handle intensive video editing or professional workloads, web browsing, email, light photo editing, and general productivity should pose no problems. The chip's efficiency also contributes to impressive battery life.

The Neo represents the revival of the ultra-portable concept that Intel couldn't execute. Apple silicon has finally made it possible to create a truly thin and light laptop that doesn't thermal throttle or compromise on real-world performance. This is the 12-inch MacBook Apple always wanted to make.

Trade-offs are intentional and appropriate for the target audience. The lack of Thunderbolt, haptic trackpad, and base model Touch ID won't matter to students and casual users who are the intended market. These compromises enable the aggressive pricing while maintaining the core MacBook experience.

Who Should Pay Attention

The MacBook Neo is clearly designed for students, casual users, and anyone seeking a reliable secondary computer without spending premium money. Parents shopping for their children's first "real" computer should pay close attention, as should educational institutions evaluating laptop options. The device also appeals to consumers who simply need a capable machine for basic tasks and prefer the Apple ecosystem without the $1,100+ investment required for a MacBook Air.

However, technically-minded users who find themselves immediately questioning specifications—wondering about Thunderbolt ports or RAM limitations—will likely want to look elsewhere in Apple's lineup. This isn't a machine designed for power users, and those users will likely find the compromises frustrating rather than acceptable. The Neo serves a different master: the billions of people who need a computer that just works without technical complications, at a price that doesn't require justification.
