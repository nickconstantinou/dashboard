Introduction

For decades, conventional wisdom held that more exercise was unambiguously better — that pushing harder, running further, and training longer would always translate to a longer, healthier life. But what happens when the very activity we associate with heart health becomes the source of potential cardiac damage?

This question lies at the heart of a passionate, evidence-rich exploration by Dr. Andy Galpin, a renowned exercise physiologist and professor at California State University, Fullerton. In this episode of the *Perform* podcast, Galpin tackles one of the most contentious debates in sports science: whether extreme exercise — the kind undertaken by ultra-marathoners, elite cyclists, and master swimmers — might carry hidden cardiovascular risks that our prevailing narratives have overlooked.

The episode opens with a story that haunts the running community: Micah True, better known as Caballo Blanco, who died at age 58 during a routine 12-mile jog in the Mexican canyons he had made famous. True was a legend — a man who had inspired an entire generation of ultra-runners by living between villages in the Sierra Madre, running 30-60 miles weekly to gather resources and return to his hut. Despite decades of extraordinary training volume, his heart gave out, most likely from left ventricular cardiac myopathy. This tragic case serves as the entry point for a much larger conversation about what we know, what we don't know, and what we might be getting dangerously wrong about the relationship between intense exercise and cardiac health.

Galpin approaches this topic not as an alarmist, but as a rigorous scientist who wants to separate signal from noise. He walks through heart anatomy, the electrical systems that govern cardiac function, the various ways exercise can reshape the heart over time, and the conflicting evidence about whether we're pushing our cardiovascular systems past their breaking point. The goal isn't to scare people away from exercise — quite the opposite — but to provide the kind of nuanced, evidence-based understanding that allows intelligent decisions.

The Heart's Architecture: Understanding What We're Talking About

Before diving into the controversy, Galpin spends considerable time establishing foundational cardiac anatomy. He emphasizes that understanding these basics is essential for anyone trying to make sense of the conflicting information circulating about exercise and heart health.

The heart consists of four chambers. The two on top are the atria — left and right — and the two below are the ventricles, also left and right. Blood enters through the right atrium, flows into the right ventricle, which then contracts to pump blood to the lungs. After oxygenation in the lungs, blood returns to the left atrium, moves into the left ventricle, and then the left ventricle squeezes to pump this oxygen-rich blood throughout the entire body via the aorta, our first major artery.

This distinction matters enormously. The left ventricle must generate enough pressure to supply blood to every cell in the body — every muscle, organ, and tissue. The right ventricle, by contrast, only needs to pump blood to the lungs, a much shorter journey. This explains why the left ventricle is substantially larger than any of the other three chambers, and why it dominates the visual appearance of the heart. When we draw hearts in cartoons with symmetrical, vertically aligned chambers, we're creating a fictionalized version. In reality, the heart almost always tilts to the right because the left ventricle is so much larger than the other chambers. This structural reality becomes important when we start talking about exercise-induced remodeling.

Galpin emphasizes that understanding these anatomical basics allows us to properly interpret the research on athlete's heart. When we talk about wall thickness, chamber size, or structural changes, we need to know which part of the heart we're discussing and what function it serves.

The Electrical System: Why Your Heartbeat Matters

Beyond structural anatomy, Galpin explores the electrical system that governs cardiac function — the network of signals that coordinate the heart's contractions. This becomes particularly relevant when discussing conditions like atrial fibrillation (AFib), Wolff-Parkinson-White syndrome (WPW), and the various electrical abnormalities that can cause sudden cardiac death in young athletes.

The electrical signal that initiates each heartbeat follows specific pathways through the heart, coordinating the sequential contractions that pump blood effectively. When this system malfunctions, the consequences can be catastrophic — ranging from irregular heartbeats that cause symptoms like fatigue and dizziness to sudden cardiac arrest, where the heart simply stops beating effectively.

Galpin specifically mentions WPW (Wolff-Parkinson-White syndrome) as an example of a condition most listeners will have encountered, even if they don't recognize the name. This electrical pathway abnormality can cause episodes of very rapid heart rate and is one of several genetic conditions that can lead to sudden cardiac death during exercise. He notes that these genetic predispositions represent a significant portion of the risk profile for young athletes who die unexpectedly during physical activity.

The challenge with electrical abnormalities is that they're often invisible without specialized testing. Someone can have no symptoms, appear completely healthy, and then collapse during exertion if their electrical system is abnormal. This is why screening matters and why understanding one's family history becomes so critical — many of these conditions have genetic components that can be identified if we know to look.

The 2012 Controversy: When Science Made Headlines

One of the episode's central narratives involves a scientific controversy that erupted in 2012. Galpin describes how several highly prominent cardiologists published papers in respected journals — including the Mayo Clinic Proceedings — claiming that exercise causes inflammation in the heart, leads to fibrosis and scar tissue formation, and is "not only deleterious but dangerous." This claim "took off everywhere," appearing in media outlets across the country.

The frustration Galpin expresses with this situation is palpable. He argues that the studies in question cherry-picked certain findings while ignoring the overwhelming body of evidence demonstrating exercise's benefits. The narrative became sensationalized: "Exercise causes heart damage!" despite the underlying data being far more nuanced.

The controversy centers on whether the structural changes that occur in response to long-term, high-volume exercise represent adaptive, healthy remodeling — the kind of "what doesn't kill you makes you stronger" response predicted by the Specific Adaptation to Imposed Demand (SAID) principle — or whether they represent genuine pathology that puts athletes at risk.

Galpin emphasizes that this controversy remains unresolved. We are, in his words, "smack in the middle" of ongoing scientific debate. The same morphological changes that might indicate disease in a sedentary person might represent normal adaptation in an endurance athlete, and the challenge lies in determining which is which.

Athlete's Heart: The Structural Changes Exercise Produces

The phrase "athlete's heart" refers to the collection of structural changes that occur in response to long-term exercise training. Galpin breaks these down systematically, explaining both what happens and why.

When you engage in endurance exercise — running, cycling, swimming, skiing — your heart must pump large volumes of blood for extended periods. Unlike the right ventricle, which faces low resistance as it pumps to the lungs, the left ventricle works against what's called peripheral resistance as it pumps blood throughout your entire body. Over time, this sustained volume demand causes the left ventricle to enlarge, developing larger chamber volume and greater wall thickness.

These changes are largely beneficial. Larger chambers can hold more blood, and thicker walls can generate more forceful contractions. Together, these adaptations allow the heart to pump more blood with each beat (increased stroke volume) and to do so more efficiently, which is why endurance athletes typically have lower resting heart rates — their hearts simply need fewer beats to move the same amount of blood.

However, Galpin explains that these adaptations exist on a spectrum, and the challenge lies in determining where normal adaptation ends and pathology begins. He describes several classifications of exercise-induced cardiac remodeling, including one called "supernormal diastole," which sounds counterintuitive but describes a heart that relaxes so efficiently that it fills with blood even more quickly than normal — an adaptation that allows better performance during endurance activities.

The key principle here is that exercise produces both eccentric and concentric remodeling. Eccentric remodeling involves the lengthening and expansion of cardiac chambers — like what happens in runners. Concentric remodeling involves the thickening of walls without chamber expansion — more common in strength-trained individuals and also in people with chronic high blood pressure. The critical distinction is that exercise-induced concentric remodeling is the result of sustained high pressure demand, not pathological disease.

This is where the specificity principle becomes crucial: different types of exercise produce different types of cardiac adaptation. If you strength train, you develop thicker ventricular walls. If you endurance train, you develop larger ventricular chambers. Neither is inherently bad — in fact, both represent the heart's intelligent response to imposed demands. The problem emerges when we try to determine whether adaptation has crossed some threshold into damage.

The AFib Question: Atrial Fibrillation in Endurance Athletes

Perhaps no topic generates more concern — or controversy — than the relationship between endurance exercise and atrial fibrillation. Galpin presents data showing that endurance athletes experience AFib at rates 2-5 times higher than the general population, with some studies of master cyclists showing prevalence rates between 10-13%.

AFib occurs when the electrical signals in the atria become chaotic, causing the upper chambers of the heart to quiver rather than beat effectively. This can lead to blood pooling in the atria, which increases the risk of blood clots that can travel to the brain and cause stroke. AFib also often produces symptoms like fatigue, shortness of breath, and a sensation of the heart "flip-flopping" or beating irregularly.

The question is why endurance athletes seem more susceptible. Galpin outlines several potential mechanisms. First, there's the issue of structural remodeling — larger atria may be more prone to electrical instability. Second, there's the vagal tone consideration: endurance athletes often develop very low resting heart rates due to increased parasympathetic (vagal) activity, and this "relaxed" state of the heart might predispose it to AFib episodes. Third, there's the inflammatory question — whether years of intense exercise produce chronic inflammation that affects the heart's electrical system.

The vagal tone explanation is particularly interesting. A highly conditioned heart operates with what's essentially a "zen" approach — very relaxed, very efficient, beat less frequently. But this relaxed state may actually predispose the atria to fibrillate. Galpin notes that many AFib cases in athletes appear during rest periods or recovery phases, not during training — when the vagal system is dominant.

Importantly, Galpin emphasizes that we simply don't have enough data on female athletes to know whether sex-specific differences exist in this relationship. The overwhelming majority of research has been conducted on male subjects, and the few studies including women suggest the patterns may be different — though we lack the evidence to say exactly how.

The Calcification Question: What Happens to Arteries?

Beyond concerns about the heart muscle itself, Galpin addresses another emerging area of research: coronary artery calcification (CAC) in endurance athletes. This represents a different concern — not about the heart's structure or electrical system, but about the plumbing that feeds it.

The question here is whether high-volume, high-intensity endurance exercise accelerates atherosclerosis — the buildup of plaque in the arteries. Some studies have suggested that masters athletes have higher CAC scores than matched controls, leading to concerns that decades of intense exercise might actually damage the coronary arteries.

Galpin is characteristically nuanced in his treatment of this topic. He acknowledges that the data is concerning in some respects — if we see elevated CAC scores in athletes, that suggests something is happening. But he also emphasizes that CAC scores measure calcified plaque, and there's ongoing debate about whether calcified plaque represents the same risk as soft, vulnerable plaques.

The more important consideration, in Galpin's view, is that we don't fully understand the clinical significance of these findings. An elevated CAC score in an athlete who has been training for decades might represent a different risk profile than the same score in a sedentary person. The athlete's vascular system may have adapted differently, and the relationship between CAC score and actual cardiac events may not be the same.

This leads Galpin to recommend that anyone concerned about this issue consider getting a CAC scan. He's personally undergone this testing, using an FDA-cleared machine that employs AI and machine learning to assess atherosclerosis, plaque, stenosis, and ischemia risk. He acknowledges the test isn't perfect, but argues the information is valuable — particularly for people who want to make informed decisions about their training.

The harder question, which Galpin admits he can't fully answer, is what to do with elevated CAC scores. Some cardiologists argue for statin therapy as a precautionary measure; others are more cautious. Galpin's position is that given the uncertainty, and given that statin therapy appears relatively low-risk, he would tend to defer to physicians recommending it — but he acknowledges this remains a genuine area of scientific uncertainty.

The J-Curve Controversy: Does Too Much Exercise Exist?

Central to this episode is the question that animates much debate in sports medicine: Is there a point where more exercise becomes harmful? The "J-curve" hypothesis suggests that while some exercise is beneficial, very high volumes might increase cardiovascular risk — creating a J-shaped curve where benefit increases initially but then reverses at high doses.

Galpin presents evidence on both sides. Against the J-curve: running 40 miles per week has been shown to reduce coronary heart disease risk by 25% compared to running just 13 miles per week. We see dramatic reductions in all-cause mortality in people who exercise versus those who don't. VO2 max — our maximum oxygen uptake — is one of the strongest predictors of longevity, and exercise improves VO2 max.

For the J-curve: the Swedish study of 50,000 cross country skiers found that the fastest, most training-intensive athletes had higher cardiovascular mortality than slower competitors. A study of master athletes showing concerning AFib rates. Emerging data suggesting that high-intensity exercise might produce different patterns than moderate exercise.

Galpin's synthesis is that while the evidence for benefits is overwhelming, the evidence for potential harms is also real — just less prevalent. "The good news," he says, "is really is, we just don't see it as much in women, and there hasn't been enough research in female athletes." He emphasizes that these concerning outcomes appear to represent a small subset of athletes, and that the vast majority of people exercising at any level are benefiting substantially.

The practical question becomes: who should be concerned? Galpin suggests that the highest-risk group includes elite rowing, running, cycling, swimming, and skiing athletes — people training at very high volumes for many years. For recreational exercisers, the risk appears minimal.

The Prevention Equation: Screening and Family History

Perhaps the most actionable portion of the episode involves Galpin's emphatic recommendations for cardiovascular screening. He argues that despite all the uncertainty about exercise risk, we do have actionable information that too few people act upon.

The first and most important question is family history. If anyone in your immediate or even secondary family has died of heart disease before age 55, Galpin strongly and deeply recommends cardiovascular screening. This includes having a detailed conversation with your physician about family history, getting appropriate imaging (likely including an ECG or echocardiogram), and potentially pursuing genetic testing if indicated.

The second question involves symptoms. Do you experience chest discomfort during exercise? Do you feel your heart racing or pounding in ways that seem abnormal? These symptoms deserve investigation.
