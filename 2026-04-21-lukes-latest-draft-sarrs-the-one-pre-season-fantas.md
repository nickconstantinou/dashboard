# Luke's Latest Draft | Sarr's the One! | Pre Season | Fantasy Premier League Tips 2025/26

## Introduction

For Fantasy Premier League managers gearing up for the 2025/26 season, the pre-season period presents both opportunity and anxiety. With transfer windows still open, team lineups in flux, and striker situations across the league unresolved, crafting a draft that balances safety with upside feels more challenging than ever. Luke from FPL Blackbox has returned with what he calls his "most sensible" and "boring" draft—one that represents his safety net heading into an unpredictable season.

In a video shared across various Discord communities where reactions have included the pregnant woman emoji (a nod to the heavily pregnant midfield), Luke walks viewers through his current thinking on every position. The draft reflects a philosophy built from years of experience: prioritize security at the start of the season, avoid players with immediate uncertainty, and concentrate resources where points are most reliably earned. While Luke readily admits this may not be the most exciting selection, he makes a compelling case that it might well be the one he ultimately commits to, especially given how much remains unresolved across the Premier League regarding team signings and attacking situations.

## The Goalkeeper Strategy: Why 4.5 Million is the Sweet Spot

Luke's approach to goalkeepers is straightforward and consistent with his recent history: he simply does not want to spend more than 4.5 million on the position. His reasoning is rooted in both value preservation and practical experience. "I have in like the last three seasons started the season with Raya and basically had Raya for the whole season," he explains, noting that while there is considerable luck involved in goalkeeper selection, premium options like Raya or Allison almost guarantee top-three scoring finishes when healthy.

However, this guaranteed security comes at a cost that Luke questions. "It's just that we always know that there's usually a five or a 4.5 that'll either beat them or get very close," he observes. At the very beginning of the season when budget is tightest, Luke prefers to take his chances with the cheaper option and address the position later if necessary. For his current draft, Keller sits at the top of the hierarchy as his preferred 4.5 million selection.

Luke acknowledges that Brentford, Keller's club, changed managers recently and "do let up a lot of shots," meaning clean sheets are not guaranteed. Still, he believes Keller is a good goalkeeper capable of emulating the fine season Mark Flekken had previously at Brentford. The goalkeeper's fantasy output depends more on shot-stopping ability and bonus points than clean sheets alone, making individual quality more important than defensive solidity at the team level for FPL purposes.

He also mentions that he has not heavily considered alternatives, though he notes Draven is priced at 4 million and will likely start—making two playing goalkeepers available at that price point. When asked about alternatives, he mentions West Ham's goalkeeper situation is uncertain ("they're in the market for one and maybe even signing one very shortly"), explaining why he has avoided that club despite rotation potential that might otherwise appeal. "I hate rotating goalkeepers," he admits. "I always get it wrong."

## Defensive Philosophy: Clean Sheets Are a Premium Asset

Moving to the backline, Luke immediately establishes his core philosophy: clean sheets have become increasingly rare in the Premier League, making defensive contributions from fantasy defenders much harder to collect than in years past. "I feel like they're at a premium these days in the Premier League," he says. "It's very hard to collect them. Always seem to struggle."

This reality shapes his defender selection in two key ways. First, he focuses on players who consistently contribute defensive actions—blocks, clearances, and interceptions—that generate regular three and four-point returns even without clean sheets. Players like MELO and Tokoski, who each returned eight points in a recent gameweek with their defensive contributions, exemplify this approach. "You know, they can just pick me up threes and fours," Luke says. "You know, if I was to come back and MELO and Tokoski have got four and four each, eight points, lovely. I'll take that all day."

Second, Luke resists the temptation of highly attacking defenders who look spectacular but may not convert that attacking prowess into actual goals and assists. "You can have the most attacking defender in the world, there's no guarantee they actually end up getting as many goals and assists even if they look really sexy and they're right up top," he explains. The randomness of attacking returns from defenders makes chasing those returns a mugs game, in his view.

He also notes that players like Tokoski and MELO have established track records with consistent setups, same managers, and the same teams. "Players don't really change their stripes too much," he argues. "These are giant blockheads and they just love to get involved. It'd be a surprise if they weren't able to achieve that regularly again. It's quite a repeatable behavior, I think."

## Porro and the Spurs Situation

Luke's Spurs defender, Porro, represents a calculated bet despite some reservations. "I have got my reservations with Porro just because I mean, Spurs from last season, you know, who wouldn't be slightly worried," he admits, noting the team clearly underperformed relative to expectations.

However, several factors make Porro an attractive option despite those concerns. First, the fixtures are favorable—"if you put it into virtually any model that you want to, and you've got Porro's minutes vaguely accurate, he comes top over like the first five or six weeks for expected points." Second, Tottenham has signed Palinha as a defensive midfielder under new management, which should theoretically improve defensive structure. Third, and perhaps most importantly, the unfortunate injury to Maddison creates additional set piece opportunities for Porro.

"I know Porro's actually been getting them in friendlies and he is a bit of a business all-action player," Luke explains, noting that even in a recent friendly where Spurs lost 4-0 to Bayern Munich, Porro created their only big chance. "Like he is just a very creative player." Porro is confirmed on all set pieces for now, giving him a reliable baseline of assists even if his attacking contributions fluctuate.

While Luke initially wanted to pursue a purely defensive contribution route with his defenders, Porro's combination of favorable fixtures, set piece duties, and creative ability makes him a decent bet that Luke plans to keep. "There's some players at the end I'll talk about that, you know, maybe I still might have or I prefer even or just can't get them in," he hints, suggesting there may be alternative defenders he is still evaluating.

## The Midfield Investment: Where You Earn Your Coin

Luke readily admits that his midfield is "just stacked with all the money," which is why community reactions have been so emphatic. "And maybe I'm spending too much there," he continues, "but I feel like midfield is like the place where you get your points, where you earn your coin."

This is a deliberate philosophical choice shaped by experience. While cheaper midfielders will inevitably emerge as standout performers during the season—players who look like incredible value at 5.5 or 6 million—"knowing who they are at the beginning of the season is quite tough." Rather than gambling on unproven budget options, Luke prefers to secure premium talent in midfield where attacking returns are more reliable and more frequent.

The complete midfield selection is not fully revealed in this portion of the video, but the philosophy is clear: concentrate resources where goals and assists are most likely to come from, and accept that the front line will look somewhat underwhelming as a consequence. This trade-off reflects Luke's preference for certainty over ceiling in the early weeks of the season.

## Critical Advice: Avoiding Newly Signed Players at Season Start

Perhaps the most valuable general advice Luke offers concerns players who have recently been transferred to new clubs. His core recommendation is blunt: "if we get to the start of season and you've got players in your team that even if they've just been signed by the other team and you expect them to be like a starter for that team, it may not happen straight away."

The reasoning is straightforward. Managers universally want new signings to "work him into our system" and "get him into training for a few days before we do that. Introduce him slowly, get him up to speed." This means players may begin the season on the bench, coming on as substitutes, or being eased into full matches over several gameweeks.

This pattern creates a predictable value trap for FPL managers. "They probably likely to go down in value if you've got a team other people will probably have them, they start getting sold, um, and suddenly it's transfers you got to make." Even if the player eventually succeeds, carrying them through early weeks means missing value at a time when the player base is most active and every transfer matters.

Luke reflects on personal experience: "I was one of the people who had Barco for example... I did fall for Barco cheers as I don't want to be in a situation like that again because things crop up right so try and play as secure as I can and I won't have them in my team."

The qualification, of course, is that every case is unique. Some newly signed players will start immediately and repay early investment. But the general principle holds: avoid the uncertainty unless you have explicit reason to believe integration will be smooth and immediate.

## Detailed Takeaways

**Takeaway 1: Budget Concentration Matters More Than Premium Guarantees**

Luke's goalkeeper philosophy demonstrates a broader principle: spending premium prices does not necessarily yield premium returns, and those premium prices limit flexibility elsewhere. While Raya at 7 million virtually guarantees top-three finishes, a 4.5 million option like Keller offers enough saves and bonus points to compete while freeing up budget for positions where attacking returns are more abundant and more valuable. Managers should evaluate whether they are paying for security they actually need or paying for reputation that statistical models no longer support.

**Takeaway 2: Defensive Contributions Are More Repeatable Than Attacking Returns**

The distinction Luke draws between defenders who reliably accumulate defensive points (blocks, clearances, interceptions) versus those who offer occasional attacking returns (goals, assists) reflects a fundamental truth about FPL defender selection. Attacking contributions from defenders are rare enough that even excellent attacking fullbacks rarely convert their underlying numbers into consistent fantasy returns. Managers should seek defenders who generate three and four-point hauls regularly through defensive work rather than chasing the occasional explosive return from attacking players.

**Takeaway 3: Fixture Models Should Override Player Concerns When the Gap Is Large**

Luke's willingness to keep Porro despite reservations about Tottenham's form illustrates an important principle: when fixture models consistently favor a player by a significant margin over the first several gameweeks, that quantitative signal should override qualitative concerns. "Like who am I to argue with it if everyone's saying that that's all going to be the case?" Luke asks. Managers who agonize over whether a player will recapture form or whether a team will improve should remember that fixture-driven expected points represent thousands of data points and should outweigh hunches and narratives.

**Takeaway 4: Plan for the Beginning, Not the Fantasy of Future Transfers**

Luke's explicit goal is to avoid any uncertainty around his starting squad when the season begins. "I don't want to be carrying any worries at the beginning of the season like that when the player base is so active." This is forward-looking pragmatism: managers should construct their initial squads assuming they will not make transfers for at least the first several gameweeks, not assuming they can quickly adjust to new signings or emerging situations. The cost of being wrong is not just a transfer lost but the psychological burden of managing uncertainty during the most active period of the season.

**Takeaway 5: The Midfield Ceiling Is Worth More Than the Forward Ceiling**

Luke's strategic concentration of resources in midfield while accepting budget constraints in attack reflects a valuation judgment about where points are most efficiently harvested. Premium forwards are expensive and often depend on penalties, cards, and other randomness that midfielders avoid. Premium midfielders benefit from cleaner attacking metrics and more consistent involvement in goals. Managers should resist the temptation to stack premium forwards and instead consider whether their budget would generate more expected points concentrated in midfield with budget options up front.

Ultimately, Luke's draft serves as a solid foundation that managers can adapt to their own risk tolerances—particularly useful as a baseline that proves safety-first drafting is not actually boring but rather strategically sound in an environment where so much remains uncertain heading into the new season.
