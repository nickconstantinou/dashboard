Israel widens Lebanon bombing campaign to target north and central Beirut • FRANCE 24 English

An Escalating Crisis: Israel's Military Operations Expand Deep Into Lebanese Territory

The situation in Lebanon has reached a dangerous new phase as Israel dramatically expands its bombing campaign beyond the traditional Hezbollah strongholds in southern Beirut to target areas throughout the capital and deep into southern Lebanon. This widening offensive, reported by FRANCE 24's senior correspondent Katherine Norris Trent from Beirut, represents a significant escalation in the conflict that has already displaced hundreds of thousands of people and raised fears of a broader regional war.

For weeks, Israeli air strikes have concentrated on the southern suburbs of Beirut, known as Dahieh, which have long been considered a Hezbollah stronghold. However, as Norris Trent reported, Beirut is now waking up to strikes in a much broader variety of areas, signaling that Israel's military campaign is no longer confined to those traditional target zones. The expansion comes as Israel has pledged to widen its air operations throughout the Lebanese capital, carrying out strikes sometimes with advance evacuation warnings and sometimes without, leaving residents in a state of constant fear and uncertainty about where and when the next strike might occur.

The escalation coincides with intensifying diplomatic efforts to contain the conflict, as international leaders watch nervously for signs that the violence could draw in Iran and trigger a wider Middle Eastern confrontation. The stakes could not be higher: Lebanon's capital is now being treated as an active battlefield, and the humanitarian toll continues to mount with each passing day.

The Geographic Expansion of Israel's Campaign

Strikes Reach Northern Beirut for the First Time

In what represents a significant tactical shift, Israeli airstrikes hit northern Beirut for the first time since the current conflict began. The strike targeted the capital of Nabah, an Armenian neighborhood that had previously remained untouched by the bombing campaign. According to Norris Trent, this appeared to be a targeted strike on an apartment building, following a pattern that observers have seen in recent days of precision strikes against individual buildings rather than broader area attacks.

This expansion into previously unaffected neighborhoods marks a worrying development for Beirut residents who had believed they lived outside the conflict zone. The northern reaches of the capital had largely been spared the destruction seen in the southern suburbs, but that relative safety has now evaporated. The attack on Nabah demonstrates that no area of Beirut can now be considered truly safe from Israeli targeting, a reality that is profoundly affecting the psychology of the city.

Central Beirut Strikes Near Government Buildings

Perhaps the most striking development reported was an unprecedented evacuation warning issued by Israel for buildings in the heart of Beirut, mere hundreds of meters from government buildings including the prime minister's office, the United Nations building, and numerous foreign embassies. This represented a dramatic escalation in both the geographic scope and the political symbolism of the strikes.

Israeli forces told residents to evacuate to at least 300 meters away from these buildings before a series of large strikes hit the targeted structures. Israel claimed these buildings housed what it described as Hezbollah terrorist infrastructure, asserting that one particular building contained millions of dollars in cash that the militant group had been hiding in its basement. While journalists have not been able to independently verify this claim, the specificity of the Israeli intelligence suggests a significant expansion of targeting capabilities or informant networks within Beirut.

The choice of targets so close to the seat of Lebanese government represents something more than a military operation—it is a pointed message about Israel's willingness to strike at the heart of Lebanese state territory and its tolerance for the presence of Hezbollah infrastructure in sensitive areas. The proximity to embassies also raises serious concerns about international diplomatic presence in the city and the potential for the conflict to complicate Lebanon's already fragile diplomatic relationships.

Southern Edge Attacks in Jana

Adding to the picture of expanding operations, Israeli strikes also hit the southern edge of Beirut toward the coastline in an area known as Jana. This attack, combined with the northern and central strikes, demonstrates that Israeli operations now cover the full geographic extent of the capital, from north to south and from the Mediterranean coast inward. The pattern makes clear that Israel's campaign is no longer about containing Hezbollah in its traditional strongholds but about systematically degrading the group's presence throughout Beirut and beyond.

The Southern Lebanon Offensive Deepens

Expansion Beyond the Litani River

While Beirut bears the brunt of the expanded air campaign, the situation in southern Lebanon is also deteriorating rapidly. Israel has significantly widened its mass evacuation orders, extending the zone from which residents must flee. For days, the Israeli military had called for everyone south of the Litani River to evacuate further north, claiming it needed to establish a buffer zone to protect residents of northern Israel from Hezbollah rocket attacks.

However, Israel has now expanded this call dramatically. The evacuation orders now extend south of another river—the Zarani River—which pushes the designated danger zone approximately 40 kilometers further north than before. According to Norris Trent, this covers roughly 10 percent of Lebanese territory, essentially the entire southern region south of the city of Sidon. This represents one of the largest forced population movements in the region's recent history and threatens to permanently alter the demographic and social fabric of southern Lebanon.

The Israeli army has stated explicitly that it intends to carry out extensive military operations in this expanded zone and is trying to establish what it terms a buffer zone to provide security for northern Israeli communities that have borne the brunt of Hezbollah rocket fire over the past year. The creation of such a zone would require the forcible displacement of tens of thousands of additional Lebanese civilians and would represent a significant territorial conquest, raising serious questions about the long-term political and humanitarian implications.

The Human Cost in Ty and Beyond

Journalists from FRANCE 24 traveled to the city of Ty in southern Lebanon, located south of the Litani River, to assess the situation on the ground. What they found was a city caught between the hammer of Israeli military pressure and the anvil of displacement. The roads out of southern Lebanon have been clogged with hundreds of thousands of people fleeing the advancing Israeli offensive, seeking safety in Beirut and further north.

However, not everyone is leaving. The reporting team observed considerable numbers of people remaining in Ty despite the evacuation orders. Some of these holdouts told journalists they had nowhere to go—they lacked the resources, the connections, or the family support networks in other parts of Lebanon to enable them to relocate. Others expressed a deeply emotional attachment to their land, stating simply that they did not want to leave their homes, their orchards, and their history behind. This refusal to evacuate, while perhaps understandable from a human perspective, places these individuals in grave danger as Israeli military operations intensify.

The Israeli military has been clear that it intends to operate extensively in this region regardless of civilian presence, and the consequences for those who remain could be devastating. International humanitarian law requires that civilians be protected during military operations, but the reality on the ground suggests that the distinction between military and civilian targets is becoming increasingly blurred in southern Lebanon.

Hezbollah's Response and the Path Forward

Continued Military Operations

Despite the intensity of the Israeli bombing campaign and the expansion of evacuation orders, Hezbollah shows no signs of backing down. The militant group has continued its military operations in the south, firing barrages of rockets toward northern Israel in what appears to be coordination with Iran. These attacks have been launched from the countryside around Ty and other areas of southern Lebanon, demonstrating that Hezbollah retains significant military capabilities despite the ongoing Israeli offensive.

Perhaps most significantly, Hezbollah has announced that it has launched what it describes as a new and more intense phase of military operations. This declaration suggests that the group intends to escalate its own activities rather than negotiate a ceasefire, even as Israel widens its campaign and the humanitarian crisis deepens. The statement directly contradicts international calls for de-escalation and indicates that both sides are preparing for a prolonged conflict rather than a diplomatic solution.

The timing of this escalation is particularly concerning given the broader regional context. Iran, Hezbollah's main sponsor, has warned that it could be drawn into the conflict if Israeli operations continue to expand, raising the prospect of a multi-front war that could involve not only Lebanon and Israel but potentially also Palestinian militant groups in Gaza, Iranian proxies in Iraq and Syria, and perhaps Iran itself. Such a scenario would represent a catastrophic failure of diplomacy and could reshape the political landscape of the Middle East for generations.

Key Insights: What This Development Means

The Conflict Has Fundamentally Changed Character

The expansion of Israeli operations from the southern suburbs of Beirut to the capital's north, center, and coastline represents a fundamental shift in the nature of this conflict. What began as a targeted campaign against Hezbollah military infrastructure has evolved into something approaching a war against the Lebanese state itself, with strikes occurring within hundreds of meters of the prime minister's office and embassies representing dozens of countries. This escalation dramatically increases the risk of miscalculation and unintended consequences.

No Part of Beirut Is Now Safe

The geographic expansion means that the entire capital city is now effectively a military target zone. Residents who previously believed they lived outside the conflict are now confronting the reality that their neighborhoods could be struck at any time. This widespread sense of vulnerability is creating a psychological crisis in Beirut, with people living in constant fear and unable to predict where danger might come from next.

The Buffer Zone Plan Threatens Massive Displacement

Israel's stated intention to create a buffer zone extending to the Zarani River would require the forced displacement of hundreds of thousands of additional Lebanese civilians from approximately 10 percent of the country's territory. This represents not just a military operation but a fundamental alteration of Lebanon's social and demographic structure, with implications that will long outlast the current conflict.

Hezbollah Remains Defiant

Despite the intensity of Israeli operations, Hezbollah has made clear that it intends to continue fighting. The announcement of a "new and more intense phase" of operations suggests the group is prepared to absorb significant losses and continue its rocket attacks on northern Israel. This means the conflict is likely to intensify rather than resolve in the near term, regardless of diplomatic efforts.

The Humanitarian Crisis Is Worsening Rapidly

With hundreds of thousands already displaced and evacuation orders expanding to cover ever-larger areas of southern Lebanon, the humanitarian situation is deteriorating quickly. Those who remain in the targeted zones face grave danger, while those who have fled face uncertain futures as shelters overflow and basic services strain to cope with the influx.

Regional analysts and intelligence professionals will find the geographic expansion of operations particularly significant as they assess potential flashpoints and escalation risks.

News consumers with interest in international affairs will find this update essential for understanding one of the most dangerous moments in the region's recent history. Business leaders with interests in Lebanon, Israel, or the broader Middle East will need to understand how this escalation could affect regional stability and their operations.

The widening of Israel's campaign represents a watershed moment in the current conflict, one that dramatically increases the potential for regional escalation and civilian casualties while simultaneously making diplomatic solutions more difficult to achieve. Understanding the specifics of this expansion—where strikes are occurring, what Israel is targeting, and how civilians are responding—is essential for anyone seeking to comprehend the full implications of this rapidly evolving crisis.
