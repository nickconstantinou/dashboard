    Introduction

    The Smartless podcast—hosted by Jason Bateman, Sean Hayes, and Will Arnett—has built its reputation on effortless chemistry, rapid-fire banter, and the occasional deep dive into unexpected territory. In this episode, the trio welcomes comedian, actor, and voice artist Nick Kroll for a conversation that oscillates wildly between juvenile humor (Cheese Whiz, bollard-related injuries) and surprisingly tender reflections on friendship, creative partnership, and the pitfalls of beginning one's day with a news spiral.

    Kroll arrives already mic'd up and camera-ready, wearing a jacket that prompts immediate commentary from the hosts about his "smart looking" appearance. The episode unfolds organically, beginning with what becomes a recurring segment about the hosts' morning routines—Jason's obsessive relationship with recorded baseball games, his Wordle variants, and his growing awareness that the news is designed to "start himself out in a rut." Into this framework comes Kroll, whose career spans stand-up comedy, sketch writing, voice acting, and premium television content like *The Sex Lives of College Girls* and the beloved *Big Mouth*.

    What emerges is less an interview than a gathering of comedic souls comparing notes on how they've learned (or failed) to navigate modern life. The conversation touches on scavenger hunts from Los Angeles in the nineties, the absurdities of high school "Senior Assassin" games, the art of voice acting, and the surprising ways that childhood friendships can define an entire creative career.

    The Ritual of Morning: Baseball, Games, and News Consumption

    The episode opens with Jason Bateman revealing his deeply structured morning routine, which has become a recurring theme in Smartless episodes. He tapes every Dodgers game, then wakes at 5 AM to watch them at accelerated speed—"Wheel of Fortune the same way," he explains, fast-forwarding through letter selection until the puzzle becomes guessable. This ritual provides what he calls "a reason to wake up in the morning."

    Jason then describes his Wordle-related activities: he plays *quartle* and *octordle* (variations requiring multiple words per day), along with a third game called "turtl." The group has developed a competitive structure where the previous day's winner selects the "starter word" for all three games the following day, creating ongoing interpersonal dynamics. Jason admits to making an error today that caused him to audibly say "no" in frustration—a moment that reveals how invested he remains in these seemingly trivial games.

    Crucially, Jason reflects on how news consumption affects his mood. After completing his games, he turns to the New York Times, Bloomberg, and Washington Post, only to find himself demotivated by the time he finishes: "I'm so low by the time I stopped looking at the news. I gotta rally to die at this point." He considers either algorithmic adjustments or simply avoiding news entirely, noting that a conversation with Shani (likely his wife) served as a better "palate cleanser."

    Will, ever practical, suggests he try meditation. Jason is skeptical, asking if he should "do a good morning fuck up" instead. This exchange captures the podcast's recurring interest in how adults structure their days, balance stimulation, and manage mental health without sacrificing their sense of self.

    The Anatomy of a Voice Acting Career

    When Nick Kroll enters the conversation, the hosts immediately address his prolific voice work. "You've voiced about 80 animated characters, for real," Sean states, and Kroll confirms this is accurate. The conversation shifts to specific roles, and Kroll demonstrates his versatility by recalling characters on command.

    He performed Gunter from *Sing* and *Sing 2*, the flamboyant pink pig whose ambition drives much of those films' plots. More interesting is the story of Douche from *Sausage Party*, the Rogen-directed comedy about sentient grocery items. Kroll initially approached the character with a "classic Disney British villain" voice—comparable to Jafar or Scar—but this wasn't working. On a final recording session, the character was rewritten as "Bobby Bottleservice," a flamboyant club promoter type, and the comedy finally clicked.

    This anecdote reveals the collaborative and often experimental nature of voice acting, where a character's essence might take multiple approaches before landing. Kroll's willingness to share this process—particularly the failed attempts—demonstrates his grounded perspective on a career that might seem magical from the outside.

    Big Mouth, Arnette, and the Origin of Creative Partnership

    The conversation then turns to *Big Mouth*, Netflix's long-running animated series about kids navigating puberty. The hosts work to "clear the air" about a persistent fan confusion: many viewers believe the character Arnette, the hormone monster, is voiced by Sean Hayes. In fact, Kroll performs the role.

    Here's where the backstory becomes compelling. Kroll and Andrew Goldberg met on the first day of first grade—more than thirty years ago. They've remained best friends and creative partners, and when they developed *Big Mouth*, they drew heavily on their own "humiliating stories about puberty." This personal origin story explains the show's emotional authenticity amid its raunchy humor.

    The revelation that Kroll voices Arnette—a character whose design and mannerisms clearly evoke Hayes's public persona—raises questions about creative borrowing. Did Kroll model the voice on Hayes? Or did Hayes simply have a generic "hornball energy" that Kroll unconsciously captured? The conversation implies the latter, though Kroll admits to finding the ongoing confusion amusing.

    Also noteworthy: Kroll's father worked tracking the hidden fortunes of dictators, a detail that suggests Kroll grew up with access to unusual information about global financial skulduggery. Combined with his Georgetown history major, this background informs a worldview that occasionally surfaces in his comedy and character work.

    Scavenger Hunts, Senior Assassins, and Social Games

    The episode takes an unexpected nostalgic turn when the conversation drifts toward elaborate social games from Los Angeles in the nineties. Jason remembers an event called LARF (Los Angeles Role Find or similar), which operated like *The Amazing Race* through the city. Nick confirms he attended, along with many other actors "in their twenties in the nineties." The game involved visiting locations like Madam Tussauds to find specific items or complete challenges.

    Will recalls another variant where participants carried squirt guns, tagging people in public spaces—actors at Warner Brothers lots, for instance. The idea of walking onto a studio lot today armed with a water gun strikes the hosts as charmingly retro.

    More recent is the "Senior Assassin" game Jason describes at his daughter Franny's school. Students are assigned targets and must hit them with water guns; those hit are eliminated. The only defense is wearing swim goggles or floaties throughout the day, leading to absurd scenes of teenagers navigating supermarkets and shopping centers in full aquatic preparedness. The hosts express nostalgia for these high-end scavenger hunts and briefly discuss reviving them for adult celebrities.

    This tangent reveals something about the Los Angeles entertainment community: a culture of playful competition and creative games that built social bonds outside formal work contexts. The absence of such events today seems to genuinely disappoint Jason, who asks, "Why have those gone?"

    A Childhood Accident and a Two-Year-Old's Language

    Nick shares that he accidentally taught his two-year-old son to say "I'm a coward," which Jason notes made him laugh when he read about it. This anecdote exemplifies the chaos of early parenthood—Kroll's child has absorbed a phrase whose meaning he doesn't yet understand, creating comedic potential in any future public declaration.

    Will also admits to running into a bollard (a low cement stopper at sidewalk corners) while leaving the Waverly Inn restaurant, specifically "right above the knee." He sustained what sounds like a significant injury, and while the hosts joke about calling medical assistance, the story underscores the mundane physical hazards of daily life in urban environments. Bollards painted black become particularly treacherous, Will notes, as they blend into their surroundings.

    Takeaways

    The structure of your morning determines your emotional baseline. Jason's self-awareness about news consumption—"I gotta rally to die at this point"—suggests that ambient information intake shapes mood more profoundly than many realize. The takeaway isn't necessarily avoiding news but recognizing that certain inputs systematically depress mood before creative work begins. Adjusting algorithms, meditating, or simply calling a friend might constitute legitimate productivity strategies.

    Creative partnerships forged in childhood carry unique advantages. Kroll and Goldberg's thirty-plus-year friendship generated *Big Mouth* specifically because they could excavate genuinely humiliating shared experiences from adolescence. This depth of material wouldn't be available to collaborators who met professionally, making childhood friendships a potential long-term creative asset that adult career-builders should nurture rather than abandon.

    Voice acting requires willingness to fail repeatedly. Kroll's Douche from *Sausage Party* story demonstrates that even experienced performers can't always find a character's voice on the first attempt—or the fifth. Success came only after a complete reconceptualization, suggesting that voice acting functions less as talent expression than as collaborative problem-solving. Aspiring voice actors should expect extended experimentation before landing performances.

    The blurring of personal identity and public persona is an occupational hazard. That fans routinely mistake Sean for Arnette—despite Kroll performing the role—reveals how audiences project associations onto recognizable figures. For public-facing performers, this misrecognition might become permanent, requiring either acceptance or explicit correction in every interaction.

    Social games serve real bonding functions for creative communities. The LARF and squirt gun games weren't merely diversions; they built trust and familiarity among actors who would later collaborate professionally. Contemporary Los Angeles lacks such informal bonding mechanisms, potentially weakening creative networks. Communities seeking to strengthen connections might consider reviving structured social games as legitimate networking tools.
