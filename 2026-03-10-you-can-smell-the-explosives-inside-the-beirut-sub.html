'You Can Smell the Explosives': Inside the Beirut Suburbs Targeted by Israel

Introduction

The southern suburbs of Beirut, known as Dahieh, have long served as the primary stronghold for Hezbollah in Lebanon. For approximately one week, this densely populated residential area has faced relentless Israeli air strikes, transforming what was once a bustling urban neighborhood into a landscape of devastation and fear. FRANCE 24 English gained rare access to this contested territory during a lull in the bombing, offering viewers an intimate look at how civilians are coping with the escalating conflict—and why many refuse to leave despite explicit evacuation orders from the Israeli Defense Forces.

The report captures a community in limbo: hundreds of thousands have fled, but thousands remain, caught between the terror of bombardment and the impossibility of abandoning homes, businesses, and generations of rooted identity. This is not merely a story about military strategy or political posturing—it is a human story about ordinary people navigating impossible choices in a war that seems to have no end.

The Landscape of Destruction

Journalists entering Dahieh during a temporary cessation of strikes encountered an almost surreal emptiness. Danny, a longtime resident who guided reporters through the neighborhood, walked them through what he called "the heart of Dahieh" along Nasala Boulevard. The atmosphere, he explained, was "very tense now" and the streets were "almost empty"—a stark contrast to what this area would normally resemble.

The most immediate sensory experience for anyone entering these neighborhoods is the lingering smell of explosives. "You can smell all the explosives from the missiles and bombings," Danny explained, standing amid the wreckage. This haunting odor serves as a constant reminder of the violence that has rained down on this community day and night for seven consecutive days.

The roadsides of Dahieh are lined with the debris of destruction from what witnesses describe as "dozens of Israeli air strikes." The reporters visited the Ratel district, where yet another building had been reduced to rubble. Perhaps most significantly, two missiles struck a building located directly beside the Iranian embassy—an attack that carries profound geopolitical implications given Iran's longstanding support for Hezbollah. The proximity of this strike to a diplomatic mission underscores the escalating nature of the conflict and the willingness of Israeli forces to strike targets in close proximity to civilian infrastructure and foreign interests.

The Evacuation Dilemma

Israel has issued mass evacuation orders to the residents of Dahieh, warning that continued presence in the area carries fatal consequences. The Israeli Defense Forces have been relentless in their bombardment of what they characterize as Hezbollah military infrastructure embedded within civilian neighborhoods. Yet despite these orders—and the very real danger they represent—not everyone is complying.

The FRANCE 24 team encountered residents who had developed elaborate strategies for surviving the bombardment while refusing to abandon their homes entirely. One man explained that he sleeps in the mountains but returns to Dahieh every day, moving back and forth between safety and home. "I'm better off here," he said. "When I leave, I feel something's missing. I just feel better here." This sentiment captures a profound psychological truth about displacement: for many, the threat of death feels less intolerable than the complete severance from one's home, community, and identity.

Others have returned briefly to collect belongings before heading elsewhere, recognizing that the strikes may continue indefinitely. "They fear that the Israeli strikes will continue," the reporter noted, describing the careful calculus many families are making—gathering what they can carry while they still can.

Even in the midst of what is essentially a war zone, some businesses remain operational. The journalists found a garage that was still open and busy with customers, a testament to both economic necessity and a peculiar form of defiant normalcy. "There are people who don't have the money to leave," one resident explained, "so they either sleep by the roadside or stay at home. And even if they die, at least they'll die [here]."

Voices from the Community

The human cost of this conflict becomes clearest through individual stories. One father, identified only as a man with a family of five—himself, his wife, and their three sons—articulated the impossible position facing many residents. "We've got nowhere else to go," he said simply. His son Abbas, a young boy, has developed acute psychological trauma from the constant bombardment. "He's scared as soon as he hears gunfire," the father explained. "He's so scared he puts his hand over his ears and just stays like that until it calms down. He's scared whenever there are air raids or drones flying over the house."

This child's reaction—a small hand pressed desperately over his ears, a body frozen in fear—encapsulates the psychological violence being inflicted on an entire generation of Lebanese children. The sound of drones overhead has become a source of terror, a constant reminder that death may arrive without warning at any moment.

Another family, this one with seven members, was busily repairing their car in the street—desperate repairs meant to enable their escape. The vehicle represented their only means of fleeing, and its malfunction threatened to trap them in the path of ongoing bombardment.

One woman spoke of the compounding displacement she has experienced. Her mother and uncle had originally fled from southern Lebanon to shelter with her in Dahieh—they had been displaced once, forced from their original homes by earlier rounds of conflict. Now, they face displacement again, ordered to leave Dahieh as the Israeli offensive intensifies. "We have nowhere else to go," she said, reflecting a sentiment echoed throughout the neighborhood.

The Scale of Displacement

The Lebanese government has documented what can only be described as an exodus. According to authorities, nearly 700,000 people have already fled their homes across the country—a figure that represents a massive humanitarian crisis unfolding in real time. This wave of displacement joins the hundreds of thousands of Palestinians who have been displaced from Gaza, creating a regional humanitarian catastrophe of enormous proportions.

The footage captured by FRANCE 24 shows families once again on the move, carrying what belongings they can, searching for safety that seems increasingly difficult to find. The roads out of Dahieh are filled with vehicles packed with household goods, children, and the elderly—all of them joining what has become a vast procession of people displaced by war.

Key Takeaways

The human cost of urban warfare extends far beyond immediate casualties. The report reveals that the psychological impact on children like Abbas may prove as damaging as any physical injury. The trauma of constant air raids, the sound of drones, and the perpetual uncertainty of whether one's home will survive the next strike creates a generation of children who associate their neighborhood with fear and survival rather than safety and childhood.

Economic constraints prevent evacuation even when death is imminent. The garage owner who remains open for business and the residents who "don't have the money to leave" illuminate a brutal truth: poverty becomes a death sentence in times of war. Those without resources cannot comply with evacuation orders because evacuation requires money—for fuel, for temporary housing, for food in a strange place. The war thus disproportionately kills the poor.

Refusal to evacuate is not simply irrational stubbornness. While Israeli officials and military analysts may characterize remaining residents as reckless or foolish, the report reveals a more complex psychological reality. For many, home represents something more than physical shelter—it is identity, community, history, and meaning. Leaving, for these residents, feels not like survival but like a kind of death already.

Hezbollah's integration into civilian neighborhoods makes civilian casualties virtually inevitable. The report notes that these neighborhoods "are under Hezbollah control" and that Israeli strikes have targeted buildings described as Hezbollah infrastructure. This military reality creates an impossible situation for civilians: they live among military assets that make them targets, yet they have no control over whether those assets remain or where strikes will land.

The 700,000 figure represents only those who have already fled—a fraction of those at risk. Lebanese authorities' count of displaced persons does not include those who have been ordered to evacuate but have not yet done so, nor those who have fled temporarily and may return. The true number of people whose lives have been disrupted by this conflict is likely far higher.

Who This Is For

This report serves anyone seeking to understand the human reality behind headlines about the Israel-Hezbollah conflict. It is particularly valuable for policy analysts and humanitarian workers who need to comprehend why civilians make the choices they do—including choices that may seem irrational from outside perspectives. News consumers who want deeper context beyond brief television segments will find here a nuanced portrait of a community under siege.

The report also provides essential context for anyone studying displacement, refugee crises, or the psychological effects of warfare on civilian populations. The story of Abbas, a child who covers his ears whenever he hears aircraft, may be the most memorable and devastating image this conflict has produced—because it reminds viewers that war is not fought by armies alone. It is lived, suffered, and survived by families like the one in Dahieh who refuse to leave, not because they do not understand the danger, but because they understand that leaving may mean losing everything that makes life worth living.
