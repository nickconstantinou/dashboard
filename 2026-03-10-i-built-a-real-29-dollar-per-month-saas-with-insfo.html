I built a real 29 dollar per month SAAS with Insforge

Building a $29/month SaaS with Insforge: An AI-Powered Real-Time Experiment

Introduction

The reliability of modern digital services has become increasingly frustrating for developers and businesses alike. In a recent video, a coding content creator documented their journey to build a functional SaaS product that monitors service uptime—essentially a "down detector" for the many tools developers rely on daily. The timing couldn't be more relevant: major services like Gemini, Claude, Superbase, Twitter, and even GitHub have experienced significant outages recently, often without proper status page updates. This video represents a real-time experiment to see whether modern AI tools can actually build a sellable software product from scratch, with no pre-written code or script.

The core idea emerged from the creator's frustration with companies monitoring their own downtime and frequently failing to update their status pages promptly. What if instead, independent monitors could track these services and provide reliable, third-party verification of outages? The creator recognized this as a proven SaaS market opportunity, pointing to Accenture's acquisition of Okla (which owns Down Detector and Speed Test) as evidence that uptime monitoring is a legitimate and valuable business segment. The experiment uses Insforge, an open-source backend platform for AI agent development that positions itself as a Supabase alternative, combined with Cursor's AI-powered development environment to build the entire application autonomously.

The Problem: A Gap in the Market

The video opens by highlighting a widespread issue that many developers have experienced but rarely articulate as a business opportunity. When services like Gemini or Claude go down, developers scramble to verify whether the problem lies with their own implementation or the external service itself. Currently, they must rely on the very companies experiencing outages to accurately report those outages—a clear conflict of interest. The creator describes seeing status pages that feel like static PNG images rather than dynamically updated resources, leaving users without reliable information during critical downtime events.

Existing solutions in this space, particularly Uptime Robot, charge approximately $7 per month for solopreneurs seeking 10 monitors with 60-second checking intervals. While functional, these services represent an opportunity for a more affordable or feature-rich alternative. The creator noted that even services with significant user bases, such as those with 100,000 active users, remain priced at just $25 per month—suggesting the market could support additional competition. The key insight is that reliability itself has tremendous value: a service that consistently provides accurate downtime information could capture significant market share from established players who have grown complacent.

The Technology Stack: Insforge and Cursor

The creator chose Insforge as their backend infrastructure for several compelling reasons. The platform describes itself as "backend built for agentic AI development" and positions itself as an AI-native Supabase alternative—a bold claim that intrigued the creator. Crucially, Insforge offers one-click Docker deployment, meaning the entire backend can be self-hosted on any compatible infrastructure. This provides complete control over the data and eliminates dependency on third-party platforms that could themselves experience outages.

The pricing structure proved attractive for an experimental project. The free tier costs nothing and includes $1 in AI model credits, while the $25 per month plan provides $10 in credits along with 100,000 monthly active users, 8GB storage, and 25GB bandwidth. For a solopreneur just starting out, the free tier offers more than enough capability to validate the concept before upgrading. The creator explicitly stated they would not hesitate to upgrade if the SaaS proved viable, demonstrating a pragmatic approach to scaling the business.

For the frontend development environment, the video showcases Cursor with MCP (Model Context Protocol) tools. The setup process reveals how AI-native development works in practice: the extension automatically installs, configures API keys, and loads 17 different tools including functions for fetching documentation, managing SDK references, and executing code. This represents a fundamental shift in how developers interact with their IDEs—rather than writing code line-by-line, the creator describes giving their "agents the autonomy to act on behalf of me."

The Build Process: A Real-Time Experiment

What makes this video particularly compelling is that the creator explicitly states they have no script and no existing code. Viewers witness the entire development process as it happens, including the inevitable failures and iterations that characterize real software development. The creator prepared tea for the session, joking that they would "sip the tea and let the agent work"—a testament to the autonomous capabilities of modern AI coding assistants.

The workflow involves crafting detailed prompts that describe the desired functionality. The creator studied example prompts from the platform, such as building a Reddit-like community application, to understand how to structure requests effectively. For their down detector, they began specifying features including user authentication, the ability to add endpoints for monitoring, configuration of check intervals, and notification systems. The AI then autonomously planned and executed the implementation steps, fetching relevant documentation through the MCP tools to understand Insforge's capabilities and constraints.

The video demonstrates the practical reality of AI-assisted development: it involves significant back-and-forth, iterative refinement, and the occasional roadblock that requires human intervention. Rather than a magical instant solution, the process shows how AI tools can dramatically accelerate development while still requiring direction and oversight from a human developer.

Business Potential and Market Validation

The creator makes a compelling case for why this particular SaaS idea has genuine market viability. The downtime monitoring space is not theoretical—it's already proven through acquisitions like Accenture's purchase of Okla. The key differentiator they aim to achieve is reliability: building a service that users can trust during critical moments when other tools have failed them. This positioning addresses a genuine pain point that many developers experience but rarely find satisfactory solutions for.

The pricing strategy centers on offering a free tier to attract initial users while maintaining a paid option that provides sufficient functionality for professional use. At $29 per month (as suggested in the title), the service would position itself competitively against Uptime Robot while potentially offering additional features or better reliability. The creator's approach demonstrates the lean startup methodology: build a minimum viable product, validate it with real users, and only invest in infrastructure upgrades once the concept proves viable.

Key Takeaways

The market for infrastructure reliability tools remains underserved. Major services continue experiencing outages without adequate transparency, creating opportunities for independent monitoring solutions that users can trust. The acquisition of Down Detector by Accenture validates that this market has significant value that larger corporations are willing to pay for.

AI development tools have reached a practical tipping point. The ability to build a functional SaaS product using natural language prompts and autonomous agents represents a fundamental shift in software development. While the process still requires human guidance and domain expertise, the speed at which a complete application can be conceptualized and implemented has dramatically improved.

Self-hosted infrastructure provides strategic advantages. By choosing Insforge with Docker deployment, the creator ensures their monitoring service cannot experience the same outages it would be monitoring for. This architectural decision eliminates a single point of failure that would undermine the service's core value proposition.

The verification-first approach reduces risk. Rather than investing significant capital before validating the concept, the creator uses free tiers and experimental tools to test whether the market actually wants this product. This pragmatic approach to SaaS development allows for rapid iteration without substantial financial commitment.

Community engagement determines success. The creator explicitly requests viewer feedback, recognizing that audience response provides crucial validation for whether to continue developing this product line. This represents a modern approach to product development where the audience actively shapes the creator's priorities.

The audience will gain insights into how to use tools like Cursor and Insforge effectively, understand the real costs and considerations when launching a SaaS product, and learn how to identify market opportunities in infrastructure and reliability services.
