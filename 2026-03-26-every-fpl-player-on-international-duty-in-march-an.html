Every FPL player on international duty in March – and when

📖 Read original on Fantasy Football Scout →

The March international break has arrived as the final such break of the Premier League season, bringing together a substantial contingent of Fantasy Premier League assets who will represent their nations over the coming fortnight. From March 25 through early April, dozens of FPL players will be jetting off to fulfill international commitments, with their countries competing in everything from friendly matches to high-stakes World Cup qualification playoffs. This period represents a critical juncture for fantasy managers, who must weigh the risk of injury or burnout against the potential for players to return fresh and ready for the decisive run-in.

The Good News: No Late-Return Concerns This Time

Unlike typical international windows—particularly those featuring South American qualifiers that often conclude in the early hours of Wednesday mornings, leaving players scrambling back to their clubs with minimal preparation time—this March break presents significantly reduced concerns. The final international fixture of this window concludes in the early hours of Wednesday, April 1. Crucially, Gameweek 32 does not kick off until Friday, April 10, providing a generous ten-day gap between international duty and domestic resumption. When Premier League football returns, it will be with the FA Cup quarter-finals, meaning FPL managers need not worry about jet lag, quick turnarounds, or players returning too fatigued to feature immediately. This extended layover essentially eliminates the primary risk factor that typically makes international breaks stressful for fantasy owners.

What's Actually at Stake This Break

While most nations will be conducting friendly matches—raising hopes among Premier League managers that minutes will be carefully managed to protect players—the March break also features fixtures with substantial competitive importance. The final six places in the 2026 FIFA World Cup will be determined during this window, with four European nations and two inter-confederation qualifiers battling through playoffs. This means certain FPL players will be participating in high-pressure matches rather than low-intensity friendlies, potentially increasing injury or fatigue risks.

All Affected FPL Players by Nation

The World Cup playoff qualifiers feature Premier League players from numerous nations. From DR Congo, Manchester United loanee Axel Tuanzebe (Burnley), Brentford's Yoane Wissa, Luton trio Noah Sadiki and Trei Hume, and West Ham's Aaron Wan-Bissaka will all be involved. Suriname's sole Premier League representative is Leeds' Joel Piroe. Jamaica fields Aston Villa's Leon Bailey and Brentford's Ethan Pinnock.

Wales presents particular concerns for FPL managers, with Bournemouth's David Brooks, Crystal Palace's Brennan Johnson, Everton's Tom King, Fulham's Harry Wilson, Leeds' quartet of Ethan Ampadu, Karl Darlow, Dan James, and Joe Rodon, plus Nottingham Forest's Neco Williams all potentially facing competitive matches.

Italy's Premier League contingent includes Arsenal's Riccardo Calafiori, Manchester City's Gianluigi Donnarumma, and Newcastle's Sandro Tonali. Why This Matters for FPL Managers

The March international break carries outsized importance in the FPL calendar because it marks the final such interruption before the season's business end. Following this window, there are no more breaks before the campaign concludes, meaning any player who returns injured or fatigued faces a compressed timeline to regain fitness. The World Cup playoff participants, particularly those from Wales with five Leeds players alone, present concentrated risk for managers heavily invested in those squads. However, the extended gap between April 1 and GW32's Friday kickoff provides unusual comfort—managers can afford to wait and assess rather than panic about immediate availability.

What to Monitor

FPL managers should track the outcomes of World Cup playoff matches closely, particularly for players in squads with deep Premier League representation. Friendly results matter less from a fatigue perspective, but any injuries sustained—even in low-stakes matches—could prove costly given the limited time remaining to make transfers. The key is patience: with ten days between the final international fixture and GW32, there's sufficient buffer to make informed decisions based on confirmed team news rather than speculation.
