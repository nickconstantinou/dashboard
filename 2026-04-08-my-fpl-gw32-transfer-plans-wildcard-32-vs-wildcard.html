FPL Gameweek 32 Transfer Planning: Wildcard Now or Wait Until 35?

A Comprehensive Strategy Guide for Fantasy Premier League Managers

---

For Fantasy Premier League managers approaching the business end of the season, the biggest tactical decision has arrived: when to deploy your wildcard. With the season entering its decisive phase and double gameweeks creating massive scheduling complexities, the choice between activating your wildcard in gameweek 32 versus saving it for gameweek 35 could be the difference between finishing in the top 10% or missing out on valuable points. FPL Harry, a popular content creator known for his detailed planning videos, breaks down both strategies in depth, examining how each path affects your team across multiple gameweeks and helping you determine which approach suits your current squad better.

The timing of this decision carries extra weight this season because of how the double gameweeks have fallen. Gameweek 33 presents a blank for most teams, while gameweeks 34, 35, 36, and 37 contain various doubles and blanks that create massive differentiation opportunities. The key question becomes: do you use your wildcard immediately to maximize the bench boost in gameweek 33, or do you save it for gameweek 35 when you can target teams with double fixtures later in the season? Harry walks through both scenarios with specific team examples, player recommendations, and transfer plans that demonstrate the trade-offs involved in each approach.

---

Understanding Your Current Position Before Making the Wildcard Decision

Before diving into the specific strategies, Harry emphasizes the importance of honestly assessing your current squad. He explains that he currently has five free transfers available and just £0.1 million in the bank, meaning he must make at least one transfer this week regardless of his wildcard plans. However, he notes that he feels "pretty happy with how my team looks" at the moment, which is a crucial factor when deciding whether a wildcard is truly necessary.

The core question Harry poses is straightforward: does your gameweek 32 team need a wildcard? Not just in terms of minor improvements, but whether the current squad has fundamental problems that require a complete reset. He identifies several "luxury changes" he could make—such as potentially swapping between Jamespicker and Anderson or upgrading Tarasov—but these are upgrades rather than necessities. The key distinction is whether you look at your team and feel like you "definitely need a wild card" versus simply wanting to make incremental improvements.

Harry also highlights that he has "a lot of players going into gameweek 34," which affects the calculus. If your squad already contains many players with favorable fixtures in the upcoming double gameweeks, the pressure to wildcard diminishes. Conversely, if your current team is poorly positioned for the fixture congestion ahead, wildcarding now makes more sense. He demonstrates this by showing his team on FPL.team, the planning website he uses to visualize future fixture runs and make informed decisions.

---

The Wildcard 32 Strategy: Immediate Action for Fixture Maximization

The first path Harry examines is the traditional approach: wildcard in gameweek 32, bench boost in gameweek 33, and free hit in gameweek 34. He explains that this strategy works best for managers whose current squads are poorly optimized for the immediate fixture run and need significant restructuring to take advantage of the upcoming doubles.

Under this approach, Harry identifies his planned transfer for gameweek 32: replacing Echikitique with Jared Burn. He explains that "Echikitique's minutes either side of the PSG games or in between the PSG games either side I should say is a slight risk," making Burn a safer option. Burn has Wolves at home and Everton in gameweek 34—the only two weeks worth considering if you're planning to wildcard in gameweek 35, making him a strategic acquisition for either path.

For gameweek 33, Harry provides an early look at what a free hit draft might resemble. He's built around a triple up on Leeds, explaining that "their double is slightly better than that of Bournemouth with Leeds at home, Newcastle away, Wolves at home." His draft includes triple Leeds coverage through Darlow Stra and Cal Leuen, with Antoine Stack appearing as a doubt after an FA Cup injury. For the forward line, he's gone with O'Reilly, Seno, and Harland as a triple up for Manchester City, while selecting Cole Palmer, Erling Harland (for the captaincy), and Smeno in midfield, with Calbertt Lewen and a final midfielder spot "quite up in the air" between Pascal Gross and Ryan.

Harry candidly notes that the benefit to wildcarding in gameweek 32 isn't massive: "You're not getting that much benefit. Maybe you have an extra Leeds player. Maybe you can change something if someone was to get injured. But if things look as they do now, you're basically going to be freehitting the same starting 11 that people who wildcarded in 32 have in 33, but just not play the bench boost on top." The real value comes from having more flexibility to adjust your squad to specific matchday conditions and having additional transfers available for later gameweeks.

Moving into gameweek 34, Harry shows how his team would look after the free hit expires. Van Dijk would start over either Anderson or Tarasov (likely dropping to the bench), and he identifies his weakest point as "Chargoway at Manchester United." He considers potential transfers such as downgrading Erling Harland to Gimenez and upgrading Anderson to MoSalah, though he notes "I probably wouldn't want to use two free transfers. I probably would only want to use one of them to take five transfers into gameweek 35."

---

The Wildcard 35 Strategy: Patience as a Competitive Advantage

The second strategic path involves using your free hit in gameweek 33, wildcarding in gameweek 35, and bench boosting in either gameweek 36 or 37. Harry explains that this approach is "definitely better if you don't have lots of transfers available. It allows you to sort of cut the remainder of the season."

Under this strategy, you save your wildcard for when you have maximum information about which teams have double fixtures in the season's final weeks. Gameweek 35 becomes the optimal time to completely restructure your team, allowing you to target specific teams with favorable fixture runs in gameweeks 36 and 37 while carrying your current squad through gameweeks 32 and 34.

Harry demonstrates what a gameweek 35 wildcard draft might look like. He's planning a triple up on Arsenal defense with "Fulham at home, O'Reilly, Semeno, and again it's taken out here, but it will be Erling Harland. We got the money for that." His draft includes Calbertt Lewen, Jar Pedro, Gordon, Semeno, Bruno, and a triple up on Crystal Palace "with their plan double in gameweek 36." This would give him exposure to both the Arsenal double in gameweek 35 and the Crystal Palace double in gameweek 36.

The beauty of the gameweek 35 wildcard is revealed when looking at gameweek 36: "I would have a triple up on Crystal Palace. I'd have a triple up on Manchester City if they double. I've got Ryan, Gabrielle, Bruno, Juice Hall, Anthony Gordon." This flexibility allows you to completely rebuild your squad around the specific doubles that emerge as you get closer to the season's end.

For bench boost positioning, Harry actually prefers gameweek 37 over 36, noting that "I think it looks slightly better." His reasoning involves having "Darlow at home to Brighton" and potentially transferring "Richards to Harry Maguire" as his one transfer in gameweek 37. This approach would give him a stronger bench boost activation in the final week of the season.

The wildcard 35 strategy also provides more flexibility for making aggressive transfers in gameweek 34 if needed. Harry explains: "I have a lot of spare transfers. So I can be more aggressive with things like buying Salorin 34 if I wanted to. If I wanted to make bigger upgrades on the likes of Tarovski, Echitik this week, I have lots of spare transfers further down the line if I'm going to wildcard 35."

Specific Transfer Options for Gameweek 32

Regardless of which wildcard strategy you choose, Harry outlines the specific transfers he's considering for gameweek 32 given his current constraints.

The primary transfer he's planning is Echikitique to Jared Burn, as discussed earlier. He also mentions that if "Echikitique is not looking like he's going to start at home to Fulham," he could alternatively transfer him to Bowen. However, if Echikitique is starting, "I actually think he's equally good to Bowen. And then he has Palace at home in the blank in gameweek 34."

Harry references the fixture difficulty rankings that show West Ham at the top, making "Jared Burn would be a good potential buy." He also mentions several other transfer possibilities:

  • Richards to Muki: Muki has "Spurs at home and Forest at home in the two fixtures that I'd be looking to buy him for"
  • Burmo to Kuna: Given uncertainty about "Brian and Burmo's minutes" at Manchester United, "Mattz Kuna is probably slightly safer"

    • Despite considering these options, Harry concludes: "I'll probably still only make one, maybe two transfers in gameweek 32 and continue to carry the rest of them as far forward as possible." This conservative approach allows him to preserve transfers for more impactful moves later in the season, particularly if he goes with the wildcard 35 strategy.

    Key Strategic Takeaways for FPL Managers

    The wildcard 32 strategy makes sense when your current squad is fundamentally misaligned with the immediate fixture run, particularly if you have players lacking returns in gameweeks 32 and 34. The bench boost in gameweek 33 provides value if you have strong bench options, but as Harry notes, you're largely free-hitting the same squad that wildcard managers will have anyway.

    The wildcard 35 strategy becomes preferable when you have more transferable assets and want maximum flexibility to target the final doubles. This approach requires patience and tolerance for potentially suboptimal teams in the short term, but it pays off when you can completely restructure around specific doubles in gameweeks 36 and 37.

    Your current squad strength matters more than you might think. Harry emphasizes that he doesn't feel he "definitely needs a wild card," which is a crucial self-assessment. If your team already has good coverage of favorable fixtures, wildcarding becomes less urgent.

    Preserving transfers can be more valuable than using them immediately. Harry's approach of carrying five transfers forward gives him options to make bigger moves later, whether that's upgrading to MoSalah, bringing in new differentials, or responding to injuries and form changes.

    The bench boost timing is flexible and can be adjusted even after your initial planning. While Harry initially considered bench boost in gameweek 36, his analysis led him to prefer gameweek 37, showing that these decisions should evolve as more information becomes available.
