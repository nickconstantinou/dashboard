Origin of World of Warcraft: The early days of WoW development | Jeff Kaplan and Lex Fridman

In the pantheon of video game history, World of Warcraft stands as one of the most influential and transformative titles ever created. But the story of its inception is far from the tale of seasoned veterans executing a carefully planned vision. Instead, it emerges from something far more chaotic, passionate, and surprisingly consequential: a ragtag group of developers who, by their own admission, had no idea what they were doing. In a revealing conversation with Lex Fridman, former WoW lead developer Jeff Kaplan offered a candid look back at the early days of development, sharing insights that challenge conventional wisdom about what makes a successful game—and a successful team.

The conversation takes place years after WoW's meteoric rise, with Kaplan having later led development on Overwatch, but the reflections on World of Warcraft's origins carry particular weight precisely because of what followed. Kaplan and his colleagues went on to enormous success, yet their first major project began with inexperience, self-doubt, and an almost reckless confidence in the face of seemingly insurmountable competition. Understanding how this unlikely team succeeded reveals something profound about innovation, team dynamics, and the nature of creative work—lessons that extend far beyond the gaming industry.

The "Bad News Bears" of Game Development

Kaplan describes the original WoW development team with remarkable self-deprecation, characterizing them as "the Bad News Bears" of game development. This baseball movie reference captures the essence of a team that was neither cohesive nor conventionally qualified, yet somehow managed to achieve the extraordinary. The team represented an unusual mix of Blizzard veterans and complete newcomers, a combination that could have easily resulted in disaster but instead produced something unexpected.

Leading the team was Mark Kern, whom Kaplan credits with running the show during those formative years. Alongside Kern stood Shane Deiri, whom Kaplan calls a "legendary Blizzard developer," and Bill Petus, who served as art director. These veterans commanded respect from the younger developers and provided a stabilizing presence. However, as Kaplan acknowledges, the team also included many people who had never worked on an MMO before—and in some cases, had never worked in game development at all.

Perhaps most significantly, the team drew heavily from outside the traditional MMO development pipeline. The developers tasked with building the game's dungeons were hired directly from the Quake community, bringing with them expertise in first-person shooter level design but zero experience with massively multiplayer online role-playing games. Kaplan himself fit this mold, admitting he was "just some idiot who played a lot of EverQuest" before finding himself unexpectedly on the development side of a competing product. This absence of hardcore MMO expertise on the staff proved to be both a liability and, as the story would ultimately reveal, an unexpected advantage.

Building Worlds with the Wrong Tools

The technical origins of World of Warcraft reflect the improvisational nature of the entire project. Because the team lacked experience with MMO development tools, they initially built dungeons using QE Radiant, the level editor that had been designed for Quake. Kaplan describes how the developers essentially created "Quake dungeons" that functioned as Quake levels, simply repurposing tools and workflows they already knew. Only later did the team transition to using a proprietary engine designed specifically for their needs.

This makeshift approach extended to every aspect of early development. The team operated without the benefit of established MMO design patterns, without industry-standard tools, and without the confidence that comes from having successfully completed similar projects. Yet this very lack of convention forced a kind of creative freedom that more experienced developers might never have allowed themselves. The team wasn't constrained by "how things are done" because they didn't know how things were done—and consequently, they were free to imagine possibilities that established developers might have immediately dismissed as impractical.

The Crucial Advantage of Not Knowing

Perhaps the most significant insight Kaplan offers is his assertion that "a huge part of WoW's success... was that we didn't know what we were doing." This statement, made with the benefit of hindsight, represents a profound challenge to the conventional belief that success comes from expertise, careful planning, and methodological rigor. According to Kaplan, the team's very ignorance became their greatest asset.

He illustrates this point by contrasting the WoW development experience with that of Titan, Blizzard's later attempt to create an MMO after World of Warcraft had already achieved massive success. Titan assembled what Kaplan describes as "the best of the best"—veteran MMO developers with decades of combined experience and proven track records. Despite having every possible advantage, Titan failed "horribly," in Kaplan's words. The difference, he suggests, lies precisely in the relationship between expertise and creative possibility.

When you know all the reasons something cannot be done, you become constrained by that knowledge. The WoW team approached their project with what Kaplan describes as a "sure-of-itself" attitude typical of a company that had successfully shipped StarCraft, Diablo, and Warcraft. Combined with the audacity of developers who thought they could compete with Sony Online Entertainment—the company behind EverQuest—they possessed something more valuable than experience: the confidence to try things that "experts" would have immediately rejected as impossible.

Competing Against Giants

The competitive landscape when World of Warcraft was being developed made the team's ambitions seem almost absurd. EverQuest had established the MMO genre as a commercial and cultural phenomenon, and its sequel was already in development. Kaplan recalls that at the time, EverQuest 2 had been announced and EverQuest fans—including himself and his colleagues—were "drooling" for its release. The expectation was that EQ2 would simply inherit the throne and build upon an already proven formula.

To make matters more daunting, Star Wars Galaxies entered the race with what Kaplan describes as a "brilliant designer" named Raph Coer, who had come from Ultima Online and possessed a reputation for innovative game design. Coer's lectures at the Game Developers Conference were legendary, and his involvement with Star Wars Galaxies represented exactly the kind of pedigree that inspired both respect and fear in the WoW team. When Kaplan and his colleagues looked at the competition, they saw nothing but overwhelming force: experienced developers, proven franchises, massive budgets, and established player bases.

Yet the team pushed forward anyway. Kaplan describes the attitude as essentially saying, "Yeah, we can compete with Sony Online." This wasn't blind arrogance so much as a refusal to be intimidated by conventional wisdom about what was possible. The team recognized that their competitors were making specific choices based on specific assumptions about what players wanted—and they believed there was room for a different approach, even if they couldn't articulate exactly what that approach would look like.

The Fuel of Passionate Work

Beyond the strategic and technical dimensions of the project, Kaplan emphasizes the extraordinary dedication that drove the team during WoW's development. Describing the work culture, he speaks of "crazy work hours" but immediately clarifies that this wasn't the result of corporate pressure or mandatory crunch. Instead, developers worked long hours "because your heart was in it" and because they felt like they were working on something that mattered.

This distinction between forced labor and passionate commitment appears repeatedly in Kaplan's reflections. The team wasn't grinding through a job; they were pursuing a vision that excited them personally and professionally. The long hours weren't a cost to be minimized but rather an expression of genuine engagement with the work. When you care deeply about what you're creating, the boundaries between work and play begin to blur, and what might otherwise feel like sacrifice becomes simply what you do because you can't imagine doing anything else.

This passion proved essential to the project's success. World of Warcraft didn't ship on time because of efficient project management or realistic scheduling—it shipped because a group of people cared too much about the project to let it fail. The emotional investment provided a kind of fuel that no amount of process or methodology could replicate.

The Paradox of Expertise and Innovation

The deeper lesson that emerges from Kaplan's account concerns the relationship between knowledge and innovation. Expertise provides valuable tools: domain knowledge, technical skills, industry connections, and the ability to avoid common mistakes. Yet expertise also carries the weight of accumulated assumptions about what is possible, what works, and what players want. These assumptions can become invisible constraints, shaping decisions in ways that experts never consciously recognize.

The WoW team lacked the burden of these assumptions. They didn't know that certain features were impossible or that certain design choices would never work. More importantly, they didn't know which sacred cows could be challenged without destroying the entire enterprise. This ignorance created space for the kind of radical rethinking that defines truly innovative products. World of Warcraft succeeded not by copying EverQuest more effectively than EverQuest 2 or Star Wars Galaxies could, but by approaching the genre with fresh eyes that saw opportunities others had overlooked.

This observation carries implications far beyond game development. Any organization seeking to disrupt an established industry faces the same fundamental tension: the people with the most experience are also the people most constrained by their experience. Innovation often requires deliberately incorporating perspectives from outside the established order—people who don't know what can't be done and therefore attempt it anyway.

The Unlikely Team as Ideal Model

Kaplan's description of the original WoW team as the "Bad News Bears" initially seems like self-deprecation, but it actually points toward a broader truth about effective innovation teams. The ideal team for groundbreaking work may not be the team of recognized experts in the relevant field, but rather a team that combines deep expertise with genuineoutsiders who bring fresh perspectives. The veterans provided credibility, stability, and enough knowledge to avoid catastrophic errors. The newcomers provided the creative freedom to imagine possibilities that veterans might have dismissed.

This balance is difficult to achieve in practice. Organizations naturally gravitate toward hiring people with relevant experience, believing that credentials predict performance. Yet credentialed expertise can become an echo chamber, with everyone sharing the same assumptions and approaching problems in the same ways. The breakthrough that transforms an industry often comes from someone who doesn't fully understand the rules—someone who sees the established practices as arbitrary rather than inevitable and therefore feels empowered to question them.

The Heart Behind the Innovation

Beyond the strategic and organizational insights, Kaplan's account reveals something about the role of emotional investment in creative work. The WoW team succeeded not merely because they had good ideas, but because they cared intensely about what they were creating. This caring manifested in countless small decisions that added up to a product players could feel the difference in. When you're building something you genuinely love, you notice details that purely professional developers might overlook. You push through obstacles that purely mercenary motivations couldn't sustain. You make sacrifices that only passion can justify.

This observation points toward a fundamental truth about exceptional work: it rarely emerges from purely transactional relationships between workers and their projects. Extraordinary achievements require people who have staked something personal on the outcome, who care whether the thing they create succeeds or fails for reasons that go beyond their paycheck. The WoW developers weren't trying to ship a competent product that met minimum specifications; they were trying to create something they could be proud of, something that would matter in the world.

What This Story Teaches Us

Jeff Kaplan's reflections on World of Warcraft's early days offer several important lessons for anyone engaged in creative or innovative work. First, the project demonstrates that ignorance can be an asset when pursuing genuinely novel directions. Knowing too much about what has been tried before can blind you to possibilities that exist outside your existing framework. Second, the story illustrates the importance of combining veteran experience with fresh perspectives rather than relying exclusively on either.

Third, and perhaps most importantly, the account reveals that exceptional work emerges from passionate commitment rather than mere professional competence. The WoW team didn't succeed because they were the most qualified developers or because they had the best resources. They succeeded because they cared more than the competition, because they were willing to work not just harder but more personally invested in the outcome.

These insights apply whether you're building video games, launching startups, or pursuing any form of creative work. The path to something genuinely new rarely looks like the path of maximum expertise. Sometimes, the most valuable thing you can bring to a challenge is the willingness to try what experts would dismiss as impossible—and the passion to sustain that effort through the inevitable difficulties.

Who Should Learn From This Story

This conversation between Jeff Kaplan and Lex Fridman holds particular value for several distinct audiences. Game developers and designers will find Kaplan's behind-the-scenes account of WoW's development fascinating, but more importantly, they will find insights applicable to their own creative challenges. The lessons about team composition, the role of expertise versus fresh perspective, and the importance of passionate commitment extend far beyond any single project or genre.

Entrepreneurs and innovators will recognize in Kaplan's story a pattern common to many successful startups: a team of unlikely people pursuing an improbable vision with more passion than resources. The story challenges the assumption that success requires accumulating the right credentials and building the right team according to conventional wisdom. Sometimes, the best team is the one that doesn't know it's supposed to fail.

Finally, anyone interested in understanding how cultural phenomena emerge will find value in this account of one of the most significant entertainment products of the past two decades. World of Warcraft didn't just succeed commercially—it transformed an entire genre and introduced millions of people to online gaming communities. Understanding how such transformations begin helps us recognize and support similar opportunities in other domains.
