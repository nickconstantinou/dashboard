The most influential video games of the 20th century | Jeff Kaplan and Lex Fridman

The Evolution of an Industry: Jeff Kaplan on the Games That Shaped a Generation

In the rapidly evolving landscape of video games, few individuals possess the unique perspective of Jeff Kaplan. Having transitioned from an obsessed teenage gamer in Southern California to one of the most celebrated game designers in the industry—leading the teams behind World of Warcraft and Overwatch—Kaplan offers a rare window into the formative years of interactive entertainment. In this wide-ranging conversation with Lex Fridman, Kaplan traces his personal journey through the golden age of gaming, identifying the specific titles and moments that not only captured his imagination but fundamentally shaped the medium's trajectory. The discussion serves as both a nostalgic trip through gaming history and a thoughtful meditation on how early experiences influence creative vision.

What makes this conversation particularly compelling is Kaplan's dual lens: he experienced these games as an enthusiastic consumer before becoming a craftsman who would ultimately serve millions of players himself. This perspective allows him to articulate not just what made certain games memorable, but why they mattered culturally and developmentally. From primitive arcade cabinets to complex online worlds, Kaplan demonstrates how each era built upon the last, creating the foundation for modern multiplayer experiences. His insights reveal that the games we play don't merely entertain—they shape our understanding of community, achievement, and virtual spaces.

The Arcade Era: Where It All Began

Kaplan's gaming origins trace back to the unmistakable magic of the 1970s and 1980s arcade scene, a period he describes as a "golden era of coin-op." Born in 1972, he grew up in Southern California immersed in the skateboard and BMX culture of the era, but the local arcades proved equally irresistible. He recalls with vivid clarity his first encounter with Pac-Man at a pizza parlor, where his uncle Ronnie would feed him quarters while playing himself—perhaps, Kaplan suggests with amusement, because his uncle was too intimidated to compete directly with the machine. This early exposure to cabinet games planted a seed that would grow into a lifelong passion.

The arcade experience extended beyond individual games to an entire social ecosystem. Kaplan remembers discovering an Asteroids machine in the lobby of his brother's graduation ceremony in Philadelphia, where he and his brother would desperately attempt to achieve the high score before being summoned to bed. The following morning, they would inevitably discover that someone had surpassed their achievement—a humbling introduction to the competitive nature of gaming communities. These formative experiences established gaming as a social pursuit, one defined by shared spaces, rivalry, and the relentless pursuit of mastery.

The Home Console Revolution and Zork's Textual Magic

The transition from arcades to home gaming marked a pivotal shift in Kaplan's relationship with video games. When friends began acquiring Pong systems, the experience of playing games directly on a television felt revolutionary—a democratization of technology that previously required a trip to the arcade. However, Kaplan's family owned an IBM PC before MS-DOS existed, a machine he admits was "better for gaming" than alternatives like the Commodore or Amiga available to his friends. His mother, recognizing her sons' passion, purchased them copies of Zork, the influential text-based adventure game from Infocom.

Zork holds a particularly sacred place in Kaplan's heart, and his description of the game reveals why it influenced so many future game designers. Players typed commands like "go west" or "open mailbox," with the game responding through text that required players to fill in the gaps with their imagination. Kaplan describes this as "the power of imagination"—the same principle that makes books superior to their film adaptations. Where modern games render every detail explicitly, Zork invited players to co-create the world in their minds, fostering a kind of creative engagement that purely visual media cannot replicate. Kaplan notes that when Infocom later released graphically enhanced versions of Zork in the CD-ROM era, something essential was lost—the version players had imagined in their heads "didn't exist anymore."

Ultima and the Birth of World-Building

The PC gaming revolution of the 1980s introduced Kaplan to Richard Garriott's Ultima series, particularly Ultima 2, which arrived just as Kaplan was old enough to engage with its complexity. Garriott, known by his in-game persona "Lord British," embedded himself directly into his creations—a decision that seemed eccentric at the time but established enduring conventions for developer presence in gaming communities. The Ultima games presented unprecedented worlds: rich, detailed environments where players could board rocket ships in fantasy settings, encounter sophisticated NPC behaviors from town bouncers to merchants, and make meaningful moral choices—including attempting to kill Lord British himself, an achievement so difficult it became legendary.

Kaplan recalls spending endless hours attempting to break the game's systems, to push against the boundaries designers had created. This desire to explore, subvert, and transcend intended game mechanics would later inform his own design philosophy at Blizzard Entertainment. The Ultima series demonstrated that games could be more than challenges to overcome—they could be spaces to inhabit, cultures to engage with, and identities to assume. These lessons proved foundational when Kaplan himself would later help create some of the most immersive online worlds in gaming history.

The Multiplayer Awakening: Quake and the Human Element

The conversation takes a significant turn when Kaplan discusses the emergence of networked multiplayer gaming, which he identifies as the experience that "defined online gaming" for him. While Doom had introduced the possibility of playing with others—initially through LAN parties or modem connections—Quake refined the technology and created the infrastructure for a genuine online gaming community. The differences between players became immediately apparent: those with low ping times (the "LPBs," or low ping bastards) dominated, while players like Kaplan connecting via dial-up with 300-ping connections struggled to compete. Yet the mere existence of other human players on the other end of the connection felt magical regardless of performance.

The Quake community that emerged was sophisticated and organized, spawning websites, mods, and a distinct gaming culture. Kaplan specifically remembers Blues News, a gaming news site he continues to read to this day—though he jokes that linking to its content recently prompted friends to ask what "time machine" he had stepped out of. It was through this community that Kaplan first learned about EverQuest, after an id Software programmer named Brian Hook posted about leaving the legendary studio to work at Verant Interactive (later Sony Online Entertainment) on a new game called EverQuest. The post intrigued Kaplan enough to investigate, and that single announcement would ultimately reshape his career path.

The MMO Frontier: EverQuest's Lasting Legacy

Though Kaplan would eventually become synonymous with World of Warcraft, he traces his entrance into massively multiplayer online gaming directly to EverQuest. He admits with characteristic self-deprecation that he missed Ultima Online—the earlier pioneer in the MMO space—due to being in graduate school at the time. The stories he heard about Ultima Online, however, revealed a chaotic and often brutal virtual world: players who discovered how to poison apples and leave them for strangers' horses to eat, thereby killing the animals and stealing their riders' belongings. Ultima Online, in essence, represented an early experiment in what Kaplan diplomatically calls "grief-based" gameplay—testing how humans would behave when freed from real-world consequences.

EverQuest offered a different experience: a persistent world where thousands of players could simultaneously explore, quest, and interact. For Kaplan, this represented the culmination of everything that had come before—the textual adventures of Zork, the world-building of Ultima, the multiplayer connections of Quake—all combined into a single persistent universe. The game launched in 1999 and became one of the most influential titles in gaming history, establishing many of the conventions that would define the MMO genre. It also provided Kaplan with his entry point into game development, as he transitioned from player to creator, eventually joining Blizzard Entertainment and rising to lead the teams behind some of the most successful online games ever made.

The Designer's Eye: What Makes Games Endure

Throughout the conversation, Kaplan offers insight into what separates memorable games from forgettable ones—a perspective that proves invaluable coming from someone who has spent decades on both sides of the development equation. He emphasizes that the most influential games create spaces where players feel transported, where they can "lose themselves" and "step into this world." Whether through the imaginative engagement required by text adventures or the visual immersion of modern 3D environments, the best games facilitate a kind of alternate existence.

Kaplan also notes the importance of community in gaming's evolution. From the high-score competitions of the arcade era to the sophisticated online communities surrounding Quake and EverQuest, games have always been social technologies—systems for bringing people together, whether cooperatively or competitively. His own career would be built on understanding these social dynamics, creating spaces where millions of players could form communities, share experiences, and find belonging. The games that influenced him most were those that understood this social dimension, that treated players not as isolated consumers but as participants in shared worlds.

Key Takeaways

The power of imagination in game design cannot be overstated. Kaplan's reverence for Zork reveals a crucial insight: the most impactful games often provide frameworks for player imagination rather than complete sensory experiences. Text adventures forced players to mentally construct worlds, creating personal investment that graphically superior games often fail to achieve. For modern developers, this suggests that over-specification can diminish player engagement—leaving room for interpretation creates ownership and emotional connection that photorealistic rendering cannot replace.

Early arcade experiences established enduring gaming psychology. The arcade era taught players that games were spaces of competition, mastery, and social interaction—lessons that would define decades of game design. The high-score culture, the communal nature of arcades, and the challenge of limited resources (your quarters) created a mindset that persisted as gaming moved to the home. Understanding this psychological foundation helps explain player motivations even in modern free-to-play environments.

Community and networking transformed gaming from hobby to culture. The shift from single-player experiences to networked multiplayer represented a fundamental change in what games could be. Kaplan's descriptions of discovering other human players in Quake, of reading Blues News daily, of following developer posts about upcoming projects—these reveal games as social infrastructure rather than mere entertainment. The most successful modern games understand this social dimension intuitively, building community features as core rather than supplementary elements.

Influence flows from player to creator, and the best designers remain students of their craft. Kaplan's journey from obsessed teenager consuming games to industry leader creating them illustrates a common pattern in creative fields. His ability to articulate what made earlier games meaningful—his continued enthusiasm for titles like Zork, his specific memories of discovering secrets in Super Mario—demonstrates that great designers maintain their player sensibilities even as they gain technical and managerial capabilities.

The boundary between intended experience and player exploration defines game longevity. The Ultima series captivated Kaplan precisely because it allowed him to attempt things the designers never anticipated—killing Lord British, robbing merchants, breaking systems. Games that anticipate and enable player creativity, that reward experimentation and system mastery, create the kind of deep engagement that sustains communities for years. This insight directly informs the design philosophy Kaplan would later bring to World of Warcraft and Overwatch.

Who This Is For

This conversation holds particular value for several distinct audiences. Game designers and developers will find Kaplan's retrospective analysis invaluable—heartening proof that understanding what makes games meaningful as a player translates directly into capability as a creator. His specific examples of what engaged him as a teenager provide a template for thinking about player psychology and engagement that remains relevant decades later.

Gaming historians and enthusiasts will appreciate the firsthand account of a transformative era in interactive entertainment. Kaplan's memories of Southern California arcades, early home computing, and the emergence of online gaming offer primary source material for understanding how the industry developed. His specific references to games, developers, websites, and communities create a detailed map of gaming culture in the 1980s and 1990s.

Finally, anyone interested in creative industries and the psychology of play will find this discussion illuminating. Kaplan demonstrates how early experiences shape creative vision, how communities foster innovation, and how the distinction between consumption and creation is often blurrier than it appears. Whether you professionally create games or simply love playing them, understanding the roots of modern interactive entertainment enriches your appreciation of the medium's possibilities.
