    The Work of an Architectural Historian

    Cooper began by explaining the two primary facets of his profession: the educational side involving teaching and research, and the applied side he inhabits, working for a cultural resource management firm. His company employs approximately ten architectural historians alongside a larger team of archaeologists, and their work is primarily driven by government mandates. Specifically, whenever federal money funds a project in the United States, a cultural resource survey becomes legally required. This ensures the project doesn't negatively affect cultural heritage—whether that heritage is architectural, historic, or archaeological.

    The practical work involves what Cooper described as "above ground" survey work. When a project is proposed—say, a roundabout near a historic house—Cooper's job is to determine whether the construction will impact that structure and, if so, what mitigations might be possible. Can they move the project slightly? Can they move the historic house? The goal is preservation without paralysis, finding workable solutions that satisfy both development needs and heritage protection.

    This professional context immediately generated comedic tension. Conan observed that developers must hate Cooper's work, to which Cooper confirmed they face regular opposition. "It's mostly homeowners," Cooper clarified, explaining that driving around in an orange vest taking photos of houses naturally invites strange looks. The orange vest, Conan noted, isn't mandated by the job—it's optional branding—which Cooper acknowledged. "I have concerns about this sorority and its historical value," Conan joked, noting the structure was built in 1993. The exchange mined humor from the genuine awkwardness of a profession that requires photographing properties without necessarily receiving enthusiastic welcomes.

    Brutalism and the Appreciation of the Unloved

    When Conan asked about architectural preferences, Cooper pivoted to what he described as "historically underappreciated styles"—brutalism, postmodernism, and late modernist work from the 1970s and 1980s. "I'm not talking about the gorgeous Victorian house surrounded by stately elms," Cooper explained. His enthusiasm clearly lies with buildings most people dismiss.

    Conan pressed on brutalism, and Cooper described the style's characteristic elements: giant blocks, poured concrete, massive windows, and hard shiny surfaces. Conan joked about baby-proofing such homes, noting the "danger" involved. Cooper confirmed the aesthetic, referencing Boston City Hall as a building he finds "really cool," a statement Conan greeted with vocal skepticism.

    The exchange revealed Cooper's genuine attachment to the structure while also acknowledging the complexity of his appreciation. When Conan asked whether Cooper's fondness for the building stems from changed cultural views or simply contrarianism—"you're a contrarian, right?"—Cooper confirmed, "Correct." This moment of self-awareness illustrated that Cooper understands his preferences exist outside mainstream taste and has made peace with that positioning.

    The conversation became more animated when Cooper revealed he actually visited Boston City Hall the previous year, attending a conference on modern architecture there. Conan immediately assumed criminal implications, asking if Cooper was arrested. "It was a conference, thank you," Cooper replied, before acknowledging that everyone at the conference was indeed arrested. "They called me in for some reason to give testimony or something about beatings and who deserves them and who doesn't," Cooper added, with a straight face that made clear he was playing along with Conan's bit. The exchange culminated in Conan showing a photograph of Boston City Hall, which Cooper noted has "been voted like the ugliest building in the country multiple times."

    The defense of Boston City Hall connected to Cooper's larger point about appreciation arriving through non-mainstream channels. He and his wife Abby were joined at that Boston conference by fellow enthusiasts, suggesting his appreciation, while contrarian in nature, isn't entirely solitary. The building represents a style that rejects conventional beauty standards in favor of bold, uncompromising design—and Cooper clearly finds that intellectual honesty compelling.

    The Building Assignment Game

    The episode's most substantial segment involved a game where Cooper assigned architectural styles to Conan, Sona, and David Hopping. Cooper had clearly prepared for this exercise, responding to Conan's question about their potential architectural identities with evident enthusiasm.

    For Conan, Cooper selected postmodernism, specifically referencing the Team Disney building in Los Angeles—the structure suspended by cartoonish dwarves that supports the roof. The choice emphasized postmodernism's defining characteristics: bright colors, oversized scale, deliberate iconography, and a self-aware theatricality. "Very bright. Very larger than life. And very kind of in-your-face purposefully," Cooper explained, noting the building both demands attention and accepts what it gets. The description captured the way postmodern architecture rejects the cool austerity of modernism in favor of narrative, color, and cultural reference.

    Conan reacted with indignation, repeatedly insisting viewers look up the Team Disney building. "You're going to notice it," Cooper acknowledged, turning the apparent insult into a form of flattery—the building, like Conan, commands attention simply by existing. The exchange revealed Conan's investment in how he's perceived, his desire for significance even when packaged as self-deprecation.

    For Sona, Cooper selected the Gamble House in Pasadena, the quintessential example of Arts and Crafts style architecture. The house also serves as Doc Brown's residence in Back to the Future, a connection Cooper clearly found meaningful. Sona's section generated the episode's most elaborate comedic tangent, with Conan imagining museum-based hide-and-seek scenarios: "You wait until closing time. Conan escalated the bit further, claiming he'd spent "six days at the Dwight Eisenhower Museum" wearing the former president's pajamas.     For David Hopping, Cooper selected aPrairie-style foursquare, describing it as "very simple, understated, but still just like a nice place to be. Something you'd pass a thousand times and never think twice." The choice reflected David being someone whose significance might not announce itself loudly but provides consistent, comfortable presence. The contrast between David's understated selection and Conan's attention-commanding postmodernism nicely illustrated different approaches to making one's mark.

    Conan's Quest for Historic Recognition

    The episode's final substantial segment found Conan returning repeatedly to an obsession: getting a National Historic Registry plaque placed on buildings associated with his life while he remains alive. Cooper confirmed that part of his job involves writing nominations for the National Register of Historic Places, the country's official registry of buildings deemed worthy of recognition.

    Conan immediately asked whether he'd need to be dead for a building to receive historic status based on him, and Cooper confirmed this is typically the case. "I think there are often times where they're done when people are living," Cooper acknowledged, "so I'd have to be dead in order for a building to be given historic status based on you." This prompted Conan to invoke London's blue plaques, which honor living figures like Mick Jagger at their former residences. "You could be the case study for the first one in the United States," Cooper offered, generating evident excitement from Conan.

    The conversation became increasingly elaborate as Conan generated lists of buildings he wanted recognized. His childhood home in Brookline, Massachusetts. Various elementary schools: Baldwin School, Michael Driscoll School, Brookline High. His Larchmont Studios location in Los Angeles where he worked early in his career. "I moved around a lot when I was a young man," Conan explained. He wanted plaques everywhere, eventually specifying they should be "cheaply made... rain resistant initially but then" noting he didn't want to spend too much money on what would evidently be a large quantity.

    Cooper offered a crucial clarification: plaques can be independently created without official involvement, as the plaque itself isn't the official part of historic registration. Conan latched onto this, assigning David to "make one and go tape it up," with David responding "one, no" before Conan corrected him to "all kinds of plaques." The exchange captured Conan's approach to celebrity: taking the mickey out of himself while genuinely enjoying the fantasy of commemorating his own significance.

    Cooper mentioned his wife Abby actually works for the Ohio State Historic Preservation Office as one of the National Register of Historic Places reviewers, making her uniquely positioned to advise on such matters. Conan immediately tried to leverage this connection, and when Cooper noted the registry operates nationally, not just in Ohio, the conversation suggested Cooper would take the question to Abby. The segment demonstrated both Conan's playful approach to his own legacy and Cooper's willingness to engage with the absurdity while maintaining professional knowledge.

    Detailed Takeaways

    The relationship between government regulation and preservation reveals how private interests and public heritage protection interact in practice. Cultural resource surveys, triggered by federal funding requirements, create documented pathways for architectural historians to influence development decisions. This bureaucratic mechanism means preservation isn't simply a matter of aesthetic preference but operates through enforceable legal frameworks. Cooper's job exists because someone decided federal money should come with heritage protection conditions—a policy choice that creates professional opportunities for people like him while limiting what developers can do unilaterally.

    Contrarian taste often carries more credibility when paired with genuine expertise. Cooper openly acknowledged his appreciation of Boston City Hall involves an element of contrarianism, yet the admission didn't undermine his position—it humanized it. His expertise remained visible in the specificity of his knowledge (architectural movements, specific buildings, the reasons behind stylistic developments), while the self-aware acknowledgment of his contrarian tendencies suggested intellectual honesty. This combination allowed him to defend unpopular preferences while maintaining listener respect.

    Architectural style assignment functions as a form of personality assessment that reveals as much about the assessor as the assessed. Cooper's choices—postmodernism for Conan, Arts and Crafts for Sona, Prairie style for David—demonstrated how architectural knowledge becomes a lens for reading people. His selections reflected not just his assessment of his colleagues but his own values: the playful theatricality of postmodernism, the craftsmanship appreciation of Arts and Crafts, the grounded comfort of Prairie style. Understanding someone's architectural preferences reveals their aesthetic priorities and, by extension, their broader worldview.

    The line between official recognition and self-commemoration is thinner than most people assume. Conan's enthusiasm for creating cheap plaques and taping them to significant locations captures a truth about heritage: formal recognition often follows personal significance rather than preceding it. While the National Register operates through official channels, the impulse to mark meaningful places as significant belongs to everyone. Plaques, monuments, and commemorations begin as personal or communal gestures before becoming institutional ones. Understanding this encourages people to take preservation into their own hands rather than waiting for official recognition.
