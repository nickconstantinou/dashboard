    Will AI Populism Decide the 2028 Election?

    Introduction

    In the high-stakes world of American electoral politics, few forces shape outcomes more powerfully than the ability to make voters feel that their anxieties are understood and their opponents are to blame. In 2024, immigration and inflation dominated the narrative. By 2028, a new villain may be emerging from the data centers and corporate headquarters of the artificial intelligence industry itself. That, at least, is the bet being placed by a growing coalition of politicians, regulators, and activists who Jasmine Sun, a contributing writer at The Atlantic, has coined "AI populists."

    Sun, who also authors the Substack series *AI Populism* at jasmine.news and recently published a New York Times opinion piece on AI and the "permanent underclass," sat down with the Bankless podcast to explore whether this nascent political movement has the momentum to reshape American elections. Her thesis is both straightforward and unsettling: AI is no longer simply a technology story or an economics story. It is rapidly becoming a political story—and one with unusual power to unite groups that rarely agree on anything.

    The conversation arrives at a pivotal moment. Polling data shows AI rising faster than any other issue in voter salience, even as it remains far from the top of most Americans' priority lists. Meanwhile, the technology's own creators are making apocalyptic predictions about job displacement, and communities across the country are organizing against the data centers being built in their backyards. The question is not whether AI will become politically salient, Sun argues, but whether the opportunistic coalition forming around AI backlash will coalesce into something that actually decides elections.

    Defining AI Populism

    The framework Sun introduces at the outset of the conversation is essential to understanding everything that follows. "AI populism," she explains, is "a worldview in which AI is viewed not only as a normal technology, but as an elite political project to be resisted." This is a crucial distinction. Populism, in the populist tradition, is fundamentally about identifying a villain—usually an elite group that is perceived as enriching itself at the expense of ordinary people. What Sun has observed is that AI is increasingly being cast in that villain role, not merely as a neutral technological force but as a political project driven by coastal elites, venture capitalists, and tech executives who are promising to transform society in ways that working-class Americans never asked for and may not survive.

    This framing matters because it changes what kind of political coalition can form around AI. It is not just programmers, regulators, or tech critics who are mobilizing. Sun describes a striking phenomenon she has observed in Washington: "family first conservatives sitting with antitrust people, sitting with environmentalists, people who would never be working side by side, but who have united in order to push for AI regulation." These are groups that disagree on almost everything else—the role of government in the economy, immigration, social issues—but have found in AI a common enemy. That kind of cross-ideological coalition is unusual and politically potent.

    The Polling Paradox: Rising Fast, Still Low

    One of the most counterintuitive aspects of AI populism that Sun explores is the gap between the issue's current polling rank and its trajectory. According to the Blue Rose Research polling that Sun cites, AI ranks 29th out of 40 issues that American voters might care about. This is not the stuff of single-issue voting blocs or defining campaign promises. The top five issues—cost of living, the economy, corruption, inflation, and healthcare—have likely dominated voter priorities for decades and show no signs of losing their grip.

    Yet Sun urges caution against reading this as evidence that AI populism is merely a fringe phenomenon. The key insight is that AI is rising faster than any other issue in the survey. The twenty-ninth place ranking, she suggests, is a lagging indicator. Americans have not yet fully processed what AI means for their lives, but the processing is happening—and happening quickly. When Sun points out that "AI is not just a separate issue" and that "people who are thinking about AI" are really seeing it as "part of these broader conversations around affordability, economic mobility, geopolitics," she is identifying the mechanism by which a mid-tier issue can vault to the top of the political agenda. If AI becomes the explanation for why jobs disappear, why neighborhoods change, why the economy feels rigged, then it stops being a discrete technology question and becomes the lens through which voters interpret everything they already care about.

    Why Bernie Sanders and Other Politicians Are Betting on AI

    The interview's most provocative suggestion is that politicians like Bernie Sanders are "doubling down" on AI populist messaging and "tripling down" on making it a cornerstone of their political identity. The question is why, if AI is only the twenty-ninth most important issue, would established politicians stake so much on it?

    Sun offers two interlocking explanations. The first is that AI is genuinely entangled with the issues that voters care about most. She notes that roughly 30 to 40 percent of US GDP growth in 2025 came from data center and AI-related investments, making AI not a peripheral tech story but a central driver of the broader economy that voters already distrust. When tech CEOs promise that AI will take jobs, when investors pour unprecedented sums into AI infrastructure, and when ordinary people see data centers sprouting up in their communities, the connection between AI and economic anxiety becomes impossible to ignore. If voters believe that AI is driving inflation, killing jobs, and enriching a new class of tech oligarchs, then attacking AI is not a distraction from the bread-and-butter issues—it *is* the bread-and-butter issues, reframed.

    The second explanation Sun identifies is political opportunism. Politicians who have long advocated for universal healthcare, higher minimum wages, and wealth redistribution are now discovering that AI provides a fresh and compelling justification for policies they already wanted. The logic is simple: if AI is the new threat, then stronger labor protections, greater corporate accountability, and expanded social safety nets become urgent responses to an existential danger rather than ideological preferences. Sun is unsparing in her assessment: "You don't just see that from Bernie. You also see it from folks who want to say, increase speech regulation and censorship or content moderation for tech platforms, where there are folks who are already very interested in applying stronger age and kid safety laws or stronger speech regulations on tech platforms. And now that AI has showed up, it's become kind of an extra reason to push for the thing that people are already pushing for."

    This dynamic, Sun suggests, explains why the AI populism coalition is so ideologically diverse. Everyone is using AI to justify their existing agenda. The environmentalist opposes data centers because of energy consumption. The antitrust warrior opposes AI monopolies. The social conservative worries about content moderation and cultural degradation. The progressive fears automation and inequality. AI has become, in Sun's framing, "the shiny new thing" that allows different political factions to agree on a common enemy while pursuing their separate goals.

    Why AI Populism May Be More Durable Than Crypto Populism

    The interview's most directly comparative argument comes when the hosts invoke the example of Elizabeth Warren's 2023-2024 anti-crypto campaign. After the collapse of FTX and the broader crypto winter, Warren attempted to build what she called an "anti-crypto army," casting digital assets as a corrupt, incomprehensible, and socially harmful technology that warranted aggressive regulation. The effort, as the hosts note, largely failed to resonate. "Normal people just looked at it and were like, 'Anti crypto army? I care about jobs and the economy and inflation. Like, what are you talking about?'" The messaging did not stick.

    Sun engages seriously with this parallel, acknowledging its relevance while identifying crucial differences. The first and most fundamental is scale. "Crypto was not driving like 30 or 40 percent of GDP growth over the course of the year," she observes. Crypto remained a niche asset class, confusing to use, and irrelevant to most Americans' daily working lives. AI, by contrast, is not optional or abstruse—it is already embedded in workplaces, consumer products, and the physical landscape. "Most people at their work are not being forced or encouraged to use crypto as part of their jobs, nor is there as high of consumer adoption," Sun notes. "ChatGPT is, like, the fastest growing app in human history." This matters enormously for political salience. When a technology is part of your daily experience—whether you use it at work, see it reshaping your neighborhood, or hear your own employer talking about it—you have a direct stake in the political debate about it. Crypto never achieved that kind of ambient relevance.

    The second difference Sun identifies is the credibility of the threat narrative. In the crypto case, there was a sharp divide between Elizabeth Warren's characterization of crypto as dangerous and the industry's self-understanding as an innovative and democratizing force. In AI, this divide is notably absent. "One thing that's really interesting," Sun says, "is that the risks that the populists are talking about are many of the same risks that people in the industry are talking about." She cites Dario Amodei, the CEO of Anthropic, who has publicly predicted that 50 percent of entry-level white collar jobs will disappear by 2030. When the people building the technology are the ones issuing the warnings, the populist narrative gains enormous credibility. Voters do not have to trust politicians' motives or parse complex technology policy—they just have to listen to the tech executives themselves confirming that disruption is coming for their livelihoods.

    The Physical Politics of AI: Data Centers and Direct Action

    Beyond polling and political rhetoric, the interview explores a more visceral dimension of AI populism: the physical communities where AI infrastructure is being built. The hosts raise a striking observation. While AI ranks only twenty-ninth in voter priorities, "entire communities [are] showing up in town halls to tell people that they don't want data centers in their communities' backyards." This is not abstract policy disagreement. It is homeowners, local activists, and neighborhood associations organizing to oppose the construction of facilities that will consume enormous amounts of electricity, strain water supplies, and transform the character of their communities.

    Sun does not dismiss this as irrational NIMBYism. Instead, she treats it as evidence of how AI becomes politically galvanizing precisely because it is not abstract. When a data center is going to be built next to your house, when the trucks start coming, when the energy bills rise because of industrial power consumption, the issue moves from the realm of elite political debate into lived experience. This is the mechanism by which a "twenty-ninth place" issue becomes a twenty-ninth-place issue that mobilizes real people in real communities.

    The conversation grows darker when the hosts raise the question of violence. Sam Altman's home has been targeted twice—once with a Molotov cocktail and once with bullets. The health insurance CEO Luigi Mangione's assassination by Luigi Mangione generated enormous public sympathy and ignited protests. These incidents, the hosts suggest, indicate that AI anger has crossed a threshold. Sun agrees, though carefully, describing AI as having become "almost this political bogeyman" and drawing a comparison to the way China dominated Washington discourse over the past decade, where "everything was because China." In both cases, a powerful, vaguely understood, and seemingly inexorable force becomes the explanation for everything—and the target for intense political mobilization.

    The China Parallel and the Future of AI as Political Justification

    Sun's invocation of the China comparison is one of the most analytically rich moments of the interview. She suggests that AI is beginning to occupy the same rhetorical space that China occupied in American political discourse: as an all-purpose justification for a wide range of policy choices. "We gotta reinvest in manufacturing because China. We need to educate our kids better because China. The specter of China competition in China eating America's lunch on the economy, on geopolitics, on whatever was sort of used as an all purpose justification in Washington, DC." The parallel implies that AI may become similarly万能—useful not because the specific policy being advocated is actually tied to AI, but because invoking AI gives the advocate access to a reservoir of fear and urgency that primes voters to accept almost any conclusion.

    This is, Sun acknowledges, not entirely illegitimate. Some AI risks are real. Some China concerns were and are justified. But the all-purpose bogeyman dynamic also creates opportunities for bad-faith political theater, where every policy preference gets retrofitted onto the AI threat du jour. The question for 2028 and beyond is whether this dynamic will continue to intensify. Sun's answer is yes. "I think that this opportunism is probably going to accelerate going into the twenty twenty eight primary season. I mean, it's gonna be a crowded primary, most likely, on both the Republican and the Democratic" side.

    Detailed Takeaways

    1. AI Populism Is Rising Faster Than It Is Rising High

    The polling data Sun cites is the most important empirical foundation of her argument. AI's twenty-ninth-place ranking masks its trajectory. It is rising faster than any other issue in American politics, which means that the window between current irrelevance and future dominance may be shorter than observers expect. For political strategists, investors, and technology companies, this trend line should be a signal to prepare for a political environment in which AI is not a niche concern but a lens through which voters evaluate economic policy, cultural change, and electoral choices.

    2. The Link Between AI and Bread-and-Butter Issues Is the Key Variable

    Sun's most important analytical contribution is the insight that AI populism's electoral significance depends not on AI being a top-tier issue in isolation, but on AI being successfully linked to the issues voters already prioritize. If voters come to believe that AI is the cause of job losses, inflation, neighborhood change, and economic inequality, then AI becomes a prism for existing anxieties rather than a new concern competing for attention. The political risk—and opportunity—is that this linking process is already underway, driven by both genuine concerns and opportunistic politicians who have found a new justification for familiar policy goals.

    3. Industry Credibility Amplifies Populist Fears in Ways Crypto Never Achieved

    The crypto populism comparison fails, Sun argues, because crypto's advocates and critics operated in different epistemic universes. In AI, the populists are citing the industry's own executives as evidence of danger. Dario Amodei's fifty-percent-job-displacement forecast is not a fringe prediction—it comes from one of the most respected AI labs in the world. This industry-validated fear narrative is far more politically durable than one that requires voters to distrust the technology's creators.

    4. Physical Proximity to AI Infrastructure Creates Mobilization Beyond Traditional Political Channels

    The data center phenomenon deserves more attention than it typically receives in tech policy analysis. Town hall meetings packed with homeowners opposing data center construction represent a form of political mobilization that bypasses traditional party structures, interest groups, and even voting. These are citizens who may not care about AI as a technology or a political abstraction, but who are galvanized by its physical presence in their lives. For campaigns seeking to energize base voters, this grassroots infrastructure opposition may be a resource waiting to be tapped.

    5. 2028 Will Likely See AI Populism Enter the Electoral Mainstream

    Sun's prediction that AI opportunism will accelerate through the 2028 primary season should be taken seriously. Crowded primaries reward distinctive messaging, and AI offers something rare: a common enemy that can unite voters who disagree on everything else. Whether the specific form this takes is regulation, antitrust action, labor protections, or cultural backlash against tech elites, the underlying dynamic of AI as a populist bogeyman is likely to intensify.
