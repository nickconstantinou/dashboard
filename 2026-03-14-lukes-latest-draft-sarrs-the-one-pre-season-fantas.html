Luke's Latest Draft | Sarr's the One! | Pre Season | Fantasy Premier League Tips 2025/26

A Comprehensive Analysis of FPL Blackbox's Conservative Draft Strategy

---

Introduction

Fantasy Premier League managers up and down the country are currently wrestling with one of the most challenging pre-season periods in recent memory. With transfer windows still open, team lineups uncertain, and numerous striker situations up in the air, the task of constructing a viable FPL team for the 2025/26 season feels more like navigating a minefield than the straightforward decision-making process many had hoped for. In this context, Luke from FPL Blackbox (@this_fpl on social media) has released a video sharing what he describes as his "most sensible" current draft—a deliberately conservative approach that he acknowledges might actually be the team he ends up going with when the season begins.

The video represents a fascinating window into how experienced FPL managers approach uncertainty. Rather than chasing the latest transfer rumors or attempting to predict which newly-signed players will break into starting lineups immediately, Luke has opted for what he calls his "safety net"—a team built primarily around players whose situations are relatively stable and whose positions in their respective teams appear secure. This conservative philosophy stands in stark contrast to the often headline-grabbing suggestions from other FPL content creators who favor more aggressive, high-upside strategies. For viewers looking to understand how to build resilience into their FPL teams during a period of maximum uncertainty, Luke's approach offers a masterclass in risk management.

The Philosophy Behind a "Boring" Draft

Luke opens his presentation by acknowledging that his current draft represents the more conservative end of the spectrum. He explicitly states that this is "probably my most sensible one" and apologizes in advance for its relatively straightforward nature, describing it as "the boring one." However, he quickly clarifies that he doesn't want to engage in pure clickbait with his content—instead, this genuinely might be the team he settles on when the season kicks off. The key reason for this caution is the sheer amount of uncertainty still surrounding the Premier League landscape: team signings remain incomplete, striker situations at several clubs are unresolved, and the typical pre-season volatility means that many players could see their stock rise or fall dramatically before Gameweek 1.

The most important insight Luke shares early in the video concerns the general advice he would offer regarding newly-signed players. Drawing on his own experience—specifically mentioning that he was "one of the people who had Barco" and noting that he thankfully "didn't fall for Quana"—Luke emphasizes that players who have recently been signed by a new club may not immediately break into the starting lineup. Managers often need time to integrate new signings into their systems, and there's frequently a period where the player is being "worked into our system" and "brought into training for a few days" before being introduced gradually. This creates a problematic scenario for FPL managers because these players are likely to lose value if the broader FPL community expects them to start but they don't, leading to mass selling and subsequent price drops. Luke's solution is straightforward: avoid these players at the start of the season entirely if possible, as you "don't want to be carrying any worries at the beginning of the season when the player base is so active."

Goalkeeper Selection: The 4.5 Million Strategy

Moving into the specifics of his draft, Luke begins with his goalkeeper selection, explaining his decision to stick with a 4.5 million starter between the posts. His reasoning centers on the value proposition at the budget end of the goalkeeper market: with Nottingham Forest's Marko Dravka (likely meaning Matz Sels or another Forest keeper) available at exactly 4 million and likely to be a starting goalkeeper, the 4.5 million bracket offers two playing goalkeepers—a significant upgrade over the cheaper options. Among these 4.5 options, Luke states that "Keller's best," referring to Frankfurt's Kevin Trapp or another keeper at that price point (the exact identification is unclear from the transcript, but the strategy is clear).

Interestingly, Luke acknowledges that Brentford recently changed managers and "let up a lot of shots," meaning there's no guarantee his chosen goalkeeper will keep many clean sheets. However, he views this as acceptable because the goalkeeper is "a good goalkeeper" who he hopes can "kind of imitate Flecken who was fine last season." He also notes that he hasn't seriously considered other options, including West Ham's goalkeeper situation—which appears to be in flux with the club "in the market for one and maybe even signing one very shortly." Luke mentions that West Ham rotate reasonably well with their backup goalkeepers, but he "hate[s] rotating goalkeepers" and always seems to "get it wrong."

The broader philosophy here is revealing: Luke has started each of the last three seasons with Raya (presumably David Raya at Arsenal or Brentford) and "basically had [him] for the whole season." While acknowledging that goalkeeper selection involves "a lot of luck," he argues that when you buy someone like Raya or Allison "outside of injury, you are pretty much guaranteeing yourself like they're going to be in the top three scoring goalkeepers. And that security is quite good. That's what you're paying for." His conclusion is that he'll "be very surprised outside injury if [Raya] isn't in the top three scoring goalkeepers again," but that there's "usually a five or a 4.5 that'll either beat them or get very close" right at the beginning when money is tight—so he's sticking with the budget option for now.

Defensive Strategy: Prioritizing Clean Sheet Points

The defensive portion of Luke's draft reveals a clear theme: he favors defenders who consistently contribute clean sheet points, rather than those who might offer occasional attacking returns. He describes himself as "a former Sky player" (referring to the now-defunct Sky Fantasy Premier League) where "collecting bonus points for your defenders was like the name of the game." This background has shaped his approach to FPL defense, where he "just don't seem to have any luck with clean sheets" and finds them "at a premium these days in the Premier League" and "very hard to collect."

Rather than attempting to chase fixture changes or rotate based on upcoming opponents—a strategy that often leads to wasted transfers—Luke prefers to "leave these guys in and hopefully if they can replicate last season." Even if these defenders don't replicate their previous season's returns, he notes that "they can just pick me up threes and fours" which he says "lovely, I'll take that all day." This contrasts sharply with his experience of typically getting "twos and ones from my defenders" in previous seasons, making the consistency of clean sheet points particularly valuable.

Specific defenders in his draft include two players who earn "defcons"—a term likely referring to defensive contributions that earn clean sheet points. He mentions Tarsoski (likely Ethan Tatterski or a similar player) and Marillo, noting that these players each scored four goals last season for "eight points lovely." While Luke admits he's "not completely set on Tarsoski" given concerns about repeating last season's performance, he argues that "players don't really change their stripes too much" and that these players "have pretty much got the same setups, the same managers, they're the same players" which makes their returns "quite a repeatable behavior."

The most notable inclusion in Luke's defense is Pedro Porro of Tottenham Hotspur. He acknowledges having "reservations" about Porro given Tottenham's "bad" performance last season, but emphasizes that "the fixtures... are just so good" for the Spanish full-back. He notes that "virtually any model" will show Porro ranking "top over like the first five or six weeks for expected points" once minutes are factored in. While he's uncertain about Porro's attacking output under Tottenham's new management, he points to the player's involvement in pre-season friendlies—including a created chance in a match against Bayern Munich—as evidence of his creative ability. The injury to Tottenham's Madison has "probably even boosted his stock slightly" because Porro might take more set pieces. Ultimately, Luke concludes that Porro is "definitely on all them set pieces" and "a decent bet" for the season ahead.

Midfield Philosophy: Loading the Most Point-Rich Area

Luke's draft shows a clear intention to allocate significant resources to the midfield, describing the area as "just stacked with all the money." He acknowledges that people who have seen his team in various Discord communities "give me like a pregnant woman emoji with a obviously big around the middle" when they see his midfield, but he stands by his approach: "midfield is like the place where you get your points, where you earn your coin."

The reasoning behind this strategy is pragmatic: while there will always be cheaper midfielders who perform exceptionally well in any given season, "knowing who they are at the beginning of the season is quite tough." By loading up on expensive, proven midfield options, Luke aims to minimize variance and maximize his baseline points floor, trusting that his premium selections will deliver consistent returns throughout the season. Detailed Takeaways

Takeaway 1: Newly Signed Players Represent Hidden Risk at Season Start

Perhaps the most universally applicable insight Luke provides concerns the danger of selecting players who have recently transferred to new clubs. Even when these players appear destined for starting positions, the reality of Premier League football often involves integration periods where managers gradually ease new signings into the team. This creates a scenario where early-season price drops are likely if the player doesn't start immediately, forcing managers into difficult transfer decisions during the most active period of the FPL calendar. Luke's solution is to avoid these players entirely at the start unless their position in the starting lineup is absolutely guaranteed, which is rarely the case with recent signings.

Takeaway 2: Budget Goalkeepers Offer Value That Outweighs Premium Alternatives

Rather than spending 5.0 or 5.5 million on premium goalkeepers like Allison or Ederson, Luke advocates for the 4.5 million bracket where multiple playing options exist. His reasoning is compelling: the difference between premium and budget goalkeepers is often minimal in terms of actual points scored, while the savings can be allocated to more impactful areas of the team. The key is identifying the 4.5 million keeper with the best fixture run and clean sheet potential—not attempting to predict which premium keeper will outperform the field.

Takeaway 3: Clean Sheet Points Represent Undervalued Consistency

Luke's defensive strategy centers on players who consistently keep clean sheets rather than chasing attacking returns from defenders. While defenders like Porro occasionally deliver goals and assists, the reality is that "it's quite rare for defenders to get really high attacking numbers" and relying on these returns often leads to disappointment. Players who simply play week-in and week-out and accumulate clean sheet points offer more predictable returns than their more glamorous attacking counterparts.

Takeaway 4: Fixture Runs Matter Enormously at the Start of the Season

When evaluating players like Porro, Luke emphasizes that "the fixtures... are just so good" and that models consistently show certain players ranking among the top expected points earners for the first five or six weeks based on fixture difficulty alone. This analytical approach—using expected points models that factor in upcoming fixtures—provides a data-driven foundation for early-season decisions before form and other variables become more reliable.

Takeaway 5: Stacking Midfield Represents the Optimal Allocation of Resources

Rather than spreading budget evenly across all positions, Luke deliberately loads his midfield with premium options, reasoning that midfield is "the place where you get your points, where you earn your coin." This philosophy accepts that premium defenders and forwards might be underweighted in favor of maximizing the highest-scoring position in FPL, where the difference between a 7.0 million midfielder and a 12.0 million midfielder often proves more significant than similar differences in other positions.
