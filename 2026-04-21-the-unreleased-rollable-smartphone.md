# The Unreleased Rollable Smartphone: A Deep Dive into LG's Final Gamble

## The Rise and Fall of LG's Mobile Division

For a brief moment in smartphone history, LG occupied a peculiar corner of the market—one defined by audacious experimentation and a willingness to pursue ideas that most manufacturers wouldn't dare attempt. The South Korean electronics giant built a reputation on phones that challenged conventional design, from the G2's rear-mounted buttons to the circular-faced LG G5 modular system. But as narrator and tech reviewer Dave Lee explains in this revealing video, LG's greatest strength—relentless innovation—ultimately became the albatross that dragged the company down.

The fundamental problem, according to Lee, was that LG produced "a bunch of crazy phones without also having, like, a steady moneymaker at the same time." This create a paradox that defined the company's mobile strategy: they captured the attention and admiration of tech enthusiasts worldwide, but that admiration never translated into sustainable profit. The company迭代 through experimental designs while competitors focused on incremental improvements that actually resonated with mainstream consumers. By 2021, LG found itself in an untenable position—technically respected but financially exhausted—and the mobile division shut down operations entirely.

Before that final curtain fell, however, LG had one more trick waiting in the wings. The company had been developing a rollable smartphone that represented the logical endpoint of their philosophy: what if a phone could transform from a pocketable slab into a miniature tablet without the structural compromises of a fold? Two years after LG first showcased rolling display technology in a television at CES, rumors had begun circulating about a phone-sized implementation that never officially launched. Now, years after LG's exit from the smartphone market, Lee has obtained one of these mythical devices—courtesy of the accessories company dbrand—and his hands-on impressions reveal both the brilliance and the fatal flaws of what might have been LG's final masterpiece.

## The Wing and Its Predecessors: Understanding LG's Experimental DNA

To fully appreciate the rollable phone's significance, Lee believes we need to understand the context of LG's previous experimental efforts. The final phones LG released before shutting down were the LG Velvet and the LG Wing, both of which demonstrated the company's willingness to pursue form factors that defied conventional logic. The Wing, in particular, represents the kind of engineering ambition that made LG both beloved and unprofitable.

The LG Wing appeared as an ordinary smartphone at first glance, but it contained a hidden mechanism that allowed the top layer to swivel open in a horizontal orientation, revealing a second, smaller square display underneath. The result was a T-shaped screen experience that Lee describes as "for some reason??"—acknowledging the inherent absurdity of the concept while simultaneously recognizing the engineering marvel it represented. Creating a spring-loaded mechanism that could reliably switch between orientations while maintaining a premium feel required sophisticated hardware engineering and extensive software optimization. The fact that this complex system felt "intentional" rather than kludgy represents a genuine accomplishment, even if the market never rewarded it with significant sales.

Prior to the Wing, LG had also developed the G8X, which represented an alternative approach to the dual-screen concept. Rather than building a folding phone, LG created an accessory case containing a secondary display that users could plug their phone into for a dual-screen experience. This approach offered functionality similar to folding phones at a fraction of the development cost, and it incorporated clever engineering touches like pass-through magnetic charging adapters that allowed fast charging even while the USB port was occupied by the case. These earlier experiments established a pattern that would culminate in the rollable phone: LG consistently pursuing novel interactions even when established alternatives existed.

## The Rollable Concept: How It Works and What It Promises

The core concept behind LG's unreleased rollable phone is elegantly simple in theory. When closed, the device presents as a conventional smartphone with a 6.7-inch display measured diagonally—roughly comparable to the screen real estate users expected from flagship phones of that era. The exterior appearance resembles a standard slab phone, which means the form factor remained instantly familiar despite the revolutionary internal mechanism.

The magic happens through a three-finger swipe gesture across the display, which triggers the transformation. The phone rolls open to reveal a slightly more square-shaped display measuring 7.4 inches diagonally, effectively creating a mini-tablet experience from a device that fits comfortably in a pocket. Lee notes that "the number 6.7 to 7.4 doesn't sound like that big of a difference on paper, but it's obviously the shape of the display that's changing quite a bit." The distinction matters because the shift from 6.7 inches in a 20:9 aspect ratio to 7.4 inches in something closer to 4:3 fundamentally changes how content displays and how apps can organize information.

What makes this possible is a flexible display that curves around the phone's edges rather than terminating at the frame. When the device appears closed, it actually functions as a single screen that wraps over both edges, similar to some of Samsung's older "edge" display designs. The additional screen area remains hidden in plain sight, curved around a rail on the right side where it dips out of view. Flipping the phone over reveals the display continuing to curve over the back surface, and LG made this portion from clear glass so users could actually see the rolled portion—a design choice that Lee connects to Xiaomi's Mi Mix Alpha concept phone, which featured an all-screen design wrapping around the device's sides and back for an extreme screen-to-body ratio.

## Engineering Details and Software Sophistication

The physical mechanism driving the rollable phone reveals the extraordinary level of engineering LG invested in this project. Two motors handle the opening and closing motion, while three spring-loaded arms push the chassis over to the side as the display unrolls. This multi-motor approach ensures smooth, controlled movement that can adapt to resistance—Lee discovered that the motors are "stronger than you would expect," capable of pushing a 16-inch MacBook Pro with rubber feet across a table, which he estimates represents about five pounds of resistance.

The phone also incorporates safety features that Lee finds surprisingly sophisticated. If the device is open and a user squeezes it hard in their hand, the mechanism recognizes the pressure and triggers itself to finish closing, preventing potential damage to the display or internal components. After executing this protective close, the phone displays an error message. Lee interprets this as evidence that "clearly they've really thought about this," suggesting extensive testing and refinement during the development process.

LG also developed software features specifically optimized for the rollable form factor. The lock screen features a distinctive blooming flower animation when the device expands, with the animation reversing to a single petal when the phone contracts. The Settings app includes a subtle slide-over animation when transitioning between states, adding a column of options when the display unrolls and removing it when rolling closed. These details might seem trivial, but Lee suggests they indicate how close LG had come to shipping this product: "Honestly, it feels like they're really close to releasing this."

## Practical Limitations and Trade-offs

Despite the engineering sophistication, Lee identifies several practical limitations that likely contributed to the phone never reaching consumers. The most significant concern involves durability against environmental factors. When open, the device reveals visible gears at the top and bottom with apparent air gaps between components. Unlike modern folding phones where the flexible display remains protected within a sealed chassis when not in use, the LG rollable phone's display is constantly exposed to the elements. Lee notes that "there's no way this is water-resistant or dust-resistant, probably," acknowledging that while LG may never have officially sought an IP rating for this unreleased device, the physical design makes such certification essentially impossible.

The mechanism also presents potential pinch hazards. While human fingers are generally too large to fit into the gaps where the rolling mechanism operates, the phone can easily pinch smaller items and lift them up, including microfiber cloths. Lee demonstrates this by showing the phone picking up a cleaning cloth, which suggests users would need to exercise caution with small objects like coins or paper clips.

Another interesting limitation relates to the display curvature itself. One theoretical advantage of rollable displays over folding displays is the elimination of creases, since the rolling mechanism doesn't require the display to fold around a tight radius. However, Lee discovered that the LG rollable phone doesn't achieve "no crease"—there's still some curve where the display rolls over to the side, creating a larger radius but not a completely flat surface. The wavering is more visible when the display is off and is "not really a big deal compared to some of the creases we've seen" in folding phones, but it demonstrates that the technology involves trade-offs rather than pure advantages.

## User Interface Innovations

The user interface design reveals both creative solutions and necessary compromises stemming from the unique form factor. The volume buttons present an interesting case study in adaptation. Because the curved display takes up the space on the phone's left side where buttons would normally reside, LG couldn't install traditional physical buttons. Instead, they implemented pressure-sensitive areas at the top of the device that function as virtual volume controls. The power button, meanwhile, is located on the back of the phone, positioned beneath the camera module where it also serves as a fingerprint reader. Lee admits this seems "kind of weird at first, but surprisingly reachable," suggesting that LG's designers had considered real-world usability during development.

The secondary display on the phone's back opens possibilities for software innovation. Users can activate this rear display to show messages or other notifications even when the phone is face-down on a surface. More interestingly, the back display can present camera UI, allowing users to take selfies using the primary cameras—which remain on the back of the device—without needing to flip the phone around. This represents the kind of creative problem-solving that characterized LG's approach to product design, finding ways to leverage unusual hardware configurations into genuine utility.

The audio design includes an interesting detail: every time the phone opens or closes, it plays a sound through the speakers that Lee suspects is meant to mask the noise of the motors. He describes the effectiveness as "about as effective as coughing to cover up a fart," while acknowledging he appreciates the concept. The video includes an anecdote imagining "some engineer that had to sit in a room and come up with the perfect sound to match the frequencies of the motor to try to hide it," which Lee finds "hilarious." Users can select different sounds from a menu, all roughly timed to match the unrolling duration, or disable the sound entirely to hear the motor alone.

## The Broader Context: Why This Matters for Mobile Innovation

The existence of the LG rollable phone raises broader questions about the relationship between innovation and commercial viability in the smartphone industry. Lee argues that "LG is the only one who would actually try to ship a phone like this," suggesting that the company's experimental culture enabled form factors that more profit-focused manufacturers would never pursue. The rollable phone represents an approach to mobile devices that prioritized novel interaction patterns over market research and proven demand.

Yet the phone also demonstrates that LG had learned from previous experiments. The company invested in software features to make the unrolling experience feel polished, developed safety mechanisms to protect the device and its users, and even engineered audio solutions to minimize mechanical noise. This suggests a development process that had moved well beyond proof-of-concept into genuine product refinement. Lee observes that "they got insanely far in the development process. This is functional hardware, working software, special features, all that stuff. They just didn't survive long enough to actually ship it."

The video also touches on potential future developments. Lee mentions that "there is word on the street that Samsung is also trying to do a rollable type of smartphone even still," suggesting that the rollable form factor may not be exclusively a relic of LG's experimental era. If Samsung is pursuing similar technology, it could indicate that the underlying concept has merit even if the implementation challenges proved too great for LG to overcome. Lee expresses interest in covering Samsung's implementation if it materializes, promising to "definitely get subscribed to be among the first to see that if it does come out."
