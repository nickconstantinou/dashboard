The Fixture-by-Fixture Analysis

Friday Evening: Sunderland vs Nottingham Forest

Harry begins his fixture analysis with Friday night's match between Sunderland and Nottingham Forest. Going into GW33, he admits he had planned to include a couple of Sunderland players in his team, potentially doubling up in defense with players like Alderete, Mukielli, or goalkeeper Roing. He's still open to these options, particularly for defenders who offer reasonable defensive contributions, but his confidence has diminished somewhat from where it was a week or two ago.

Nottingham Forest impressed Harry in their GW33 fixture against Burnley at home. Despite the relatively straightforward opposition, Forest continue to score goals and maintain their position in the Premier League. Morgan Gibbs-White in particular is finding exceptional form at a crucial stage of the season for Forest, who still need points between now and the end of the campaign to ensure their Premier League survival—though they have established a five-point buffer against Spurs sitting at the top of the relegation zone.

The key dilemma Harry identifies is whether to chase Gibbs-White's form despite his recent hat-trick. While the Nottingham Forest midfielder will undoubtedly attract significant popularity after scoring three goals this week, Harry is hesitant about targeting Sunderland defensively. Sunderland have been excellent at the back this season, and he doesn't expect Forest to score "loads of goals" in this particular fixture. The risk-reward calculation suggests Gibbs-White might be over-priced relative to his actual ceiling in this specific matchup, making him a more marginal option than his ownership might suggest.

Fulham vs Aston Villa: Attacking Opportunities Abound

Moving to the Fulham versus Aston Villa fixture, Harry sees this as a matchup that favors attacking selections rather than defensive ones. Aston Villa just put four goals past Sunderland—a team that most managers perceive as better defensively than they are going forward. However, the same Villa side also conceded three goals to Sunderland, creating a complex picture of a team that can both score and concede freely.

This fixture profile makes players like Ollie Watkins, who is finding form alongside Morgan Rogers, particularly attractive. Harry specifically mentions Harry Wilson as another option worth considering for Aston Villa exposure. The alternative of picking defensive assets like Antonee Anderson or Matty Cash doesn't appeal to him as much given the evidence of both teams' attacking capabilities. When a fixture presents goalscoring potential from both sides, the mathematically optimal play is often to load up on attackers rather than defenders, and this match appears to fit that profile.

Liverpool vs Crystal Palace: Options at Anfield

Crystal Palace's Monday night fixture against West Ham means they'll be playing their second match in quick succession when they face Liverpool on the weekend. Harry doesn't see much appeal in Palace assets for this gameweek, though he acknowledges that Ismaila Sarr has a pretty good record against Liverpool. He specifically references gameweek 38 of the previous season, when many managers sold Sarr after his disappointing performance in the Crystal Palace doubles, only to watch him score in that game against Liverpool at Anfield. Sometimes history rhymes, and Sarr's historical success against Liverpool is worth noting even in a tough fixture.

For Liverpool, the options are more plentiful. Alexander Isak is starting to see more minutes, though Harry doesn't think he looks particularly involved in the game when he's on the pitch. The midfielders offer more consistent potential, with Mohamed Salah starting to find a bit of form and scoring a few more goals. Dominic Szoboszlai remains reliable with set pieces, open play threat, and defensive contribution potential. Harry identifies Cody Gakpo as an interesting differential—he's been starting games on the left, but because Isak can't play 90 minutes, Gakpo often finishes games through the middle as the number nine, giving him a different attacking profile than the other options.

There's one significant concern for Liverpool defenders: Mamadou Sakho and Alisson are likely out for this game, which raises questions about their defensive solidity. However, Virgil van Dijk always carries goal threat from set pieces, making him a viable option despite the potential defensive instability around him.

West Ham vs Everton: Fighting for Different Reasons

West Ham versus Everton presents an interesting contrast in motivations. West Ham continue their fight for Premier League survival, making players like Mohammed Kudus or Jarrod Bowen attractive options if you're looking for West Ham exposure. Harry specifically mentions Duke for Somerville or Hermanson as interesting options for those wanting to punt on West Ham, though these represent more differential approaches.

Everton, meanwhile, still have plenty to play for as well, which makes players like Iliman Ndiaye and potentially Dida Tarroki interesting. Harry doesn't expect a clean sheet for Everton in this game, which slightly reduces the appeal of their defenders but increases the potential for attacking returns from their forwards. The matchup feels relatively evenly matched, with both teams capable of scoring but neither particularly trustworthy at the back.

Wolves vs Tottenham: The High-Risk Punt

The Wolves versus Tottenham fixture emerges as the punt of the week for Harry, and he explains why this match carries both significant risk and potential reward. Tottenham were probably better than he's seen them in quite some time against Brighton, but they let emotion get to them at the end of that game, with Brighton coming back to level and draw two—a result that will have frustrated Spurs fans considerably.

For managers on free hit, Tottenham assets become interesting because if they don't win this game, they find themselves in even more trouble than they already are. This feels like probably their most winnable fixture remaining in the season, which means the pressure and expectation will be significant. Harry identifies players like Dejan Kulusevski, Pedro Porro, and James Maddison as Tottenham assets worth considering, though he notes that going the complete other direction and picking Wolves players represents a contrarian strategy for those who think the pressure might prove too much for Tottenham.

Harry explicitly states he wouldn't recommend the Wolves route, but acknowledging it exists helps managers understand the full range of possibilities in this fixture. Tottenham's desperation for a win creates both opportunity and risk, and how managers navigate that depends on their overall strategy and risk tolerance.

Newcastle vs Arsenal: Defensive Considerations

Newcastle look "all over the place" according to Harry, while Arsenal looked okay defensively in their game against Manchester City. This creates a complex puzzle for managers looking to pick Newcastle or Arsenal attackers. Players like Martin Ødegaard and Ezy Sakser, if he's back, represent Arsenal attacking options, but Harry feels there's too much risk of minutes combined with a lack of attacking form to make them confident picks.

Defensively, Gabriel has been impressive, especially after he wasn't sent off against Manchester City, while Timber is close to a return from injury. The Newcastle defenders, particularly those facing Arsenal, represent a different proposition—Harry mentions players like Dan Burn as potentially popular picks for managers looking for cheap defensive options with good fixture appeal. The Monday night match creates additional complexity for managers not on free hit, who might be wrestling with how to handle their Brentford exposure, but for free hit users, the calculus is different.

Manchester United vs Brentford: The Monday Night Conundrum

The final fixture on Monday night pits Manchester United against Brentford, and Harry acknowledges that many managers not on free hit will have significant Brentford exposure—whether defensively with players like Nathan Tha or offensively with Thiago. He expects many free hit managers will look to go against Brentford, perhaps by picking Manchester United assets or avoiding Brentford entirely.

Harry currently doesn't have any Brentford players in his free hit draft despite acknowledging that picking a second forward is actually quite difficult and might force him toward someone like Thiago by the deadline. This honest admission about his own uncertainty demonstrates the genuine difficulty of constructing a free hit squad in gameweek 34—there are no obvious answers, and every manager must weigh their own risk tolerance and existing squad structure.

For Manchester United, Harry sees more options but also more uncertainty. It's not clear exactly which players will start between Cesco, Mainoo, and Amad, which makes picking United assets feel risky. Instead, he's considering more established names like Casemiro, Bruno Fernandes, or Mainoo as more reliable options, with Harry Maguire representing an interesting punt given his ability to deliver a 15-pointer if things break right.

Key Strategic Insights

The Free Hit Requires Patience and Specificity

Perhaps the most important takeaway from Harry's analysis is that the free hit is not a license to simply pick the most expensive players. While having unlimited transfers for a single gameweek is powerful, the fixture difficulty and form considerations still matter enormously. Harry repeatedly emphasizes that he's not simply stacking his team with the highest-priced assets but rather identifying specific matchups where he believes the points potential justifies the investment. This means understanding not just which teams are playing, but how they match up tactically, who is in form, and who might be rotation risks.

Fixture Symmetry Doesn't Guarantee Points

The temptation with free hit is to look at teams with good fixture runs and simply load up on their players. Harry's analysis demonstrates why this approach can be flawed. Sunderland, for example, have been excellent defensively this season, but that doesn't automatically make every Sunderland player a good pick in every fixture. Similarly, teams playing for survival like Forest or West Ham might have more motivation, but that motivation doesn't translate linearly into Fantasy points without the right players in the right matchups.

Differential Picks Carry Context

Harry's discussion of players like Cody Gakpo, Harry Maguire, and Wolves defenders demonstrates that differential picks aren't just about finding cheap options—they're about understanding why a player might outperform their ownership percentage. Gakpo's ability to finish games through the middle as Liverpool's number nine creates a different attacking profile than Salah or Szoboszlai, making him a legitimate differential rather than just a speculative pick. Managers should seek similar contextual justifications for their own differential plays rather than simply chasing low ownership for its own sake.

Form Versus Fixture: The Constant Tension

Throughout his analysis, Harry navigates the perennial FPL tension between recent form and fixture difficulty. Gibbs-White just scored a hat-trick, but Sunderland at home presents a different challenge than Burnley at home. Watkins is finding form, but Fulham away is no guarantee of points. Salah is starting to score again, but Liverpool's defensive questions against Palace complicate the clean sheet potential. The managers who succeed with their free hit are those who can accurately weigh these competing factors and make decisions based on probability rather than recency bias.
