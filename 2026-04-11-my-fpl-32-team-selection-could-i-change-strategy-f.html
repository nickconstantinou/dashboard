My FPL Gameweek 32 Team Selection: Navigating the Chip Strategy Puzzle

Introduction

The 2025/26 Fantasy Premier League season has reached its critical phase, and Gameweek 32 represents the most consequential decision point for managers still in the hunt for rank gains. With exactly seven weeks remaining in the season, the strategic deployment of chips—wildcard, free hit, and bench boost—can mean the difference between a mediocre finish and a genuinely competitive rank. The complexity of this moment is amplified by the fact that many managers have accumulated five free transfers through careful planning, creating a genuine fork in the road: use the wildcard now in Gameweek 32, or preserve it for a potentially more advantageous future gameweek?

FPL Harry, a well-known content creator in the Fantasy Premier League community, addresses this exact dilemma in his detailed team selection video. Having achieved a Green Arrow in Gameweek 31—climbing approximately 10,000 places with a 65-point haul—Harry finds himself unusually blessed with squad flexibility while simultaneously facing the strategic headache of determining exactly when and how to deploy his remaining chips. His team currently maintains five free transfers, a luxury that many managers would envy, yet this very flexibility has created uncertainty about the optimal path forward. The conversation around chip strategy in the final third of the season requires careful consideration of fixture difficulty, player form, and the ever-present risk of unexpected blanks.

The Chip Strategy Conundrum

Weighing the Wildcard Option

The central question Harry addresses revolves around whether to play his wildcard in Gameweek 32 or adhere to his previously preferred strategy of using the free hit in Gameweek 33, the wildcard in Gameweek 35, and bench boost in Gameweek 36. "I still think I'm about a 66% chance that I don't play my wildcard and I go with the free at 33, wild card 35, and bench boost after chip strategy," Harry explains, noting that this represents a shift from his earlier assessment when the probability sat at approximately 75%. The decrease in confidence about the original plan stems from the specific flexibility his five free transfers have created, particularly around potentially maximizing the bench boost chip by selling Gabriel rather than being forced into a wildcard.

The reasoning against playing the wildcard in Gameweek 32 centers on his team appearing well-constructed for the immediate fixtures. Both Gameweek 32 and Gameweek 34, in his assessment, present favorable matchups for his current squad composition, making the idea of overhauling the team mid-window seem unnecessarily disruptive. "I think it suits my team better" is his blunt assessment of sticking with the original strategy. However, Harry acknowledges the seductiveness of the five free transfers as a powerful tool that could enable transformative changes if deployed correctly, creating the nagging question of whether leaving unused transfers on the table represents a missed opportunity.

The Gabriel Question

One specific scenario Harry considers involves selling Gabriel to enable a maximizing bench boost in Gameweek 33. Gabriel, who played for Arsenal in the Champions League on Tuesday evening, is expected to return for a home fixture against Bournemouth, meaning there is no immediate concern about his availability. Yet the strategic mathematics of transferring him out before deploying the bench boost chip—potentially giving managers an extra playable asset in that crucial week—represents exactly the kind of forward-thinking move that separates top-tier FPL managers from the pack. This consideration demonstrates how chip strategy cannot be viewed in isolation but must account for the specific player assets in one's squad and how they interact with the timing of chip deployments.

Gameweek 31 Review: A Modest Success

Before diving into his Gameweek 32 plans, Harry provides a brief retrospective on Gameweek 31, which predates the FA Cup chaos and the international break that created such disruption for many managers. "I got a Green Arrow, went up about 10,000 places," Harry reports with evident satisfaction, adding that his 65-point haul exceeded his safety score by approximately five points. It represented a solid consolidation week that maintained his upward trajectory despite what he describes as not thinking his team looked "too great" heading into the gameweek.

The decision to roll his transfer once again in Gameweek 31 proved instrumental in creating the five-free-transfer flexibility he now enjoys. By delaying the use of his transfer, Harry ensured he would have maximum optionality in Gameweek 32, accepting some short-term suboptimality in exchange for long-term strategic advantage. "James Tarosski" (Eze) started his gameweek with a modest six points, while Bruno Fernandes proved the highest scorer in the squad—though Harry notes that "everyone seemed to captain him on Monday night," diminishing the differential value of his selection. Harry Wilson provided a welcome return, helping push the score to an acceptable level. "It was a pretty good week. Definitely could have been worse," Harry sums up with characteristic understatement.

Detailed Team Analysis

Goalkeeping and Defensive Options

Harry begins his squad breakdown with David Ryui in goal, facing Bournemouth at home, while doubling up with Gabriel in the Arsenal defensive slot. His starting defenders include "Tokoski" (Eze) away at Brentford, Chris Richards at home to Newcastle, and "Burgan Djk" (Bot) as his third starting option—which he acknowledges represents a somewhat reluctant acceptance of his current defensive roster given the limited alternatives offering both clean sheet probability and attacking threat in equal measure.

The specific challenge Harry faces involves deciding between retaining Eze or Richards, with both Crystal Palace players appearing somewhat interchangeable in their profiles. His analysis identifies a crucial factor: Crystal Palace plays on Thursday evening in the Conference League, raising questions about whether their focus might shift away from Premier League matters. "I just feel like their focus is absolutely going to be on that Conference League, not only this Thursday, but also next Thursday as well," Harry observes, adding that depending on how Thursday's fixture progresses, Palace might either secure qualification or be eliminated, potentially changing their approach to subsequent league games.

Harry acknowledges that with five free transfers available, this represents an area where upgrading is possible. He specifically considers "maverel su" (Mavropanos) of West Ham at home to Wolves on Friday night or "Diego Dalo" (likely Dalot) of Manchester United at home to Leeds, though the latter's availability remains uncertain. Ultimately, he decides against making a defensive transfer, concluding that "my transfer this week is probably better used elsewhere" given the limited upside in moving between these particular options.

Midfield Strategy and The Harry Wilson Decision

Harry's midfield features Bruno Fernandes alongside Burno double up on Manchester United, though he expresses slight concern about the Portuguese international's minutes. "I do think he will start for Manchester United at home to Leeds," Harry offers, "but do I expect him to play more than 70 minutes is where I start to have a bit of doubt." Despite these concerns, he retains Bruno in his squad.

The most interesting midfield decision involves choosing between Harry Wilson and "Drewsbury Hall" (Szoboszlai), with Liverpool away at Brentford presenting perhaps the most difficult fixture of the lot. Harry ultimately opts for Wilson despite the tougher matchup, citing Liverpool's Champions League commitments on Wednesday night followed by another Champions League fixture the following Tuesday. "Wilson being in very good form, Liverpool not looking great defensively, meaning I have gone with Wilson as the starting player in my team," he explains, though he remains "not fully locked in" with this selection, acknowledging that Juhul (Szoboszlai) could still force his way into the starting lineup.

The bench composition includes Elliot Anderson at home to Aston Villa and Drewsbury Hall away at Brentford, while Harry benches Chris Richards, maintaining only single Everton representation in his squad. This approach reflects his desire to avoid over-loading players into the Brentford versus Everton fixture, where he already starts Eze and Haaland.

The Forward Line and Transfer Considerations

Harry's forward line features "Thiago" alongside "Echk" (Erling Haaland), acknowledging that Haaland "still feels like the one that comes with the most amount of risk." The reasoning centers on Manchester City's fixture pile: Champions League on Tuesday, Saturday at home to Fulham, and then the Manchester derby against Everton in Gameweek 33—all within a compressed timeframe. "There is a chance that Alexander Isac (likely Darwin Nunez) is back in contention for Liverpool, but I don't think he'll be starting any of these games over the short-term horizon," Harry notes, suggesting his concern about minutes continues beyond the immediate gameweek.

The most likely transfer Harry identifies is moving from "Ekit" (Foden) to Bowen, utilizing his free transfer to improve his squad for the run-in. The reasoning involves fixture analysis: Liverpool hosts Crystal Palace in Gameweek 34 while Bowen faces Everton at home. "I think it's slightly better for Bowen than it is for Ekit who will continue to be a minutes risk once we get that and Alexander Each comes closer to being back to full fitness in a few weeks as well," Harry explains, adding that Foden's continued minutes risk—combined with the impending return of other attackers—tips the balance toward making the move now.

Harry acknowledges holding Haaland provides protection against those wildplaying their wildcard in Gameweek 32. While others may have pivoted toward Gordon or other attacking options, maintaining Haaland represents a "shield" that could prove valuable if City begin rediscovering their best form in the season's final third.

Key Takeaways

Takeaway One: The Power of Free Transfers Cannot Be Overstated

Harry's primary strategic insight centers on how his five free transfers create optionality that fundamentally changes the calculus of his chip decisions. By rolling transfers through Gameweek 31, he entered Gameweek 32 with maximum flexibility—no obligation to make changes, but the ability to address any opportunity or problem that presents itself. This approach recognizes that in FPL, flexibility is often more valuable than certainty, and managers should consider the compound value of preserved transfers rather than focusing solely on immediate gameweek optimization.

Takeaway Two: Fixture Convergence Matters More Than Individual Fixtures

Throughout his analysis, Harry repeatedly references how fixtures cluster together—the Thursday Conference League match affects Palace's Sunday performance, Liverpool's Champions League commitments impact their Premier League fixture, Manchester City's packed schedule creates minutes risk. Effective FPL management requires thinking beyond single gameweeks and considering how teams' short-term schedules affect player availability and motivation over two or three matches at a time.

Takeaway Three: Form and Defensive Vulnerability Trump Fixture Difficulty

Harry's decision to start Wilson over Szoboszlai despite Liverpool's "difficult" away fixture exemplifies his prioritization of current form and opponent defensive weaknesses over traditional fixture difficulty ratings. Liverpool's defensive struggles combined with Wilson's excellent form presented a compelling case that outweighed the theoretical challenge of playing at Brentford. This approach suggests managers should update their assumptions about teams regularly—as the season progresses, defensive capabilities shift significantly, and what was true in September may no longer apply in April.

Takeaway Four: The Wildcard Remains Overpriced at This Stage

Despite having the transfers available, Harry's reluctance to play the wildcard reflects a broader truth about the chip: it represents maximum optionality but also maximum investment. With only seven weeks remaining, using the wildcard to make speculative changes doesn't provide enough time to benefit from the full effect of those changes. The free hit in Gameweek 33 or 34—when blank gameweeks create genuine squad gaps—remains more valuable than an early wildcard that might simply shuffle rank without substantially improving final position.

Takeaway Five: Differentiation Through Retention, Not Just Selection

Harry's retention of Haaland while others seemingly pivoted toward Gordon represents his acknowledgment that maintaining differential assets—even somewhat unfashionable ones—provides a unique rank protection. If Haaland hauls, his relative ownership means Harry gains ground on those who sold; if Haaland blanks, he's lost no more ground than anyone else who maintained the popular premium option. This approach recognizes that ownership dynamics matter as much as selection本身的 quality in determining final rank.
