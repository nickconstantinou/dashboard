95% of New ETH Wallets Are Scams

Introduction

The cryptocurrency market's recent bull run told a tale of two narratives. While Bitcoin dominated headlines and captured the spotlight, altcoins—including Ethereum, the second-largest cryptocurrency by market capitalization—languished in relative obscurity. ETH barely surpassed its all-time high from the 2021 cycle, leaving many investors wondering whether the promise of Ethereum's ecosystem had finally run its course. Now, with the market entering a pronounced bear phase, questions about Ethereum's fundamental strengths and future trajectory have grown increasingly urgent.

Enter Kulpa Research, an investigative research firm that recently published a deeply bearish report titled "Ethereum ETHUSD, What Vitalik Knows and Tom Lee Doesn't." The report challenges the prevailing bullish sentiment surrounding Ethereum's post-Fusaka future—particularly the optimistic projections from Bitwise (the world's largest Ethereum treasury company) and its chairman Tom Lee, who once predicted ETH could reach $15,000 by the end of 2025 before revising that target downward to $9,000. But the Kulpa report argues that Lee's optimism is fundamentally misplaced, asserting that the metrics he cites as evidence of Ethereum's strength are actually symptoms of a deeply troubling reality: the network has become overrun with scam transactions. The report's central claim—that 95% of new Ethereum wallet activity since the Fusaka upgrade consists of dusting and address poisoning attacks—has sent shockwaves through the crypto community and raises serious questions about whether Ethereum's signature upgrade may have fundamentally broken more than it fixed.

Ethereum's Core Use Cases and the Fusaka Upgrade

To understand the Kulpa report's significance, one must first grasp what Ethereum actually does today. According to the researchers, Ethereum now serves two primary functions: acting as the settlement layer for stablecoins like USDT and USDC, and hosting tokenized real-world assets (RWAs). These use cases represent a narrowing of Ethereum's original ambitious vision, which once promised to be a world computer capable of supporting decentralized applications at scale.

The Fusaka upgrade, implemented in December 2025, was marketed as the solution to Ethereum's long-standing scalability problems. The upgrade doubled the network's gas limit from 30 million to 60 million units, theoretically enabling the network to process significantly more transactions. Vitalik Buterin himself declared in January that Fusaka had solved Ethereum's "trilemma"—the supposed impossibility of achieving decentralization, security, and scalability simultaneously. This was presented as a triumph, and transaction counts did indeed skyrocket following the upgrade, a pattern that typically doesn't occur when other major cryptocurrencies like Bitcoin or BNB undergo upgrades.

However, the Kulpa report contends that this surge in activity is not the positive development it appears to be. The researchers argue that Tom Lee, who has been among Ethereum's most vocal bulls, is either ignorant of or deliberately ignoring the fact that the bullish metrics he cites are actually being driven by bearish factors—specifically, a massive increase in scam activity that has transformed Ethereum into a haven for fraud.

The Address Poisoning Epidemic

At the heart of the Kulpa report is a deep dive into what are called "poisoning transactions" or "address poisoning attacks." This particular scam mechanism works by having attackers send victims tiny dust transactions from wallet addresses that closely resemble addresses the victim has previously interacted with. The fraudulent address appears in the victim's transaction history, making it easy for users to mistakenly copy it when initiating future transfers. While the odds of any individual falling for this scam might seem low, the economics make it massively profitable for attackers. Because dust transactions are extremely cheap to send, scammers can target millions of crypto wallets and turn a profit even if only a small fraction of victims send funds to the fake addresses.

The report reveals that before Fusaka, attackers focused primarily on alternative blockchains like BNB Smart Chain, where transaction costs are much lower—sometimes as little as one cent per transaction. Between 2022 and 2024, there were 252 million poison transactions on BNB compared to just 17 million on Ethereum. The cost differential made Ethereum a less attractive target. But Fusaka changed everything. By reducing transaction fees by approximately 90%, the upgrade made address poisoning attacks on Ethereum economically viable. Each poisoning attempt now costs roughly three cents per address—higher than BNB, but dramatically lower than Ethereum's pre-Fusaka fees.

The scale of this problem is staggering. The report's analysis found that since Fusaka, 95% of the increase in active wallets on Ethereum and its major stablecoins were created explicitly for dusting or address poisoning attacks. Dust transactions now comprise an estimated 18% of all Ethereum transactions, and this figure is rising rapidly. Before Fusaka in November, there were just 4.4 million poison transactions, representing 9.5% of all transactions. By December, this rose to 6.3 million (12% of the total). After Fusaka's deployment in January, poison transactions jumped to 13 million—over 18% of all Ethereum transactions. By February, they reached nearly 14 million, more than 22% of the total.

The researchers put this into concrete perspective by conducting their own experiment. They created two new wallet addresses and sent $500 from one to the other. Within just five minutes, both wallets were hit by poisoning attempts with transactions from three separate wallets that shared the same first and last characters as the new addresses. This is particularly alarming because wallet addresses are randomly generated and the attacking wallets had no prior transaction history—in other words, attackers using automated bots identified the Ethereum transaction, generated new wallet addresses until one closely matched the target, and sent a scam transaction all within minutes.

The scale of losses is equally troubling. Research from Carnegie Mellon University found that more than 270 million poisoning attacks occurred between July 2022 and June 2024 across both Ethereum and BNB Smart Chain. Over 6,600 transfers were made to fake addresses, earning attackers more than $83 million in illicit gains. But in just two months between December 2025 and January 2026—after Fusaka was deployed—more than $50 million had been stolen in the same way. At the time of the report's publication, the researchers estimated that at least $348 million in crypto had been siphoned through poison address attacks, and these attacks show no signs of slowing down.

Vitalik's Selling and the Tokenomics Crisis

The report draws a direct connection between Ethereum's problems and the behavior of its co-founder Vitalik Buterin, who has been aggressively selling ETH. In late January, Vitalik announced he would sell over 16,000 ETH, declaring that "the Ethereum Foundation is entering a period of mild austerity." The following month, he dumped over 19,000 ETH more—at today's prices, that's tens of millions of dollars worth. While Vitalik framed the austerity measures as necessary to speed up Ethereum's development and sustain the Foundation long-term, the Kulpa report suggests a more ominous interpretation: that Vitalik's selling could signal he believes Fusaka may have broken Ethereum's tokenomics.

This gets to the heart of Ethereum's economic model. Because transaction fees have plunged so dramatically on Ethereum following Fusaka, the fee burn mechanism—introduced under EIP1559 in 2021—has also weakened significantly. EIP1559 changed how gas fees are structured and how validators are rewarded, introducing a base transaction fee designed to reduce congestion. But the report argues that the elasticity estimates underlying this mechanism "appear to have never been updated." More critically, Vitalik's original formula for predicting how gas limit increases would impact fees was developed in 2018, when Ethereum's ecosystem looked radically different. Today, Ethereum is largely driven by Layer 2 solutions like Arbitrum, Optimism, and Base, many of which didn't exist until years later. These solutions weren't factored into the original calculations.

The result, according to the report, is that fees didn't fall by the predicted 10-30%—they collapsed by over 90%. This has profound implications for Ethereum's tokenomics. As more blocks fill with tiny dust transactions—essentially spam—larger, legitimate transactions must compete for space, potentially driving away users and further undermining the network's value proposition. The authors argue that Fusaka didn't just open the door to the problems discussed above; it may have fundamentally broken ETH's economic model entirely.

Detailed Takeaways

The 95% Statistic Reveals a Network in Crisis: The report's finding that 95% of new Ethereum wallet activity since Fusaka consists of scam-related transactions is perhaps the most damning statistic in the entire analysis. This isn't a minor inconvenience or a rounding error—it's a fundamental corruption of the network's activity metrics. Anyone evaluating Ethereum based on raw transaction counts or active wallet numbers is essentially measuring scam volume rather than genuine adoption.

Fusaka's Fee Reduction Backfired Dramatically: Rather than creating the healthy fee reduction of 10-30% that Vitalik and the "Pump the Gas" collective predicted, Fusaka caused fees to collapse by over 90%. This wasn't a minor miscalculation—it was a fundamental failure to account for how the Ethereum ecosystem has evolved, particularly the rise of Layer 2 solutions that weren't part of the original 2018 calculations. The lesson here is that blockchain upgrades can have unintended consequences that ripple through the entire system in unexpected ways.

Address Poisoning Attacks Are Exploding Post-Fusaka: The economic incentives created by lower fees have transformed Ethereum into a prime target for address poisoning attacks. The researchers' own experiment—watching their newly created wallets get attacked within five minutes—demonstrates how automated and pervasive these attacks have become. With over $348 million already stolen through these methods and losses accelerating, this represents a serious and growing threat to any Ethereum user.

Tom Lee's Bullish Thesis May Be Built on Faulty Data: The report explicitly accuses Tom Lee of either ignorance or deliberate blindness in citing Ethereum's transaction and address growth as evidence of strength. If the Kulpa researchers are correct, then Bitwise's investment thesis—built on the premise of growing institutional adoption reflected in on-chain metrics—is fundamentally flawed because those metrics are dominated by malicious activity rather than legitimate use.

Vitalik's Selling Should Not Be Ignored: When the co-founder and public face of a cryptocurrency begins selling substantial portions of his holdings while describing a period of "austerity," investors should pay attention. The report suggests this could signal that even Vitalik recognizes the severity of Ethereum's current trajectory, though the researchers acknowledge this interpretation is speculative.

Who This Is For

This content is essential reading for anyone with a stake in the Ethereum ecosystem—whether as an investor, developer, or user. Cryptocurrency traders and investors considering ETH positions will find the report's bearish thesis essential for counterbalancing the prevailing bullish narratives from major firms like Bitwise. The analysis provides a framework for understanding why raw transaction numbers and wallet growth statistics can be deeply misleading indicators of network health.

Additionally, anyone responsible for securing cryptocurrency holdings should take note of the address poisoning data. The Carnegie Mellon research cited in the report documents hundreds of millions of attack attempts and hundreds of millions of dollars in losses—the scale is too large to ignore. Understanding how these attacks work and how quickly they can target new wallets is critical for anyone transacting in ETH or using Ethereum-based applications.

Finally, the report serves as a sobering case study in the unintended consequences of blockchain upgrades. For developers, researchers, and anyone interested in cryptocurrency governance, the Fusaka saga demonstrates how even well-intentioned changes to network parameters can create cascading effects that undermine the very problems they intended to solve.
