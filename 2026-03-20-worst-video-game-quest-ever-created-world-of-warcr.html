Worst video game quest ever created: World of Warcraft's Green Hills of Stranglethorn | Jeff Kaplan

In the pantheon of World of Warcraft's most memorable moments, few achievements carry quite the same notoriety as the Green Hills of Stranglethorn quest. This infamous piece of content, designed by Jeff Kaplan during his early days at Blizzard Entertainment, has become legendary among gamers as one of the most aggravating and poorly conceived quests in MMO history. Yet as Kaplan himself openly admits in this candid discussion about his design philosophy, this notorious quest actually represents a crucial turning point in his career—and in Blizzard's approach to iterative game design.

The conversation reveals a remarkably self-aware look at game design from one of the industry's most influential figures. Kaplan, who would later go on to serve as the game director for Overwatch and eventually lead the entire Blizzard Entertainment game development effort, demonstrates the kind of honest introspection that separates good designers from great ones. Rather than hiding his failures, he uses this quest as a teaching moment about the fundamental differences between designing for your own enjoyment versus designing for player satisfaction. His willingness to dissect his own missteps provides invaluable insight into how game designers should think about creating engaging experiences, particularly in massively multiplayer environments where player behavior becomes impossible to predict or control.

The Genesis of a Notorious Quest

The story behind Green Hills of Stranglethorn reveals much about the mentality of junior game designers, and Kaplan is remarkably forthright about his own hubris during this period. He wrote the quest during what he describes as a winter break when most of the office was empty, and he was simply thrilled to be at Blizzard, working late nights on content that would eventually reach millions of players. The entire concept originated from his love of writing—a rare moment when he actually contributed a short story directly into the game world.

The quest's narrative framework was built around a book called "Green Hills of Stranglethorn," written by a quest giver named Hemet Nestingwary. Kaplan openly acknowledges that this name is simply an anagram of Ernest Hemingway, one of his literary influences. There's another quest giver in the area whose name is similarly scrambled, representing what Kaplan describes as the typical arrogance of a junior designer who believes they're being clever but is actually just being self-indulgent. The entire premise involved the book's pages being scattered across the Stranglethorn Vale zone, with players needing to collect all of them to complete the quest and read the full story.

What makes this quest particularly interesting from a design perspective is that it represented Kaplan's attempt to create what he terms "ant farm designer" thinking. This approach treats players as subjects in an experiment where the designer plays God, watching to see what they'll do and how they'll react. Kaplan readily admits this was the wrong approach to multiplayer game design, though he notes he hadn't learned this lesson yet at the time. The fundamental flaw in this philosophy is that it prioritizes the designer's curiosity and entertainment value over the actual experience of the people playing the game.

The Three Types of Fun: A Framework for Understanding Design Failure

To explain where his thinking went wrong with this quest, Kaplan invokes a famous framework from legendary game designer Sid Meyer that categorizes fun into three distinct types. The first is fun for the player, which should always be the primary concern of any game designer. The second is fun for the designer, which can be tempting to prioritize but ultimately leads to problematic design decisions. The third is fun for the computer—a more abstract concept referring to the emergent behaviors and calculations that happen within the game system itself.

In the case of Green Hills of Stranglethorn, Kaplan recognizes that he was designing primarily for his own fun rather than considering what would be enjoyable for the player. He wanted to see how players would react to the scarcity mechanic, imagining that they would band together and help each other complete their collections. His fantasy involved players in Stranglethorn zone chat exchanging messages like "Hey, I'm looking for a page six, anyone got a page three?"—creating organic social interactions and community building around the shared frustration.

This reveals a common pitfall for game designers: confusing the intellectual interest of a mechanic with its actual playability. The concept of collecting scattered pages from a book sounds intriguing on paper, and the social element of trading duplicates with other players seemed like it could create meaningful interactions. However, the execution fell apart in several critical ways that Kaplan only recognized in hindsight.

The Technical and Design Failures That Made the Quest Infamous

The fundamental problems with Green Hills of Stranglethorn extended far beyond its conceptual flaws. According to Kaplan, the interface of World of Warcraft at that time simply wasn't equipped to handle this type of collection quest. The pages didn't stack in players' inventories, meaning each individual page took up a precious slot in already-limited bag space. As players fought through StranglethornVale fighting creatures that could drop any of the pages, their inventories became cluttered with duplicates they didn't need.

The randomization was brutally unforgiving. Some players might get multiple copies of the same page numbers while missing others entirely, creating an experience that felt less like a challenging quest and more like a lottery. Kaplan describes this as similar to the McDonald's Monopoly game where you need all the pieces to win, but without any of the compensatory rewards or excitement. Players ended up with pages they didn't need taking up space they desperately needed for actual gameplay items, creating genuine frustration rather than the social bonding Kaplan had envisioned.

The quest became so notorious that rather than forming communities and helping each other, players simply stopped doing it altogether. The frustration overwhelmed any potential reward, and many players opted to simply ignore the quest rather than deal with the inventory management nightmare it created. The auction house became flooded with the unwanted pages as players tried to clear bag space, demonstrating just how completely the design had failed to achieve its intended goals.

Learning From Failure: The Cultural Impact of Honest Self-Criticism

Perhaps the most valuable aspect of Kaplan's discussion is what this failure taught him about the importance of designing for players rather than for himself. He describes the experience as a crucial learning moment that shaped his subsequent approach to game design, particularly in multiplayer environments where player agency and enjoyment must be the primary considerations.

What makes this story particularly significant is how openly Kaplan has discussed this failure throughout his career. He notes that because he was very open about the fact that he didn't think the quest was good and that it had failed in its objectives, it opened the door for Blizzard as a company to be more critical of their own work. By being the first to admit failure, he created space for others to do the same without fear of judgment or punishment.

This culture of honest self-assessment became valuable internally at Blizzard. Kaplan describes how it's always easier to be critical of work when someone else has already gone first and said "Hey guys, I think I made one of the shittiest quests in the game and here's why." This vulnerability created permission for the entire design team to strive for better versions of their content, to iterate more aggressively, and to prioritize player experience over clever but ultimately frustrating mechanics.

The lesson here extends far beyond World of Warcraft development. Any creative professional can learn from Kaplan's example—the willingness to acknowledge failure, to analyze what went wrong, and to use that knowledge to improve future work. The most successful designers aren't those who never make mistakes, but rather those who learn from them most effectively and share those lessons with others.

Key Insights for Aspiring Game Designers

This discussion offers several crucial takeaways for anyone interested in game design or creative work in general. First and foremost is the importance of distinguishing between designer fun and player fun. Just because a mechanic is interesting to implement or creates fascinating emergent behavior doesn't mean it will be enjoyable for the people actually playing the game. The Green Hills of Stranglethorn quest seemed clever in concept but was miserable in practice.

The second major insight concerns the danger of treating players as subjects in an experiment rather than as customers whose experience matters. The ant farm approach of wanting to see what players will do fails to recognize that players are spending their time and money on entertainment, not participating in a designer's personal research project. Their satisfaction must always come first.

Third, technical constraints and interface considerations are just as important as conceptual design. Even brilliant ideas can fail if the infrastructure to support them doesn't exist or creates its own set of problems. The fact that pages couldn't stack in World of Warcraft's inventory system transformed what might have been a tolerable collection quest into an inventory management nightmare.

Finally, there's immense value in honest self-criticism and transparency about failures. Kaplan's willingness to discuss this quest openly throughout his career has made it a valuable teaching tool for the entire industry. By admitting his mistakes, he gained credibility and helped create a culture where others could do the same—ultimately leading to better games as a result.

This discussion is essential viewing for anyone interested in game design, particularly those working in multiplayer or online environments. The insights about interface constraints, player psychology, and the importance of iteration apply broadly across many types of games and interactive experiences.

Professionals in other creative industries can also benefit from Kaplan's approach to failure and self-criticism. His example demonstrates how admitting mistakes and analyzing them honestly can actually build credibility rather than diminish it, and how this kind of transparency can improve not just individual work but entire organizational cultures.

The behind-the-scenes look at how such quests came to be, and what the designers were thinking at the time, provides unique insight into the development process for one of the most influential games ever made.
