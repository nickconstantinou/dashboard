# The Greatest Real Estate Scam in History: How Greenland Got Its Misleading Name

## A Conversation Between History Expert Lars Brownworth and Lex Fridman

In the vast landscape of historical place names, few are as thoroughly misleading as Greenland. This conversation between podcast host Lex Fridman and historian Lars Brownworth of the "12 Byzantine Rulers" podcast reveals the fascinating origin story behind one of the world's most famous naming controversies—and what it tells us about the desperation, ambition, and marketing tactics of Viking-era Scandinavia.

The discussion emerges from Fridman's ongoing interest in historical narratives and the stories we tell ourselves about exploration and settlement. Brownworth, known for making Byzantine history accessible and engaging, brings his expertise to bear on this particular episode of Norse history, examining how marketing and propaganda shaped one of the most enduring geographical names in the Western world.

## The Marketing Genius of Erik the Red

The conversation centers on Erik Thorvaldsson, better known as Erik the Red, the Norwegian-born explorer who would eventually give Greenland its deceptive name. Brownworth explains that Erik the Red made his reputation through extensive exploration, and when he encountered the massive island that would become Greenland, he understood immediately that the key to attracting settlers would be presentation.

"You have to understand the context," Brownworth tells Fridman. "In medieval Europe, and particularly in Iceland where Erik had spent much of his life, land was the ultimate measure of wealth and status. There was no other commodity that held such intrinsic value." This scarcity mindset shaped everything about how Erik approached recruitment for his proposed settlement.

When Erik described the island he had found, he painted a picture that Brownworth characterizes as "the greatest real estate scam in history." According to Brownworth, Erik claimed that the rivers and fjords teemed with salmon so abundant that settlers could simply scoop them out with their hands—no actual fishing required. This image of effortless abundance would have been irresistible to land-hungry peasants and farmers who had watched their children inherit increasingly fragmented plots back in Iceland.

Fridman's reaction is immediate: "Was this real? It's a lie. Okay, not true at all." Brownworth confirms that Erik was engaging in what amounts to ancient propaganda, deliberately constructing an enticing narrative to attract followers. The salmon claim, Brownworth notes, was completely false—there were no such rivers with such effortlessly harvestable fish. What Erik was selling was a fantasy designed to appeal to the desperate and the ambitious alike.

## Land Hunger and the Crowded Iceland of the 10th Century

The conversation delves into why such a transparent marketing ploy would work at all. Brownworth explains that Iceland in Erik's time had reached a critical saturation point. "There's no more room in Iceland," he says. "It's too restrictive. We're going to go further west." The island had become densely populated by Scandinavian standards, and the best arable land was already claimed. Younger sons and families without inheritances faced limited prospects.

"Even in Iceland of the time, the dream was to have land," Brownworth tells Fridman. "I mean, land equaled wealth in Europe, and Iceland was particularly acute in this regard." The combination of population pressure and the cultural emphasis on land ownership created a pool of potential settlers who were highly motivated to take risks for the promise of new territory.

Erik understood this desperation intimately. He had himself been exiled from Iceland for a period after being convicted of manslaughter—a common occurrence in the violent culture of Norse settlement. During his exile, he explored the western lands that would become Greenland, and when his exile ended, he saw an opportunity. Rather than returning to cramped Iceland, he could establish himself as a leader of a new colonial venture, recruiting followers from among those who shared his hunger for land and opportunity.

The plan was audacious: Erik would take people from Iceland, transport them across the treacherous North Atlantic, and establish a colony on an island that, despite its name, offered harsh living conditions and limited agricultural potential.

## The Voyage and Its Casualties

The scale of Erik's recruitment was impressive. According to Brownworth, Erik managed to gather approximately 500 people willing to leave Iceland for this uncertain venture. This represented "a significant chunk of the population" of the time, demonstrating both Erik's persuasive abilities and the desperation of those who agreed to follow him.

Erik organized a fleet of 25 ships for the crossing. For modern audiences accustomed to reliable navigation and modern vessels, this might seem like an impressive armada. But Brownworth emphasizes the dangers involved: "You're sailing without a compass. You just kind of hoping." Navigation in the Viking age relied on a combination of techniques—sun compass, stars, birds, and intuition—but crossing thousands of miles of open ocean remained fundamentally perilous.

The results reflected this danger: only 14 of the original 25 ships completed the journey. While Brownworth describes this as "pretty good" under the circumstances, the loss rate was substantial. Those 14 ships carried approximately 300 people, who would go on to establish what became known as the Western Settlement on Greenland's southwestern coast.

The timing of the voyage was critical. These Norse settlers were farmers and herders, and their entire economy depended on livestock. But the first winter proved catastrophic: "All their cattle die." For a people who practiced husbandry as their primary subsistence strategy, the loss of their animals represented a fundamental crisis. Without cattle to provide milk, cheese, and meat, and without hay stored to sustain them through winter, the colonists faced starvation.

## Survival Against the Odds

Despite these devastating losses, the colony survived—and Brownworth makes clear this was far from guaranteed. The survival depended entirely on resupply from Norway, which was "2,000 plus miles away." The logistics of maintaining contact across such a vast distance, with only sail-powered ships and no modern communication, were extraordinary.

The return voyage was as dangerous as the outbound journey. "The chances of making it to Norway and back are actually not that great," Brownworth notes, again emphasizing the compass problem—these navigators were essentially guessing their way across enormous distances of open ocean. Yet somehow, the Greenland colony managed to maintain this tenuous connection to the wider Norse world for centuries.

The colony that began with such hardship endured for hundreds of years, surviving through the medieval period until roughly the 1400s. This longevity speaks to both the colonists' resilience and the value Erik's marketing had created—they had a name, Greenland, that suggested fertility and opportunity, and they clung to it and their settlement despite overwhelming evidence that the name was a lie.

## The Irony of Names

The conversation concludes with Brownworth and Fridman marveling at the absurdity of the naming. "It should be called Iceland," Brownworth jokes, noting that the actual island of Iceland is far greener and more hospitable than the ice-covered territory Erik claimed for his colony. "Glacier land or something," he suggests with evident amusement.

Yet the name stuck. As Fridman notes, "It just stuck to this day. Yeah, it's the most misnamed place in the world." Despite centuries of knowledge that Greenland is largely covered in ice and offers relatively little agricultural land, the name persists—a permanent monument to one of history's most successful marketing campaigns.

## Key Takeaways

**The power of narrative often outweighs reality in shaping human behavior.** Erik the Red understood that people's perception of a place matters more than its actual characteristics when it comes to settlement decisions. By creating an enticing story, he convinced hundreds to risk their lives crossing the Atlantic. Modern applications: This principle extends to real estate marketing, startup pitches, and political campaigns, where compelling narratives often triumph over inconvenient facts.

**Desperation creates opportunity for those willing to exploit it.** Erik the Red's recruitment succeeded because he targeted people with limited options. The crowded, land-scarce conditions in Iceland created a population willing to accept extraordinary risks for the promise of land. Modern applications: Understanding what populations are locked into limited choices can reveal opportunities for those with resources and creativity to offer alternatives.

**Persistence and resupply can overcome initial failures.** The Greenland colony nearly collapsed in its first winter, but the Norse managed to maintain supply lines across 2,000 miles of open ocean for centuries. Modern applications: Building resilient supply chains and maintaining connections to resource centers matters more than having ideal initial conditions. Many successful enterprises have survived near-death experiences through stubborn maintenance of essential connections.

**Names and branding have enduring power beyond their accuracy.** Greenland has been known for what it actually is—mostly frozen, largely uninhabitable—for over a thousand years, yet the deceptive name persists. Modern applications: Branding decisions made early can outlast any subsequent evidence of their inaccuracy. Understanding this can inform both how we evaluate others' marketing and how we approach our own naming decisions.
