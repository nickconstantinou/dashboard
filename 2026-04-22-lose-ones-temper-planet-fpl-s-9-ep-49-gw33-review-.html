Planet FPL Gameweek 33 Review: The Wild Card Dilemma and Double Gameweek Strategy Breakdown

Introduction

The Fantasy Premier League landscape has reached a critical inflection point as Gameweek 33 unfolds, marking the midpoint of what hosts Serge and James describe as "the biggest double game week of the season." The episode, recorded on a Monday morning as early results trickled in, captures the tension and strategic deliberation that defines the FPL community's experience during these pivotal gameweeks. With approximately 12 players still to blank for many managers, and Erling Haaland's match yet to be played, the episode opens with palpable uncertainty about how the gameweek will ultimately resolve.

The discussion centers on the clash between two dominant strategies this week: James's decision to play his Wildcard, versus the more popular Bench Boost approach that much of the engaged FPL community appears to have utilized. According to data cited from Live FPL, an astonishing 2.7 million chips were played across the community this gameweek—a figure James quips "tells you how many dead teams there are." The hosts acknowledge that while Bench Boost was "by far and away the most popular" chip at around 800,000 activations, there remained "quite a spread across wild cards and free hits," reflecting the diverse approaches managers took to navigate this complex fixture situation. Serge notes that ordinarily, hitting a double gameweek with everyone on Bench Boost would feel universal, but "doesn't quite feel that way" this time around, suggesting increased strategic sophistication within the community.

The Wild Card vs. Bench Boost Showdown

The episode delivers one of its most compelling segments when Serge reveals actual performance data comparing the two strategies. Teams in the top 10K who played Bench Boost are sitting at approximately 69 points, while Wildcard teams languish at 53—a 16-point differential. James immediately interjects with characteristic humor: "Stop laughing at the back. Whereas wild card is on 53. That's 16 point difference... that's a bench roughly." The jest captures the frustrating reality for those on Wildcard: they are essentially competing with opponents who have activated six effective extra players on average, all of whom have blanked for their teams. James, despite being on Wildcard and "obviously on a red arrow," remains philosophical, observing that "it's not gone too badly so far" and that "the small decisions I made have kind of gone in my favor."

The reasoning behind James's Wildcard choice reveals the calculated thinking that separates strategic managers from reactive ones. Rather than simply chasing the double gameweek points, he considered the broader fixture calendar and future planning. Serge highlights this by noting James has "treble City for me was O'Reilly, Son, Haaland" and explains that despite having Danny Welbeck in his Wildcard squad, James "couldn't get Calvin Leuen," illustrating how budget constraints and player ownership create ripple effects across one's squad construction. The hosts then pivot to discussing future rotation risks, with Serge warning that "we may well be seeing some Leeds rotation on Wednesday night"—a heads-up about potential changes to the playing XI that could impact managers' plans.

James's Squad Composition and Decision-Making

The episode provides a fascinating window into James's specific team construction and the thought process behind each transfer decision. His Wildcard team features Cole Palmer, Son Heung-min, and Erling Haaland in his forward line, with Darwin Núñez, Vaníček, and Calvert-Lewin rounding out his attacking options. On defense, he doubled up on Chelsea with Palmer and João Pedro, while also doubling Bournemouth with Hill and Tavernier. He carried Bruno Fernandes despite acknowledging him as "the biggest loser in my book at the moment," reasoning that if João Pedro doesn't show up for the Brighton matchup, he would still capture Bruno's points through the blank gameweek.

The Harry Wilson situation illustrates James's long-term planning philosophy. He explains multiple reasons for keeping the Fulham midfielder: "a big value in him that would that's impacted by the next transfer that I could end up making in gameweek 35 and also impacts what I might be able to squeeze out in gameweek 34 on a free hit as well because I bought him at 5.2 and have no problem keeping him and playing him for gameweek 34." Beyond the numerical considerations, James reveals he "really like[s] the last three fixtures," which he believes will save him a transfer further down the line—demonstrating how fixture analysis extends beyond immediate gameweeks to the entire remaining schedule.

James also carried Dean Henderson as his only Crystal Palace representative, explaining his reasoning: "the only player at this moment I've got any confidence might play two games for Crystal Palace as and when that double game week happens, still most likely gameweek 36." This represents classic FPL logic—holding a player through a single-gameweek blank in anticipation of future double gameweek returns.

The Bench Boost Internal Debate

One of the episode's most revealing moments comes when Serge discusses his internal struggle about whether to actually bench boost given his bench composition at the time of decision. "I was really contemplating not bench boosting on Saturday morning," he admits, "I was looking at it, looking at my bench thinking my bench at the time was Darlow, Bruno Fernandes, Jared Bowen and Gabriel." He then explains why he couldn't bring himself to sell any of these assets: "Gabrielle definitely I think is going to go on a bit of a run of clean sheets between now and the end of the season. Bruno Fernandes we know. And Jared Bowen I think is going to do all right. Like I didn't really want to get Xiao Pedro um in that game week."

Serge's dilemma crystallizes the fundamental tension in FPL strategy. When you have three single-gameweek players on your bench with fixtures like "Chelsea away, Man City away, and Crystal Palace away," the logic of bench boosting collapses—you cannot improve your starting XI by replacing those difficult fixtures with bench players who face equally challenging or worse matchups. He describes his decision process: "if I'm going to bench boost James with three single gameweek players, then there's got to be a week where I've got three single gameweek players that have better fixtures than Chelsea away, Man City away, and Crystal Palace away. It just doesn't make sense that those are the three fixtures I'm bench boosting into."

Despite these reservations, Serge ultimately decided to "bit the bullet" and activate the bench boost, selling Jared Bowen for João Pedro just before the gameweek commenced. He acknowledged he "didn't know anything about João Pedro" at the time, making the transfer essentially a gamble on the Chelsea midfielder's involvement. The decision paid off modestly as of recording, with Serge sitting at 67 points—a position he describes as "swimming" given his Wildcard disadvantage.

The gameweek's early returns provide both validation and frustration for the hosts. For Serge, every player in his starting XI delivered except Cole Palmer, with the bench contributions providing crucial supplementary points. "My only blanker of the 10 players who's actually showed up so far this week is KL Palmer," Serge notes, before listing the returns: "Six for Darlow, Van Heck, Brazil, seven for O'Reilly, four for Hill with a defcon... Marcus Son seven, Cal Lewin six, Haaland six times two." The double-digit haul from Haaland (six points doubled by captaincy equals twelve) represents the cornerstone of his gameweek, while the 2-3 points from bench players like Darren Barker completing the picture.

For James, the results mirrored much of what Serge experienced, with Botany, Vaníček, Riley Hill, Tavernier, Haaland, Bruno Fernandes, and Arnarson all contributing points through the game's first half. However, James expresses particular frustration about Danny Welbeck and Vermeulen blanking while he remains uncertain about João Pedro's status. He notes that Welbeck "probably should have been minus two" points based on performance, explaining that the Brighton forward appears likely to lose his starting spot: "Rash obviously came off the bench and scored at the weekend and could easily start ahead of him."

Strategic Takeaways for the Remainder of the Season

The Fixture-Dependent Nature of Bench Boost

The episode makes clear that bench boost is not automatically optimal even in double gameweeks. Serge's internal debate reveals that the chip's value depends entirely on having three bench players who genuinely improve one's lineup. When those bench assets face challenging fixtures themselves—like Chelsea away or Manchester City away—the marginal gain from bench boost diminishes substantially. Managers must evaluate not just whether their starters have double fixtures, but whether their bench provides meaningful upgrade potential.

Long-Term Value Trumps Short-Term Gains

James's Harry Wilson decision demonstrates how budget players at specific price points create future optionality. By keeping a player purchased at 5.2, James preserves both his ability to free hit in gameweek 34 without sacrificing depth and his flexibility for gameweek 35 transfers when the fixture list becomes clearer. This represents sophisticated squad construction that accounts for the unpredictable nature of team selections and injuries throughout the season's final stretch.

Rotation Risk Requires Constant Monitoring

The discussion of Leeds City's potential midweek rotation serves as a reminder that double gameweeks create additional complexity beyond simply having doubled fixtures. Players returning from injury (like Arnarson who "only just came back the previous game") face uncertainty about their minutes, while established players may be rested ahead of important upcoming fixtures. Managers must balance the potential of double-gameweek points against the real possibility of rotation, which could leave them with unexpectedly low returns despite having the "right" players.

The Psychological Challenge of Red Arrows

Perhaps the most human moment in the episode comes when James acknowledges sitting on a Wildcard with an inevitable red arrow, knowing he competes "against probably effective six extra players probably on average." This demonstrates that even experienced FPL managers face psychological challenges when their strategy produces worse short-term results than alternatives. The key insight is understanding that FPL is a marathon, not a sprint—and a Wildcard now may position James more advantageously for the remaining gameweeks than if he had simply followed the crowd with Bench Boost.

Chip Sequencing Creates Compound Effects

The episode subtly emphasizes how chip decisions create lasting impacts on squad construction. Serge's decision to carry Dean Henderson through a blank while awaiting a Crystal Palace double in gameweek 36 illustrates the patience required to maximize chip value. Similarly, James's Wildcard usage now affects his ability to strategically deploy his free hit in gameweek 34, creating a ripple effect that extends well beyond the immediate gameweek.
