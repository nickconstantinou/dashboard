How to Prepare for Any Negotiation: FBI Negotiator Chris Voss on the Science of Getting What You Want

The Hidden Framework Behind Every Successful Negotiation

When former FBI hostage negotiator Chris Voss speaks about negotiation, he speaks from a place most of us will never experience: the highest-stakes conversations imaginable, where lives hang in the balance of every word chosen. Yet in this candid conversation, Voss makes clear that the principles he learned in crisis situations translate directly—and perhaps even more effectively—into the everyday negotiations we face from the boardroom to the living room.

The core insight Voss shares is deceptively simple: the way most people prepare for negotiations is fundamentally backwards. We spend countless hours rehearsing our own arguments, researching the other party's weaknesses, and calculating our walk-away position. We arrive at the table desperate to be heard, to make our case, to ensure our perspective is understood. But according to Voss, this approach—however intuitive it feels—puts the negotiation cart before the horse in a way that undermines our effectiveness before we even open our mouths.

What Voss advocates instead is a counterintuitive preparation strategy that focuses not on what you want to say, but on what the other person is already thinking about you. This shift in preparation perspective, he argues, is the fastest path to building the trust necessary for any successful negotiation.

The Denial Strategy: Preempting What They Already Think

Before walking into any negotiation—whether a business deal, a salary discussion, or a conversation with a car salesman—Voss suggests asking yourself a single powerful question: "What would I want to deny walking in?" He emphasizes that "everybody could do this right away, even if it's a first interaction."

The reasoning behind this approach rests on a fundamental understanding of human psychology. The human brain, Voss explains, is "75% negative." This means that when you enter a negotiation, the other party is already harboring reservations about you. They may expect you to be greedy, to act like a "slimy salesperson," to waste their time, or to try to maximize your own advantage at their expense. These negative expectations exist before you've said a single word.

Rather than ignoring these perceptions or waiting for them to surface defensively, Voss recommends making what you want to deny into a straight observation. You might say, "I'm probably going to seem greedy" or "I'm probably just going to seem like another slimy salesperson" or "I'm probably going to seem like somebody that's wasting your time." According to Voss, this simple rhetorical move "instantly makes me the straight shooter, the honest person."

The genius of this approach lies in its mechanism: by articulating the negative perception yourself, you defuse it. You've acknowledged the elephant in the room, taken ownership of the concern, and signaled that you're self-aware enough to recognize how you might be perceived. This makes you appear trustworthy precisely because you've done the thing no one expects—you've handed your adversary their ammunition and shown you're not afraid of it.

Building Tactical Empathy: The Two-Component Formula

Once you've addressed the negatives the other party might harbor, Voss describes the second component of what he calls "tactical empathy." This involves recognizing and articulating what the other person will be offended if you don't appreciate about them.

"It's taking what a lot of people would refer to as flattery," Voss explains, "but then articulating that it's not vacuous flattery. It's genuine appreciation from where you're coming from."

He offers a sample script that illustrates this principle: "You worked very hard to get to where you are today. You have been in this business for a very long time and you know what you're doing. Otherwise, if you didn't, you wouldn't be sitting here in front of me at all, and you have things that are valuable and worthy of my attention and of my respect."

The key distinction here is between hollow flattery and genuine recognition of someone's legitimate accomplishments and expertise. Voss notes that the simple fact of someone being in the negotiation at all means "you have value and that you have legitimate reasons in your own right to be respected and admired." By articulating this recognition explicitly, you signal that you see them as a full human being rather than simply an obstacle to overcome.

When you combine the denial strategy with this genuine appreciation, Voss says you "kind of got empathy. That's what you were calling tactical empathy." The order matters: you start with the negatives, then move to the positives. Neuroscience, Voss notes, backs up this sequencing approach.

The Neuroscience of Being Understood

The interviewer in this conversation pushes Voss to explain why feeling understood is so crucial to human psychology—calling it "the most important thing" in negotiation. Voss's answer reveals the biological machinery underlying effective negotiation.

When you make someone feel genuinely heard, you trigger the release of two key neurochemicals. First, oxytocin: "If you get a hit of oxytocin in interacting with me, you're going to bond with me and you're going to be far more honest with me." Oxytocin, Voss explains, is "bonding and truth." Second, serotonin: "Serotonin is a drug of satisfaction, which means you're going to be less demanding."

The implications are striking. If you can make your negotiation partner feel heard, you simultaneously increase their bonding with you, their honesty toward you, and their willingness to be flexible with their demands. As Voss puts it, "That's a pretty good start for a negotiation."

This explains why the common default approach—rushing to make your case so that you feel heard—often backfires. People "come to the table with such a desire to be heard and to make their case, they can't imagine that's what's driving" the negotiation. That desire "is blinding." By recognizing that the biggest swing you can make is to "let go of my need of that" and instead "hone in on your need for that," you transform yourself from just another person fighting to be heard into someone who creates genuine connection.

Real-World Application: The Art of the Craigslist Deal

The conversation shifts to practical application, with the interviewer asking how Voss handles everyday negotiations like buying a car or purchasing furniture on Craigslist. This is where Voss's principles become concrete.

When buying something from a private seller, Voss says he makes "their case" for them—articulating why what they're selling is worth what they're asking. "That's what they're going to say to me," he notes, "so what I've done is I've just now left them with nothing to say."

This approach mirrors his earlier point about denials: by taking away the other person's ammunition before they can use it, you defuse the adversarial dynamic. The seller can't argue about their own item's value because you've already acknowledged it. This leaves you free to negotiate on price without appearing to dismiss the seller's legitimate stake in the transaction.

Voss is refreshingly candid about the limits of relationship-building in certain contexts. "There are few deals that are complete one-offs," he acknowledges. When buying from a car dealership, where he expects poor service regardless of the outcome, he takes a different approach: "I no longer ever get any vehicle I own serviced at a car dealership. I don't care what the warranty says because I don't trust them." In such cases, he has "no problem" with being aggressive on price.

His technique with car dealers involves making their case first—acknowledging the dealer's legitimate arguments—then becoming "very passive aggressive." His favorite line, delivered after stripping away all the dealer's potential objections, is simply: "How am I supposed to pay that?"

By this point, he's exhausted the dealer's talking points. "If I hadn't said them first," Voss notes, "you would have said all the things that I just got done saying. But now if I said all...took away all the wind out of your sails, took all the bullets out of your gun, then when I give you the opportunity to point the gun at me, you got empty chambers. You got nothing to say."

This allows him to "get you down to your bottom line" quickly, at which point he can decide whether to make a deal or walk away. The caveat Voss offers is crucial: "But I better never need to come back to you because once I've killed you on price, you are not going to forget that and you are not going to lift a finger to help me ever again."

Parenting as Negotiation: The Developmental Framework

When asked about negotiating with his own young children—a 5-year-old, a 4-year-old, and a 1-year-old—Voss offers insights that may surprise parents who expect tactical techniques from a hostage negotiator.

His core advice is philosophical rather than tactical: "With your kids, what you're really trying to do as a parent is help your kids think." He shares a developmental framework he's found useful: up to age five, "treat a kid like a king"; from five to fifteen, "work them like an indentured servant"; at fifteen, "let him go."

This framework, Voss notes, overlaps closely with brain development science. Throughout all stages, though, "all along the way is you want your kids to feel slightly challenged and you want them to get them to think."

The opposite approach—ordering kids around with "Because I said so" or "Because I'm the father"—fails, Voss argues, because it's "not really helping them think. It's demonstrating to them that the only way you can get stuff done is by through the use of force." This doesn't mean tolerating bad behavior, but it does mean recognizing the difference between enforcement and genuine connection.

Voss acknowledges that sometimes you simply have to act—a humorous anecdote about watching a video of a mom trying to get her 5-year-old into an SUV ends with the 9-year-old sister simply picking him up and throwing him in the car. "At some point in time, you got to toss the kid in the car," Voss concedes. But the underlying goal remains helping children develop their own capacity for reasoning, even when immediate compliance is required.

The Easiest Technique to Implement

When pressed to identify the most accessible entry point for someone wanting to improve their negotiation skills, Voss doesn't hesitate: "It really is going to be the different ways to repeat back to somebody what they just said."

He identifies three related techniques that accomplish this: labeling (focusing on the other person's emotions), affect mirrors (reflecting their emotional state), and paraphrases (restating specifically what they said). These techniques "focus specifically on what they said" and, counterintuitively, "seem like a waste of time and it ends up being"—and here the transcript cuts off, but the implication is clear—the most powerful tool in a negotiator's toolkit.

The power of simple reflection lies in its dual function: it ensures you actually understand what the other person is saying (preventing costly misunderstandings) while simultaneously making them feel heard (triggering the oxytocin and serotonin release discussed earlier). It's a technique that costs nothing, requires no special training, and can be deployed in virtually any conversation.

Key Takeaways for Effective Negotiation

Lead with denials of negative perceptions. Before any negotiation, ask yourself what reservations the other party likely has about you. Articulate these concerns yourself before they can raise them. This transforms you from a potential adversary into a straight shooter who acknowledges the obvious.

The order of empathy matters. Address negatives first, then express genuine appreciation for the other person's legitimate accomplishments and expertise. This sequencing—backed by neuroscience—creates the optimal psychological conditions for productive negotiation.

Make them feel understood to get honesty and flexibility. When people feel heard, they release oxytocin (promoting bonding and truth-telling) and serotonin (reducing demanding behavior). Prioritizing the other person's need to feel understood over your own need to make your case is the single biggest shift you can make toward becoming a better negotiator.

Use their arguments before they can use them against you. Whether buying a car on Craigslist or negotiating a business deal, articulating the other party's strongest arguments removes their ability to deploy them. This positions you as fair-minded while giving you control of the negotiation's direction.

Consider whether you'll need the relationship later. Aggressive tactics are appropriate in one-off transactions where relationship continuity doesn't matter. But in ongoing relationships—whether professional or personal—destroying goodwill on price or principle creates costs that may far exceed any immediate gain.

Start practicing with simple reflection. The easiest way to improve your negotiation skills immediately is to begin repeating back what people say to you, labeling their emotions, and paraphrasing their key points. This costs nothing, builds comprehension, and triggers the neurochemical cascade that makes people more cooperative.

Who Benefits Most from This Approach

This content is essential viewing for anyone who negotiates regularly: business leaders, salespeople, managers, real estate professionals, and anyone involved in procurement or contract management. But Voss's insights extend far beyond professional contexts.

Parents of children old enough to reason will find his framework for age-appropriate negotiation particularly valuable. Anyone who has ever felt stuck in an adversarial dynamic—whether with contractors, service providers, or even family members—will recognize the practical applicability of his "denial strategy" and "tactical empathy" concepts.

The underlying principle running through all of Voss's advice is deceptively simple: negotiation is not about winning arguments or extracting concessions. It's about creating the conditions under which other people willingly choose to give you what you want. That requires trust, which requires empathy, which requires first making the other person feel understood. Every technique Voss discusses serves this fundamental aim.
