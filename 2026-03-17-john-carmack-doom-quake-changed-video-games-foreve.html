The Revolutionary Impact of John Carmack and id Software

Wolfenstein 3D: The Birth of the First-Person Shooter

Jeff Kaplan begins by acknowledging the profound debt he owes to the pioneering work of John Carmack and id Software, stating directly that he "wouldn't be sitting here talking to you right now" if not for the breakthroughs they achieved. This is not mere hyperbole—Kaplan's entire career in game development, including his work on some of the most successful massively multiplayer games in history, exists on a foundation that Carmack and his colleagues built through their relentless pursuit of technological advancement.

The conversation centers first on Wolfenstein 3D, which Kaplan describes as the first 3D first-person shooter he ever played. He recalls with remarkable clarity the experience of moving from the 2D version of Wolfenstein, where players "run around, dress up as a German, you throw a grenade," to the revolutionary 3D implementation. Looking back at screenshots or videos of Wolfenstein 3D now, Kaplan admits it seems "almost childish"—the pixelated graphics and simple geometry appear primitive by modern standards. Yet the emotional impact at the time was profound. He describes being "transported" into the game, emphasizing the "intimacy of first person" perspective—putting your hands in front of you holding a gun, being the hero fighting Nazis in Nazi Germany. The game created an unprecedented sense of presence and immersion, despite what would now be considered laughable graphics by any contemporary standard.

Doom and the Evolution of Gameplay Feel

From Wolfenstein 3D, the conversation naturally progresses to Doom, which represented another quantum leap forward in both technology and gameplay design. Kaplan reveals himself to be "a huge Army of Darkness fan," describing his reaction to Doom as essentially "This is Army of Darkness, the video game. Give me the boomstick. Here we go." This connection between the game and Sam Raimi's cult classic film speaks to the visceral, action-oriented nature that Doom brought to the medium.

However, Kaplan is careful to emphasize that Doom's significance extended far beyond its graphical advances. The gameplay feel—the responsiveness, the smoothness, the "sharpness" of the controls—represented a qualitative leap that separated truly great games from mere technical demonstrations. The frame rate improvements, the input responsiveness, the way the game felt in the player's hands: these were the elements that made Doom not just visually impressive but genuinely enjoyable to play. Kaplan explicitly states that players "have to credit id in those days and Carmack and Romero" for this attention to gameplay feel, acknowledging that his own work on first-person shooters "wouldn't have existed without them."

The Threshold of Realism

Perhaps the most philosophically interesting aspect of Kaplan's reflection is his discussion of what he calls "the threshold of realism"—that point at which technological advancement crosses from mere simulation into genuine immersion. He describes this as a threshold "which you pass of realism where you can immerse yourself into that world." His personal memory of the transition from Wolfenstein 2D to Wolfenstein 3D brings tears to his eyes: "this is incredible... it was like ultra realistic." He acknowledges how silly this sounds now but insists that "it was a feeling like you're there." This observation speaks to something fundamental about human perception and emotional response to media—the technology doesn't need to be objectively realistic to create a subjective sense of presence and emotional investment.

Kaplan draws a direct line from these early technological breakthroughs to modern gaming, noting that "a lot of that... is the technology that brings that kind" of immersive experience to life. The storytelling possibilities, the emotional resonance, the sense of actually being present in a virtual world—all of this traces back to the pioneering work of id Software in finding ways to make players feel truly transported into the games they were playing.

Detailed Takeaways

1. The Immersion Threshold Transforms Entertainment

Kaplan's observation about crossing "the threshold of realism" offers profound insight into how technology and artistry combine to create transformative experiences. This threshold isn't about achieving photorealism—it's about creating sufficient coherence and responsiveness that players suspend disbelief and emotionally invest in the virtual world. Game developers must understand that technical metrics matter less than the subjective feeling of presence, which requires attention to every aspect of how a game feels, not just how it looks.

2. Gameplay Feel Is as Important as Graphics

The Doom example powerfully illustrates that technical graphical advances mean nothing without corresponding attention to how the game plays. The "sharpness" of gameplay, the responsiveness of controls, the smoothness of movement—these qualitative factors determined Doom's lasting impact more than its visual innovations. Any developer seeking to create influential games must prioritize gameplay feel alongside visual ambition.

3. Early Adaptation Trumps Perfection

Eric's decision to wildcard in gameweek two, despite having just completed his initial team, demonstrates the value of rapid adaptation over rigid adherence to initial plans. He recognized his mistakes quickly and acted decisively rather than waiting for circumstances to improve on their own. This willingness to accept failure and immediately adjust course—rather than doubling down on failing strategies—proved crucial to his ultimate success.

4. Overthinking Can Be a Competitive Advantage (With Limits)

Eric's admission of extreme overthinking—"I think I've changed captain already six times this week"—presents an interesting paradox. Excessive deliberation typically leads to decision paralysis, yet his approach of thoroughly analyzing decisions "three, four more times than I would normally" appears to contribute to his success. The key seems to be balancing thorough analysis with the ability to commit to decisions once made, rather than constantly second-guessing after the fact.

5. Differentiating While Maintaining Core Selections

Eric's strategy of holding "differentials" that make the difference between top ranks reflects a fundamental tension in competitive games: you need enough popular picks to remain competitive when they succeed, but enough unique selections to climb the rankings when differentials pay off. This requires identifying players who offer both upside potential and reasonable probability of delivering returns—a nuanced assessment that combines statistical analysis with football knowledge.
