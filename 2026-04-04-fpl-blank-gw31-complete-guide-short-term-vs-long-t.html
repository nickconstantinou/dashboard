FPL Blank Gameweek 31 Complete Guide: Strategic Decisions for the Season's Crucial Phase

Introduction

For Fantasy Premier League managers across the world, gameweek 31 represents one of the most anticipated—and dreaded—blank weeks of the 2025/26 season. The long-awaited confirmation arrived this week when Crystal Palace's European victory officially sealed the fate of four major clubs: Arsenal, Wolves, Manchester City, and Crystal Palace will all blank in gameweek 31. With the deadline less than 24 hours away at time of recording, FPL veteran content creator FP Harry emerged to deliver what many managers desperately needed: a comprehensive breakdown of what this confirmation means for chip strategy, transfer decisions, and captaincy choices heading into the season's most critical juncture.

The implications extend far beyond a single blank gameweek. What makes gameweek 31 particularly significant is that it marks the beginning of a complex series of fixture permutations that will determine how managers deploy their remaining chips—wildcards, bench boosts, and free hits. The outcome of the upcoming FA Cup fixture between Liverpool and Manchester City will be the primary determinant of which chip strategy proves optimal, making this week's decisions some of the most consequential of the entire season. FP Harry emphasizes that managers should resist the temptation to act hastily, as premature transfers could lock them into sub-optimal positions before all the information becomes available.

Understanding the Blank Confirmation and What It Means

The crystal-clear confirmation arrived when Crystal Palace progressed in European competition on Thursday evening. This result had been pending for weeks, with managers holding their breath over whether both Palace and Manchester City might suffer early European exits that could have accelerated their postponed fixture into earlier gameweeks. Those "if, buts, and maybes" are now officially resolved: the City-Palace fixture cannot be played in either the midweek of gameweek 31 or gameweek 32.

This means gameweek 32 will proceed as a normal gameweek without the complication of catch-up fixtures. The critical matchup between Manchester City and Crystal Palace will not be rescheduled until at least the midweek of gameweek 33 at the earliest. This deferral has significant implications for double gameweek planning, as gameweek 33 now appears increasingly likely to become the biggest double of the entire season. FP Harry notes that this remains the primary anticipated double gameweek, though nothing is guaranteed until official announcements confirm the fixture list.

The timing of this confirmation matters enormously for team management. With the gameweek 31 deadline looming, managers now know exactly which players to bench and which to potentially captain among the remaining playing teams. However, the real strategic puzzle lies in what happens beyond this single blank—the complex interplay of future blanks, doubles, and chip deployments that will define successful managers' seasons from this point forward.

The Two Chip Strategy Paths: Understanding Your Options

FP Harry identifies two primary chip strategy paths that managers are likely to deploy in the season's final stretch, with the FA Cup result between Liverpool and Manchester City serving as the primary determining factor between them.

Option One: Wildcard 32, Bench Boost 33, Free Hit 34

This strategy becomes more likely if Liverpool defeat Manchester City in the FA Cup semi-final. Under this framework, managers would deploy their wildcard at gameweek 32—a normal gameweek but one that precedes the anticipated double in gameweek 33. By wildcarding during gameweek 32, managers can completely restructure their squads to capitalize on double gameweek opportunities in gameweek 33. The bench boost then comes in gameweek 33, allowing managers to maximize points from their entire squad when multiple fixtures are available. Finally, the free hit arrives in gameweek 34 to navigate whatever blank situation exists that week.

Option Two: Free Hit 33, Wildcard 35, Bench Boost 36

This alternative path becomes more attractive if Manchester City defeat Liverpool in the FA Cup. The reasoning here relates to how fixture congestion and rescheduling would play out depending on which team progresses in cup competition. If City advance, their fixture schedule becomes more complex, potentially creating different double gameweek opportunities in later weeks. Under this scenario, managers would free hit during gameweek 33 to capitalize on early double opportunities, then wildcard at gameweek 35 when the picture becomes clearer, before using bench boost at gameweek 36 as the season approaches its conclusion.

FP Harry acknowledges that this is not an exact science. Even if Manchester City win the FA Cup, some managers might still prefer Option One, and vice versa. The FA Cup result is one of the biggest deciding factors, but individual squad circumstances and personal risk tolerance also play important roles. The key insight is that this decision cannot be made definitively until the FA Cup results are known, making premature transfers particularly dangerous.

The Newcastle Dilemma: Short-Term Appeal vs. Long-Term Strategy

One of the most practically relevant discussions in the video centers on Newcastle players and whether managers should be rushing to acquire them for gameweek 31. FP Harry's assessment is nuanced and worth examining closely.

On the surface, Newcastle looks attractive this week—they have favorable fixtures and sit near the top of projected goal-scoring charts for gameweek 31. Players like Anthony Gordon and Alexander Isak represent appealing options for managers looking to navigate the blank. However, FP Harry explicitly states that he is not forcing himself to buy Newcastle players this week, and he explains precisely why.

The core issue relates to chip strategy flexibility. If managers end up following Option Two (Free Hit 33, Wildcard 35, Bench Boost 36), Newcastle players become significantly less useful. Under that strategy, Newcastle's gameweek 32 fixture against Crystal Palace away would not be particularly valuable—the wildcard comes later. Gameweek 33 is a double, but managers would be using their free hit to navigate that week, meaning they wouldn't necessarily need Newcastle assets during that particular double. Furthermore, Newcastle blank in gameweek 34, which under Option Two would be the free hit week.

In contrast, Newcastle fits much better with Option One. If managers wildcard in gameweek 32 and bench boost in gameweek 33, Newcastle players could be valuable assets to have in the squad for those pivotal double gameweek opportunities.

This analysis demonstrates why FP Harry emphasizes maintaining flexibility. Newcastle players aren't inherently bad picks—they're excellent assets for the right strategy. But without knowing which strategy will ultimately prove optimal, committing to them too early could pigeonhole managers into a specific path they might need to abandon.

Critical Timing Advice: Why You Must Wait

Perhaps the most important practical advice in this entire video comes when FP Harry implores managers to avoid making early wildcard transfers before the FA Cup results are known. His reasoning is detailed and compelling.

The gameweek 31 deadline is followed by an international break, then the FA Cup semi-finals, and then a week later—approximately three weeks from the current moment—gameweek 32 arrives. In that midweek between the FA Cup and gameweek 32, managers will finally begin receiving confirmation about which teams are doubling and blanking in subsequent weeks. This is when the strategic picture becomes clear enough to make informed decisions.

Making transfers now, before those FA Cup results and fixture announcements, carries substantial risk. If something unexpected happens in the FA Cup—perhaps an underdog victory or an unexpected result that affects fixture scheduling—doubles and blanks might go into weeks that managers don't currently anticipate. This would change chip strategies for many managers. Someone who wildcarded early based on one set of assumptions might find themselves locked into a strategy that no longer makes sense.

The potential gain from early transfers is minimal. FP Harry estimates managers might gain 0.2, 0.3, or possibly even 0.5 in value by acting before everyone else. This is not worth the risk of potentially being locked into the wrong chip strategy or the wrong player investments. The information arriving in the next few weeks will be transformative for decision-making, and managers should preserve their optionality until that information becomes available.

This advice extends beyond just wildcard transfers. FP Harry recommends that managers try to be as flexible as possible generally, avoiding excessive use of their limited weekly transfers unless absolutely necessary. The goal should be maintaining maximum optionality to respond to the evolving fixture landscape rather than committing prematurely to specific scenarios.

Captaincy Analysis for Gameweek 31

With gameweek 31 representing a blank for several top teams and relatively low-scoring predictions across the board, FP Harry provides his captaincy rankings with detailed reasoning for each option.

Bruno Fernandes remains number one on FP Harry's rankings despite acknowledging that his away form is noticeably worse than his home form. The reasoning centers on reliability and safety. Fernandes is the most captained player this week, with approximately 75% of managers considering him as their captaincy pick. He offers the highest probability of a goal or assist among available options, is joint highest in form, and appears as the joint highest projected point scorer on the Fantasy Scout members area. Even though Manchester United are away to Bournemouth—a team FP Harry notes has been one of the best home defenses this season—Fernandes's consistent threat under Michael Carrick makes him the safest choice.

Harry Wilson emerges as the best differential alternative for managers looking to go against the popular pick. The data supporting Wilson is specific and interesting: Burnley have conceded significant chances to right wingers throughout the season, making Wilson's positioning particularly advantageous. As a Fulham player at home to Burnley, Wilson represents a calculated risk with statistical backing rather than pure gamble.

Anthony Gordon deserves consideration as an interesting alternative. Newcastle are playing at home to Southampton in what FP Harry describes as a big derby atmosphere. Gordon has been deployed up front for Newcastle, and the team is coming off a heavy Champions League defeat and a derby loss in their previous fixture. This creates motivation for a strong response. Gordon also takes penalties, adding to his appeal. However, FP Harry recognizes that Southampton will be fighting desperately for survival, making this fixture less straightforward than it might appear.

Other options include Cole Palmer away at Everton despite recent poor Chelsea performances, Eberechi Eze for Liverpool (though noting a potential Saturday morning update regarding Mohamed Salah could impact this), and Diogo Jota as another Liverpool option.

FP Harry's overall projection is sobering: every team in gameweek 31 is predicted to score less than two goals. Fulham sit at the top with 1.83 expected goals at home to Burnley, Manchester United at 1.72, and Newcastle at 1.76. Even Chelsea and Liverpool sit at similar numbers. This suggests a week where clean sheets might be limited—perhaps only Fulham and Newcastle offer reasonable clean sheet potential—so differentiation through captaincy could prove particularly valuable.

Recommended Action Points for Managers

FP Harry concludes with several concrete recommendations that managers should internalize before making any decisions.

First, do not make wildcard transfers until after the FA Cup results are known. This cannot be emphasized enough. The potential value gain from acting early is minimal compared to the strategic damage that could result from being locked into the wrong approach.

Second, maintain maximum flexibility in your squad construction. Avoid players who are good only for a single specific scenario. Instead, prioritize players who remain valuable regardless of which chip strategy path the season takes.

Third, don't pigeonhole yourself into one specific scenario. The fixture list will change, unexpected results will occur, and new information will arrive. Successful managers in this period are those who can adapt rather than those who committed early to a particular plan.

Fourth, wait for the official doubles and blanks confirmation before making major roster decisions. This information should arrive before the gameweek 32 deadline, giving managers the clarity they need to execute their strategies effectively.
