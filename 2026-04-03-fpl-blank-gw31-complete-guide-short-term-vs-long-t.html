FPL Blank Gameweek 31 Complete Guide: Short-Term vs Long-Term Strategy

Introduction

Fantasy Premier League managers face one of the most complex decision points of the entire season as Gameweek 31 officially becomes a blank, with the Friday evening deadline less than 24 hours away. After weeks of uncertainty surrounding European competition results, Crystal Palace's advancement on Thursday evening has finally confirmed that Arsenal, Wolves, Manchester City, and Crystal Palace will all blank this gameweek—a fixture situation that cannot be rearranged in either the midweek of Gameweek 31 or 32. The implications stretch far beyond this single blank, however, reshaping the entire strategic landscape for the remaining weeks of the season.

FP Harry, a prominent FPL content creator, breaks down everything managers need to know about navigating this confusing period. With Gameweek 32 set to be a normal gameweek, the focus shifts to Gameweek 33, which is expected to become the biggest double of the season—though exactly which teams will double and when remains shrouded in uncertainty until FA Cup results are resolved. Harry emphasizes that this is not a time for hasty decisions, warning managers against making early wildcard transfers before critical information becomes available. The content specifically addresses two distinct manager groups: those still holding both their wildcard and free hit, and those who have already deployed their wildcard and need alternative strategies.

Understanding the Blank and Its Implications

The Crystal Palace European victory eliminates any remaining ambiguity about Gameweek 31. When Palace progressed in European competition, it confirmed that their fixture against Manchester City—and consequently the individual blanks for Arsenal, Wolves, City, and Palace—could not be played in either the midweek of Gameweek 31 or 32. This means Gameweek 32 will proceed as a completely normal gameweek with all teams in action, and that postponed City-Palace fixture will not be played until at least the midweek of Gameweek 33 at the earliest.

Harry notes that the situation became clear only on Thursday evening, making this one of the latest fixture confirmations in recent memory. The Manchester City loss in European competition was already known, but the critical factor was whether both City and Palace would get knocked out of Europe simultaneously. Since Palace won through, the game cannot be rescheduled earlier than Gameweek 33's midweek slot, eliminating any possibility of catching teams off-guard with early transfers targeting that fixture.

The Two Chip Strategy Frameworks

Harry presents two primary chip deployment strategies that managers should be considering, with the deciding factor being the outcome of the FA Cup semi-final between Liverpool and Manchester City.

Option One: Wildcard 32, Bench Boost 33, Free Hit 34

This strategy becomes more likely if Liverpool defeat Manchester City in the FA Cup. Under this framework, managers would use their wildcard immediately after Gameweek 31 concludes, then deploy bench boost in Gameweek 33 when the biggest double of the season is expected, followed by a free hit in Gameweek 34 to navigate another blank. This approach prioritizes maximizing points during the confirmed double gameweek while accepting limitations in the blank weeks that follow.

Option Two: Free Hit 33, Wildcard 35, Bench Boost 36

This alternative becomes more probable if Manchester City beat Liverpool in the FA Cup. Here, managers would use their free hit in Gameweek 33 when doubles are confirmed, save the wildcard for Gameweek 35 when the landscape becomes clearer, and deploy bench boost in Gameweek 36. Harry acknowledges this is not an exact science—some managers might still prefer Option One even if City win, and vice versa—but the FA Cup result will significantly influence how clubs prioritize their fixture scheduling, particularly regarding potential blanks and doubles in the later weeks.

Harry references the predicted doubles data from FPL.team and Planet FPL, noting that their combined analysis provides fantastic insights at this time of year. Currently, teams like Arsenal, Chelsea (with fixtures against Newcastle and Brighton respectively), Manchester City, and Crystal Palace are predicted to double in Gameweek 33. However, these predictions will only be confirmed before the Gameweek 32 deadline, leaving a significant window of uncertainty for managers trying to make transfer decisions.

The Critical Warning: Don't Transfer Early

Perhaps the most important message in the entire video is Harry's urgent plea for managers not to make early wildcard transfers until after the FA Cup results are known. The timeline creates a natural break: after Gameweek 31, there is an international break followed by the FA Cup, and then a week later comes Gameweek 32. That midweek between the FA Cup and Gameweek 32 is when doubles and blanks will start being officially announced.

Harry explicitly states that making early transfers before this information is available carries significant risk. While some managers might gain 0.2, 0.3, or even 0.5 in player value by moving early, he maintains this is "not worth it at this point in the season" because a single unexpected FA Cup result could completely reshape everyone's chip strategy. If something happens that managers don't expect—such as unexpected results affecting blank and double weeks—those early transfers could leave managers locked into suboptimal strategies.

The advice extends beyond just wildcard timing. Harry recommends that managers should try to stay as flexible as possible this week, avoiding the temptation to use multiple transfers unless absolutely necessary. Rather than targeting short-term gains based on current predictions, managers should focus on buying players who provide value over the long term regardless of which chip strategy they ultimately choose. The goal is to avoid "pigeon holing" yourself into one specific scenario when the information needed to make optimal decisions simply isn't available yet.

Newcastle Players: A Case Study in Strategy Dependency

Harry uses Newcastle players as a specific example of how chip strategy should influence transfer decisions. Players like Malick O払ery and Anthony Gordon are popular targets this week given Newcastle's favorable fixtures, and while Harry acknowledges they are good picks, he explains why blindly targeting them could be problematic.

Newcastle's upcoming schedule looks attractive on the surface: a good fixture in Gameweek 31, Sunderland at home in Gameweek 32, and Crystal Palace away in Gameweek 33. However, Harry points out that if managers end up on Option Two of the chip strategy (free hit 33, wildcard 35, bench boost 36), Newcastle players become significantly less useful. In Gameweek 33, Newcastle would be on a double—so managers using a free hit that week wouldn't need Newcastle players because they could include multiple Newcastle assets without using their own squad slots. Then in Gameweek 34, Newcastle blank, meaning those expensive players provide no value during a critical blank week.

This is why Harry reveals that he is not forcing himself to buy Newcastle players this week despite their obvious appeal. Unless he commits fully to Strategy One, the investment doesn't make sense over the longer term. This demonstrates how the uncertainty around chip strategy should influence present-day transfer decisions, even when the immediate fixtures look excellent.

Captaincy Rankings for Gameweek 31

With all teams predicted to score fewer than two goals in Gameweek 31, Harry acknowledges that the differentiation between captains is minimal, but he still provides his rankings for managers weighing their options.

Bruno Fernandez remains the number one pick despite his away form being notably worse than his home form. Harry estimates approximately 75% of managers are considering Fernandez as their captain pick heading into Friday's deadline. The reasoning is straightforward: Fernandez has the highest probability of a goal or assist this week, is in the best form, and is the joint highest projected point scorer according to FPL Scout's members area. While the Bournemouth home defense has been one of the best in the league this season, Harry maintains that Bruno's consistency under Michael Carrick—with his assist threat particularly strong—makes him the safest choice for those prioritizing reliability over ceiling.

Harry Wilson emerges as the best differential alternative for managers considering going against Bruno. Harry cites specific data showing Burnley have conceded significant chances to right wingers throughout the season, making Wilson an attractive option outside of the obvious captaincy picks. Wilson offers genuine game-winning potential at a lower ownership percentage, which becomes valuable in mini-league battles where differential captains can swing rankings significantly.

Anthony Gordon receives consideration as Newcastle face Sunderland in a local derby. Gordon is playing up front and has shown capable of contributing across multiple positions. Harry notes that Newcastle lost heavily in the Champions League recently and lost the derby last time out, meaning the team will be motivated to secure their best possible result. Gordon also takes penalties, adding to his appeal for home fixtures against weaker opposition like Sunderland.

Noni Madueke and Cole Palmer represent Chelsea options away at Everton, though Harry acknowledges Chelsea's recent performances have been poor, creating some risk in backing Chelsea attacking assets this week.

Eberechi Eze remains a consideration for Liverpool despite potential updates on Mohamed Salah that could emerge. Harry expects Eze to start given Liverpool's demonstrated inability to perform well without him—he is described as their main goal threat and the player the team revolves around. However, this ranking depends heavily on the Salah situation, which Harry flags as something that might be updated before the deadline stream.
